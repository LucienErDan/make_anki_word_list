deranged,&quot; late 14c., an alteration of <span class="foreign">distract</span> (mid-14c.), which in its older form is long obsolete, a past-participle adjective from the Middle English verb <span class="foreign">distracten</span> or else from Latin <span class="foreign">distractus</span> &quot;distracted, perplexed,&quot; past participle of <span class="foreign">distrahere</span> &quot;draw in different directions,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dis-</span> &quot;away&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">dis-</span>) + <span class="foreign">trahere</span> &quot;to draw&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">tract</span> (n.1)).</p>

<p>The Middle English alteration in form is perhaps by association with native past-participle forms in <span class="foreign">-ght</span>, such as <span class="foreign">caught, bought, taught, brought</span>. The traditional guess is that it is literally &quot;the unforgetting,&quot; from <span class="foreign">a-</span> &quot;not&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">a-</span> (3)) + root of <span class="foreign">lathein</span> &quot;to forget,&quot; aorist of <span class="foreign">lanthanein</span> &quot;to lie hidden, escape notice,&quot; from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ladh-</span> &quot;to be hidden&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">latent</span>). The Germanic word is from PIE <span class="foreign">*leus-</span>, an extended form of root <span class="crossreference">*leu-</span> &quot;to loosen, divide, cut apart.&quot;</p>
<p>The verb also is merged with, or has taken the (weaker) sense of, the related Middle English <span class="foreign">leese</span> &quot;be deprived of, lose&quot; (Old English <span class="foreign">leosan</span>, a class II strong verb whose past participle <span class="foreign">loren</span> survives in <span class="crossreference">forlorn</span> and <span class="crossreference">love-lorn</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*leusanan</span> (source also of Old High German <span class="foreign">virliosan</span>, German <span class="foreign">verlieren</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">urliasa</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fraliusan</span> &quot;to lose&quot;).</p>
<p>Hence <span class="foreign">lose</span> in the transitive senses &quot;part with accidentally, be deprived of, miss the possession or knowledge of&quot; (money, blood, sleep, hair, etc.), c. 1200; &quot;fail to keep, lose track of&quot; (mid-13c.). Meaning &quot;fail to preserve or maintain&quot; is from mid-15c. Meaning &quot;fail to gain or win&quot; (something) is from c.1300; intransitive meaning &quot;fail to win&quot; (a game, contest, lawsuit, etc.) is from late 14c. Meaning &quot;to cause (someone) to lose his way&quot; is from 1640s; meaning &quot;cease to have, be rid of&quot; (something unwanted) is from 1660s.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>lose heart</strong></span> &quot;become discouraged&quot; is from 1744; to <span class="foreign"><strong>lose (one&#x27;s) heart</strong></span> &quot;fall in love&quot; is from 1630s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>lose (one&#x27;s) mind</strong></span> &quot;become insane&quot; is attested from c. 1500. To <span class="foreign"><strong>lose out</strong></span> &quot;fail&quot; is 1858, American English. To <span class="foreign"><strong>lose it</strong></span> &quot;become distraught, break down and lose control of oneself&quot; is by 1990s; the <span class="foreign">it</span> probably being one&#x27;s self-control or grip on reality.
