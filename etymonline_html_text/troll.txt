and meaning of troll" href="/word/troll#etymonline_v_17856"><em>troll</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;to go about, stroll,&quot; later (early 15c.) &quot;roll from side to side, trundle,&quot; probably from Old French <span class="foreign">troller</span>, a hunting term, &quot;wander, to go in quest of game without purpose&quot; (Modern French <span class="foreign">trôler</span>), from a Germanic source (compare Old High German <span class="foreign">trollen</span> &quot;to walk with short steps&quot;), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*truzlanan</span>.</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;sing in a full, rolling voice&quot; (first attested 1570s) and that of &quot;fish with a moving line&quot; (c. 1600) both are extended technical uses from the general sense of &quot;roll, trundle,&quot; the former from &quot;sing in the manner of a catch or round,&quot; the latter perhaps confused with <span class="crossreference">trail</span> or <span class="crossreference">trawl</span>. Figurative sense of &quot;to lure on as with a moving bait, entice, allure&quot; is from 1560s. Meaning &quot;to cruise in search of sexual encounters&quot; is recorded from 1967, originally in homosexual slang.</p>
<p>The internet sense (everyone seems to have his own definition of it) seems to date to the late 1980s or early 1990s and the Newsgroups era, and the verbal use is perhaps older than the noun. It seems to combine <span class="foreign">troll</span> (v.) in the &quot;fish with a moving line&quot; sense (itself confused with <span class="foreign">trawl</span>) and <span class="crossreference">troll</span> (n.1) &quot;troublesome imp supposed to live underground.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/troll#etymonline_v_17856"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of troll" href="/word/troll#etymonline_v_17857"><em>troll</em> (n.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>supernatural being in Scandinavian mythology and folklore, 1610s (with an isolated use mid-14c.), from Old Norse <span class="foreign">troll</span> &quot;giant being not of the human race, evil spirit, monster.&quot; Some speculate that it originally meant &quot;creature that walks clumsily,&quot; and derives from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*truzlan</span>, from <span class="foreign">*truzlanan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">troll</span> (v.)). But it seems to have been a general supernatural word, such as Swedish <span class="foreign">trolla</span> &quot;to charm, bewitch;&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">trolldomr</span> &quot;witchcraft.&quot;</p>
<p>The old sagas tell of the <span class="foreign">troll-bull</span>, a supernatural being in the form of a bull, as well as <span class="foreign">boar-trolls</span>. There were <span class="foreign">troll-maidens, troll-wives</span>, and <span class="foreign">troll-women</span>; the <span class="foreign">trollman</span>, a magician or wizard, and the <span class="foreign">troll-drum</span>, used in Lappish magic rites. The word was popularized in literary English by 19c. antiquarians, but it has been current in the Shetlands and Orkneys since Viking times. The first record of the word in modern English is from a court document from the Shetlands, regarding a certain Catherine, who, among other things, was accused of &quot;airt and pairt of witchcraft and sorcerie, in hanting and seeing the Trollis ryse out of the kyrk yeard of Hildiswick.&quot;</p>
<p>Originally conceived as a race of malevolent giants, they have suffered the same fate as the Celtic <span class="foreign">Danann</span> and by 19c. were regarded by peasants in in Denmark and Sweden as dwarfs and imps supposed to live in caves or under the ground.</p>
<blockquote>They are obliging and neighbourly; freely lending and borrowing, and elsewise keeping up a friendly intercourse with mankind. But they have a sad propensity to thieving, not only stealing provisions, but even women and children.
