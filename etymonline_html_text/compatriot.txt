<br/><br/>
In English it began to be used 1760s, by orientalists and linguists (Alexander Dow, William Jones), in historical contexts, and usually with a footnote identifying it with modern Persia; as recently as 1903 &quot;Century Dictionary&quot; defined it as &quot;the ancient name of the region lying between Kurdistan and India.&quot; In 1935 the government of Reza Shah Pahlavi requested governments with which it had diplomatic relations to call his country <span class="foreign">Iran</span>, after the indigenous name, rather than the Greek-derived <span class="crossreference">Persia</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Iran#etymonline_v_12217"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of patriot" href="/word/patriot#etymonline_v_10166">patriot (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1590s, &quot;compatriot,&quot; from Middle French <span class="foreign">patriote</span> (15c.) and directly from Late Latin <span class="foreign">patriota</span> &quot;fellow-countryman&quot; (6c.), from Greek <span class="foreign">patriotes</span> &quot;fellow countryman,&quot; from <span class="foreign">patrios</span> &quot;of one&#x27;s fathers,&quot; <span class="foreign">patris</span> &quot;fatherland,&quot; from <span class="foreign">pater</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">patros</span>) &quot;father&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">father</span> (n.)); with <span class="foreign">-otes</span>, suffix expressing state or condition. Liddell &amp; Scott write that <span class="foreign">patriotes</span> was &quot;applied to barbarians who had only a common [patris], [politai] being used of Greeks who had a common [polis] (or <span class="foreign">free-state</span>).&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;loyal and disinterested supporter of one&#x27;s country&quot; is attested from c. 1600, but became an ironic term of ridicule or abuse from mid-18c. in England, so that Johnson, who at first defined it as &quot;one whose ruling passion is the love of his country,&quot; in his fourth edition added, &quot;It is sometimes used for a factious disturber of the government.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The name of patriot had become [c. 1744] a by-word of derision. Horace Walpole scarcely exaggerated when he said that ... the most popular declaration which a candidate could make on the hustings was that he had never been and never would be a patriot. [Macaulay, &quot;Horace Walpole,&quot; 1833]</blockquote>
<p>Somewhat revived in reference to resistance movements in overrun countries in World War II, it has usually had a positive sense in American English, where the phony and rascally variety has been consigned to the word <span class="foreign">patrioteer</span> (1928). Oriana Fallaci [&quot;The Rage and the Pride,&quot; 2002] marvels that Americans, so fond of <span class="crossreference">patriotic</span>, <span class="foreign">patriot</span>, and <span class="crossreference">patriotism</span>, lack the root noun and are content to express the idea of <span class="foreign">patria</span> by cumbersome compounds such as <span class="foreign">homeland</span>. (Joyce, Shaw, and H.G. Wells all used <span class="foreign">patria</span> as an English word early 20c., but it failed to stick.) <span class="foreign">Patriots&#x27; Day</span> (April 19, anniversary of the 1775 skirmishes at Lexington and Concord Bridge) was observed as a legal holiday in Maine and Massachusetts from 1894.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/patriot#etymonline_v_10166"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Aryan" href="/word/Aryan#etymonline_v_17058">Aryan </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1600, as a term in classical history, from Latin <span class="foreign">Arianus</span>, <span class="foreign">Ariana</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">Aria</span>, <span class="foreign">Areia</span>, names applied in classical times to the eastern part of ancient Persia and to its inhabitants. Ancient Persians used the name in reference to themselves (Old Persian <span class="foreign">ariya-</span>), hence <span class="crossreference">Iran</span>. Ultimately from Sanskrit <span class="foreign">arya-</span> &quot;compatriot;&quot; in later language &quot;noble, of good family.&quot;</p>
<p>Also the name Sanskrit-speaking invaders of India gave themselves in the ancient texts. Thus it was the word early 19c. European philologists (Friedrich Schlegel, 1819, who linked it with German <span class="foreign">Ehre</span> &quot;honor&quot;) applied to the ancient people we now call Indo-Europeans, suspecting that this is what they called themselves. This use is attested in English from 1851. In German from 1845 it was specifically contrasted to <span class="foreign">Semitic</span> (Lassen).</p>
<p>German philologist Max Müller (1823-1900) popularized <span class="foreign">Aryan</span> in his writings on comparative linguistics, recommending it as the name (replacing <span class="foreign">Indo-European</span>, <span class="foreign">Indo-Germanic</span>, <span class="crossreference">Caucasian</span>, <span class="crossreference">Japhetic</span>) for the group of related, inflected languages connected with these peoples, mostly found in Europe but also including Sanskrit and Persian. The spelling <span class="foreign">Arian</span> was used in this sense from 1839 (and is more philologically correct), but it caused confusion with <span class="crossreference">Arian</span>, the term in ecclesiastical history.</p>
<blockquote>The terms for God, for house, for father, mother, son, daughter, for dog and cow, for heart and tears, for axe and tree, identical in all the Indo-European idioms, are like the watchwords of soldiers. We challenge the seeming stranger; and whether he answer with the lips of a Greek, a German, or an Indian, we recognize him as one of ourselves. [Müller, &quot;History of Ancient Sanskrit Literature,&quot; 1859]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Aryan</span> was gradually replaced in comparative linguistics c. 1900 by <span class="crossreference">Indo-European</span>, except when used to distinguish Indo-European languages of India from non-Indo-European ones. From the 1920s <span class="foreign">Aryan</span> began to be used in Nazi ideology to mean &quot;member of a Caucasian Gentile race of Nordic type.&quot; As an ethnic designation, however, it is properly limited to Indo-Iranians (most justly to the latter) and has fallen from general academic use since the Nazis adopted it.</p></section></object><a data-more="true"
