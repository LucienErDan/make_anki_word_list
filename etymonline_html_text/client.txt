Also from c. 1300 in reference to answering for one&#x27;s conduct, especially at the Last Judgment. Meaning &quot;estimation, consideration,&quot; especially in the eyes of others, is from late 14c.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>On account</strong></span> in the financial sense &quot;as an item to be accounted for at the final settlement&quot; is from 1610s, hence <span class="foreign"><strong>on account of</strong></span> in the general sense &quot;for the sake of, in regard to, in consideration of&quot; (1640s, originally <span class="foreign">upon account of</span>). Also <span class="foreign">on (my, your, etc.) account</span> &quot;on (one&#x27;s) behalf.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>give accounts</strong></span> &quot;prepare or present a statement of funds and property&quot; is from mid-15c; the older term was <span class="foreign">cast accounts</span> (mid-14c.); to <span class="foreign"><strong>take account of</strong></span> originally was to make an inventory; <span class="foreign"><strong>take into account</strong></span> &quot;take account of&quot; is from 1680s.</p>
<blockquote>The spellings <span class="foreign">accompt</span>, <span class="foreign">accomptable</span>, etc. are artificial forms used, not prevailingly, in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. They are now obsolete, or nearly so, though <span class="foreign">accompt</span> and <span class="foreign">accomptant</span> may still be used in the formal or legal style. The pronunciation has always conformed to the regular spelling, <span class="foreign">account</span>, <span class="foreign">accountable</span>, etc. [Century
