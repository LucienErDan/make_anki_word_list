<p>Sense shifted in Middle English to denote association, combination, and union, partly by influence of Old Norse <span class="foreign">vidh</span>, and also perhaps by Latin <span class="foreign">cum</span> &quot;with&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">pugnare cum</span> &quot;fight with&quot;). In this sense, it replaced Old English <span class="foreign">mid</span> &quot;with,&quot; which survives only as a prefix (as in <span class="crossreference">midwife</span>). Original sense of &quot;against, in opposition&quot; is retained in compounds such as <span class="crossreference">withhold</span>, <span class="crossreference">withdraw</span>, <span class="crossreference">withstand</span>.</p>
<p>Often treated as a conjunction by ungrammatical writers and used where <span class="foreign">and</span> would be correct. <br/><br/>
That is the suggestion in Barnhart, but other sources regard the &quot;among, between, before, in the presence of&quot; sense of Old English prefix and preposition <span class="foreign">under</span> as other meanings of the same word. &quot;Among&quot; seems to be the sense in many Old English compounds that resemble <span class="foreign">understand</span>, such as <span class="foreign">underniman</span> &quot;to receive,&quot; <span class="foreign">undersecan</span> &quot;examine, investigate, scrutinize&quot; (literally &quot;underseek&quot;), <span class="foreign">underðencan</span> &quot;consider, change one&#x27;s mind,&quot; <span class="foreign">underginnan</span> &quot;to begin.&quot; It also seems to be the sense still in expressions such as <span class="foreign">under such circumstances</span>.
<br/><br/>
Perhaps the ultimate sense is &quot;be close to;&quot; compare Greek <span class="foreign">epistamai</span> &quot;I know how, I know,&quot; literally &quot;I stand upon.&quot; Similar formations are found in Old Frisian (<span class="foreign">understonda</span>), Middle Danish (<span class="foreign">understande</span>), while other Germanic languages use compounds meaning &quot;stand before&quot; (German <span class="foreign">verstehen</span>, represented in Old English by <span class="foreign">forstanden</span> &quot;understand,&quot; also &quot;oppose, withstand&quot;). For this concept, most Indo-European languages use figurative extensions of compounds that literally mean &quot;put together,&quot; or &quot;separate,&quot; or &quot;take, grasp&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">comprehend</span>). Old English <span class="foreign">oferstandan</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">overstonden</span>, literally &quot;over-stand&quot; seem to have been used only in literal senses. <p>To <span class="foreign">take it on the chin</span> &quot;be hit hard&quot; in a figurative sense (sometimes suggesting &quot;ability to withstand punishment&quot;), is from 1924, an image from pugilism.
