<p>Meaning &quot;solid mineral consisting of fossilized carbon, combustible and used as fuel,&quot; is from mid-13c. The thing itself is mentioned 370 B.C.E. by Theophrastus in his treatise &quot;On Stones&quot; under the name <span class="foreign">lithos anthrakos</span> (see <span class="crossreference">anthrax</span>). Traditionally good luck, coal was given as a New Year&#x27;s gift in England, said to guarantee a warm hearth for the coming year.</p>
<p>The phrase <span class="foreign">drag (or rake) over the coals</span> was a reference to the treatment meted out to heretics by Christians. To <span class="foreign">carry coals</span> &quot;do dirty work,&quot; also &quot;submit to insult&quot; is from 1520s.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign">carry coals to Newcastle</span> &quot;add to that of which there is already an abundance, do unnecessary labor &quot; (c. 1600) is a local variant on an ancient class of expression: Latin had <span class="foreign">in litus harenas fundere</span> &quot;pour sand on the beach,&quot; <span class="foreign">in silvam ligna ferre</span> &quot;carry wood to the woods;&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">glauk eis Athenas</span> &quot;owls to Athens.&quot; Newcastle is in the midst of a great coal-producing region.
