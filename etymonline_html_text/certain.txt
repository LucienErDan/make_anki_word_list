title="Origin and meaning of certain" href="/word/certain#etymonline_v_8371"><em>certain</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;determined, fixed,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">certain</span> &quot;reliable, sure, assured&quot; (12c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*certanus</span>, extended form of Latin <span class="foreign">certus</span> &quot;&quot;determined, resolved, fixed settled,&quot; of things whose qualities are invariable, &quot;established, settled, fixed,&quot; also &quot;placed beyond doubt, sure, true, proved, established; unerring, fixed, to be depended upon&quot; (also source of Old French <span class="foreign">cert</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">certo</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">cierto</span>), originally a variant past participle of <span class="foreign">cernere</span> &quot;to distinguish, decide,&quot; literally &quot;to sift, separate.&quot; This Latin verb comes from the PIE root <span class="crossreference">*krei-</span> &quot;to sieve,&quot; thus &quot;discriminate, distinguish,&quot; which is also the source of Greek <span class="foreign">krisis</span> &quot;turning point, judgment, result of a trial&quot; (compare <span class="crossreference">crisis</span>).</p>
<p>Transferred sense, in reference to persons, &quot;full of confidence in one&#x27;s knowledge or judgment, made certain in reference to a matter or thing,&quot; from mid-14c. (also a sense in Latin). Meaning &quot;established as true beyond doubt&quot; in English is from c. 1400. Meaning &quot;indefinite, not specifically named, known but not described&quot; is from late 14c.</p>
<blockquote>Different as this seems to be from sense I, it is hardly separable from it in a large number of examples: thus, in [<span class="foreign">a certain hour</span>], the <span class="foreign">hour</span> was quite &#x27;certain&#x27; or &#x27;fixed&#x27;, but it is not communicated to the reader; to him it remains, so far as his knowledge is concerned, quite indefinite; it may have been, <span class="foreign">as far as he knows</span>, at any hour; though, <span class="foreign">as a fact</span>, it was at a particular hour. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Lewis &amp; Short write that Latin <span class="foreign">certus</span> also was sometimes indefinite, &quot;of things, the certainty of whose existence is given, but whose nature is not more definitely designated, or comes not into consideration ....&quot;</p>
<p>Hence the euphemistic use, attested from mid-18c., as in <span class="foreign">woman of a certain age</span> &quot;an old maid;&quot; <span class="foreign">woman of a certain description</span> &quot;disreputable woman;&quot; <span class="foreign">in a certain condition</span> &quot;pregnant;&quot; <span class="foreign">a certain disease</span> &quot;venereal disease;&quot; <span class="foreign">of a certain weight</span> &quot;obese.&quot; Used with proper names from 1785, &quot;often conveying a slight shade of disdain&quot; [OED]. <span class="foreign">Certainer</span>, <span class="foreign">certainest</span> were common to c. 1750, but have fallen from proper use for some reason. Expression <span class="foreign">for certain</span> &quot;assuredly&quot; is attested by early 14c.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/certain#etymonline_v_8371"><span>Relate entries &amp;
