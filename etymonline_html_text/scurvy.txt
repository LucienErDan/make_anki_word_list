<p>Not used as a color word until 1540s (colors similar to modern orange in Middle English might be called <span class="foreign">citrine</span> or <span class="foreign">saffron</span>). Loss of initial <span class="foreign">n-</span> probably is due to confusion with definite article (as in <span class="foreign">une narange, una narancia</span>), but perhaps also influence of French <span class="foreign">or</span> &quot;gold.&quot; The name of the town of <span class="foreign">Orange</span> in France (see <span class="crossreference">Orangemen</span>) perhaps was deformed by the name of the fruit. <span class="foreign">Orange juice</span> is attested from 1723.</p>
<p>The tree&#x27;s original range probably was northern India. The Persian orange, grown widely in southern Europe after its introduction in Italy 11c., was bitter; sweet oranges were brought to Europe 15c. from India by Portuguese traders and quickly displaced the bitter variety, but only Modern Greek still seems to distinguish the bitter (<span class="foreign">nerantzi</span>) from the sweet (<span class="foreign">portokali</span> &quot;Portuguese&quot;) orange.</p>
<p>Portuguese, Spanish, Arab, and Dutch sailors planted citrus trees along trade routes to prevent scurvy. On his second voyage in 1493, Christopher Columbus brought the seeds of oranges, lemons and citrons to Haiti and the Caribbean. Introduced in Florida (along with lemons) in 1513 by Spanish explorer Juan Ponce de Leon.
