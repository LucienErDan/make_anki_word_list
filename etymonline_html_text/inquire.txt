<br/><br/>
In Church history, inquisitors were appointed from 382 C.E. to root out heretics; the ecclesiastical court appointed 13c. by Innocent III to suppress heresy never operated in Britain. The English word began to be used in this sense (and with a capital initial letter) after c. 1500, and usually refers to the office&#x27;s reorganization 1478-1483 in Spain, where it fell under the control of the state as what is commonly called the <span class="foreign">Spanish Inquisition</span>, noted especially for its severity, secrecy, and the number of its victims.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/inquisition#etymonline_v_9311"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of in-" href="/word/in-#etymonline_v_6285">in- (2)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>element meaning &quot;into, in, on, upon&quot; (also <span class="foreign">im-</span>, <span class="foreign">il-</span>, <span class="foreign">ir-</span> by assimilation of <span class="foreign">-n-</span> with following consonant), from Latin <span class="foreign">in-</span> &quot;in,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*en</span> &quot;in.&quot;</p>

<p>In Old French (and hence in Middle English) this often became <span class="foreign">en-</span>, which in English had a strong tendency to revert to Latin <span class="foreign">in-</span>, but not always, which accounts for pairs such as <span class="crossreference">enquire</span>/<span class="crossreference">inquire</span>. There was a native form, which in West Saxon usually appeared as <span class="foreign">on-</span> (as in Old English <span class="foreign">onliehtan</span> &quot;to enlighten&quot;), and some of those verbs survived into Middle English (such as <span class="foreign">inwrite</span> &quot;to inscribe&quot;), but all now seem to be extinct.</p>
<p>Not related to <span class="crossreference">in-</span> (1) &quot;not,&quot; which also was a common prefix in Latin, causing confusion: to the Romans <span class="foreign">impressus</span> could mean &quot;pressed&quot; or &quot;unpressed;&quot; <span class="foreign">inaudire</span> meant &quot;to hear,&quot; but <span class="foreign">inauditus</span> meant &quot;unheard of;&quot; in Late Latin <span class="foreign">investigabilis</span> could mean &quot;that may be searched into&quot; or &quot;that cannot be searched into.&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">invocatus</span> was &quot;uncalled, uninvited,&quot; but <span class="foreign">invocare</span> was &quot;to call, appeal to.&quot;</p>

<p>The trouble has continued in English; the hesitation over what is meant by <span class="foreign">inflammable</span> being a commonly cited example. <span class="foreign">Implume</span> (1610s) meant &quot;to feather,&quot; but <span class="foreign">implumed</span> (c. 1600) meant &quot;unfeathered.&quot; <span class="foreign">Impliable</span> can mean &quot;capable of being implied&quot; (1865) or &quot;inflexible&quot; (1734). <span class="foreign">Impartible</span> in 17c. could mean &quot;incapable of being divided&quot; or &quot;capable of being imparted.&quot; <span class="foreign">Impassionate</span> can be &quot;free from passion&quot; or it can mean &quot;strongly stirred by passion.&quot; <span class="foreign">Inanimate</span> (adj.) is &quot;lifeless,&quot; but Donne uses <span class="foreign">inanimate</span> (v.) to mean &quot;infuse with life or vigor.&quot; <span class="foreign">Irruption</span> is &quot;a breaking in,&quot; but <span class="foreign">irruptible</span> is &quot;unbreakable.&quot;</p>

<p>In addition to <span class="foreign">improve</span> &quot;use to one&#x27;s profit,&quot; Middle English also had a verb <span class="foreign">improve</span> meaning &quot;to disprove&quot; (15c.). To <span class="foreign">inculpate</span> is &quot;to accuse,&quot; but <span class="foreign">inculpable</span> means &quot;not culpable, free from blame.&quot; <span class="foreign">Infestive</span> has meant &quot;troublesome, annoying&quot; (1560s, from <span class="foreign">infest</span>) and &quot;not festive&quot; (1620s). In Middle English <span class="foreign">inflexible</span> could mean &quot;incapable of being bent&quot; or &quot;capable of being swayed or moved.&quot; In 17c., <span class="foreign">informed</span> could mean &quot;current in information,&quot; formed, animated,&quot; or &quot;unformed, formless&quot; (&quot;This was an awkward use&quot; [OED]). <span class="foreign">Inhabited</span> has meant &quot;dwelt in&quot; (1560s) and &quot;uninhabited&quot; (1610s); <span class="foreign">inhabitable</span> likewise has been used on opposite senses, a confusion that goes back to Late Latin.</p>
