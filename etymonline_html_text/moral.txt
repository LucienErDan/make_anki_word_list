undefined"><p>mid-14c., &quot;associated with or characterized by right behavior,&quot; also &quot;associated with or concerning conduct or moral principles&quot; (good or bad), from Old French <span class="foreign">moral</span> (14c.) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">moralis</span> &quot;proper behavior of a person in society,&quot; literally &quot;pertaining to manners,&quot; coined by Cicero (<span class="foreign">&quot;De Fato,&quot;</span> II.i) to translate Greek <span class="foreign">ethikos</span> (see <span class="crossreference">ethics</span>) from Latin <span <p>From late 14c. as &quot;of or pertaining to rules of right conduct&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">non-moral, amoral</span>) and &quot;morally good, in accordance with rules of right conduct&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">immoral</span>). Of persons, &quot;habitually conforming to moral rules,&quot; 1630s. From 1680s with reference to rights, duties, etc., &quot;founded on morality&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">legal</span>).</p>

<p>Applied to indirect effect in <span class="foreign"><strong>moral support</strong></span> (1823), <span class="foreign"><strong>moral victory</strong></span> (1888), where the notion is &quot;pertaining to or affecting the character or conduct&quot; (as distinguished from the intellectual or physical nature), a sense attested from 1590s; in this sense, compare <span class="crossreference">morale</span>.
