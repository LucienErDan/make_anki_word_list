<p>In classical writings, an Egyptian animal of some sort, the mortal enemy of the crocodile, which it tracks down and kills. This vague sense became hopelessly confused in the Christian West, and in England the word ended up applied to the equivalent of the <span class="crossreference">basilisk</span>. Popularly associated with <span class="foreign">cock</span> (n.1), hence the fable that it was a serpent hatched from a cock&#x27;s egg. It also sometimes was confused with the crocodile. Belief in them persisted even among the educated because the word was used in the KJV several times to translate a Hebrew word for &quot;serpent.&quot; In heraldry, a beast half cock, half serpent.
