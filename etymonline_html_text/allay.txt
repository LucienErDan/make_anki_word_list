<p>In words derived from Old English, it commonly represents Old English <span class="foreign">an</span> &quot;on, in, into&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">on</span> (prep.)), as in <span class="foreign">alive</span>, <span class="foreign">above</span>, <span class="foreign">asleep</span>, <span class="foreign">aback</span>, <span class="foreign">abroad</span>, <span class="foreign">afoot</span>, <span class="foreign">ashore</span>, <span class="foreign">ahead</span>, <span class="foreign">abed</span>, <span class="foreign">aside</span>, etc., forming adjectives and adverbs from nouns, with the notion &quot;in, at; engaged in.&quot; In this use it is identical to <span class="crossreference">a</span> (2).</p>
<p>It also can represent Middle English <span class="crossreference">of</span> (prep.) &quot;off, from,&quot; as in <span class="foreign">anew</span>, <span class="foreign">afresh</span>, <span class="foreign">akin</span>, <span class="foreign">abreast</span>. <p>Or it can be the Old English intensive <span class="foreign">a-</span>, originally <span class="foreign">ar-</span> (cognate with German <span class="foreign">er-</span> and probably implying originally &quot;motion away from&quot;), as in <span class="foreign">abide</span>, <span class="foreign">arise</span>, <span class="foreign">awake</span>, <span class="foreign">ashamed</span>, marking a verb as momentary, a single event. <p>In words from Romanic languages, often it represents reduced forms of Latin <span class="foreign">ad</span> &quot;to, toward; for&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ad-</span>), or <span class="foreign">ab</span> &quot;from, away, off&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ab-</span>); both of which by about 7c. had been reduced to <span class="foreign">a</span> in the ancestor of Old French.
