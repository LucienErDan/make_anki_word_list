<p>From the first often in plural form; sometimes in late Middle English <span class="foreign">accompt</span> (see <span class="crossreference">account</span> (v.)). Meaning &quot;course of business dealings requiring records&quot; is from 1640s; hence &quot;arrangement to keep money in a business, bank, etc.&quot; (1833), also &quot;customer or client having an account&quot; (1937). <span class="foreign"><strong>Money of account</strong></span> (1690s), that used in reckoning but not circulating as coin or paper, preserves the &quot;counting&quot; sense of the word.</p>
<p>From the notion of &quot;rendering an account&quot; comes the sense &quot;statement answering for conduct&quot; (mid-14c.) and the general sense &quot;narration, recital of facts,&quot; attested by 1610s. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>by all accounts</strong></span> is attested from 1798. From the notion of &quot;statement of reasons&quot; comes <span class="foreign"><strong>on no account</strong></span> &quot;under no circumstances&quot; (1704). Also from c. 1300 in reference to answering for one&#x27;s conduct, especially at the Last Judgment. Meaning &quot;estimation, consideration,&quot; especially in the eyes of others, is from late 14c.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>On account</strong></span> in the financial sense &quot;as an item to be accounted for at the final settlement&quot; is from 1610s, hence <span class="foreign"><strong>on account of</strong></span> in the general sense &quot;for the sake of, in regard to, in consideration of&quot; (1640s, originally <span class="foreign">upon account of</span>). Also <span class="foreign">on (my, your, etc.) account</span> &quot;on (one&#x27;s) behalf.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>give accounts</strong></span> &quot;prepare or present a statement of funds and property&quot; is from mid-15c; the older term was <span class="foreign">cast accounts</span> (mid-14c.); to <span class="foreign"><strong>take account of</strong></span> originally was to make an inventory; <span class="foreign"><strong>take into account</strong></span> &quot;take account of&quot; is from 1680s.</p>
<blockquote>The spellings <span class="foreign">accompt</span>, <span class="foreign">accomptable</span>, etc. are artificial forms used, not prevailingly, in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. They are now obsolete, or nearly so, though <span class="foreign">accompt</span> and <span class="foreign">accomptant</span> may still be used in the formal or legal style.
