Originally a blood sprinkling on pagan altars.
<br/><br/>
This word was chosen in Old English bibles to translate Latin <span class="foreign">benedicere</span> and Greek <span class="foreign">eulogein</span>, both of which have a ground sense of &quot;to speak well of, to praise,&quot; but were used in Scripture to translate Hebrew <span class="foreign">brk</span> &quot;to bend (the knee), worship, praise, invoke blessings.&quot; L.R. Palmer (&quot;The Latin Language&quot;) writes, &quot;There is nothing surprising in the semantic development of a word denoting originally a special ritual act into the more generalized meanings to &#x27;sacrifice,&#x27; &#x27;worship,&#x27; &#x27;bless,&#x27; &quot; and he compares Latin <span class="foreign">immolare</span> (see <span class="crossreference">immolate</span>).
<br/><br/>
The meaning shifted in late Old English toward &quot;pronounce or make happy, prosperous, or fortunate&quot; by resemblance to unrelated <span class="crossreference">bliss</span>. Meaning &quot;invoke or pronounce God&#x27;s blessing upon&quot; is from early 14c. No cognates in other languages. Related: <span class="crossreference">Blessed</span>; <span class="crossreference">blessing</span>.
