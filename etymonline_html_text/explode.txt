title="Origin and meaning of explode" href="/word/explode#etymonline_v_14094"><em>explode</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1530s (transitive), &quot;to reject with scorn,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">explodere</span> &quot;drive out or off by clapping, hiss off, hoot off,&quot; originally theatrical, &quot;to drive an actor off the stage by making noise,&quot; hence &quot;drive out, reject, destroy the repute of&quot; (a sense surviving in <span class="foreign">an exploded theory</span>), from <span class="foreign">ex-</span> &quot;out&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ex-</span>) + <span class="foreign">plaudere</span> &quot;to clap the hands, applaud,&quot; which is of uncertain origin. Athenian audiences were highly demonstrative. clapping and shouting approval, stamping, hissing, and hooting for disapproval. The Romans seem to have done likewise.</p>
<blockquote>At the close of the performance of a comedy in the Roman theatre one of the actors dismissed the audience, with a request for their approbation, the expression being usually <span class="foreign">plaudite</span>, <span class="foreign">vos plaudite</span>, or <span class="foreign">vos valete et plaudite</span>. [William Smith, &quot;A First Latin Reading Book,&quot; 1890]</blockquote>
<p>English used it to mean &quot;drive out with violence and sudden noise&quot; (1650s), later &quot;cause to burst suddenly and noisily&quot; (1794). Intransitive sense of &quot;go off with a loud noise&quot; is from 1790, American English; figurative sense of &quot;to burst with destructive force&quot; is by 1882; that of &quot;burst into sudden activity&quot; is from 1817; of population by 1959.
