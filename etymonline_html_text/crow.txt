class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of crow" href="/word/crow#etymonline_v_393"><em>crow</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>general common name of birds of the genus <span class="foreign">Corvus</span> (the larger sort being sometimes called <span class="foreign">ravens</span>), Old English <span class="foreign">crawe</span>, which is held to be imitative of the bird&#x27;s cry. Compare Old Saxon <span class="foreign">kraia</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kraai</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">chraja</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kräke</span>.</p>
<p>Noted for sagacity and sociability. The British and North American species are very similar.  Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>as the crow flies</strong></span> &quot;in a straight line&quot; is from 1810; the image is attested in different form from 1800. </p>
<p>American English figurative phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>eat crow</strong></span> &quot;do or accept what one vehemently dislikes and has opposed defiantly, accept things which, though not unbearable, are yet scarcely to be wished for,&quot; is attested by 1870 (originally often <span class="foreign">eat boiled crow</span>), and seems to be based on the notion that the bird is edible when boiled but hardly agreeable.</p>
<p>There was an oft-reprinted mid-19c. joke about a man who, to settle a bet that he could eat anything, agrees to eat a boiled crow. As he with great difficulty swallows the first to mouthfuls, he says to the onlookers, &quot;I can eat crow, but I don&#x27;t <span class="foreign">hanker</span> arter it.&quot; The joke is attested by 1854 (Walter <span class="foreign">Etecroue</span> turns up 1361 in the Calendar of Letter Books of the City of London).</p>
<blockquote>I tried my best to eat crow, but it was too tough for me. &quot;How do you like it?&quot; said the old man, as, with a desperate effort, he wrenched off a mouthful from a leg. &quot;I am like the man,&quot; said I, &quot;who was once placed in the same position: &#x27;I ken eat crow, but hang me if I <span class="foreign">hanker</span> arter it.&#x27;&quot; &quot;Well,&quot; says the captain, &quot;it is somewhat hard; but try some of the soup and dumplings and don t condemn crow-meat from this trial, for you shot the grandfather and grandmother of the flock: no wonder they are tough; shoot a young one next time.&quot; &quot;No more crow-meat for me, thank you,&quot; said I. [James G. Swan, &quot;The Northwest Coast, or Three Years&#x27; Residence in Washington Territory,&quot; New York, 1857] </blockquote>
<p>The image of a <span class="foreign"><strong>crow&#x27;s foot</strong></span> for the wrinkles appearing with age at the corner of the eye is from late 14c. (&quot;So longe mote ye lyve Til crowes feet be growen under youre ye.&quot; [Chaucer, Troilus and Criseyde, c. 1385]).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/crow#etymonline_v_393"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of crow" href="/word/crow#etymonline_v_394"><em>crow</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">crawan</span> &quot;make a loud noise like a crow,&quot; probably imitative (see <span class="crossreference">crow</span> (n.)). Compare Dutch <span class="foreign">kraaijen</span>, German <span class="foreign">krähen</span>. From mid-13c. as &quot;make a loud noise like a cock,&quot; which oddly has become the main sense, the use of the word in reference to crows (and cranes) having faded. Sense of &quot;exult in triumph&quot; is from 1520s, probably an image of the cock&#x27;s crow, but perhaps also in part because the English crow is a carrion-eater. Related: <span class="foreign">Crowed</span>; <span class="foreign">crowing</span>. As a noun, &quot;characteristic cry of the cock,&quot; also &quot;the crowing of the cock at dawn,&quot; c. 1200.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/crow#etymonline_v_394"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of cock-crow" href="/word/cock-crow#etymonline_v_53412">cock-<em>crow</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;dawn of the day,&quot; mid-15c., from <span class="crossreference">cock</span> (n.1) + <span class="crossreference">crow</span> (v.). An Old English word on a similar notion was <span class="foreign">hanered</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/cock-crow#etymonline_v_53412"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Jim Crow" href="/word/Jim%20Crow#etymonline_v_1744">Jim <em>Crow</em> </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;black man,&quot; 1838, American English, originally the name of a black minstrel character in a popular song-and-dance act by T.D. <blockquote class="poetry">Wheel about, an&#x27; turn about, an&#x27; do jis so;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Eb&#x27;ry time I wheel about, I jump Jim Crow.</blockquote>

<p>Where and how Rice got it, or wrote it, is a mystery. Even before that, <span class="crossreference">crow</span> (n.) had been a derogatory term for a black man. As an adjective from 1833, in reference to the song. Association with segregation dates from 1841, in reference to separate railroad cars for blacks in Massachusetts. Modern use as a type of racial discrimination is from 1943. <span class="foreign">Jim Crow</span> also could be a reference to someone&#x27;s change of (political) principles (1837, from the &quot;jump&quot; in the song) or reversible machinery (1875, &quot;wheel about&quot;).</p>
<blockquote>On his arrival in Boston, Mr. [Charles Lenox] R[emond] went to the Eastern rail-road depot, in order to visit his parents in Salem; but, instead of being allowed to ride with other passengers, he was compelled to take a seat in what is contemptuously called the &quot;Jim Crow car,&quot; as though he were a leper or a wild animal!
