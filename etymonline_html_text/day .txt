<p>Meaning originally, in English, &quot;the daylight hours;&quot; it expanded to mean &quot;the 24-hour period&quot; in late Anglo-Saxon times. The day formerly began at sunset, hence Old English <span class="foreign">Wodnesniht</span> was what we would call &quot;Tuesday night.&quot; Names of the weekdays were not regularly capitalized in English until 17c.</p>
<p>From late 12c. as &quot;a time period as distinguished from other time periods.&quot; <span class="foreign">Day-by-day</span> &quot;daily&quot; is from late 14c.; <span class="foreign">all day</span> &quot;all the time&quot; is from late 14c.  <span class="foreign">Day off</span> &quot;day away from work&quot; is attested from 1883; <span class="foreign">day-tripper</span> first recorded 1897. The <span class="foreign">days</span> in <span class="foreign">nowadays</span>, etc. is a relic of the Old English and Middle English use of the adverbial genitive.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>All in a day&#x27;s work</strong></span> &quot;something unusual taken as routine&quot; is by 1820.
