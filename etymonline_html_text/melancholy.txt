title="Origin and meaning of melancholy" href="/word/melancholy#etymonline_v_12544"><em>melancholy</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">melancolie</span>, <span class="foreign">malencolie</span>, &quot;mental disorder characterized by sullenness, gloom, irritability, and propensity to causeless and violent anger,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">melancolie</span> &quot;black bile; ill disposition, anger, annoyance&quot; (13c.), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">melancholia</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">melankholia</span> &quot;sadness,&quot; literally (excess of) &quot;black bile,&quot; from <span class="foreign">melas</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">melanos</span>) &quot;black&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">melano-</span>) + <span class="foreign">khole</span> &quot;bile&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cholera</span>).</p>
<p>Old medicine attributed mental depression to unnatural or excess &quot;black bile,&quot; a secretion of the spleen and one of the body&#x27;s four &quot;humors,&quot; which help form and nourish the body unless altered or present in excessive amounts. The word also was used in Middle English for &quot;sorrow, gloom&quot; (brought on by love, disappointment, etc.), by mid-14c. As belief in the old physiology of humors faded out in the 18c. the word remained with a sense of &quot;a gloomy state of mind,&quot; particularly when habitual or prolonged.</p>
<p>The Latin word also is the source of Spanish <span class="foreign">melancolia</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">melancolia</span>, German <span class="foreign">Melancholie</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">melankoli</span>, etc. Old French variant <span class="foreign">malencolie</span> (also in Middle English) is by false association with <span class="foreign">mal</span> &quot;sickness.&quot;</p>
