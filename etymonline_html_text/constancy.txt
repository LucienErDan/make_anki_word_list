<p>This is from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhle-was</span> &quot;light-colored, blue, blond, yellow,&quot; from root <span class="crossreference">*bhel- </span>(1) &quot;to shine, flash, burn,&quot; also &quot;shining white&quot; and forming words for bright colors. The same PIE root yielded Latin <span class="foreign">flavus</span> &quot;yellow,&quot; Old Spanish <span class="foreign">blavo</span> &quot;yellowish-gray,&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">phalos</span> &quot;white,&quot; Welsh <span class="foreign">blawr</span> &quot;gray,&quot; showing the slipperiness of definition in Indo-European color-words. Many Indo-European languages seem to have had a word to describe the color of the sea, encompassing blue and green and gray; such as Irish <span class="foreign">glass</span> (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*ghel-</span> (2) &quot;to shine,&quot;); Old English <span class="foreign">hæwen</span> &quot;blue, gray,&quot; related to <span class="foreign">har</span> (see <span class="crossreference">hoar</span>); Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">sinji</span> &quot;gray-blue, sea-green;&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šyvas</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">sivyj</span> &quot;gray.&quot;</p>
<p>The present spelling in English is since 16c., common from c. 1700. The sense &quot;lead-colored, blackish-blue, darkened as if by bruising&quot; is perhaps by way of the Old Norse cognate <span class="foreign">bla</span> &quot;livid, lead-colored.&quot; It is the meaning in <span class="foreign">black and blue</span>, and <span class="foreign"><strong>blue in the face</strong></span> &quot;livid with effort&quot; (1864, earlier <span class="foreign">black and blue in the face</span>, 1829).</p>
<p>The color of constancy since Chaucer at least, but apparently for no deeper reason than the rhyme in <span class="foreign"><strong>true blue</strong></span> (c. 1500). Figurative meaning &quot;sad, sorrowful, afflicted with low spirits&quot; is from c. 1400, perhaps from the &quot;livid&quot; sense and implying a bruised heart or feelings. Of women, &quot;learned, pedantic,&quot; by 1788 (see <span class="crossreference">bluestocking</span>). In some phrases, such as <span class="foreign">blue murder</span>, it appears to be merely intensive. Blue was by c. 1600 the distinctive color of the dress of servants, which may be the reason police uniforms are blue, a tradition Farmer dates to Elizabethan times.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Blue pencil</strong></span> as an editor&#x27;s characteristic tool to mark corrections in copy is from 1885; also as a verb from 1885. The fabulous story of <span class="foreign"><strong>Blue-beard</strong></span>, who kept his murdered wives in a locked room, is from 1798. For <span class="foreign"><strong>blue ribbon</strong></span> see <span class="foreign">cordon bleu</span> under <span class="crossreference">cordon</span>. <span class="foreign"><strong>Blue whale</strong></span> attested from 1851, so called for its color. <span class="foreign"><strong>Blue cheese</strong></span> is from 1862. <span class="foreign"><strong>Blue water</strong></span> &quot;the open ocean&quot; is from 1822. <span class="foreign"><strong>Blue streak</strong></span>, of something resembling a bolt of lightning (for quickness, intensity, etc.) is from 1830, Kentucky slang. Delaware has been the <span class="foreign"><strong>Blue Hen State</strong></span> at least since 1830, supposedly from a nickname of its regiments in the Revolutionary War.</p>
