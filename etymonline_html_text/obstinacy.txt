<blockquote>The mule combines the strength of the horse with the endurance and surefootedness of the ass, and is extensively bred for certain employments for which it is more suited than either; it is ordinarily incapable of procreation. With no good grounds, the mule is a proverbial type of obstinacy. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Properly, the offspring of a he-ass and a mare; that of a she-ass and a stallion is technically a <span class="foreign">hinny.</span> The males are ordinarily incapable of procreation. Used allusively of hybrids and things of mixed nature. Meaning &quot;obstinate, stupid, or stubborn person&quot; is from 1470s; the sense of &quot;stupid&quot; seems to have been older, that of &quot;stubborn&quot; is by 18c.</p>
<p>As a type of spinning machine, it is attested from 1793 (as <span class="foreign">mule-jenny</span>, 1788), so called because it is a &quot;hybrid&quot; of Arkwright&#x27;s drawing-rollers and Hargreaves&#x27; jenny. The underworld slang sense of &quot;narcotics smuggler or courier for a drug trafficker&quot; is attested by 1935. <p>The more usual Old English words were <span class="foreign">hals</span> (the general Germanic word, cognate with Gothic, Old Norse, Danish, Swedish, Dutch, German <span class="foreign">hals</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*halsaz</span>, which is perhaps cognate with Latin <span class="foreign">collum</span> (see <span class="crossreference">collar</span> (n.)); and <span class="foreign">sweora</span>, <span class="foreign">swira</span> &quot;neck, nape,&quot; probably also from a PIE root meaning &quot;column&quot; (cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">swer</span> &quot;column,&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">svaru-</span> &quot;post&quot;).</p>
<p>Oxen and other draught animals being yoked by the neck, it became a symbol of burdens, of submission or subjugation, and also resistance or obstinacy (compare <span class="crossreference">stiff-necked</span>). Figuratively, &quot;life&quot; (late 15c.) from the breaking or severing of the neck in legal executions. Meaning &quot;narrow part at the top of a bottle&quot; is from late 14c.; meaning &quot;part of a garment which covers the neck&quot; is from 1520s. Meaning &quot;long, slender part of a stringed musical instrument&quot; is from 1610s.</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;isthmus, long, narrow strip of land connecting two larger ones&quot; is from 1550s. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>neck of the woods</strong></span> (American English) is attested from 1780 in the sense of &quot;narrow stretch of woods;&quot; 1839 with meaning &quot;settlement in a wooded region.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>stick (one&#x27;s) neck out</strong></span> &quot;take a risk&quot; is recorded by 1919, American English. Horses running <span class="foreign"><strong>neck and neck</strong></span> &quot;at an equal pace&quot; is attested from 1799; to <span class="foreign"><strong>win by a neck</strong></span> is from 1823.
