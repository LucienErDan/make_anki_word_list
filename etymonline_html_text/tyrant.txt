title="Origin and meaning of tyrant" href="/word/tyrant#etymonline_v_18897"><em>tyrant</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;absolute ruler,&quot; especially one without legal right; &quot;cruel, oppressive ruler,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">tiran</span>, <span class="foreign">tyrant</span> (12c.), from Latin <span <blockquote>In the exact sense, a tyrant is an individual who arrogates to himself the royal authority without having a right to it. This is how the Greeks understood the word &#x27;tyrant&#x27;: they applied it indifferently to good and bad princes whose authority was not legitimate. [Rousseau, &quot;The Social Contract&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>Originally in Greek the word was not applied to old hereditary sovereignties (<span class="foreign">basileiai</span>) and despotic kings, but it was used of usurpers, even when popular, moderate, and just (such as Cypselus of Corinth), however it soon became a word of reproach in the usual modern sense. The unetymological spelling with <span class="foreign">-t</span> arose in Old French by analogy with present-participle endings in <span class="foreign">-ant</span>.
