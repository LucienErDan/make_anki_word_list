title="Origin and meaning of machine" href="/word/machine#etymonline_v_2135"><em>machine</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1540s, &quot;structure of any kind,&quot; from Middle French <span class="foreign">machine</span> &quot;device, contrivance,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">machina</span> &quot;machine, engine, military machine; device, trick; instrument&quot; (source also of Spanish <span class="foreign">maquina</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">macchina</span>), from Greek <span class="foreign">makhana</span>, Doric variant of Attic <span class="foreign">mēkhanē</span> &quot;device, tool, machine;&quot; also &quot;contrivance, cunning,&quot; traditionally (Watkins) from PIE <span class="foreign">*magh-ana-</span> &quot;that which enables,&quot; from root <span class="crossreference">*magh-</span> &quot;to be able, have power.&quot; But Beekes, on formal grounds, objects to the connection to words in Germanic and Slavic. He finds the Greek word isolated and is convinced that it is Pre-Greek.</p>

<p>Main modern sense of &quot;device made of moving parts for applying mechanical power&quot; (1670s) probably grew out of mid-17c. senses of &quot;apparatus, appliance&quot; and &quot;military siege-tower.&quot;  It gradually came to be applied to an apparatus that works without the strength or skill of the workman.</p>
<p>From 17c.-19c. also &quot;a vehicle; a stage- or mail-coach; a ship,&quot; and, from 1901, &quot;a motor-car.&quot; Also in late 19c. slang the word was used for both &quot;penis&quot; and &quot;vagina,&quot; one of the few so honored.</p>

<p>The political sense &quot;a strict organization of the working members of a political party to secure a predominating influence for themselves and their associates&quot; is U.S. slang, attested by 1876. <span class="foreign"><strong>Machine age</strong></span><span class="foreign">,</span> a time notable for the extensive use of mechanical devices, is attested by 1882, though there is this:</p>
<blockquote>The idea of remodelling society at public meetings is one of the least reasonable which ever entered the mind of an agitator: and the notion that the relations of the sexes can be re-arranged and finally disposed of by preamble and resolution, is one of the latest, as it should have been the last, vagary of a machine age. [&quot;The Literary World,&quot; Nov. 1, 1851]</blockquote>
