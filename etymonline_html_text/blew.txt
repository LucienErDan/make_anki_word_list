The word, which is native Germanic, in Middle English was <span class="foreign">clewe</span>, also <span class="foreign">cleue</span>; some words borrowed from Old French in -<span class="foreign">ue</span>, -<span class="foreign">eu</span> also were spelled <span class="foreign">-ew</span> in Middle English, such as <span class="foreign">blew, imbew,</span> but these later were reformed to <span class="foreign">-ue</span>, and this process was extended to native words (<span class="foreign">hue, true, clue</span>) which had ended in a vowel and <span class="foreign">-w</span>. <p>The sense shift is originally in reference to the clew of thread given by Ariadne to Theseus to use as a guide out of the Labyrinth in Greek mythology. <p>Of unknown origin; perhaps from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*kwat-</span> &quot;to ferment, become sour&quot; (source also of Prakrit <span class="foreign">chasi</span> &quot;buttermilk;&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kvasu</span> &quot;leaven; fermented drink,&quot; <span class="foreign">kyselu</span> &quot;sour,&quot; <span class="foreign">-kyseti</span> &quot;to turn sour;&quot; Czech <span class="foreign">kysati</span> &quot;to turn sour, rot;&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kvathati</span> &quot;boils, seethes;&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">hwaþjan</span> &quot;foam&quot;). <p>Earliest references would be to compressed curds of milk used as food; pressed or molded cheeses with rinds are from 14c. Transferred to other cheese-like substances by 1530s. Probably for the forced smile involved in making the <span class="foreign">-ee-</span> sound.</p>
