Sense of &quot;local branch of a society or organization&quot; (1815) is from the Church sense &quot;body of the canons of a cathedral or collegiate church, members of a religious order&quot; (late 14c.), which seems to trace to the convocations of canons at cathedral churches, during which the rules of the order by chapter, or a chapter (<span class="foreign">capitulum</span>) of Scripture, were read aloud to the assembled. <span class="foreign">Chapter and verse</span> &quot;in full and thoroughly&quot; (1620s) is a reference to Scripture.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/chapter#etymonline_v_8432"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of evolution" href="/word/evolution#etymonline_v_29764">evolution (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">1620s, &quot;an opening of what was rolled up,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">evolutionem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">evolutio</span>) &quot;unrolling (of a book),&quot; noun of action from past participle stem of <span class="foreign">evolvere</span> &quot;to unroll&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">evolve</span>).
<br/><br/>
Used in medicine, mathematics, and general writing in various senses including &quot;growth to maturity and development of an individual living thing&quot; (1660s). Modern use in biology, of species, first attested 1832 in works of Scottish geologist Charles Lyell. Charles Darwin used the word in print once only, in the closing paragraph of &quot;The Origin of Species&quot; (1859), and preferred <span class="foreign">descent with modification</span>, in part because <span class="foreign">evolution</span> already had been used in the discarded 18c. homunculus theory of embryological development (first proposed under this name by Bonnet, 1762) and in part because it carried a sense of &quot;progress&quot; not present in Darwin&#x27;s idea. But Victorian belief in progress prevailed (and the advantages of brevity), and Herbert Spencer and other biologists after Darwin popularized <span class="foreign">evolution</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/evolution#etymonline_v_29764"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of camouflage" href="/word/camouflage#etymonline_v_638">camouflage </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1917, noun, verb, and adjective, from French <span class="foreign">camoufler</span>, Parisian slang, &quot;to disguise,&quot; from Italian <span class="foreign">camuffare</span> &quot;to disguise,&quot; which is of uncertain origin, perhaps a contraction of <span class="foreign">capo muffare</span> &quot;to muffle the head.&quot; Probably altered in French by influence of French <span class="foreign">camouflet</span> &quot;puff of smoke, smoke puffed into a sleeper&#x27;s face&quot; (itself of unknown origin) on the notion of &quot;blow smoke in someone&#x27;s face.&quot; The British navy in World War I called it <span class="foreign">dazzle-painting</span>.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">Since the war started the POPULAR SCIENCE MONTHLY has published photographs of big British and French field pieces covered with shrubbery, railway trains &quot;painted out&quot; of the landscape, and all kinds of devices to hide the guns, trains, and the roads from the eyes of enemy aircraft.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Until recently there was no one word in any language to explain this war trick. Sometimes a whole paragraph was required to explain this military practice. Hereafter one word, a French word, will save all this needless writing and reading. <span class="foreign">Camouflage</span> is the new word, and it means &quot;fooling the enemy.&quot; [&quot;Popular Science Monthly,&quot; August 1917]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/camouflage#etymonline_v_638"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of gay" href="/word/gay#etymonline_v_1327">gay (adj.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;full of joy, merry; light-hearted, carefree;&quot; also &quot;wanton, lewd, lascivious&quot; (late 12c. as a surname, <span class="foreign">Philippus de Gay</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">gai</span> &quot;joyful, happy; pleasant, agreeably charming; forward, pert; light-colored&quot; (12c.; compare Old Spanish <span class="foreign">gayo</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">gaio</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">gajo</span>, probably French loan-words). Ultimate origin disputed; perhaps from Frankish <span class="foreign">*gahi</span> (related to Old High German <span class="foreign">wahi</span> &quot;pretty&quot;), though not all etymologists accept this.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;stately and beautiful; splendid and showily dressed&quot; is from early 14c. Of things, &quot;sumptuous, showy, rich, ornate,&quot; mid-14c. of colors, etc., &quot;shining, glittering, gleaming, bright, vivid,&quot; late 14c.; of persons, &quot;dressed up, decked out in finery,&quot; late 14c.</p>
<p>In the English of Yorkshire and Scotland formerly it could mean &quot;moderately, rather, considerable&quot; (1796; compare sense development in <span class="foreign">pretty</span> (adj.)).</p>

<p>The word <span class="foreign">gay</span> by the 1890s had an overall tinge of promiscuity -- a <span class="foreign">gay house</span> was a brothel. The suggestion of immorality in the word can be traced back at least to the 1630s, if not to Chaucer:</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">But in oure bed he was so fressh and gay</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Whan that he wolde han my bele chose.</blockquote>

<p>Slang meaning &quot;homosexual&quot; (adj.) begins to appear in psychological writing late 1940s, evidently picked up from gay slang and not always easily distinguished from the older sense:</p>
<blockquote>After discharge A.Z. lived for some time at home. He was not happy at the farm and went to a Western city where he associated with a homosexual crowd, being &quot;gay,&quot; and wearing female clothes and makeup. He always wished others would make advances to him. [&quot;Rorschach Research Exchange and Journal of Projective Techniques,&quot; 1947, p.240]</blockquote>
<p>The association with (male) homosexuality likely got a boost from the term <span class="foreign">gay cat</span>, used as far back as 1893 in American English for &quot;young hobo,&quot; one who is new on the road, also one who sometimes does jobs.</p>
<blockquote>&quot;A Gay Cat,&quot; said he, &quot;is a loafing laborer, who works maybe a week, gets his wages and vagabonds about hunting for another &#x27;pick and shovel&#x27; job. Do you want to know where they got their monica (nickname) &#x27;Gay Cat&#x27;? See, Kid, cats sneak about and scratch immediately after chumming with you and then get gay (fresh). That&#x27;s why we call them &#x27;Gay Cats&#x27;.&quot; [Leon Ray Livingston (&quot;America&#x27;s Most Celebrated Tramp&quot;), &quot;Life and Adventures of A-no. 1,&quot; 1910]</blockquote>
<p>Quoting a tramp named Frenchy, who might not have known the origin. Gay cats were severely and cruelly abused by &quot;real&quot; tramps and bums, who considered them &quot;an inferior order of beings who begs of and otherwise preys upon the bum -- as it were a jackal following up the king of beasts&quot; [Prof. John J. McCook, &quot;Tramps,&quot; in &quot;The Public Treatment of Pauperism,&quot; 1893], but some accounts report certain older tramps would dominate a gay cat and employ him as a sort of slave. In &quot;Sociology and Social Research&quot; (1932-33) a paragraph on the &quot;gay cat&quot; phenomenon notes, &quot;Homosexual practices are more common than rare in this group,&quot; and <span class="foreign">gey cat</span> &quot;homosexual boy&quot; is attested in Noel Erskine&#x27;s 1933 dictionary of &quot;Underworld &amp; Prison Slang&quot; (<span class="foreign">gey</span> is a Scottish variant of <span class="foreign">gay</span>).</p>

<p>The &quot;Dictionary of American Slang&quot; reports that <span class="foreign">gay</span> (adj.) was used by homosexuals, among themselves, in this sense at least since 1920. Rawson [&quot;Wicked Words&quot;] notes a male prostitute using <span class="foreign">gay</span> in reference to male homosexuals (but also to female prostitutes) in London&#x27;s notorious Cleveland Street Scandal of 1889. Ayto [&quot;20th Century Words&quot;] calls attention to the ambiguous use of the word in the 1868 song &quot;The Gay Young Clerk in the Dry Goods Store,&quot; by U.S. female impersonator Will S. Hays, but the word evidently was not popularly felt in this sense by wider society until the 1950s at the earliest.</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Gay&quot; (or &quot;gai&quot;) is now widely used in French, Dutch, Danish, Japanese, Swedish, and Catalan with the same sense as the English. It is coming into use in Germany and among the English-speaking upper classes of many cosmopolitan areas in other countries. [John Boswell, &quot;Christianity, Social Tolerance, and Homosexuality,&quot; 1980]</blockquote>
<p>As a teen slang word meaning &quot;bad, inferior, undesirable,&quot; without reference to sexuality, from 2000.</p>
