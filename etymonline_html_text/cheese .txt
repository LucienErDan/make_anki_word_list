<p>Of unknown origin; perhaps from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*kwat-</span> &quot;to ferment, become sour&quot; (source also of Prakrit <span class="foreign">chasi</span> &quot;buttermilk;&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kvasu</span> &quot;leaven; fermented drink,&quot; <span class="foreign">kyselu</span> &quot;sour,&quot; <span class="foreign">-kyseti</span> &quot;to turn sour;&quot; Czech <span class="foreign">kysati</span> &quot;to turn sour, rot;&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kvathati</span> &quot;boils, seethes;&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">hwaþjan</span> &quot;foam&quot;). But de Vaan writes, &quot;no etymology can be found which does not require some poorly-founded assumptions,&quot; and suggests a loan-word. Also compare <span class="crossreference">fromage</span>. Old Norse <span class="foreign">ostr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">ost</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">ost</span> are related to Latin <span class="foreign">ius</span> &quot;broth, sauce, juice.&quot;</p>

<p>Earliest references would be to compressed curds of milk used as food; pressed or molded cheeses with rinds are from 14c. Transferred to other cheese-like substances by 1530s. As a photographer&#x27;s word to make subjects hold a smile, it is attested from 1930, but in a reminiscence of schoolboy days, which suggests an earlier use. Probably for the forced smile involved in making the <span class="foreign">-ee-</span> sound.</p>
