Possessive pronouns in Modern English consist of the predicative (<span class="foreign">mine, thine, his, ours, yours, theirs</span>) that come after the subject, and the attributive (<span class="foreign">my, thy, his, her, our, your, their</span>) that come before it. In Old English and early Middle English, they were identical. To keep speech fluid, speakers began to affix an <span class="foreign">-n</span> to the end of predicative <span class="foreign">my</span> and <span class="foreign">thy</span> before words that began with vowels. This began late 13c. in the north of England, and by 1500 was standard.
<br/><br/>
Then the predicative and attributive pronouns split, and the remaining pronouns in that class took up <span class="foreign">-s</span>, the regular affix of possession. But the non-standard speech of the Midlands and south of England extended <span class="foreign">-n</span> throughout (<span class="foreign">hisn, hern, yourn</span>), a habit attested from 14c. and more regular than the standard speech, which mixes <span class="foreign">-s</span> and <span class="foreign">-n</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/hers#etymonline_v_9202"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of its" href="/word/its#etymonline_v_12290">its (pron.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">neuter possessive pronoun; late 16c., from <span class="crossreference">it</span> + genitive/possessive ending <span class="crossreference">&#x27;s</span> (q.v.). &quot;[A]t first commonly written <span class="foreign">it&#x27;s</span>, a spelling retained by some to the beginning of the 19c.&quot; [OED]. The apostrophe came to be omitted, perhaps because <span class="foreign">it&#x27;s</span> already was established as a contraction of <span class="foreign">it is</span>, or by general habit of omitting apostrophes in personal pronouns (<span class="foreign">hers</span>, <span class="foreign">yours</span>, <span class="foreign">theirs</span>, etc.).
<br/><br/>
The neuter genitive pronoun in Middle English was <span class="foreign">his</span>, but the clash between grammatical gender and sexual gender, or else the application of the word to both human and non-human subjects, evidently made users uncomfortable. Restriction of <span class="foreign">his</span> to the masculine and avoidance of it as a neuter pronoun is evidenced in Middle English, and <span class="foreign">of it</span> and <span class="foreign">thereof</span> (as in KJV) were used for the neuter possessive. In literary use, <span class="foreign">his</span> as a neuter pronoun continued into the 17c. In Middle English, simple <span class="foreign">it</span> sometimes was used as a neuter possessive pronoun (c. 1300).</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/its#etymonline_v_12290"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of up" href="/word/up#etymonline_v_4533">up (adv.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Old English <span class="foreign">up, uppe</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*upp-</span> &quot;up&quot; (source also of Old Frisian, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">up</span> &quot;up, upward,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">upp</span>; Danish, Dutch <span class="foreign">op</span>; Old High German <span class="foreign">uf</span>, German <span class="foreign">auf</span> &quot;up&quot;; Gothic <span class="foreign">iup</span> &quot;up, upward,&quot; <span class="foreign">uf</span> &quot;on, upon, under;&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">oba</span>, German <span class="foreign">ob</span> &quot;over, above, on, upon&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*upo</span> &quot;under,&quot; also &quot;up from under,&quot; hence also &quot;over.&quot;
<br/><br/>
As a preposition, &quot;to a higher place&quot; from c. 1500; also &quot;along, through&quot; (1510s), &quot;toward&quot; (1590s).
