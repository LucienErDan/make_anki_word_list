title="Origin and meaning of medicine" href="/word/medicine#etymonline_v_12517"><em>medicine</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, &quot;medical treatment, cure, healing,&quot; also (early 14c.) &quot;substance used in treatment of a disease, medicinal potion or plaster,&quot; also used figuratively of spiritual remedies, from Old French <span class="foreign">medecine</span> (Modern French <span class="foreign">médicine</span>) &quot;medicine, art of healing, cure, treatment, potion&quot; and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">medicina</span> &quot;the healing art, medicine; a remedy,&quot; also used figuratively.</p>
<p>This is perhaps originally <span class="foreign">ars medicina</span> &quot;the medical art,&quot; from fem. of <span class="foreign">medicinus</span> (adj.) &quot;of a doctor,&quot; from <span class="foreign">medicus</span> &quot;a physician&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*med-</span> &quot;take appropriate measures&quot;); though OED says evidence for this path is wanting and suggests derivation directly from <span class="foreign">medicus</span>. The sense of &quot;practice, theory, or study of curing, alleviating, or preventing disease in humans&quot; is from mid-14c.</p>
<p>The figurative phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>take (one&#x27;s) medicine</strong></span> &quot;submit to something disagreeable&quot; is recorded by 1865; that of <span class="foreign"><strong>dose of (one&#x27;s) own medicine</strong></span> is by 1894. <span class="foreign"><strong>Medicine show</strong></span> &quot;traveling show meant to attract a crowd so patent medicine can be sold to them&quot; is American English, 1938. <span class="foreign"><strong>Medicine ball</strong></span> &quot;stuffed leather ball used for exercise&quot; is from 1889.</p>
<blockquote>It is called a &quot;medicine ball&quot; and it got that title from Prof. [Robert J.] Roberts, now of Springfield, whose fame is widespread, and whose bright and peculiar dictionary of terms for his prescription department in physical culture is taught in every first-class conducted Y.M.C.A. gymnasium in America. Prof. Roberts calls it a &quot;medicine ball&quot; because playful exercise with it invigorates the body, promotes digestion, and restores and preserves one&#x27;s health. <blockquote>Unless some understanding is arrived at between the American and Canadian Governments that offenders may be promptly and vigorously dealt with, I very much fear that killing and stealing will increase to such an extent that the country along the border will be scarcely habitable. When the Indians are made to understand that the mere fact of &quot;hopping&quot; across the line does not exempt them from punishment, there will be a much greater guarantee of their good behaviour. Now they call the boundary the &quot;Medicine line,&quot; because no matter what they have done upon one side they feel perfectly secure after having arrived upon the other. [Report of Superintendent L.N.F. Crozier, Dec. 1880, in &quot;North-West Mounted Police Force Commissioner&#x27;s Report,&quot; 1880]</blockquote>
