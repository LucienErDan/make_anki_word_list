and meaning of ass" href="/word/ass#etymonline_v_17954"><em>ass</em> (n.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>solid ungulate quadruped beast of burden of the horse kind, but smaller and with long ears and a short mane, native to southwest Asia, Old English <span class="foreign">assa</span> (Old Northumbrian <span class="foreign">assal</span>, <span class="foreign">assald</span>) &quot;he-ass.&quot; The English word is cognate with Old Saxon <span class="foreign">esil</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">ezel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">esil</span>, German <span class="foreign">Esel</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">asilus</span>, and, beyond Germanic, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">asilas</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">osl</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">oselŭ</span>, etc. All probably are ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">asinus</span>. De Vaan says the form of <span class="foreign">asinus</span> suggests it was a loan-word into Latin, and adds, &quot;Most IE words for &#x27;ass&#x27; are loanwords.&quot;</p>
<p>Together with Greek <span class="foreign">onos</span> it is conjectured to be from a language of Asia Minor (compare Sumerian <span class="foreign">ansu</span>). The initial vowel of the English word might be by influence of Celtic forms (Irish and Gaelic <span class="foreign">asal</span>), from Old Celtic <span class="foreign">*as(s)in</span> &quot;donkey.&quot; In Romanic tongues the Latin word has become Italian <span class="foreign">asino</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">asno</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">asne</span>, French <span class="foreign">âne</span>.</p>
<blockquote>In familiar use, the name <span class="foreign">ass</span> is now to a great extent superseded by <span class="crossreference">donkey</span> (in Scotland <span class="foreign">cuddie</span>); but <span class="foreign">ass</span> is always used in the language of Scripture, Natural History, proverb, and fable; also, in ordinary use, in Ireland. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Sure-footed and patient in domestication, since ancient Greek times, in fables and parables, the animal has typified clumsiness and stupidity (hence <span class="foreign">ass-head</span>, late 15c., etc.). To <span class="foreign"><strong>make an ass of oneself</strong></span> is from &quot;A Midsummer Night&#x27;s Dream&quot; (1590). <span class="foreign"><strong>Asses&#x27; Bridge</strong></span> (c. 1780), from Latin <span class="foreign">Pons Asinorum</span>, is fifth proposition of first book of Euclid&#x27;s &quot;Elements.&quot; In Middle English, someone uncomprehending or unappreciative would be <span class="foreign">lik an asse that listeth on a harpe</span>. In 15c., an <span class="foreign">ass man</span> was a donkey-driver.</p>
<blockquote>For al schal deie and al schal passe, Als wel a Leoun as an asse. [John Gower, &quot;Confessio Amantis,&quot; 1393]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/ass#etymonline_v_17954"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of ass" href="/word/ass#etymonline_v_17955"><em>ass</em> (n.2)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>slang for &quot;backside,&quot; first attested 1860 in nautical slang, in popular use from 1930; chiefly U.S.; from dialectal variant pronunciation of <span class="crossreference">arse</span> (q.v.). Indirect evidence of the change from <span class="foreign">arse</span> to <span class="foreign">ass</span> can be traced to 1785 (in euphemistic avoidance of <span class="foreign">ass</span> &quot;donkey&quot; by polite speakers) and perhaps to Shakespeare, if Nick <span class="foreign">Bottom</span> transformed into a donkey in &quot;A Midsummer Night&#x27;s Dream&quot; (1594) is the word-play some think it is.</p>
<blockquote>I must to the barber&#x27;s, mounsieur; for me thinks I am marvellous hairy about the face; and I am such a tender ass, if my hair do but tickle me, I must scratch. [Bottom]</blockquote>

To <span class="foreign"><strong>have (one&#x27;s) head up (one&#x27;s) ass</strong></span> &quot;not know what one is doing&quot; is attested by 1969. Colloquial <span class="foreign"><strong>(one&#x27;s) ass</strong></span> &quot;one&#x27;s self, one&#x27;s person&quot; attested by 1958. To <span class="foreign"><strong>work (one&#x27;s) ass off</strong></span> &quot;work very much&quot; is by 1946; to <span class="foreign"><strong>laugh (one&#x27;s) ass off</strong></span> &quot;laugh very much&quot; is by 1972 (implied from 1965). <span class="foreign"><strong>Ass-kissing</strong></span> (adj.) &quot;currying favor&quot; is by 1974.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/ass#etymonline_v_17955"><span>Relate entries &amp;
