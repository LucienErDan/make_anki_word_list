Both sides of me were in dead earnest; I was no more myself when I laid aside restraint and plunged in shame, than when I labored, in the eye of day, at the furtherance of knowledge or the relief of sorrow and suffering.&quot; [Robert Louis Stevenson, &quot;The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde,&quot; 1886]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Jekyll%20and%20Hyde#etymonline_v_1695"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of dern" href="/word/dern#etymonline_v_37240">dern (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;secret, hidden,&quot; from Old English <span class="foreign">derne</span> (West Saxon <span class="foreign">dierne</span>) &quot;concealed, secret, dark,&quot; from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*darnjaz</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">derni</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">dern</span> &quot;concealed, dark,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">tarni</span> &quot;secret, concealed, veiled&quot;), related to <span class="crossreference">dark</span> (adj.).</p>
<p>Archaic or poetic only after 16c., it was important and productive in Middle English, with extended senses of &quot;secluded; profound, mysterious; stealthy, deceptive; private, confidential.&quot; <span class="foreign">Dern love</span> was &quot;secret or illicit love; a mistress.&quot;</p>
<p>As a verb, meaning &quot;to conceal,&quot; it was from Old English <span class="foreign">diernan</span> &quot;to hide.&quot; Compare Old Saxon <span class="foreign">dernian</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">tarnjan</span> &quot;to conceal, hide;&quot; German <span class="foreign">Tarnkappe</span>, <span class="foreign">Tarnhelm</span> &quot;magical cap or helmet which turns the wearer invisible or allows him to assume any form.&quot; French <span class="foreign">ternir</span> &quot;to tarnish, to dull&quot; apparently is from Germanic.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/dern#etymonline_v_37240"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of devotion" href="/word/devotion#etymonline_v_8492">devotion (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">devocioun</span>, &quot;profound religious emotion, awe, reverence,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">devocion</span> &quot;devotion, piety&quot; and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">devotionem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">devotio</span>), noun of action from past-participle stem of <span class="foreign">devovere</span> &quot;dedicate by a vow, sacrifice oneself, promise solemnly,&quot; from <span class="foreign">de</span> &quot;down, away&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">de-</span>) + <span class="foreign">vovere</span> &quot;to vow&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">vow</span> (n.)). From late 14c. as &quot;an act of religious worship, a religious exercise&quot; (now usually <span class="foreign">devotions</span>).</p>
<p>In ancient Latin, &quot;act of consecrating by a vow,&quot; also &quot;loyalty, fealty, allegiance;&quot; in Church Latin, &quot;devotion to God, piety.&quot; The application to secular situations came to English via Italian and French; sense of &quot;act of setting apart or consecrating&quot; is from c. 1500.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
