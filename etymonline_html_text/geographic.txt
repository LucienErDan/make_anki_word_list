<p>The alternative <span class="foreign">Indo-Germanic</span> (1835) was coined in German in 1823 (<span class="foreign">indogermanisch</span>), based on the two peoples then thought to be at the extremes of the geographic area covered by the languages, but this was before Celtic was realized also to be an Indo-European language. After this was proved, many German scholars switched to <span class="foreign">Indo-European</span> as more accurate, but <span class="foreign">Indo-Germanic</span> continued in use (popularized by the titles of major works) and the predominance of German scholarship in this field made it the popular term in England, too, through the 19c. See also <span class="crossreference">Aryan</span> and <span class="crossreference">Japhetic</span>.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Indo-Aryan</span> (1850) seems to have been used only of the Aryans of India. <span class="foreign">Indo-European</span> also was used in reference to trade between Europe and India or European colonial enterprises in India (1813).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Indo-European#etymonline_v_6397"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of gulf" href="/word/gulf#etymonline_v_14366">gulf (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;profound depth,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">golf</span> &quot;a gulf, whirlpool,&quot; from Italian <span class="foreign">golfo</span> &quot;a gulf, a bay,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">colfos</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">kolpos</span> &quot;bay, gulf of the sea,&quot; earlier &quot;trough between waves, fold of a loose garment,&quot; originally &quot;bosom,&quot; the common notion being &quot;curved shape.&quot; This is from PIE <span class="foreign">*kuolp-</span> &quot;arch, curve, vault&quot; (compare Old English <span class="foreign">hwealf </span>&quot;vault,&quot; <span class="foreign">a-hwielfan</span> &quot;to overwhelm,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">holfinn</span> &quot;vaulted,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">welban</span> &quot;to vault&quot;).</p>
<p>Latin <span class="foreign">sinus</span> underwent the same development, being used first for &quot;bosom,&quot; later for &quot;gulf&quot; (and in Medieval Latin, &quot;hollow curve or cavity in the body&quot;). <p>Also from c. 1300 as &quot;area surrounding a walled city or town; the open country.&quot; By early 16c. the word was applied mostly to rural areas, as opposed to towns and cities. Meaning &quot;inhabitants of a country, the people&quot; is from c. 1300.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">INTERVIEWER [Steve Rossi]: &quot;Would you say you&#x27;re the best fighter in the country?</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">PUNCH-DRUNK BOXER [Marty Allen]: &quot;Yeah, but in the city they murder me.&quot; </blockquote>
<p>As an adjective from late 14c., &quot;peculiar to one&#x27;s own country (obsolete); by 1520s as &quot;pertaining to or belonging to the rural parts of a region,&quot; typically with implications of &quot;rude, unpolished.&quot;</p>

<blockquote>In Great Britain heights under 2,000 feet are generally called hills; &#x27;mountain&#x27; being confined to the greater elevations of the Lake District, of North Wales, and of the Scottish Highlands; but, in India, ranges of 5,000 and even 10,000 feet are commonly called &#x27;hills,&#x27; in contrast with the Himalaya Mountains, many peaks of which rise beyond 20,000 feet. <blockquote>The term <span class="foreign">mountain</span> is very loosely used. It commonly means any unusual elevation. <blockquote>Despite the differences in defining mountain systems, Penck (1896), Supan (1911) and Obst (1914) agreed that the distinction between hills, mountains, and mountain systems according to areal extent or height is not a suitable classification.
