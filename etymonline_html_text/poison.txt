(12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">poison</span>) &quot;a drink,&quot; especially a medical drink, later &quot;a (magic) potion, poisonous drink&quot; (14c.), from Latin <span <br/><br/>
For form evolution from Latin to French, compare <span class="foreign">raison</span> from <span class="foreign">rationem</span>. The Latin word also is the source of Old Spanish <span class="foreign">pozon</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">pozione</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">pocion</span>. The more usual Indo-European word for this is represented in English by <span class="crossreference">virus</span>. The Old English word was <span class="foreign">ator</span> (see <span class="crossreference">attercop</span>) or <span class="foreign">lybb</span> (cognate with Old Norse <span class="foreign">lyf</span> &quot;medicinal herbs;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">leaf</span> (n.)). <br/><br/>
For sense evolution, compare Old French <span class="foreign">enerber</span>, <span class="foreign">enherber</span> &quot;to kill with poisonous plants.&quot; In many Germanic languages &quot;poison&quot; is named by a word equivalent to English <span class="crossreference">gift</span> (such as Old High German <span class="foreign">gift</span>, German <span class="foreign">Gift</span>, Danish and Swedish <span class="foreign">gift</span>; Dutch <span class="foreign">gift</span>, <span class="foreign">vergift</span>). <br/><br/>
Figuratively from late 15c.; of persons by 1910.
