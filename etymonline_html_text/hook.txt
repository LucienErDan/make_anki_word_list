catch or hold something,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">hoc</span> &quot;hook, angle,&quot; perhaps related to Old English <span class="foreign">haca</span> &quot;bolt,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hokaz</span>/<span class="foreign">*hakan</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hok</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">hoek</span> <p>Also the name of a fireman&#x27;s tool for tearing into buildings, hence <span class="foreign">hook-and-ladder</span> (1821). Meaning &quot;holder for a telephone receiver&quot; is from 1885 and continued in use after the mechanism evolved. Boxing sense of &quot;short, swinging blow with the elbow bent&quot; is from 1898. Figurative sense &quot;that which catches, a snare, trap&quot; is from early 15c. Meaning &quot;projecting point of land&quot; is from 1670s; in U.S. use probably reinforced by the Dutch word.</p>
<blockquote>This name is given in New York to several angular points in the North and East rivers; as <span class="foreign">Corlear&#x27;s Hook, Sandy Hook, Powles&#x27;s Hook</span>. [Bartlett, &quot;Dictionary of Americanisms,&quot; 1848]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Off the hooks</strong></span> meant &quot;disordered&quot; (16c.), &quot;unhinged&quot; (1610s) and &quot;dead&quot; (1840). <span class="foreign"><strong>By hook or by crook</strong></span> (late 14c.) probably alludes to tools of professional thieves. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hook, line, and sinker</strong></span> &quot;completely&quot; is 1838, a metaphor from angling. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hook-nose</strong></span> (n.) is from 1680s; <span class="foreign"><strong>hook-nosed</strong></span> (adj.) from 1510s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hook-and-eye</strong></span> as a method of garment fastening is from 1620s.</p>
<blockquote><strong>Hook and eye</strong>, a metallic fastening for garments, consisting of a hook, commonly of flattened wire bent to the required shape, and an eye, usually of the same material, into which the hook fits. Under the name of <span class="foreign">crochet and loop</span>, this form of fastening was in use as early as the fourteenth century. Meaning &quot;prostitute&quot; (by 1845) often is traced to the disreputable morals of the Army of the Potomac (American Civil War) under the tenure of Gen. &quot;Fighting Joe&quot; <span class="foreign">Hooker</span> (early 1863), and the word might have been popularized by this association at that time (though evidence is wanting). But it is reported to have been in use in North Carolina c. 1845 (&quot;[I]f he comes by way of Norfolk he will find any number of pretty Hookers in the Brick row not far from French&#x27;s hotel. Take my advice and touch nothing in the shape of a prostitute when you come through Raleigh, for in honest truth the clap is there of luxuriant growth.&quot; letter quoted in Norman E. <p>One early theory traces it to <span class="foreign">Corlear&#x27;s Hook</span>, a section of New York City.</p>
<blockquote>HOOKER. A resident of the Hook, i.e. a strumpet, a sailor&#x27;s trull. So called from the number of houses of ill-fame frequented by sailors at the Hook (i.e. Corlear&#x27;s Hook) in the city of New York. [John Russell Bartlett, &quot;Dictionary of Americanisms,&quot; 1859]</blockquote>
<p>Or perhaps related to <span class="foreign">hooker</span> &quot;thief, pickpocket&quot; (1560s).</p>
<p>But the word is likely a reference to prostitutes <span class="foreign">hooking</span> or snaring clients. <span class="foreign">Hook</span> in the figurative sense of &quot;that by which anyone is attracted or caught&quot; is recorded from early 15c.; and <span class="crossreference">hook</span> (v.) in the figurative sense of &quot;catch hold of and draw in&quot; is attested from 1570s; in reference to &quot;fishing&quot; for a husband or a wife, it was in common use from c. 1800. All of which makes the modern sense seem a natural step. Compare French <span class="foreign">accrocheuse</span>, <span class="foreign">raccrocheuse</span>, common slang term for &quot;street-walker, prostitute,&quot; literally &quot;hooker&quot; of men.</p>
<p>The family name <span class="foreign">Hooker</span> (attested from c. 975) would mean &quot;maker of hooks,&quot; or else refer to an agricultural laborer who used a hook (compare Old English <span
