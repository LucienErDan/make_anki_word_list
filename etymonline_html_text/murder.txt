by a person of sound mind with premeditated malice,&quot; c. 1300, <span class="foreign">murdre</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">morþer</span>, from Old English <span <p>The spelling with <span class="foreign">-d-</span> probably reflects influence of Anglo-French <span class="foreign">murdre</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">mordre</span>, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">murdrum</span>, which is from the Germanic word. <p>In Old Norse, custom distinguished <span class="foreign">morð</span> &quot;secret slaughter&quot; from <span class="foreign">vig</span> &quot;slaying.&quot; The former involved concealment, or slaying a man by night or when asleep, and was a heinous crime. <blockquote>Mordre wol out that se we day by day. [Chaucer, &quot;Nun&#x27;s Priest&#x27;s Tale,&quot; c. 1386]</blockquote>
<p>Weakened sense of &quot;very unpleasant situation&quot; is from 1878. Inverted slang sense of &quot;something excellent or terrific&quot; is by 1940. The connection with the root is more transparent in other Germanic cognates (such as Middle Low German, Middle Dutch, Middle High German, German <span class="foreign">spinne</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">spin</span> &quot;spider&quot;).</p>
<blockquote>The male is commonly much smaller than the female, and in impregnating the female runs great risk of being devoured. The difference in sizes is as if the human female should be some 60 or 70 feet tall. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>The loss of <span class="foreign">-n-</span> before spirants is regular in Old English (compare <span class="crossreference">goose</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">tooth</span>). For shift of <span class="foreign">-th-</span> to <span class="foreign">-d-</span> compare <span class="crossreference">murder</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">burden</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">rudder</span>.</p>
<p>Not the common word in Old or Middle English, which identified the creatures as <span class="foreign">loppe</span> (Chaucer&#x27;s usual word), <span class="foreign">lobbe</span>. Old English also had <span class="foreign">atorcoppe</span> (Middle English <span class="foreign">attercop</span>, literally &quot;poison-head&quot;), and (from Latin <span class="foreign">aranea</span>), <span class="foreign">renge</span>; Middle English had <span class="foreign">araine</span>, &quot;spider,&quot; via Old French from the same Latin word; see <span class="crossreference">arachnid</span>). Another Old English word was <span class="foreign">gangewifre</span> &quot;a weaver as he goes.&quot;</p>
<p>In literature, often a figure of cunning, skill, and industry as well as venomnous predation; in 17c. English used figuratively for venomousness and thread-spinning but also sensitivity (to vibrations), lurking, independence. As the name for a type of two-pack solitaire, it is attested from 1890, probably based on resemblance of the layout of the decks in the original form of the game (see &quot;Tarbart,&quot; &quot;Games of Patience,&quot; 1901, p. 49). <span class="foreign">Spider crab</span> is from 1710, used of various species; <span class="foreign">spider monkey</span> is from 1764, so called for its long limbs.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/spider#etymonline_v_24014"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right"
