<p>The sense evolution probably is from Vulgar Latin use of <span class="foreign">follis</span> in a sense of &quot;windbag, empty-headed person.&quot; Compare also Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vatula-</span> &quot;insane,&quot; literally &quot;windy, inflated with wind.&quot; But some sources suggest evolution from Latin <span class="foreign">folles</span> &quot;puffed cheeks&quot; (of a buffoon), a secondary sense from plural of <span class="foreign">follis</span>. <blockquote>The word has in mod.Eng. a much stronger sense than it had at an earlier period; it has now an implication of insulting contempt which does not in the same degree belong to any of its synonyms, or to the derivative <span class="foreign">foolish</span>. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Also used in Middle English for &quot;sinner, rascal, impious person&quot; (late 13c.). Meaning &quot;jester, court clown&quot; in English is attested c. 1300, though it is not always possible to tell whether the reference is to a professional entertainer counterfeiting mental weakness or an amusing lunatic, and the notion of the <span class="foreign">fool sage</span> whose sayings are ironically wise is also in English from c. 1300. The French word probably also got into English via its borrowing in the Scandinavian languages of the vikings (Old Norse <span class="foreign">fol</span>, Old Danish <span class="foreign">fool</span>, <span class="foreign">fol</span>).</p>
