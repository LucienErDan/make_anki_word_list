title="Origin and meaning of arsenal" href="/word/arsenal#etymonline_v_17034"><em>arsenal</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1500, &quot;dockyard, dock with naval stores,&quot; from Italian <span class="foreign">arzenale</span>, from Arabic <span class="foreign">dar as-sina&#x27;ah</span> &quot;workshop,&quot; literally &quot;house of manufacture,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dar</span> &quot;house&quot; + <span class="foreign">sina&#x27;ah</span> &quot;art, craft, skill,&quot; from <span class="foreign">sana&#x27;a</span> &quot;he made.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Applied by the Venetians to a large wharf in their city, which was the earliest reference of the English word. Sense of &quot;public place for making or storing weapons and ammunition&quot; is from 1570s. The London football club (1886) was named for the <span class="foreign">Royal Arsenal</span>, Woolwich, where the original players worked.

<br/><br/>
The main modern verbal sense of &quot;play at courtship&quot; (1777) probably developed from the noun (see <span class="crossreference">flirt</span> (n.)) but also could have grown naturally from the 16c. meaning &quot;to flit inconstantly from object to object.&quot; To <span class="foreign">flirt a fan</span> (1660s) was to snap it open or closed with a brisk jerk and was long considered part of the coquette&#x27;s arsenal, which might have contributed to the sense shift. <br/>
The <span class="foreign">-l-</span> has been regular in English since mid-14c., in imitation of Latin. Willie and Joe always say <span class="foreign">sojer</span> in the Bill Mauldin cartoons, and this seems to mirror 16c.-17c. spellings <span class="foreign">sojar</span>, <span class="foreign">soger</span>, <span class="foreign">sojour</span>.
