<p>French <span class="foreign">department</span> came also to mean &quot;group of people&quot; (as well as &quot;departure&quot;), and from this by 1735 English had borrowed the sense of &quot;separate division of a complex whole, separate business assigned to someone in a larger organization, distinct branch or group of activities&quot; (science, business, manufacture, the military). The specific meaning &quot;separate division of a government&quot; is from 1769. As an administrative district in France, from 1792.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Department store</span> &quot;store that sells a variety of items, organized by department&quot; is from 1878.</p>

<p>The same root produced Old English <span class="foreign">blac</span> &quot;bright, shining, glittering, pale;&quot; the connecting notions being, perhaps, &quot;fire&quot; (bright) and &quot;burned&quot; (dark), or perhaps &quot;absence of color.&quot; &quot;There is nothing more variable than the signification of words designating colour&quot; [Wedgwood].</p>
<p>The usual Old English word for &quot;black&quot; was <span class="foreign">sweart</span> (see <span class="crossreference">swart</span>). <p>Of coffee with nothing added, attested by 1796. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black drop</strong></span> (1823) was a liquid preparation of opium, used medicinally. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black-fly</strong></span> (c. 1600) was used of various insects, especially an annoying pest of the northern American woods. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black Prince</strong></span> as a nickname of the eldest son of Edward III is attested by 1560s; the exact signification is uncertain.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;fierce, terrible, wicked&quot; is from late 14c. Figurative senses often come from the notion of &quot;without light,&quot; moral or spiritual. Latin <span class="foreign">niger</span> had many of the same figurative senses (&quot;gloomy; unlucky; bad, wicked, malicious&quot;). The metaphoric use of the Greek word, <span class="foreign">melas</span>, however, tended to reflect the notion of &quot;shrouded in darkness, overcast.&quot; In English it has been the color of sin and sorrow at least since c. 1300; the sense of &quot;with dark purposes, malignant&quot; emerged 1580s (in <span class="foreign">black art</span> &quot;necromancy;&quot; it is also the sense in <span class="foreign">black magic</span>). <span class="foreign"><strong>Black flag</strong></span>, flown (especially by pirates) as a signal of &quot;no mercy,&quot; is from 1590s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black dog</strong></span> &quot;melancholy&quot; attested from 1826.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Black belt</strong></span> is from 1870 in reference to district extending across the U.S. South with heaviest African population (also sometimes in reference to the fertility of the soil); it is attested from 1913 in the judo sense, worn by one who has attained a certain high degree of proficiency. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black power</strong></span> is from 1966, associated with Stokely Carmichael. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black English</strong></span> &quot;English as spoken by African-Americans,&quot; is by 1969. <p>Abstract sense of &quot;time as an indefinite continuous duration&quot; is recorded from late 14c. <blockquote><span class="foreign">to have a good time</span> ( = a time of enjoyment) was common in Eng. from c 1520 to c 1688; it was app. retained in America, whence readopted in Britain in 19th c. [OED]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Time of day</span> (now mainly preserved in negation, i.e. what someone won&#x27;t give you if he doesn&#x27;t like you) was a popular 17c. salutation (as in &quot;Good time of day vnto your Royall Grace,&quot; &quot;Richard III,&quot; I.iii.18), hence <span class="foreign">to give (one) the time of day</span> &quot;greet socially&quot; (1590s); earlier was <span class="foreign">give good day</span> (mid-14c.). <span class="foreign">The times</span> &quot;the current age&quot; is from 1590s. <span class="foreign">Behind the times</span> &quot;old-fashioned&quot; is recorded from 1831. <span class="foreign">Times</span> as the name of a newspaper dates from 1788.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Time warp</span> first attested 1954; <span class="foreign">time-traveling</span> in the science fiction sense first recorded 1895 in H.G. <blockquote>Jones [archaeologist of A.D. 5139] potters about for a while in the region which we have come to regard as New York, finds countless ruins, but little of interest to the historian except a calcified direction sheet to something called a &quot;Time Capsule.&quot; Jones finds the capsule but cannot open it, and decides, after considerable prying at the lid, that it is merely evidence of an archaic tribal ceremony called a &quot;publicity gag&quot; of which he has already found many examples. [&quot;Princeton Alumni Weekly,&quot; April 14, 1939]</blockquote>
