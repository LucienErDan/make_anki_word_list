title="Origin and meaning of silent" href="/word/silent#etymonline_v_24254"><em>silent</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1500, &quot;without speech, silent, not speaking,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">silentem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">silens</span>) &quot;still, calm, quiet,&quot; present participle of <span class="foreign">silere</span> &quot;be quiet or still&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">silence</span> (n.)). Meaning &quot;free from noise or sound&quot; is from 1580s.</p>
<p>Of letters, c. 1600; of films, 1914. In the looser sense &quot;of few words,&quot; from 1840. Phrase <span class="foreign">strong, silent (type)</span> is attested from 1905. <span class="foreign">Silent majority</span> in the political sense of &quot;mass of people whose moderate views are not publicly expressed and thus overlooked&quot; is first attested 1955 in a British context and was used by John F. Kennedy but is most associated in U.S. with the rhetoric of the Nixon administration (1969-74).</p>
<blockquote>It is time for America&#x27;s silent majority to stand up for its rights, and let us remember the American majority includes every minority. America&#x27;s silent majority is bewildered by irrational protest. [Spiro T. Agnew, May 9, 1969]</blockquote>
<p>In Victorian use, the phrase meant &quot;the dead&quot; (1874; compare Roman use of the noun plural of &quot;silent&quot; to mean &quot;the dead&quot;). <span class="foreign">Silence is golden</span> (1831) is Carlyle&#x27;s translation [&quot;Sartor Resartus&quot;] of part of the &quot;Swiss Inscription&quot; <span class="foreign">Sprechen ist silbern, Schweigen ist golden</span>.
