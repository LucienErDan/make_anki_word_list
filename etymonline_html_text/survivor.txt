Probably further popularized by use as an election slogan by the <span class="foreign">O.K. Club</span>, New York boosters of Democratic president Martin Van Buren&#x27;s 1840 re-election bid, in allusion to his nickname <span class="foreign">Old Kinderhook</span>, from his birth in the N.Y. village of Kinderhook. Van Buren lost, the word stuck, in part because it filled a need for a quick way to write an approval on a document, bill, etc. Spelled out as <span class="foreign">okeh</span>, 1919, by Woodrow Wilson, on assumption that it represented Choctaw <span class="foreign">okeh</span> &quot;it is so&quot; (a theory which lacks historical documentation); this was ousted quickly by <span class="foreign">okay</span> after the appearance of that form in 1929. Greek immigrants to America who returned home early 20c. having picked up U.S. speech mannerisms were known in Greece as <span class="foreign">okay-boys</span>, among other things.
