consists of eight decasyllabic lines and a final Alexandrine, with rhyme scheme <span class="foreign">ab ab bc bcc</span>.
<br/><br/>
&quot;The measure soon ceases to be Spenser&#x27;s except in its mere anatomy of rhyme-arrangement&quot; [Elton, &quot;Survey of English Literature 1770-1880,&quot; 1920]; it is the meter in Butler&#x27;s &quot;Hudibras,&quot; Scott&#x27;s &quot;Lady of the Lake,&quot; and notably the &quot;Childe Harold&quot; of Byron, who found (quoting Beattie) that it allowed him to be &quot;either droll or pathetic, descriptive or sentimental, tender or satirical, as the humour strikes me; for, if I mistake not, the measure which I have adopted admits equally of all these kinds of composition.&quot;</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Spenserian#etymonline_v_24002"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Yankee" href="/word/Yankee#etymonline_v_4929">Yankee (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1683, a name applied disparagingly by Dutch settlers in New Amsterdam (New York) to English colonists in neighboring Connecticut. It may be from Dutch <span class="foreign">Janke</span>, literally &quot;Little John,&quot; diminutive of common personal name <span class="foreign">Jan</span>; or it may be from <span class="foreign">Jan Kes</span> familiar form of &quot;John Cornelius,&quot; or perhaps an alteration of <span class="foreign">Jan Kees</span>, dialectal variant of <span class="foreign">Jan Kaas</span>, literally &quot;John Cheese,&quot; the generic nickname the Flemings used for Dutchmen.</p>
<blockquote>[I]t is to be noted that it is common to name a droll fellow, regarded as typical of his country, after some favorite article of food, as E[nglish] <span class="foreign">Jack-pudding</span>, G[erman] <span class="foreign">Hanswurst</span> (&quot;Jack Sausage&quot;), F[rench] <span class="foreign">Jean Farine</span> (&quot;Jack Flour&quot;). [Century Dictionary, 1902, entry for &quot;macaroni&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>Originally it seems to have been applied insultingly to the Dutch, especially freebooters, before they turned around and slapped it on the English. A less-likely theory (attested by 1832) is that it represents some southern New England Algonquian language mangling of <span class="foreign">English</span>. In English a term of contempt (1750s) before its use as a general term for &quot;native of New England&quot; (1765); during the American Revolution it became a disparaging British word for all American natives or inhabitants. Contrasted with <span class="crossreference">southerner</span> by 1828. Shortened form <span class="foreign">Yank</span> in reference to &quot;an American&quot; first recorded 1778. Latin-American form <span class="foreign">Yanqui</span> attested in English by 1914 (in Mexican Spanish by 1835).</p>
<blockquote>The rule observed in this country is, that the man who receives that name [Yankee] must come from some part north of him who gives it. To compensate us for giving each other nicknames, John Bull &quot;lumps us all together,&quot; and calls us all Yankees. [&quot;Who is a Yankee?&quot; Massachusetts Spy, June 6, 1827]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Yankee#etymonline_v_4929"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of quiz" href="/word/quiz#etymonline_v_3228">quiz (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;brief examination of a student on some subject,&quot; 1852, perhaps from <span class="crossreference">quiz</span> (v.), or from slang <span class="foreign">quiz</span> &quot;odd person&quot; (1782, perhaps originally university slang), via the notion of &quot;schoolboy prank or joke played at the expense of a person deemed a quiz&quot; (a noun sense attested frequently 1840s).</p>
<blockquote>A Quiz, in the common acceptation of the word, signifies one who thinks, speaks, or acts differently from the rest of the world in general. But, as manners and opinions are as various as mankind, it will be difficult to say who shall be termed a Quiz, and who shall not: each person indiscriminately applying the name of Quiz to every one who differs from himself .... [&quot;The London Magazine,&quot; November, 1783]</blockquote>
<p>According to OED, the anecdote that credits this word to a bet by the Dublin theater-manager Daly or Daley that he could coin a word is regarded by authorities as &quot;doubtful&quot; and the first record of it appears to be in 1836 (in Smart&#x27;s &quot;Walker Remodelled&quot;; the story is omitted in the edition of 1840).</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">The word Quiz is a sort of a kind of a word</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">That people apply to some being absurd;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">One who seems, as t&#x27;were oddly your fancy to strike</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">In a sort of a fashion you somehow don&#x27;t like</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">A mixture of odd, and of queer, and all that</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Which one hates, just, you know, as some folks hate a cat;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">A comical, whimsical, strange, droll -- that is,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">You know what I mean; &#x27;tis -- in short, -- &#x27;tis a quiz!</blockquote>
