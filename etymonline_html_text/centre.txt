<p>The spelling with <span class="foreign">-re</span> was popularized in Britain by Johnson&#x27;s dictionary (following Bailey&#x27;s), though <span class="foreign">-er</span> is older and was used by Shakespeare, Milton, and Pope. Meaning &quot;the middle of anything&quot; attested from 1590s. Figuratively, &quot;point of concentration&quot; (of power, etc.) is from 1680s. Political use, originally in reference to France, &quot;representatives of moderate views&quot; (between <span class="foreign">left</span> and <span class="foreign">right</span>) is from 1837. <span class="foreign"><strong>Center of gravity</strong></span> is recorded from 1650s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Center of attention</strong></span> is from 1868.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/center#etymonline_v_8355"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of -re" href="/word/-re#etymonline_v_25840">-re </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">word-ending that sometimes distinguish British from American English. In the U.S., the change from <span class="foreign">-re</span> to <span class="foreign">-er</span> (to match pronunciation) in words such as <span class="foreign">fibre</span>, <span class="foreign">centre</span>, <span class="foreign">theatre</span> began late 18c.; under urging of Noah Webster (1804 edition of his speller, and especially the 1806 dictionary), it was established over the next 25 years. The <span class="foreign">-re</span> spelling, like <span class="foreign">-our</span>, however, had the authority of Johnson&#x27;s dictionary behind it and remained in Britain, where it came to be a point of national pride, contra the Yankees.
<br/><br/>
Despite Webster&#x27;s efforts, <span class="foreign">-re</span> was retained in words with <span class="foreign">-c-</span> or <span class="foreign">-g-</span> (such as <span class="crossreference">ogre</span>, <span class="crossreference">acre</span>, the latter of which Webster insisted to the end of his days ought to be <span class="foreign">aker</span>, and it was so printed in editions of the dictionary during his lifetime). The <span class="foreign">-re</span> spelling generally is more justified by conservative etymology, based on French antecedents. It is met today in the U.S. only in <span class="foreign">Theatre</span> as an element in the proper names of entertainment showplaces, where it is perhaps felt to inspire a perception of <span class="foreign">bon ton</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/-re#etymonline_v_25840"><span>Relate entries &amp; <p>Sense of &quot;make a line or figure&quot; (by &quot;drawing&quot; a pencil across paper) is from c. 1200. To <span class="foreign">draw</span> a criminal (drag him at the tail of a horse to the place of execution) is from c. 1300.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;select one (from a number of lots, etc.)&quot; is from c. 1300. Sense of &quot;bring (a crowd, an audience, etc.) by inducement or attraction&quot; is from 1580s. To <span class="foreign">draw blood</span> is from c. 1400.</p>
