and meaning of lot" href="/word/lot#etymonline_v_14542"><em>lot</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">hlot</span> &quot;object used to determine someone&#x27;s share&quot; (anything from dice to straw, but often a chip of wood with a name inscribed on it), also &quot;what falls to a person by lot,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*khlutom</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">hlutr</span> &quot;lot, share,&quot; Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hlot</span> &quot;lot,&quot; Old Saxon <span class="foreign">hlot</span>, Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">lot</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hluz</span> &quot;share of land,&quot; German <span class="foreign">Los</span>), from a strong verb (the source of Old English <span class="foreign">hleotan</span> &quot;to cast lots, obtain by lot; to foretell&quot;). The whole group is of unknown origin.</p>
<p>The object was placed with others in a receptacle (such as a hat or helmet), which was shaken, the winner being the one whose name or mark was on the lot that fell out first. Hence the expression <span class="foreign"><strong>cast lots</strong></span>; to <span class="foreign"><strong>cast (one&#x27;s) lot with</strong></span> another (1530s, originally biblical) is to agree to share winnings. In some cases the lots were drawn by hand, hence to <span class="foreign"><strong>draw lots</strong></span>. The word was adopted from Germanic into the Romanic languages (Spanish <span class="foreign">lote</span>, and compare <span class="crossreference">lottery</span>, <span class="crossreference">lotto</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;choice resulting from the casting of lots&quot; first attested c. 1200. Meaning &quot;share or portion of life&quot; in any way, &quot;that which is given by fate, God or destiny&quot; is from c. 1300. Meaning &quot;number of persons or things of the same kind&quot; is from 1570s (compare Latin <span class="foreign">mala merx</span>, of persons, literally &quot;a bad lot&quot;). Sense of &quot;plot of land&quot; is first recorded 1630s, American English (distribution of the most desirable properties in new settlements often was determined by casting lots), then especially &quot;parcel of land set aside for a specified purpose&quot; (the Hollywood sense is from 1928). The common U.S. <span class="foreign"><strong>city lot</strong></span> was a rectangle 25 feet wide (along the street) by 100 deep; it was so universal as to be sometimes a unit of measure.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;group, collection&quot; is 1725, from the notion of auction lots. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lots</strong></span> in the generalized sense of &quot;great many&quot; is attested by 1812; <span class="foreign"><strong>lotsa</strong></span>, colloquial for &quot;lots of,&quot; is from 1927; <span class="foreign"><strong>lotta</strong></span> for &quot;lot of&quot; is by 1906.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/lot#etymonline_v_14542"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of sand-lot" href="/word/sand-lot#etymonline_v_22701">sand-<em>lot</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">also <span class="foreign">sandlot</span>, &quot;plot of empty land in a town or suburb,&quot; 1878, from <span class="crossreference">sand</span> (n.) + <span class="crossreference">lot</span>.
