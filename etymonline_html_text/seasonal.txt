<p>In Old English and Middle English primarily a season name, with only an implied reference to the gathering of crops. The borrowing of <span class="crossreference">autumn</span> and the use of <span class="crossreference">fall</span> (n.) in a seasonal sense gradually focused the meaning of <span class="foreign">harvest</span> to &quot;the time of gathering crops&quot; (mid-13c.), also to the action itself and the product of the action (after c. 1300), which became its main senses after 14c. <p>The sense shift from &quot;3 p.m.&quot; to &quot;12 p.m.&quot; began during 12c., and various reasons are given for it, such as unreliability of medieval time-keeping devices and the seasonal elasticity of the hours of daylight in northern regions. In monasteries and on holy days, fasting was ended at nones, which also perhaps was incentive to push it forward. Or perhaps the sense shift was based on an advance in the customary time of the (secular) midday meal. Whatever the cause, the meaning change was complete by 14c.
