<p>As spelling suggests, the word originally was the opposite of <span class="crossreference">friend</span> (n.). Both are from the active participles of the Germanic verbs for &quot;to love&quot; and &quot;to hate.&quot; Boutkan says the &quot;fiend&quot; word was a Germanic analogical formation from the &quot;friend&quot; word. According to Bammesberger [&quot;English Etymology&quot;], &quot;The long vowel in FIEND is regular. In FRIEND the vowel has been shortened; perhaps the shortening is due to compounds like FRIENDSHIP, in which the consonant group (<span class="foreign">-nds-</span>) regularly caused shortening of the preceeding long vowel.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Fiend</span> at first described any hostile enemy (male and female, with abstract noun form <span class="foreign">feondscipe</span> &quot;fiendship&quot;), but it began to be used in late Old English for &quot;the Devil, <span class="crossreference">Satan</span>&quot; (literally &quot;adversary&quot;) as the &quot;enemy of mankind,&quot; which shifted its sense to &quot;diabolical person&quot; (early 13c.). The old sense of the word devolved to <span class="foreign">foe</span>, then to the imported word <span class="crossreference">enemy</span>. For spelling with <span class="foreign">-ie-</span> see <span class="crossreference">field</span>. Meaning &quot;devotee (of whatever is indicated),&quot; as in <span class="foreign">dope fiend</span>, is from 1865.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fiend#etymonline_v_5916"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of devil" href="/word/devil#etymonline_v_8484">devil (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">deofol</span> &quot;a devil, a subordinate evil spirit afflicting humans;&quot; also, in Christian theology, &quot;the Devil, a powerful spirit of evil otherwise known as Satan,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">diabolus</span> (also the source of Italian <span class="foreign">diavolo</span>, French <span class="foreign">diable</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">diablo</span>; German <span class="foreign">Teufel</span> is Old High German <span class="foreign">tiufal</span>, from Latin via Gothic <span class="foreign">diabaulus</span>).</p>

<p>The Late Latin word is from Ecclesiastical Greek <span class="foreign">diabolos</span>, which in Jewish and Christian use was &quot;the Devil, Satan,&quot; and which in general use meant &quot;accuser, slanderer&quot; (thus it was a scriptural loan-translation of Hebrew <span class="foreign">satan;</span> see <span class="crossreference">Satan</span>). It is an agent noun from Greek <span class="foreign">diaballein</span> &quot;to slander, attack,&quot; literally &quot;to throw across,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dia</span> &quot;across, through&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">dia-</span>) + <span class="foreign">ballein</span> &quot;to throw&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwele-</span> &quot;to throw, reach&quot;).</p>

<p>Jerome re-introduced <span class="foreign">Satan</span> in Latin bibles, and English translators have used both words in different measures. In Vulgate, as in Greek, <span class="foreign">diabolus</span> and <span class="foreign">dæmon</span> (see <span class="crossreference">demon</span>) were distinct, but they have merged in English and other Germanic languages.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;false god, heathen god&quot; is from c. 1200. Sense of &quot;diabolical person, person resembling a devil or demon in character&quot; is from late 12c. Playful use for &quot;clever rogue&quot; is from c. 1600. As an expletive and in expletive phrases from c. 1200.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;sand spout, dust storm&quot; is from 1835. In U.S. place names, the word often represents a native word such as Algonquian <span class="foreign">manito</span>, more properly &quot;spirit, god.&quot; Phrase <span class="foreign">a devil way</span> (c. 1300) was originally &quot;Hell-ward, to Hell,&quot; but by late 14c. it was a mere expression of irritation. Meaning &quot;errand-boy in a printing office&quot; is from 1680s, perhaps because they were often blackened by the ink (devils then being popularly supposed to be black).</p>
