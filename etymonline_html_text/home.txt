house, abode, fixed residence; estate; village; region, country,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haimaz</span> &quot;home&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hem</span> &quot;home, village,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">heimr</span> &quot;residence, world,&quot; <span class="foreign">heima</span> &quot;home,&quot; Danish <span class="foreign">hjem</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">heem</span>, German <span class="foreign">heim</span> &quot;home,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">haims</span> &quot;village&quot;), <blockquote>&#x27;Home&#x27; in the full range and feeling of [Modern English] <span class="foreign">home</span> is a conception that belongs distinctively to the word <span class="foreign">home</span> and some of its Gmc. cognates and is not covered by any single word in most of the IE languages. To <span class="foreign"><strong>keep the home fires burning</strong></span> is a song title from 1914. To be <span class="foreign"><strong>nothing to write home about</strong></span> &quot;unremarkable&quot; is from 1907. <span class="foreign"><strong>Home movie</strong></span> is from 1919; <span class="foreign"><strong>home computer</strong></span> is from 1967. <span class="foreign"><strong>Home stretch</strong></span> (1841) is from horse racing (see <span class="crossreference">stretch</span> (n.)). <span class="foreign"><strong>Home economics</strong></span> as a school course first attested 1899; the phrase itself by 1879 (as &quot;household management&quot; is the original literal sense of <span class="foreign">economy</span>, the phrase is etymologically redundant).</p>
