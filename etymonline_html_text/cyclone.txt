<blockquote>concerning which Touffon ye are to vnderstand, that in the East Indies often times, there are not stormes as in other countreys; but euery 10. or 12. yeeres there are such tempests and stormes, that it is a thing incredible, but to those that haue seene it, neither do they know certainly what yeere they wil come. <p>This sense of the word, in reference to titanic storms in the East Indies, first appears in Europe in Portuguese in the mid-16th century. It apparently is from <span class="foreign">tufan</span>, a word in Arabic, Persian, and Hindi meaning &quot;big cyclonic storm.&quot; Yule [&quot;Hobson-Jobson,&quot; London, 1903] writes that &quot;the probability is that Vasco [da Gama] and his followers got the <span class="foreign">tufao</span> ... direct from the Arab pilots.&quot;</p>
<p>The Arabic word sometimes is said to be from Greek <span class="foreign">typhon</span>, but other sources consider it purely Semitic, though the Greek word might have influenced the form of the word in English. <span class="foreign">Al-tufan</span> occurs several times in the Koran for &quot;a flood or storm&quot; and also for Noah&#x27;s Flood. Chinese (Cantonese) <span class="foreign">tai fung</span> &quot;a great wind&quot; also might have influenced the form or sense of the word in English, and that term and the Indian one may have had some mutual influence; <span class="foreign">toofan</span> still means &quot;big storm&quot; in India.</p>
