and meaning of thing" href="/word/thing#etymonline_v_10758"><em>thing</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">þing</span> &quot;meeting, assembly, council, discussion,&quot; later &quot;entity, being, matter&quot; (subject of deliberation in an assembly), also &quot;act, deed, event, material object, body, being, creature,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*thinga-</span> &quot;assembly&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">thing</span> &quot;assembly, council, suit, matter, thing,&quot; Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">dinc</span> &quot;court-day, suit, plea, concern, affair, thing,&quot; Dutch <span class="foreign">ding</span> &quot;thing,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">ding</span> &quot;public assembly for judgment and business, lawsuit,&quot; German <span class="foreign">Ding</span> &quot;affair, matter, thing,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">þing</span> &quot;public assembly&quot;). The Germanic word is perhaps literally &quot;appointed time,&quot; from a PIE <span class="foreign">*tenk-</span> (1), from root <span class="foreign">*ten-</span> &quot;stretch,&quot; perhaps on notion of &quot;stretch of time for a meeting or assembly.&quot;</p>
<p>The sense &quot;meeting, assembly&quot; did not survive Old English. For sense evolution, compare French <span class="foreign">chose</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">cosa</span> &quot;thing,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">causa</span> &quot;judicial process, lawsuit, case;&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">res</span> &quot;affair, thing,&quot; also &quot;case at law, cause.&quot; Old sense is preserved in second element of <span class="crossreference">hustings</span> and in Icelandic <span class="foreign">Althing</span>, the nation&#x27;s general assembly.</p>
<p>Of persons, often pityingly, from late 13c. Used colloquially since c. 1600 to indicate things the speaker can&#x27;t name at the moment, often with various meaningless suffixes (see <span class="crossreference">thingamajig</span>). <span class="foreign">Things</span> &quot;personal possessions&quot; is from c. 1300. <span class="foreign">The thing</span> &quot;what&#x27;s stylish or fashionable&quot; is recorded from 1762.
