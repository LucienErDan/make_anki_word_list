Liddell &amp; Scott write that <span class="foreign">patriotes</span> was &quot;applied to barbarians who had only a common [patris], [politai] being used of Greeks who had a common [polis] (or <span class="foreign">free-state</span>).&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;loyal and disinterested supporter of one&#x27;s country&quot; is attested from c. 1600, but became an ironic term of ridicule or abuse from mid-18c. in England, so that Johnson, who at first defined it as &quot;one whose ruling passion is the love of his country,&quot; in his fourth edition added, &quot;It is sometimes used for a factious disturber of the government.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The name of patriot had become [c. 1744] a by-word of derision. Horace Walpole scarcely exaggerated when he said that ... the most popular declaration which a candidate could make on the hustings was that he had never been and never would be a patriot. [Macaulay, &quot;Horace Walpole,&quot; 1833]</blockquote>
<p>Somewhat revived in reference to resistance movements in overrun countries in World War II, it has usually had a positive sense in American English, where the phony and rascally variety has been consigned to the word <span class="foreign">patrioteer</span> (1928). Oriana Fallaci [&quot;The Rage and the Pride,&quot; 2002] marvels that Americans, so fond of <span class="crossreference">patriotic</span>, <span class="foreign">patriot</span>, and <span class="crossreference">patriotism</span>, lack the root noun and are content to express the idea of <span class="foreign">patria</span> by cumbersome compounds such as <span class="foreign">homeland</span>. (Joyce, Shaw, and H.G. Wells all used <span class="foreign">patria</span> as an English word early 20c., but it failed to stick.) <span class="foreign">Patriots&#x27; Day</span> (April 19, anniversary of the 1775 skirmishes at Lexington and Concord Bridge) was observed as a legal holiday in Maine and Massachusetts from 1894.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/patriot#etymonline_v_10166"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of piety" href="/word/piety#etymonline_v_14965">piety (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">early 14c. A hideous, sordid, and emaciated maniac, without knowledge, without patriotism, without natural affection, passing his life in a long routine of useless and atrocious self-torture, and quailing before the ghastly phantoms of his delirious brain, had become the ideal of the nations which had known the writings of Plato and Cicero, and the lives of Socrates and Cato. [Lecky, &quot;History of European Mortals&quot;]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/anchorite#etymonline_v_13404"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of chauvinism" href="/word/chauvinism#etymonline_v_8472">chauvinism (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1840, &quot;exaggerated, blind nationalism; patriotism degenerated into a vice,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">chauvinisme</span> (1839), from the character Nicholas <span class="foreign">Chauvin</span>, soldier of Napoleon&#x27;s Grand Armee, who idolized Napoleon and the Empire long after it was history, in the Cogniards&#x27; popular 1831 vaudeville <span class="foreign">&quot;La Cocarde Tricolore.&quot;</span> Meaning extended to &quot;excessive belief in the superiority of one&#x27;s race&quot; late 19c. in communist jargon, to (male) &quot;sexism&quot; late 1960s via <span class="crossreference">male chauvinism</span> (q.v.).<br/>
<br/>
The name is a French form of Latin <span class="foreign">Calvinus</span> and thus <span class="foreign">Calvinism</span> and <span class="foreign">chauvinism</span> are, etymologically, twins. The name was a common one in Napoleon&#x27;s army, and if there was a real person at the base of the character in the play, he has not been certainly identified by etymologists, though memoirs of Waterloo (one published in Paris in 1822) mention &quot;one of our principal <span class="foreign">piqueurs</span>, named Chauvin, who had returned with Napoleon from Elba,&quot; which implies loyalty.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/chauvinism#etymonline_v_8472"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of red" href="/word/red#etymonline_v_7437">red (adj.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">read</span> &quot;red,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*rauthan</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">rauðr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">rød</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">rod</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">rad</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">root</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">rood</span>, German <span class="foreign">rot</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">rauþs</span>), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*reudh-</span> &quot;red, ruddy,&quot; the only color for which a definite common PIE root word has been found. <p>Along with <span class="crossreference">dead</span>, <span class="crossreference">bread</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">lead</span> (n.1), the vowel shortened in Middle English. The surname <span class="foreign">Read</span>/<span class="foreign">Reid</span> retains the original Old English long vowel pronunciation and is the corresponding surname to <span class="foreign">Brown-</span>, <span class="foreign">Black</span>, <span class="foreign">White</span>.</p>
<p>The color designation of Native Americans in English from 1580s. The color as characteristic of &quot;British possessions&quot; on a map is attested from 1885. <span class="foreign"><strong>Red-white-and-blue</strong></span> in reference to American patriotism, from the colors of the flag, is from 1840; in a British context, in reference to the Union flag, 1852. The <span class="foreign"><strong>red flag</strong></span> was used as a symbol of defiance in battle on land or sea from c. 1600. To <span class="foreign"><strong>see red</strong></span> &quot;get angry&quot; is an American English expression first recorded 1898. <span class="foreign"><strong>Red rover</strong></span>, the children&#x27;s game, attested from 1891. <span class="foreign"><strong>Red light</strong></span> as a sign to stop is from 1849, long before traffic signals. As the sign of a brothel, it is attested from 1899. As a children&#x27;s game (in reference to the traffic light meaning) it is recorded from 1953.</p>
