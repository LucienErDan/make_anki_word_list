<p>Sense of &quot;to have to repay&quot; began in late Old English with the phrase <span class="foreign">agan to geldanne</span> literally &quot;to own to yield,&quot; which was used to translate Latin <span class="foreign">debere</span> (earlier in Old English this would have been <span class="foreign">sceal</span> &quot;shall&quot;); by late 12c. the phrase had been shortened to simply <span class="foreign">agan</span>, and <span class="crossreference">own</span> (v.) took over this word&#x27;s original sense.</p>
<p>An original Germanic preterite-present verb (along with <span class="crossreference">can</span> (v.1), <span class="crossreference">dare</span>, <span class="crossreference">may</span>, etc.). New past tense form <span class="foreign">owed</span> arose 15c. to replace <span class="foreign">oughte</span>, which developed into <span class="crossreference">ought</span> (v.).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/owe#etymonline_v_10116"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of can" href="/word/can#etymonline_v_645">can (v.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English 1st &amp; 3rd person singular present indicative of <span class="foreign">cunnan</span> &quot;to know,&quot; less commonly as an auxiliary, &quot;to have power to, to be able,&quot; (also &quot;to have carnal knowledge&quot;), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kunnjanan</span> &quot;to be mentally able, to have learned&quot; (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">kenna</span> &quot;to become acquainted, try,&quot; Old Frisian <span class="foreign">kanna</span> &quot;to recognize, admit, know,&quot; German <span class="foreign">kennen</span> &quot;to know,&quot; Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">kennen</span> &quot;to know,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">kannjan</span> &quot;to make known&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gno-</span> &quot;to know.&quot;</p>
<p>It holds now only the third sense of &quot;to know,&quot; that of &quot;to know how to do something&quot; (as opposed to &quot;to know as a fact&quot; and &quot;to be acquainted with&quot; something or someone). Also used in the sense of <span class="foreign">may</span>, denoting mere permission. An Old English preterite-present verb, its original past participle, <span class="crossreference">couth</span>, survived only in negation (see <span class="crossreference">uncouth</span>), but compare <span class="crossreference">could</span>. The present participle has spun off with a deflected sense as <span class="crossreference">cunning</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/can#etymonline_v_645"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of shall" href="/word/shall#etymonline_v_23330">shall (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">sceal</span>, Northumbrian <span class="foreign">scule</span> &quot;I owe/he owes, will have to, ought to, must&quot; (infinitive <span class="foreign">sculan</span>, past tense <span class="foreign">sceolde</span>), a common Germanic preterite-present verb (along with <span class="foreign">can</span>, <span class="foreign">may</span>, <span class="foreign">will</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skal-</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sculan</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">skil</span>, Old Norse and Swedish <span class="foreign">skola</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sullen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">solan</span>, German <span class="foreign">sollen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">skulan</span> &quot;to owe, be under obligation;&quot; related via past tense form to Old English <span class="foreign">scyld</span> &quot;guilt,&quot; German <span class="foreign">Schuld</span> &quot;guilt, debt;&quot; also Old Norse <span class="foreign">Skuld</span>, name of one of the Norns), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*skel-</span> (2) &quot;to be under an obligation.&quot;</p>
<p>Ground sense of the Germanic word probably is &quot;I owe,&quot; hence &quot;I ought.&quot; The sense shifted in Middle English from a notion of &quot;obligation&quot; to include &quot;futurity.&quot; Its past tense form has become <span class="crossreference">should</span> (q.v.). Cognates outside Germanic are Lithuanian <span class="foreign">skelėti</span> &quot;to be guilty,&quot; <span class="foreign">skilti</span> &quot;to get into debt;&quot; Old Prussian <span class="foreign">skallisnan</span> &quot;duty,&quot; <span class="foreign">skellants</span> &quot;guilty.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/shall#etymonline_v_23330"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of dare" href="/word/dare#etymonline_v_771">dare (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Middle English <span class="foreign">durren, daren</span>, from first and third person singular of Old English <span class="foreign">durran</span> &quot;be bold enough, have courage&quot; (to do something), also transitive &quot;to venture, presume,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ders-</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">dearr</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">giturran</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">gadaursan</span>), according to Watkins from PIE root <span class="foreign">*dhers-</span> &quot;bold&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">dadharsha</span> &quot;to be bold;&quot; Old Persian <span class="foreign">darš-</span> &quot;to dare;&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">thrasys</span> &quot;bold,&quot; <span class="foreign">tharsos</span> &quot;confidence, courage, audacity;&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">druzate</span> &quot;to be bold, dare;&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">drįsti</span> &quot;to dare,&quot; <span class="foreign">drąsus</span> &quot;courageous&quot;).</p>
<p>An Old English irregular preterite-present verb: <span class="foreign">darr, dearst, dear</span> were first, second and third person singular present indicative; mostly regularized 16c., though past tense <span class="foreign">dorste</span> survived as <span class="foreign">durst</span>, but is now dying, persisting mainly in northern English dialect.</p>
<p>Transitive sense of &quot;attempt boldly to do&quot; is from 1630s. Meaning &quot;to challenge or defy (someone), provoke to action,&quot; especially by asserting or implying that one lacks the courage to accept the challenge, is by 1570s. Weakened sense in <span class="foreign"><strong>I dare say</strong></span> (late 14c.) &quot;I suppose, I presume, I think likely,&quot; now usually implying more or less indifference. <span class="foreign"><strong>How dare you?</strong></span> is from c. 1200 (<span class="foreign">Hu durre ȝe</span>).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
