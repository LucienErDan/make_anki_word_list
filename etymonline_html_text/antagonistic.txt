<p>From 1840 in the general sense &quot;disposed to retain and maintain what is established, opposed to innovation and change,&quot; or, in a negative sense &quot;opposed to progress.&quot;</p>
<p>As a modern political tradition, &quot;antagonistic to change in the institutions of a country,&quot; often especially &quot;opposed to changes toward pure democracy,&quot; <span class="foreign">conservatism</span> traces to Edmund Burke&#x27;s opposition to the French Revolution (1790), but the word <span class="foreign">conservative</span> is not found in his writing. It was coined by his French disciples (such as Chateaubriand, who titled his journal defending clerical and political restoration <span class="foreign">&quot;Le Conservateur&quot;</span>).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Conservative</span> as the name of a British political faction first appeared in an 1830 issue of the &quot;Quarterly Review,&quot; in an unsigned article sometimes attributed to John Wilson Croker. It replaced <span class="crossreference">Tory</span> (q.v.) by 1843, reflecting both a change from the pejorative name (in use for 150 years) and repudiation of some reactionary policies.</p>
<blockquote>Strictly speaking, conservatism is not a political system, but rather a way of looking at the civil order. The conservative of Peru ... will differ greatly from those of Australia, for though they may share a preference for things established, the institutions and customs which they desire to preserve are not identical. [Russell Kirk (1918-1994)]</blockquote>
