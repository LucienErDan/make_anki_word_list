<p>First used in the literary sense in Latin in reference to a collection of poems in various meters on a variety of subjects by the late republican Roman poet Ennius. The matter of the little that survives of his verse does not seem to be particularly satiric, but in classical Latin the word came to mean especially a poem which assailed the prevailing vices, one after another. Altered in Latin by influence of Greek <span class="foreign">satyr</span>, on mistaken notion that the literary form is related to the Greek <span class="foreign">satyr</span> drama (see <span class="crossreference">satyr</span>). <blockquote><strong>Satire</strong>, <span class="foreign">n</span>. <p>Also in Middle English, &quot;the practice of remaining awake at night for purposes of debauchery and dissipation;&quot; hence <span class="foreign">wacches of wodnesse</span> &quot;late-night revels and debauchery.&quot; The alliterative combination <span class="foreign">watch and ward</span> preserves the old distinction of <span class="foreign">watch</span> for night-time municipal patrols and <span class="foreign">ward</span> for guarding by day; in combination, they meant &quot;continuous vigilance.&quot;</p>
<p>Military sense of &quot;military guard, sentinel&quot; is from late 14c.
