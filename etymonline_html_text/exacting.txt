<blockquote>This sense development arose in the consequence of the wars waged by Otto the Great and his successors against the Slavs, a great number of whom they took captive and sold into slavery. [Klein]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;one who has lost the power of resistance to some habit or vice&quot; is from 1550s. Applied to devices from 1904, especially those which are controlled by others (compare <span class="foreign">slave jib</span> in sailing, similarly of locomotives, flash bulbs, amplifiers). <span class="foreign">Slave-driver</span> is attested from 1807; extended sense of &quot;cruel or exacting task-master&quot; is by 1854. <span class="foreign">Slave state</span> in U.S. history is from 1812. <span class="foreign">Slave-trade</span> is attested from 1734.</p>
<p>Old English <span class="foreign">Wealh</span> &quot;Briton&quot; also began to be used in the sense of &quot;serf, slave&quot; c.850; and Sanskrit <span class="foreign">dasa-</span>, which can mean &quot;slave,&quot; apparently is connected to <span class="foreign">dasyu-</span> &quot;pre-Aryan inhabitant of India.&quot; Grose&#x27;s dictionary (1785) has under <span class="foreign">Negroe</span> &quot;A black-a-moor; figuratively used for a slave,&quot; without regard to race. More common Old English words for slave were <span class="foreign">þeow</span> (related to <span class="foreign">þeowian</span> &quot;to serve&quot;) and <span class="foreign">þræl</span> (see <span class="crossreference">thrall</span>).
