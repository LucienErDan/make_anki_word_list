title="Origin and meaning of bachelor" href="/word/bachelor#etymonline_v_173"><em>bachelor</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;young man;&quot; also &quot;youthful knight, novice in arms,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">bacheler</span>, <span class="foreign">bachelor</span>, <span class="foreign">bachelier</span> (11c.) &quot;knight bachelor,&quot; a young squire in training for knighthood, also &quot;young man; unmarried man,&quot; and a university title. Of uncertain origin.</p>
<p>Perhaps from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">baccalarius</span> &quot;vassal farmer, adult serf without a landholding,&quot; one who helps or tends a <span class="foreign">baccalaria</span> &quot;field or land in the lord&#x27;s demesne&quot; (according to old French sources, perhaps from an alteration of <span class="foreign">vacca</span> &quot;a cow&quot; and originally &quot;grazing land&quot; [Kitchin]). But Wedgwood points out that the baccalarii &quot;were reckoned as <span class="foreign">rustici</span>, and were bound to certain duty work for their lord. There is no appearance in the passages cited of their having had any military character whatever.&quot; (He favored a Celtic origin).</p>
<p>Or perhaps from Latin <span class="foreign">baculum</span> &quot;a stick,&quot; because the squire would practice with a staff, not a sword. &quot;Perhaps several independent words have become confused in form&quot; [Century Dictionary].</p>

The Medieval Latin word is of uncertain origin; perhaps ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">baculum</span> &quot;staff&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">bacillus</span>), which the young student might carry. In modern U.S. usage, <span class="foreign">baccalaureate</span> usually is short for <span class="foreign">baccalaureate sermon</span> (1864), a religious farewell address to a graduating class at an American college, from the adjectival sense &quot;pertaining to the university degree of bachelor.&quot;

<br/>
Generally used with native Germanic words. In words of Latin origin, verbs derived from past participle stems of Latin ones (including most verbs in <span class="foreign">-ate</span>) usually take the Latin ending <span class="foreign">-or</span>, as do Latin verbs that passed through French (such as <span class="crossreference">governor</span>); but there are many exceptions (<span class="crossreference">eraser</span>, <span class="crossreference">laborer</span>, <span class="crossreference">promoter</span>, <span class="crossreference">deserter</span>; <span class="crossreference">sailor</span>, <span class="crossreference">bachelor</span>), some of which were conformed from Latin to English in late Middle English.<br/>
<br/>
