and meaning of watch" href="/word/watch#etymonline_v_25448"><em>watch</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">wæcce</span> &quot;a watching, state of being or remaining awake, wakefulness;&quot; also &quot;act or practice of refraining from sleep for devotional or penitential purposes;&quot; from <span class="foreign">wæccan</span> &quot;keep watch, be awake,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wakjan</span>, from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*weg-</span> &quot;to be strong, be lively.&quot;</p>
<p>From c. 1200 as &quot;one of the periods into which the night is divided,&quot; in reference to ancient times translating Latin <span class="foreign">vigilia</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">phylake</span>, Hebrew <span class="foreign">ashmoreth</span>. From mid-13c. as &quot;a shift of guard duty; an assignment as municipal watchman;&quot; late 13c. as &quot;person or group obligated to patrol a town (especially at night) to keep order, etc.&quot;</p>
<p>Also in Middle English, &quot;the practice of remaining awake at night for purposes of debauchery and dissipation;&quot; hence <span class="foreign">wacches of wodnesse</span> &quot;late-night revels and debauchery.&quot; The alliterative combination <span class="foreign">watch and ward</span> preserves the old distinction of <span class="foreign">watch</span> for night-time municipal patrols and <span class="foreign">ward</span> for guarding by day; in combination, they meant &quot;continuous vigilance.&quot;</p>
<p>Military sense of &quot;military guard, sentinel&quot; is from late 14c. General sense of &quot;careful observation, watchfulness, vigilance&quot; is from late 14c.; to <span class="foreign">keep watch</span> is from late 14c. Meaning &quot;period of time in which a division of a ship&#x27;s crew remains on deck&quot; is from 1580s. The meaning &quot;small timepiece&quot; is from 1580s, developing from that of &quot;a clock to wake up sleepers&quot; (mid-15c.).</p>
<blockquote>The Hebrews divided the night into three watches, the Greeks usually into four (sometimes five), the Romans (followed by the Jews in New Testament times) into four. [OED]</blockquote>
