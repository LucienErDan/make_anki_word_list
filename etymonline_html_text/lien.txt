<p>Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">andwlita</span> &quot;face, countenance&quot; (from root of <span class="foreign">wlitan</span> &quot;to see, look&quot;) and <span class="foreign">ansyn</span>, <span class="foreign">ansien</span>, the usual word (from the root of <span class="foreign">seon</span> &quot;see&quot;). But in some cases, as here, the word for &quot;face&quot; means &quot;form, shape.&quot; In French, the use of <span class="foreign">face</span> for &quot;front of the head&quot; was given up 17c. and replaced by <span class="foreign">visage</span> (older <span class="foreign">vis</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">visus</span> &quot;sight.&quot;</p>
<p>From late 14c. as &quot;outward appearance (as contrasted to some other reality);&quot; also from late 14c. as &quot;forward part or front of anything;&quot; also &quot;surface (of the earth or sea), extent (of a city).&quot; Typographical sense of &quot;part of the type which forms the letter&quot; is from 1680s.</p>
