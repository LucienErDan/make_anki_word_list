Its origin remains one of the great mysteries of English etymology.</p>
<p>The word forced out Old English <span class="foreign">hund</span> (the general Germanic and Indo-European word, from root from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kwon-</span>) by 16c. and subsequently was picked up in many continental languages (French <span class="foreign">dogue</span> (16c.), Danish <span class="foreign">dogge</span>, German <span class="foreign">Dogge</span> (16c.)). The common Spanish word for &quot;dog,&quot; <span class="foreign">perro</span>, also is a mystery word of unknown origin, perhaps from Iberian. A group of Slavic &quot;dog&quot; words (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pisu</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">pies</span>, Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">pas</span>) likewise is of unknown origin. </p>
<p>In reference to persons, by c. 1200 in abuse or contempt as &quot;a mean, worthless fellow, currish, sneaking scoundrel.&quot; Playfully abusive sense of &quot;rakish man,&quot; especially if young, &quot;a sport, a gallant&quot; is from 1610s. Slang meaning &quot;ugly woman&quot; is from 1930s; that of &quot;sexually aggressive man&quot; is from 1950s.  </p>
<p>Many expressions -- <span class="foreign"><strong>a dog&#x27;s life</strong></span> (c. 1600), <span class="foreign"><strong>go to the dogs</strong></span> (1610s), <span class="foreign"><strong>dog-cheap</strong></span> (1520s), etc. -- reflect the earlier hard use of the animals as hunting accessories, not pets. In ancient times, &quot;the dog&quot; was the worst throw in dice (attested in Greek, Latin, and Sanskrit, where the word for &quot;the lucky player&quot; was literally &quot;the dog-killer&quot;), which plausibly explains the Greek word for &quot;danger,&quot; <span class="foreign">kindynos</span>, which appears to be &quot;play the dog&quot; (but Beekes is against this).</p>
<blockquote>Notwithstanding, as a dog hath a day, so may I perchance have time to declare it in deeds. [Princess Elizabeth, 1550]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;something poor or mediocre, a failure&quot; is by 1936 in U.S. slang. From late 14c. as the name for a heavy metal clamp of some kind. <span class="foreign"><strong>Dog&#x27;s age</strong></span> &quot;a long time&quot; is by 1836. Adjectival phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>dog-eat-dog</strong></span> &quot;ruthlessly competitive&quot; is by 1850s. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>put on the dog</strong></span> &quot;get dressed up&quot; (1934) may be from comparison of dog collars to the stiff stand-up shirt collars that in the 1890s were the height of male fashion (and were known as <span class="foreign">dog-collars</span> from at least 1883).</p>
