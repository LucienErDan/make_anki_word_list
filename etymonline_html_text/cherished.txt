Watkins suggests a PIE reduplicated root <span class="foreign">*bha-bhā-</span> &quot;broad bean;&quot; de Vaan writes that the Italic, Slavic and Germanic &quot;are probably independent loanwords from a European substratum word of the form <span class="foreign">*bab-</span> (or similar) &#x27;bean&#x27;.&quot;</p>
<p>As a metaphor for &quot;something of small value&quot; it is attested from c. 1300 (<span class="foreign"><strong>hill of beans</strong></span> as something not much to amount to is from 1863). Meaning &quot;head&quot; is U.S. baseball slang 1905 (in <span class="foreign"><strong>bean-ball</strong></span> &quot;a pitch thrown at the head&quot;); thus slang verb <span class="foreign">bean</span> meaning &quot;to hit on the head,&quot; attested from 1910. <span class="foreign"><strong>Bean-shooter</strong></span> as a child&#x27;s weapon for mischief, a sort of small sling-shot to fire beans, is attested from 1876. Slang <span class="foreign"><strong>bean-counter</strong></span> &quot;accountant&quot; recorded by 1971.</p>
<p>The notion of lucky or magic beans in English folklore is from the exotic beans or large seeds, carried from the Caribbean or South America by the Gulf Stream, that wash up occasionally in Cornwall and western Scotland. They were cherished, believed to ward off the evil eye and aid in childbirth.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>not know beans</strong></span> &quot;be ignorant&quot; is attested by 1842 in American English, often said to be a New England phrase; it is perhaps from the &quot;object of little worth&quot; sense. <p>Sense of &quot;lower front part of a shirt or skirt&quot; led to that of &quot;upper legs of seated person&quot; (c. 1300). <blockquote>As far as the forbidden fruit is concerned, again, the Quran does not mention it explicitly, but according to traditional commentaries it was not an apple, as believed by Christians and Jews, but wheat.
