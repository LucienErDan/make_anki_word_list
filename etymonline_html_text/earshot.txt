The use of the word in Middle English was reinforced by Anglo-French <span class="foreign">Engleis</span>. Cognates: Dutch <span class="foreign">Engelsch</span>, German <span class="foreign">Englisch</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">Engelsk</span>, French <span class="foreign">Anglais</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">Engelsche</span>), Spanish <span class="foreign">Inglés</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">Inglese</span>.</p>
<p>Technically &quot;of the Angles,&quot; but <span class="foreign">Englisc</span> also was used from earliest times without distinction for all the Germanic invaders -- Angles, Saxon, Jutes (Bede&#x27;s <span class="foreign">gens Anglorum</span>) -- and applied to their group of related languages by Alfred the Great. &quot;The name <span class="foreign">English</span> for the language is thus older than the name <span class="foreign">England</span> for the country&quot; [OED]. After 1066, it specifically meant the native population of England (as distinguished from Normans and French occupiers), a distinction which lasted about a generation. But as late as Robert of Gloucester&#x27;s &quot;Chronicle&quot; (c. 1300) it still could retain a sense of &quot;Anglian&quot; and be distinguished from &quot;Saxon&quot; (&quot;Þe englisse in þe norþ half, þe saxons bi souþe&quot;).</p>
<blockquote>... when Scots &amp; others are likely to be within earshot, <span class="foreign">Britain</span> &amp; <span class="foreign">British</span> should be inserted as tokens, but no more, of what is really meant [Fowler]</blockquote>
<p>In pronunciation, &quot;En-&quot; has become &quot;In-,&quot; perhaps through the frequency of <span class="foreign">-ing-</span> words and the relative rarity of <span class="foreign">-e-</span> before <span class="foreign">-ng-</span> in the modern language.
