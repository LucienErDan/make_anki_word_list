and meaning of clock" href="/word/clock#etymonline_v_13831"><em>clock</em> (n.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;machine to measure and indicate time mechanically&quot; (since late 1940s also electronically), late 14c., <span class="foreign">clokke</span>, originally &quot;clock with bells,&quot; probably from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">clocke</span> (Dutch <span class="foreign">klok</span>) &quot;a clock,&quot; from Old North French <span class="foreign">cloque</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">cloke</span>, Modern French <span class="foreign">cloche</span> &quot;a bell&quot;), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">clocca</span> &quot;bell,&quot; which probably is from Celtic (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">clocc</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">cloch</span>, Manx <span class="foreign">clagg</span> &quot;a bell&quot;) and spread by Irish missionaries (unless the Celtic words are from Latin). Ultimately of imitative origin.</p>
<blockquote>Wherever it actually arose, it was prob. echoic, imitating the rattling made by the early handbells of sheet-iron and quadrilateral shape, rather than the ringing of the cast circular bells of later date. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">dægmæl</span>, from <span class="foreign">dæg</span> &quot;day&quot; + <span class="foreign">mæl</span> &quot;measure, mark&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">meal</span> (n.1)). The Latin word was <span class="foreign">horologium</span> (source of French <span class="foreign">horologe</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">reloj</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">oriolo, orologio</span>); the Greeks used a water-clock (<span class="foreign">klepsydra</span>, literally &quot;water thief;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">clepsydra</span>).</p>

<p>The image of <span class="foreign">put</span> (or <span class="foreign">set</span>) <span class="foreign">the clock back</span> &quot;return to an earlier state or system&quot; is from 1862. <span class="foreign">Round-the-clock</span> (adj.) is from 1943, originally in reference to air raids. To have a <span class="foreign">face that would stop a clock</span> &quot;be very ugly&quot; is from 1886. (Variations from c. 1890 include <span class="foreign">break a mirror</span>, <span class="foreign">kill chickens</span>.)</p>
