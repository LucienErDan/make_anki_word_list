title="Origin and meaning of utopia" href="/word/utopia#etymonline_v_4583"><em>utopia</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1551, from Modern Latin <span class="foreign">Utopia</span>, literally &quot;nowhere,&quot; coined by Thomas More (and used as title of his book, 1516, about an imaginary island enjoying the utmost perfection in legal, social, and political systems), from Greek <span class="foreign">ou</span> &quot;not&quot; + <span class="foreign">topos</span> &quot;place&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">topos</span>). The current (since c. 1960) explanation of Greek <span class="foreign">ou</span> &quot;not&quot; is an odd one, as it derives the word from the PIE root <span class="crossreference">*aiw-</span> &quot;vital force, life; long life, eternity.&quot; Linguists presume a pre-Greek phrase <span class="foreign">*(ne) hoiu (kwid)</span> &quot;(not on your) life,&quot; with <span class="foreign">ne</span> &quot;not&quot; + <span class="foreign">*kwid</span>, an &quot;emphasizing particle&quot; [Watkins]. The same pattern is found elsewhere.</p>
<p>Extended to any perfect place by 1610s. Commonly, but incorrectly, taken as from Greek <span class="foreign">eu-</span> &quot;good&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">eu-</span>) an error reinforced by the introduction of <span class="crossreference">dystopia</span> (by 1844).
