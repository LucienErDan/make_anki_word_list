Literally &quot;concealed place&quot; (compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">hellir</span> &quot;cave, cavern&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kel- </span>(1) &quot;to cover, conceal, save.&quot;</p>
<p>The English word may be in part from Old Norse mythological <span class="foreign">Hel</span> (from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*halija</span> &quot;one who covers up or hides something&quot;), in Norse mythology the name of Loki&#x27;s daughter who rules over the evil dead in <span class="foreign">Niflheim</span>, the lowest of all worlds (<span class="foreign">nifl</span> &quot;mist&quot;). A pagan concept and word fitted to a Christian idiom. In Middle English, also of the <span class="foreign">Limbus Patrum</span>, place where the Patriarchs, Prophets, etc. awaited the Atonement. Used in the KJV for Old Testament Hebrew <span class="foreign">Sheol</span> and New Testament Greek <span class="foreign">Hades</span>, <span class="foreign">Gehenna</span>. Used figuratively for &quot;state of misery, any bad experience&quot; at least since late 14c. As an expression of disgust, etc., first recorded 1670s.</p>
<p>To have <span class="foreign"><strong>hell break loose</strong></span> is from 1630s. Expression <span class="foreign"><strong>hell in a handbasket</strong></span> is attested by 1867, in a context implying use from a few years before, and the notion of going to Heaven in a handbasket is from 1853, implying &quot;easy passage&quot; to the destination. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hell or high water</strong></span> (1874) apparently is a variation of <span class="foreign">between the devil and the deep blue sea</span>. To wish someone would <span class="foreign"><strong>go to hell</strong></span> is in Shakespeare (&quot;Merchant of Venice&quot;). <span class="foreign"><strong>Snowball&#x27;s chance in hell</strong></span> &quot;no chance&quot; is from 1931; <span class="foreign"><strong>till hell freezes over</strong></span> &quot;never&quot; is from 1832.</p>
<p>To do something <span class="foreign"><strong>for the hell of it</strong></span> &quot;just for fun&quot; is from 1921. To ride <span class="foreign"><strong>hell for leather</strong></span> is from 1889, originally with reference to riding on horseback. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hell on wheels</strong></span> is from 1843 as the name of a steamboat; its general popularity dates from 1869 in reference to the temporary workers&#x27; vice-ridden towns along the U.S. transcontinental railroad. <p>Also used by c. 1200 of an animal or plant much below the ordinary size of its species.&quot; The use of <span class="foreign">dwarf</span> in the Germanic mythological sense, &quot;a diminished and generally deformed being, dwelling in rocks and hills and skilled in working metals,&quot; seems to have faded after Middle English and been revived after c. 1770 from German.</p>
<blockquote>Whilst in this and other ways the dwarfs do at times have dealings with mankind, yet on the whole they seem to shrink from man; they give the impression of a downtrodden afflicted race, which is on the point of abandoning its ancient home to new and more powerful invaders. There is stamped on their character something <span class="foreign">shy</span> and something <span class="foreign">heathenish</span>, which estranges them from intercourse with christians. They chafe at human faithlessness, which no doubt would primarily mean the apostacy from heathenism. In the poems of the Mid. Ages, <span class="foreign">Laurin</span> is expressly set before us as a <span class="foreign">heathen</span>. It goes sorely against the dwarfs to see churches built, <span class="foreign">bell-ringing</span> ... disturbs their ancient privacy; they also hate the clearing of forests, agriculture, new fangled pounding-machinery for ore. [&quot;Teutonic Mythology,&quot; Jakob Grimm, transl. Stallybrass, 1883]</blockquote>
<p>The shift of the Old English guttural at the end of the word to modern <span class="foreign">-f</span> is typical (compare <span class="crossreference">enough</span>, <span class="crossreference">draft</span>) and begins to appear early 14c. In Middle English it also was <span class="foreign">dwerþ</span>, <span class="foreign">dwerke</span>. Old English plural <span class="foreign">dweorgas</span> became Middle English <span class="foreign">dwarrows</span>, later leveled down to <span class="foreign">dwarfs</span>. The use of <span class="foreign">dwarves</span> for the legendary race was popularized by J.R.R. Tolkien. As an adjective, from 1590s.</p>

<p>The use of <span class="foreign">giant</span> and <span class="foreign">dwarf</span> in reference to stars of the highest and lowest luminosity is attested by 1914, said to have been suggested by Danish astronomer Ejnar Hertzsprung, (1873-1967); hence <span class="foreign">red dwarf</span> (attested by 1922), <span class="foreign">white dwarf</span>, <span class="foreign">black dwarf</span>
