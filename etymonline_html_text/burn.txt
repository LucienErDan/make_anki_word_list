<p>All these are from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*brennanan</span> (causative <span class="foreign">*brannjanan</span>)<span class="foreign">, </span>source also of Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bernen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">branden</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">brinnan</span>, German <span class="foreign">brennen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">-brannjan</span> &quot;to set on fire;&quot; but the ultimate etymology is uncertain. Related: <span class="foreign">Burned</span>/<span class="crossreference">burnt</span> (see <span class="crossreference">-ed</span>); <span class="crossreference">burning</span>.</p>
<p>Figurative use (of passions, battle, etc.) was in Old English. Meaning &quot;be hot, radiate heat&quot; is from late 13c. Meaning &quot;produce a burning sensation, sting&quot; is from late 14c. Meaning &quot;cheat, swindle, victimize&quot; is first attested 1650s. In late 18c, slang, <span class="foreign">burned</span> meant &quot;infected with venereal disease.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>burn one&#x27;s bridges</strong></span><span class="foreign"> (behind one)</span> &quot;behave so as to destroy any chance of returning to a status quo&quot; (attested by 1892 in Mark Twain), perhaps ultimately is from reckless cavalry raids in the American Civil War. Of money, to <span class="foreign"><strong>burn a hole in (one&#x27;s) pocket</strong></span> &quot;affect a person with a desire to spend&quot; from 1850.</p>
