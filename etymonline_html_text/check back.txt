<p>The other senses seem all to have developed from the chess sense, or from the noun: &quot;To arrest, stop,&quot; then &quot;to hold in restraint&quot; (1620s); &quot;to hold up or control&quot; (an assertion, a person, etc.) by comparison with some authority or record (1690s); of baggage, etc., &quot;to hand over in return for a check that serves as a means of identifying&quot; (1846); &quot;to note with a mark as having been examined, etc., mark off from a list&quot; (1928).</p>
<p>Hence, to <span class="foreign">check off</span> (1839); to <span class="foreign">check up</span> (1883); to <span class="foreign">check in</span> or <span class="foreign">out</span> (in a hotel, of a library book, etc., 1909). Also c. 1300 in a generalized sense, &quot;harmful incident or event, hostile environment.&quot;</p>

<p>As &quot;an exposure of the king to a direct attack from an opposing piece&quot; early 15c. When his king is in check, a player&#x27;s choices are severely limited. From that notion come the many extended senses: From the notion of &quot;a sudden stoppage, hindrance, restraint&quot; (1510s) comes that of &quot;act or means of checking or restraining,&quot; also &quot;means of detecting or exposing or preventing error; a check against forgery or alteration.</p>

<p>&quot;Hence: &quot;a counter-register as a token of ownership used to check against, and prevent, loss or theft&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">hat check</span>, etc.), 1812.  Hence also the financial use for &quot;written order for money drawn on a bank, money draft&quot; (1798, often spelled <span class="foreign">cheque</span>), which was probably influenced by <span class="crossreference">exchequer</span>. Hence also &quot;mark put against names or items on a list indicating they have been verified or otherwise examined&quot; (by 1856).</p>
<blockquote>From its use in chess the word has been widely transferred in French and English. In the sense-extension, the sb. and vb. have acted and reacted on each other, so that it is difficult to trace and exhibit the order in which special senses arose [OED]</blockquote>

In other modern Germanic languages the cognates mostly have been ousted in this sense by words akin to Modern English <span class="crossreference">ridge</span> (such as Danish <span class="foreign">ryg</span>, German <span class="foreign">Rücken</span>).</p>
<p>Many Indo-European languages show signs of once having distinguished the horizontal back of an animal (or a mountain range) from the upright back of a human. In other cases, a modern word for &quot;back&quot; may come from a word related to &quot;spine&quot; (Italian <span class="foreign">schiena</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">spina</span>) or &quot;shoulder, shoulder blade&quot; (Spanish <span class="foreign">espalda</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">plecy</span>).</p>
<p>By synecdoche, &quot;the whole body,&quot; especially with reference to clothing. Meaning &quot;upright part of a chair&quot; is from 1520s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>turn (one&#x27;s) back on (someone or something)</strong></span> &quot;ignore&quot; is from early 14c. As a U.S. football position by 1876, so called from being behind the line of rushers; further distinguished according to relative position as <span class="foreign">quarterback</span>, <span class="foreign">halfback</span>, <span class="foreign">fullback</span>.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>know (something) like the back of one&#x27;s hand</strong></span>, implying familiarity, is first attested 1893 in a dismissive speech made to a character in Robert Louis Stevenson&#x27;s &quot;Catriona&quot;:</p>
<blockquote>If I durst speak to herself, you may be certain I would never dream of trusting it to you; because I know you like the back of my hand, and all your blustering talk is that much wind to me.</blockquote>
<p>The story, a sequel to &quot;Kidnapped,&quot; has a Scottish setting and context, and <span class="foreign">the back of my hand to you</span> was noted in the late 19th century as a Scottish expression meaning &quot;I will have nothing to do with you&quot; [see Longmuir&#x27;s edition of Jamieson&#x27;s Scottish dictionary]. In English generally, the <span class="foreign">back of (one&#x27;s) hand</span> has been used to imply contempt and rejection at least since 1300.
