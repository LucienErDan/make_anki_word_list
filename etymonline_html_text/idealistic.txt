<p>Modern pejorative use seems to have begun on the socialist left, mocking those who were unwilling to take a hard line. OED has this citation, from &quot;The Nation&quot; in 1923:</p>
<blockquote>There is nothing the matter with the United States except ... the parlor socialists, up-lifters, and do-goods.</blockquote>
<p>The form <span class="foreign">do-gooder</span> appears in American English by 1922, presumably because <span class="foreign">do-good</span> was no longer felt as sufficiently noun-like. A slightly older word for this was <span class="crossreference">goo-goo</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/do-gooder#etymonline_v_24636"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of commune" href="/word/commune#etymonline_v_17244">commune (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1792, in a French context, &quot;a community organized and self-governed for local interest, subordinate to the state,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">commune</span> &quot;small territorial divisions set up after the Revolution,&quot; from Middle French <span class="foreign">commune</span> &quot;free city, group of citizens&quot; (12c.), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">communia</span>, literally &quot;that which is common,&quot; noun use of neuter plural of Latin adjective <span class="foreign">communis</span> &quot;common, general&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">common</span> (adj.)).</p>
<blockquote>I am not aware that any English word precisely corresponds to the general term of the original. In France every association of human dwellings forms a <span class="foreign">commune</span>, and every commune is governed by a <span class="foreign">Maire</span> and a <span class="foreign">Conseil municipal</span>. In other words, the <span class="foreign">mancipium</span>, or municipal privilege, which belongs in England to chartered corporations alone, is alike extended to every commune into which the cantons and departments of France were divided at the Revolution. [translator&#x27;s note (Henry Reeve) to 1838 English edition of de Tocqueville&#x27;s &quot;Democracy in America&quot;] </blockquote>
<p>The English word sometimes was used in reference to the idealistic communities formed in U.S. c. 1840s, inspired by Fourier and Owen, and was used in late 1960s of hippie settlements established along similar lines.</p>
<p>The Commune of Paris usurped the government during the Reign of Terror. The word later was applied to a government on communalistic principles set up in Paris in 1871 upon the withdrawal of the Germans, which was quickly suppressed by national troops.
