The Greek word was used by Church writers in reference to various sects, schools, etc. in the New Testament: the Sadducees, the Pharisees, and even the Christians, as sects of Judaism. Hence the meaning &quot;unorthodox religious sect or doctrine&quot; in the Latin word as used by Christian writers in Late Latin. But in English bibles it usually is translated <span class="crossreference">sect</span>. <p>Meaning &quot;interior partition of a structure&quot; is mid-13c. In this case, English uses one word where many languages have two, such as German <span class="foreign">Mauer</span> &quot;outer wall of a town, fortress, etc.,&quot; used also in reference to the former Berlin Wall, and <span class="foreign">wand</span> &quot;partition wall within a building&quot; (compare the distinction, not always rigorously kept, in Italian <span class="foreign">muro</span>/<span class="foreign">parete</span>, Irish <span class="foreign">mur</span>/<span class="foreign">fraig</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">mūras</span>/<span class="foreign">siena</span>, etc.). The Latin word for &quot;defensive wall&quot; was <span class="foreign">murus</span> (see <span class="crossreference">mural</span>).</p>
<p>Anatomical use from late 14c. To <span class="foreign">give (someone) the wall</span> &quot;allow him or her to walk on the (cleaner) wall side of the pavement&quot; is from 1530s. To <span class="foreign">turn (one&#x27;s) face to the wall</span> &quot;prepare to die&quot; is from 1570s. Phrase <span class="foreign">up the wall</span> &quot;angry, crazy&quot; is from 1951; <span class="foreign">off the wall</span> &quot;unorthodox, unconventional&quot; is recorded from 1966, American English student slang. To <span class="foreign">go over the wall</span> &quot;escape&quot; (originally from prison) is from 1933. <span class="foreign">Wall-to-wall</span> (adj.) recorded 1939, of shelving, etc.; metaphoric
