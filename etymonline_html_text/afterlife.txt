<p>Originally &quot;life on earth, this world (as opposed to the afterlife),&quot; sense extended to &quot;the known world,&quot; then to &quot;the physical world in the broadest sense, the universe&quot; (c. 1200). In Old English gospels, the commonest word for &quot;the physical world,&quot; was <span class="foreign">Middangeard</span> (Old Norse <span class="foreign">Midgard</span>), literally &quot;the middle enclosure&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">yard</span> (n.1)), which is rooted in Germanic cosmology. Greek <span class="foreign">kosmos</span> in its ecclesiastical sense of &quot;world of people&quot; sometimes was rendered in Gothic as <span class="foreign">manaseþs</span>, literally &quot;seed of man.&quot; The usual Old Norse word was <span class="foreign">heimr</span>, literally &quot;abode&quot; (see home). Words for &quot;world&quot; in some other Indo-European languages derive from the root for &quot;bottom, foundation&quot; (such as Irish <span class="foreign">domun</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">duno</span>, related to English <span class="foreign">deep</span>); the Lithuanian word is <span class="foreign">pasaulis</span>, from <span class="foreign">pa-</span> &quot;under&quot; + <span class="foreign">saulė</span> &quot;sun.&quot;</p>
<p>Original sense in <span class="foreign">world without end</span>, translating Latin <span class="foreign">saecula saeculorum</span>, and in <span class="crossreference">worldly</span>. Thus <span class="foreign">kosmos</span> had an important secondary sense of &quot;ornaments of a woman&#x27;s dress, decoration&quot; (compare <span class="foreign">kosmokomes</span> &quot;dressing the hair,&quot; and <span class="crossreference">cosmetic</span>) as well as &quot;the universe, the world.&quot;</p>
<p>Pythagoras is said to have been the first to apply this word to &quot;the universe,&quot; perhaps originally meaning &quot;the starry firmament,&quot; but it later was extended to the whole physical world, including the earth. For specific reference to &quot;the world of people,&quot; the classical phrase was <span class="foreign">he oikoumene (ge)</span> &quot;the inhabited (earth).&quot; Septuagint uses both <span class="foreign">kosmos</span> and <span class="foreign">oikoumene</span>. <span class="foreign">Kosmos</span> also was used in Christian religious writing with a sense of &quot;worldly life, this world (as opposed to the afterlife),&quot; but the more frequent word for this was <span class="foreign">aiōn</span>, literally &quot;lifetime, age.&quot;</p>
