title="Origin and meaning of mundane" href="/word/mundane#etymonline_v_19272"><em>mundane</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-15c., <span class="foreign">mondeine</span>, &quot;of this world, worldly, terrestrial,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">mondain</span> &quot;of this world, worldly, earthly, secular;&quot; also &quot;pure, clean; noble, generous&quot; (12c.) and directly from Late Latin <span class="foreign">mundanus</span> &quot;belonging to the world&quot; (as distinct from the Church), in classical Latin &quot;a citizen of the world, cosmopolite,&quot; from <span class="foreign">mundus</span> &quot;universe, world,&quot; which is identical to <span class="foreign">mundus</span>  &quot;clean, elegant,&quot; but the exact connection is uncertain and the etymology is unknown.</p>

<p>Latin <span class="foreign">mundus</span> &quot;world&quot; was used as a translation of Greek <span class="foreign">kosmos</span> (see <span class="crossreference">cosmos</span>) in its Pythagorean sense of &quot;the physical universe&quot; (the original sense of the Greek word was &quot;orderly arrangement&quot;). Like <span class="foreign">kosmos</span> (and perhaps by influence of it), Latin <span class="foreign">mundus</span> also was used of a woman&#x27;s &quot;ornaments, dress,&quot; which also could entangle the adjective <span class="foreign">mundus</span> &quot;clean, elegant.&quot;</p>

<p>The English word&#x27;s extended sense of &quot;dull, uninteresting&quot; is attested by 1850. Related: <span class="foreign">Mundanely</span>. The <span class="foreign"><strong>mundane era</strong></span> was the chronology that began with the supposed epoch of the Creation (famously reckoned as 4004 B.C.E.).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mundane#etymonline_v_19272"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of antemundane" href="/word/antemundane#etymonline_v_41434">antemundane (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;existing or happening before the creation of the world,&quot; 1731; see <span class="crossreference">ante-</span> + <span class="crossreference">mundane</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/antemundane#etymonline_v_41434"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of beau-monde" href="/word/beau-monde#etymonline_v_30003">beau-monde (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">also <span class="foreign">beau monde</span>, &quot;the fashionable world,&quot; 1714, French, from <span class="foreign">beau</span> (see <span class="crossreference">beau</span>) + <span class="foreign">monde</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">mundus</span> &quot;world&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">mundane</span>).
