and meaning of dish" href="/word/dish#etymonline_v_11430"><em>dish</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">disc</span> &quot;plate, bowl, platter,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">discus</span> &quot;dish, platter, quoit,&quot; in Medieval Latin &quot;a table, dais, desk, pulpit,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">diskos</span> &quot;disk, platter&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">disk</span> (n.)).</p>
<p>A common West Germanic borrowing; Old High German took the word as <span class="foreign">tisc</span> &quot;plate,&quot; but German <span class="foreign">Tisch</span> now means &quot;table,&quot; in common with some other later Romanic forms of Latin <span class="foreign">discus</span> (such as Italian <span class="foreign">desco</span>, French <span class="foreign">dais</span>); compare <span class="crossreference">desk</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">dais</span>.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;particular variety of food served in a dish&quot; is first recorded mid-15c. Meaning &quot;what one likes, what is suited to one&#x27;s taste&quot; is by 1918; that of &quot;attractive woman&quot; is 1920s. Meaning &quot;concave reflector or antenna&quot; attested from 1948.</p>
<blockquote>Originally applied to very shallow or flat vessels, as plates and platters, the term now usually includes any large open vessel, more or less deep, and with or without a cover, used to contain food or table-drink such as tea, coffee, or chocolate. The use of the term to include drinking-vessels, as bowls and cups, is less common, and seems to be obsolescent, except as such vessels are included in the collective plural <span class="foreign">dishes</span>.
