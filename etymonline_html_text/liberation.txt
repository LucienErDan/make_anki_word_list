<blockquote>It is notable that it was thought necessary to join <span class="foreign">wif</span>, a neuter noun, representing a female person, to <span class="foreign">man</span>, a masc. noun representing either a male or female person, to form a word denoting a female person exclusively. <p>The formation is peculiar to English and Dutch. Replaced older Old English <span class="foreign">wif</span> and <span class="foreign">quean</span> as the word for &quot;female human being.&quot; The pronunciation of the singular altered in Middle English by the rounding influence of <span class="foreign">-w-</span>; the plural retains the original vowel. <p>In American English, <span class="crossreference">lady</span> is &quot;In loose and especially polite usage, a woman&quot; [Craigie, &quot;Dictionary of American English&quot;]. The effect is odd.&quot; [Harriet Martineau, 1837]</p>
