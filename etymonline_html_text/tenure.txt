<p>One early theory traces it to <span class="foreign">Corlear&#x27;s Hook</span>, a section of New York City.</p>
<blockquote>HOOKER. A resident of the Hook, i.e. a strumpet, a sailor&#x27;s trull. <p>Or perhaps related to <span class="foreign">hooker</span> &quot;thief, pickpocket&quot; (1560s).</p>
<p>But the word is likely a reference to prostitutes <span class="foreign">hooking</span> or snaring clients. <span class="foreign">Hook</span> in the figurative sense of &quot;that by which anyone is attracted or caught&quot; is recorded from early 15c.; and <span class="crossreference">hook</span> (v.) in the figurative sense of &quot;catch hold of and draw in&quot; is attested from 1570s; in reference to &quot;fishing&quot; for a husband or a wife, it was in common use from c. 1800. All of which makes the modern sense seem a natural step. Compare French <span class="foreign">accrocheuse</span>, <span class="foreign">raccrocheuse</span>, common slang term for &quot;street-walker, prostitute,&quot; literally &quot;hooker&quot; of men.</p>
<p>The family name <span class="foreign">Hooker</span> (attested from c. 975) would mean &quot;maker of hooks,&quot; or else refer to an agricultural laborer who used a hook (compare Old English <span class="foreign">weodhoc</span> &quot;weed-hook&quot;).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/hooker#etymonline_v_14430"><span>Relate entries &amp;
