<p>The Sanskrit compound is <span class="foreign">krmih</span> &quot;worm&quot; (from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kwrmi-</span> &quot;worm,&quot; source also of Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kirmis</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">cruim</span>, Albanian <span class="foreign">krimp</span> &quot;worm&quot;) + <span class="foreign">-ja-</span> &quot;produced&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gene-</span> &quot;give birth, beget&quot;). The insect lives in the Levant and southern Europe on a species of small evergreen oak (kermes oak) and in ancient Europe were the main source of red and scarlet dye. The dye is prepared from the dried bodies of pregnant females, which alive resemble small roundish grains about the size of peas and cling immobile to the tree on which they live. From this fact kermes dye was, for a long time, mistaken as being from a seed or excrescence of the tree, and the word for it in Greek was <span class="foreign">kokkos</span>, literally &quot;a grain, seed&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cocco-</span>). This was passed to Latin as <span class="foreign">coccum</span>, <span class="foreign">coccus</span> &quot;berry [sic] yielding scarlet dye,&quot; in late use &quot;scarlet color, scarlet garment.&quot;</p>
<p>So important was kermes (<span class="foreign">coccus</span>) as a commercial source of scarlet dye that derivatives of the name for it have displaced the original word for &quot;red&quot; in many languages, such as Welsh <span class="foreign">coch</span> (from Latin), Modern Greek <span class="foreign">kokkinos</span>. Also compare Russian <span class="foreign">čcermnyj</span> &quot;purple-red,&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">čruminu</span>. Compare also <span class="crossreference">crimson</span> (n.).</p>
<p>Kermes dyes have been found in burial wrappings in Anglo-Scandinavian York, but the use of kermes dyes seems to have been lost in Europe from the Dark Ages until early 15c. It fell out of use again with the introduction of <span class="crossreference">cochineal</span> (the word for which itself might be from <span class="foreign">coccus</span>) from the New World.</p>
<blockquote>Cloths dyed with kermes are of a deep red colour; and though much inferior in brilliancy to the scarlet cloths dyed with real Mexican cochineal, they retain the colour better and are less liable to stain. The tapestries of Brussels and other parts of Flanders, which have scarcely lost any thing of their original brilliancy, even after a lapse of 200 years, were all dyed with kermes. [W.T. Brande, &quot;Dictionary of Science, Literature, &amp; Art,&quot; London, 1842]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/kermes#etymonline_v_42956"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of color" href="/word/color#etymonline_v_15845">color (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 13c., &quot;skin color, complexion,&quot; from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">culur, coulour</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">color</span> &quot;color, complexion, appearance&quot; (Modern French <span class="foreign">couleur</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">color</span> &quot;color of the skin; color in general, hue; appearance,&quot; from Old Latin <span class="foreign">colos</span>, originally &quot;a covering&quot; (akin to <span class="foreign">celare</span> &quot;to hide, conceal&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kel- </span>(1) &quot;to cover, conceal, save.&quot; Old English words for &quot;color&quot; were <span class="foreign">hiw</span> (&quot;hue&quot;), <span class="foreign">bleo</span>. For sense evolution, compare Sanskrit <span class="foreign">varnah</span> &quot;covering, color,&quot; which is related to <span class="foreign">vrnoti</span> &quot;covers,&quot; and also see <span class="crossreference">chroma</span>.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Colour</span> was the usual English spelling from 14c., from Anglo-French. Classical correction made <span class="foreign">color</span> an alternative from 15c., and that spelling became established in the U.S. (see <span class="crossreference">-or</span>). </p>
<p>Meaning &quot;a hue or tint, a visible color, the color of something&quot; is from c. 1300. As &quot;color as an inherent property of matter, that quality of a thing or appearance which is perceived by the eye alone,&quot; from late 14c. From early 14c. as &quot;a coloring matter, pigment, dye.&quot; From mid-14c. as &quot;kind, sort, variety, description.&quot; From late 14c. in figurative sense of &quot;stylistic device, embellishment. From c. 1300 as &quot;a reason or argument advanced by way of justifying, explaining, or excusing an action,&quot; hence &quot;specious reason or argument, that which hides the real character of something&quot; (late 14c.).</p>
<p>From c. 1300 as &quot;distinctive mark of identification&quot; (as of a badge or insignia or livery, later of a prize-fighter, horse-rider, etc.), originally in reference to a coat of arms. Hence figurative sense as in <span class="foreign">show one&#x27;s (true) colors</span> &quot;reveal one&#x27;s opinions or intentions;&quot; compare <span class="crossreference">colors</span>.</p>
<p>In reference to &quot;the hue of the darker (as distinguished from the &#x27;white&#x27;) varieties of mankind&quot; [OED], attested from 1792, in <span class="foreign">people of colour</span>, in translations from French in reference to the French colony of Saint-Domingue (modern Haiti) and there meaning &quot;mulattoes.&quot;</p>
<p>In reference to musical tone from 1590s. <span class="foreign">Color-scheme</span> is from 1860. <span class="foreign">Color-coded</span> is by 1943, in reference to wiring in radios and military aircraft. <span class="foreign">Color-line</span> in reference to social and legal discrimination by race in the U.S. is from 1875, originally referring to Southern whites voting in unity and taking back control of state governments during Reconstruction (it had been called <span class="foreign">white line</span> about a year earlier, and with more accuracy).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/color#etymonline_v_15845"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of ambergris" href="/word/ambergris#etymonline_v_10954">ambergris (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 15c., from Old French <span class="foreign">ambre gris</span> &quot;gray amber,&quot; &quot;a wax-like substance of ashy colour, found floating in tropical seas, a morbid secretion from the intestines of the sperm-whale. Its origin was known to Constantinus Africanus (obit c. 1087) bit it was a mystery in Johnson&#x27;s day, and he records nine different theories; &quot;What sort of thing is Ambergrease?&quot; was a type of a puzzling question beyond conjecture. King Charles II&#x27;s favorite dish was said to be eggs and ambergris [Macauley, &quot;History of England&quot;].</p>
<blockquote>Praise is like ambergris; a little whiff of it, by snatches, is very agreeable; but when a man holds a whole lump of it to his nose, it is a stink and strikes you down. [Pope, c. 1720]</blockquote>

Compare Albanian <span class="foreign">kokosh</span> &quot;cock,&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">kikkos</span>, Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kukkuta</span>, Malay <span class="foreign">kukuk</span>. &quot;Though at home in English and French, not the general name either in Teutonic or Romanic; the latter has derivatives of L. <span class="foreign">gallus</span>, the former of OTeut. <span class="foreign">*hanon-</span>&quot; [OED]; compare <span class="crossreference">hen</span>.</p>
<p>Old English <span class="foreign">cocc</span> was a nickname for &quot;one who strutted like a cock,&quot; thus a common term in the Middle Ages for a pert boy, used of scullions, apprentices, servants, etc. It became a general term for &quot;fellow, man, chap,&quot; especially in <span class="foreign">old cock</span> (1630s). A common personal name till c. 1500, it was affixed to Christian names as a pet diminutive, as in <span class="foreign"><strong>Wilcox</strong></span>, <span class="foreign"><strong>Hitchcock</strong></span>, etc.</p>
<p>A <span class="foreign"><strong>cocker spaniel</strong></span> (1823) was trained to start woodcocks. <span class="foreign"><strong>Cock of the walk</strong></span> &quot;overbearing fellow, head of a group by overcoming opponents&quot; is from 1855 (<span class="foreign">cock</span> in this sense is from 1540s). <span class="foreign"><strong>Cock-and-bull</strong></span> in reference to a fictitious narrative sold as true is first recorded 1620s, perhaps an allusion to Aesop&#x27;s fables, with their incredible talking animals, or to a particular story, now forgotten. French has parallel expression <span class="foreign">coq-à-l&#x27;âne</span>.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Cock-lobster</strong></span> &quot;male lobster&quot; is attested by 1757.</p>
<blockquote>The cock-lobster is known by the narrow back-part of his tail; the two uppermost fins within his tail are stiff and hard, but those of the hen are soft, and the tail broader. The male, though generally smaller than the female, has the highest flavour in the body; his flesh is firmer, and the colour, when boiled, is redder. <p>The spelling adopted the Latin form 15c.-17c. The mineral has been so-called since Anglo-Saxon times; it was regarded by the ancients as a sort of petrified ice. In the specific sense in chemistry, &quot;body with a molecular structure that causes it to take the form of a regular solid enclosed by a certain number of plane surfaces,&quot; from 1620s.</p>
<blockquote>Crystall is a brighte stone and clere with watry colour. Men trowe that it is of snowe or yse made harde in space of many yeres. Therfore the Grekes yave a name therto. It is gendred in Asia and in Cipres, and namely in the northe moutaynes where the sonne is mooste feruent in somer. [Bartholomew Glanville, c. 1360, <span class="foreign">&quot;De proprietatibus rerum,&quot;</span> translated by John of Trevisa]</blockquote>
<p>A top-20 name for girls born in the U.S. between 1978 and 1984. As a shortened form of <span class="foreign">crystal-glass</span> it dates from 1590s. As an adjective, from late 14c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Crystal ball</strong></span> is from 1794.  <span class="foreign"><strong>Rock-crystal</strong></span> is the general name for transparent crystals of quartz. <span class="foreign"><strong>Crystal Palace</strong></span> was the name of the large building, made chiefly of glass and iron, for the universal exhibition of 1851 in London&#x27;s Hyde Park.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/crystal#etymonline_v_423"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of bias" href="/word/bias#etymonline_v_11113">bias (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1520s, &quot;oblique or diagonal line,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">biais</span> &quot;a slant, a slope, an oblique,&quot; also figuratively, &quot;an expedient, means&quot; (13c., originally in Old French a past-participle adjective, &quot;sideways, askance, against the grain&quot;), a word of unknown origin. Probably it came to French from Old Provençal <span class="foreign">biais</span>, which has cognates in Old Catalan and Sardinian, and is possibly via Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*(e)bigassius</span> from Greek <span class="foreign">epikarsios</span> &quot;athwart, crosswise, at an angle,&quot; from <span class="foreign">epi-</span> &quot;upon&quot; + <span class="foreign">karsios</span> &quot;oblique,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*krs-yo-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="crossreference">*sker-</span> (1) &quot;to cut.&quot;</p>
<p>In the old game of bowls, it was a technical term used in reference to balls made with a greater weight on one side (1560s), causing them to curve toward one side; hence the figurative use &quot;a one-sided tendency of the mind&quot; (1570s), and, at first especially in law, &quot;undue propensity or prejudice.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The bias of education, the bias of class-relationships, the bias of nationality, the political bias, the theological bias--these, added to the constitutional sympathies and antipathies, have much more influence in determining beliefs on social questions than has the small amount of evidence collected. [Herbert Spencer, &quot;The Study of Sociology,&quot; 1873]</blockquote>

<blockquote>For what a man had rather were true he more readily believes. Therefore he rejects difficult things from impatience of research; sober things, because they narrow hope; the deeper things of nature, from superstition; the light of experience, from arrogance and pride, lest his mind should seem to be occupied with things mean and transitory; things not commonly believed, out of deference to the opinion of the vulgar. Numberless in short are the ways, and sometimes imperceptible, in which the affections colour and infect the understanding. [Francis Bacon, &quot;Novum Organum,&quot; 1620]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/bias#etymonline_v_11113"><span>Relate entries &amp;
