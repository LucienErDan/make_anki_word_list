title="Origin and meaning of Mercury" href="/word/Mercury#etymonline_v_14662"><em>Mercury</em> </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;the Roman god Mercury,&quot; herald and ambassador of his father, Jupiter, mid-12c., <span class="foreign">Mercurie</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">Mercurius</span> &quot;Mercury,&quot; originally a god of tradesmen and thieves, from <span class="foreign">merx</span> &quot;merchandise&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">market</span> (n.)); or perhaps [Klein, Tucker] from Etruscan and influenced by <span class="foreign">merx</span>. De Vaan thinks it possible the whole stem *<span class="foreign">merk-</span> was borrowed and the god-name with it. Mercury later was identified with Greek Hermes and still later with Germanic Woden. From his role as a messenger and conveyor of information, since mid-17c. <span class="foreign">Mercury</span> has been a common name for a newspaper. </p>
<p>The planet closest to the sun was so called in classical Latin (c. 1300 in English). A hypothetical inhabitant of the planet was a <span class="foreign">Mercurean</span> (1855) or a <span class="foreign">Mercurian</span> (1755). For the metallic element, see <span class="crossreference">mercury</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Mercury#etymonline_v_14662"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of mercury" href="/word/mercury#etymonline_v_44655"><em>mercury</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>silver-white fluid metallic element, late 14c., from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">mercurius</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">Mercurius</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Mercury</span>). Prepared in ancient times from cinnabar, it was one of the seven metals (<span class="foreign">bodies terrestrial</span>) known to the ancients, which were coupled in astrology and alchemy with the seven known heavenly bodies. This one probably was associated with the planet for its mobility. The others were Sun/gold, Moon/silver, Mars/iron, Saturn/lead, Jupiter/tin, Venus/copper.</p>

<p>The Greek name for it was <span class="foreign">hydrargyros</span> &quot;liquid silver,&quot; which gives the element its symbol, <span class="foreign">Hg</span>. Compare <span class="crossreference">quicksilver</span>, which is its popular name. It has a freezing point of -39° C. The use of the word in reference to temperature or state of the atmosphere (by 1756) is from its use in thermometers and barometers.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mercury#etymonline_v_44655"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of mercuric" href="/word/mercuric#etymonline_v_44638">mercuric (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1828, in chemistry, &quot;relating to or containing mercury,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">mercury</span> + <span class="crossreference">-ic</span>. Meaning &quot;light-hearted, sprightly, volatile, changeable, quick&quot; (1640s) is from the qualities supposed to characterize those born under the planet Mercury (they also are the qualities of the god Mercury), probably also partly by association with the qualities of quicksilver.
