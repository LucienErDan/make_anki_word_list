and meaning of Dutch" href="/word/Dutch#etymonline_v_16017"><em>Dutch</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., of language, &quot;German, non-Scandinavian continental Germanic,&quot; also as a noun, &quot;a German language;&quot; also in <span class="foreign">Duche-lond</span> &quot;Germany.&quot; By mid-15c. distinguished into <span class="foreign">Higher</span> and <span class="foreign">Lower</span>, and used after c. 1600 in the narrower sense &quot;Hollanders, residents of the Netherlands.&quot; From Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">duutsch</span>, from Old High German <span class="foreign">duitisc</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*theudō</span> &quot;popular, national&quot; (source of Modern German <span class="foreign">Deutsch</span>), from PIE <span class="crossreference">*teuta-</span> &quot;tribe&quot; (compare <span class="crossreference">Teutonic</span>).</p>
<p>It corresponds to the Old English adjective <span class="foreign">þeodisc</span> &quot;belonging to the people,&quot; which was used especially of the common language of Germanic people (as opposed to Latin), a derivative of the Old English noun <span class="foreign">þeod</span> &quot;people, race, nation.&quot; The language name is first attested in Latin as <span class="foreign">theodice</span> (786 C.E.) in correspondence between Charlemagne&#x27;s court and the Pope, in reference to a synodical conference in Mercia; thus it refers to Old English. Its first use in reference to a German language (as opposed to a Germanic one) is two years later. The sense was extended from the language to the people who spoke it (in German, <span class="foreign">Diutisklant</span>, ancestor of <span class="foreign">Deutschland</span>, was in use by 13c.).</p>
<p>The sense in of the adjective in English narrowed to &quot;of the Netherlands&quot; in 17c., after they became a united, independent state and the focus of English attention and rivalry. In Holland, <span class="foreign">Duits</span> (formerly <span class="foreign">duitsch</span>) is used of the people of Germany. The old use of <span class="foreign">Dutch</span> for &quot;German&quot; continued in America (Irving and Cooper still distinguish <span class="foreign">High Dutch</span> &quot;German&quot; and <span class="foreign">Low Dutch</span> &quot;Dutch&quot;) and survives in <span class="foreign"><strong>Pennsylvania Dutch</strong></span> for the descendants of religious sects that immigrated from the Rhineland and Switzerland and their language.</p>
<p>Since c. 1600, <span class="foreign">Dutch</span> (adj.) has been a &quot;pejorative label pinned by English speakers on almost anything they regard as inferior, irregular, or contrary to &#x27;normal&#x27; (i.e., their own) practice&quot; [Rawson]. E.g. ironical <span class="foreign"><strong>Dutch treat</strong></span><span class="foreign">,</span> of each person paying for himself (1887), <span class="foreign"><strong>Dutch courage</strong></span> &quot;boldness inspired by intoxicating spirits&quot; (1809), nautical <span class="foreign"><strong>Dutch talent</strong></span> &quot;any piece of work not done in shipshape style (1867), etc. -- probably exceeded in such usage only by <span class="crossreference">Indian</span> and <span class="crossreference">Irish</span> -- reflecting first British commercial and military rivalry and later heavy German immigration to U.S.</p>
<blockquote><strong>Dutch concert</strong>, a concert in which each one sings his own song at the same time that his neighbor sings his; or a concert in which each one sings a verse of any song he pleases, some well-known chorus being sung after each verse. [Century Dictionary, 1897]</blockquote>
<blockquote>The Dutch themselves spoke English well enough to understand the unsavory connotations of the label and in 1934 Dutch officials were ordered by their government to stop using the term <span class="foreign">Dutch</span>. Instead, they were to rewrite their sentences so as to employ the official <span class="foreign">The Netherlands</span>. [Rawson]</blockquote>

<p>It is the hypothetical source of/evidence for its existence is provided by: Old Irish <span class="foreign">tuoth</span> &quot;people,&quot; Old Lithuanian <span class="foreign">tauta</span> &quot;people,&quot; Old Prussian <span class="foreign">tauto</span> &quot;country,&quot; Oscan <span class="foreign">touto</span> &quot;community,&quot; German <span class="foreign">Deutsch</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">þiuda</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">þeod</span> &quot;people, race, nation,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">þeodisc</span> &quot;belonging to the people.&quot; But Boutkan says it is probably a substratum word.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/*teuta-#etymonline_v_52728"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of German" href="/word/German#etymonline_v_6037">German (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;a native of Germany,&quot; 1520s, from Latin <span class="foreign">Germanus</span> (adjective and noun, plural <span class="foreign">Germani</span>), first attested in writings of Julius Caesar, who used <span class="foreign">Germani</span> to designate a group of tribes in northeastern Gaul, of unknown origin and considered to be neither Latin nor Germanic. Perhaps originally the name of an individual tribe, but Gaulish (Celtic) origins have been proposed, from words perhaps originally meaning &quot;noisy&quot; (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">garim</span> &quot;to shout&quot;) or &quot;neighbor&quot; (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">gair</span> &quot;neighbor&quot;). Middle English had <span class="foreign">Germayns</span> (plural, late 14c.), but only in the sense &quot;ancient Teuton, member of the Germanic tribes.&quot; The earlier English word was <span class="foreign">Almain</span> (early 14c., via French; see <span class="crossreference">Alemanni</span>) or <span class="crossreference">Dutch</span>. Shakespeare and Marlowe have <span class="foreign">Almain</span> for &quot;German; a German.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>Þe empere passede from þe Grees to þe Frenschemen and to þe Germans, þat beeþ Almayns. [John of Trevisa, translation of Higdon&#x27;s <span class="foreign">&quot;Polychronicon,&quot;</span> 1387]</blockquote>
<p>Their name for themselves, <span class="foreign">die Deutschen</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Dutch</span>), dates from 12c. Roman writers also used <span class="foreign">Teutoni</span> as a German tribal name, and writers in Latin after about 875 commonly refer to the German language as <span class="foreign">teutonicus</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Teutonic</span>). Meaning &quot;the German language&quot; in English is from 1748. <span class="foreign">High German</span> (1823 in English) and <span class="foreign">Low German</span> as a division of dialects is geographical: High German (from 16c. established as the literary language) was the German spoken in the upland regions in southern Germany, Low German (often including Dutch, Frisian, Flemish), also called <span class="crossreference">Plattdeutsch</span> was spoken in the regions near the North Sea. In the U.S. <span class="foreign">German</span> also was used of descendants of settlers from Germany.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/German#etymonline_v_6037"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Teutonic" href="/word/Teutonic#etymonline_v_10697">Teutonic (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">1610s, &quot;of or pertaining to the Germanic languages and to peoples or tribes who speak or spoke them,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">Teutonicus</span>, from <span class="foreign">Teutones</span>, <span class="foreign">Teutoni</span>, name of a tribe that inhabited coastal Germany near the mouth of the Elbe and devastated Gaul 113-101 B.C.E., probably via Celtic from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*theudanoz</span>, from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*teuta-</span> &quot;tribe.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Used in English in anthropology to avoid the modern political association of <span class="crossreference">German</span>; but in this anthropological sense French uses <span class="foreign">germanique</span> and German uses <span class="foreign">germanisch</span>, because neither uses its form of <span class="foreign">German</span> for the narrower national meaning (compare French <span class="foreign">allemand</span>, for which see <span class="crossreference">Alemanni</span>; and German <span class="foreign">deutsch</span>, under <span class="crossreference">Dutch</span>). In Finnish, Germany is <span class="foreign">Saksa</span> &quot;Land of the Saxons.&quot;
<br/><br/>
The <span class="foreign">Teutonic Knights</span> (founded c.1191) were a military order of German knights formed for service in the Holy Land, but who later crusaded in then-pagan Prussia and Lithuania.
