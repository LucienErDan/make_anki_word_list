undefined"><p>1560s, &quot;to handle, train, or direct&quot; (a horse), from the now-obsolete noun manage &quot;the handling or training of a horse; horsemanship&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">manege</span>, which is a modern revival of it), from Old French manège &quot;horsemanship,&quot; from Italian <span class="foreign">maneggio</span>, from <span class="foreign">maneggiare</span> &quot;to handle, touch,&quot; especially &quot;to control a horse,&quot; which ultimately from Latin noun <span class="foreign">manus</span> &quot;hand&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*man-</span> (2) &quot;hand&quot;).</p>

<p>Extended sense of &quot;control or direct by administrative ability&quot; any sort of business is by 1570s; meaning &quot;to wield (a tool or object) by hand&quot; is from 1580s. Meaning &quot;effect by effort&quot; (hence &quot;succeed in accomplishing&quot;) is by 1732. Intransitive sense of &quot;get by, carry on affairs&quot; is suggested by 1650s, in frequent use from mid-19c. Related: <span class="foreign">Managed</span>; <span class="foreign">managing</span>. <span class="foreign">Managed economy</span> was used by 1933.</p>

<blockquote><span class="foreign">Manage</span> literally implies handling, and hence primarily belongs to smaller concerns, on which one may at all times keep his hand: as, to <span class="foreign">manage</span> a house; to <span class="foreign">manage</span> a theater. Its essential idea is that of constant attention to details: as, only a combination of great abilities with a genius for industry can <span class="foreign">manage</span> the affairs of an empire.
