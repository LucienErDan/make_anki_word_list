title="Origin and meaning of department" href="/word/department#etymonline_v_29541"><em>department</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1500, &quot;a ceasing, an ending; a going away, act of leaving&quot; (obsolete in this sense), from Old French <span class="foreign">departement</span> &quot;division, sharing out; divorce, parting&quot; (12c.), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">departire</span> &quot;to divide&quot; (transitive), from <span class="foreign">de-</span> &quot;from&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">de-</span>) + <span class="foreign">partire</span> &quot;to part, divide,&quot; from <span class="foreign">pars</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">partis</span>) &quot;a part, piece, a share, a division&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*pere- </span>(2) &quot;to grant, allot&quot;).</p>
<p>French <span class="foreign">department</span> came also to mean &quot;group of people&quot; (as well as &quot;departure&quot;), and from this by 1735 English had borrowed the sense of &quot;separate division of a complex whole, separate business assigned to someone in a larger organization, distinct branch or group of activities&quot; (science, business, manufacture, the military). The specific meaning &quot;separate division of a government&quot; is from 1769. As an administrative district in France, from 1792.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Department store</span> &quot;store that sells a variety of items, organized by department&quot; is from 1878.</p>
<blockquote>The &quot;Department Store&quot; is the outgrowth of the cheap counter business originated by Butler Brothers in Boston about ten years ago. The little &quot;Five Cent Counter&quot; then became a cornerstone from which the largest of all the world&#x27;s branches of merchandising was to be reared. It was the &quot;Cheap Counter&quot; which proved to the progressive merchant his ability to sell all lines of wares under one roof. It was the Five Cent Counter &quot;epidemic&quot; of &#x27;77 and &#x27;78 which rushed like a mighty whirlwind from the Atlantic to the Pacific and all along its path transformed old time one line storekeepers into the wide-awake merchant princes of the present day. It was this same epidemic which made possible the world famed Department Stores of Houghton, of Boston; Macy, of New York; Wanamaker, of Philadelphia; and Lehman, of Chicago.
