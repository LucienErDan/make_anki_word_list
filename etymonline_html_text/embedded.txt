<p>From late 14c. in English as &quot;book-learning,&quot; also &quot;a particular branch of knowledge or of learning;&quot; also &quot;skillfulness, cleverness; craftiness.&quot; From c. 1400 as &quot;experiential knowledge;&quot; also &quot;a skill, handicraft; a trade.&quot; From late 14c. as &quot;collective human knowledge&quot; (especially that gained by systematic observation, experiment, and reasoning). Modern (restricted) sense of &quot;body of regular or methodical observations or propositions concerning a particular subject or speculation&quot; is attested from 1725; in 17c.-18c. this concept commonly was called <span class="foreign">philosophy</span>. Sense of &quot;non-arts studies&quot; is attested from 1670s. </p>
<blockquote>Science, since people must do it, is a socially embedded activity. It progresses by hunch, vision, and intuition. Much of its change through time does not record a closer approach to absolute truth, but the alteration of cultural contexts that influence it so strongly. Facts are not pure and unsullied bits of information; culture also influences what we see and how we see it. Theories, moreover, are not inexorable inductions from facts. The most creative theories are often imaginative visions imposed upon facts; the source of imagination is also strongly cultural. [Stephen Jay Gould, introduction to &quot;The Mismeasure of Man,&quot; 1981]</blockquote>
<blockquote>In <span class="foreign">science</span> you must not talk before you know. In art you must not talk before you do. In literature you must not talk before you think. [John Ruskin, &quot;The Eagle&#x27;s Nest,&quot; 1872]</blockquote>
<p>The distinction is commonly understood as between theoretical truth (Greek <span class="foreign">epistemē</span>) and methods for effecting practical results (<span class="foreign">tekhnē</span>), but <span class="foreign">science</span> sometimes is used for practical applications and art for applications of skill. To <span class="foreign"><strong>blind (someone) with science</strong></span> &quot;confuse by the use of big words or complex explanations&quot; is attested from 1937, originally noted as a phrase from Australia and New Zealand.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/science#etymonline_v_22918"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of time" href="/word/time#etymonline_v_13330">time (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">tima</span> &quot;limited space of time,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*timon-</span> &quot;time&quot; (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">timi</span> &quot;time, proper time,&quot; Swedish <span class="foreign">timme</span> &quot;an hour&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*di-mon-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="crossreference">*da-</span> &quot;to divide.&quot;</p>
<p>Abstract sense of &quot;time as an indefinite continuous duration&quot; is recorded from late 14c. Personified at least since 1509 as an aged bald man (but with a forelock) carrying a scythe and an hour-glass. In English, a single word encompasses time as &quot;extent&quot; and &quot;point&quot; (French <span class="foreign">temps</span>/<span class="foreign">fois</span>, German <span class="foreign">zeit</span>/<span class="foreign">mal</span>) as well as &quot;hour&quot; (as in &quot;what time is it?&quot; compare French <span class="foreign">heure</span>, German <span class="foreign">Uhr</span>). Extended senses such as &quot;occasion,&quot; &quot;the right time,&quot; &quot;leisure,&quot; or <span class="foreign">times</span> (v.) &quot;multiplied by&quot; developed in Old and Middle English, probably as a natural outgrowth of such phrases as &quot;He commends her a hundred times to God&quot; (Old French <span class="foreign">La comande a Deu cent foiz</span>).</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">to have a good time</span> ( = a time of enjoyment) was common in Eng. from c 1520 to c 1688; it was app. retained in America, whence readopted in Britain in 19th c. [OED]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Time of day</span> (now mainly preserved in negation, i.e. what someone won&#x27;t give you if he doesn&#x27;t like you) was a popular 17c. salutation (as in &quot;Good time of day vnto your Royall Grace,&quot; &quot;Richard III,&quot; I.iii.18), hence <span class="foreign">to give (one) the time of day</span> &quot;greet socially&quot; (1590s); earlier was <span class="foreign">give good day</span> (mid-14c.). <span class="foreign">The times</span> &quot;the current age&quot; is from 1590s. <span class="foreign">Behind the times</span> &quot;old-fashioned&quot; is recorded from 1831. <span class="foreign">Times</span> as the name of a newspaper dates from 1788.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Time warp</span> first attested 1954; <span class="foreign">time-traveling</span> in the science fiction sense first recorded 1895 in H.G. Wells&#x27; &quot;The Time Machine.&quot; <span class="foreign">Time capsule</span> first recorded 1938, in reference to the one &quot;deemed capable of resisting the effects of time for five thousand years preserving an account of universal achievements embedded in the grounds of the New York World&#x27;s fair.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>Jones [archaeologist of A.D. 5139] potters about for a while in the region which we have come to regard as New York, finds countless ruins, but little of interest to the historian except a calcified direction sheet to something called a &quot;Time Capsule.&quot; Jones finds the capsule but cannot open it, and decides, after considerable prying at the lid, that it is merely evidence of an archaic tribal ceremony called a &quot;publicity gag&quot; of which he has already found many examples. [&quot;Princeton Alumni Weekly,&quot; April 14, 1939]</blockquote>
<p>To <span class="foreign">do time</span> &quot;serve a prison sentence&quot; is from 1865. <span class="foreign">Time frame</span> is attested by 1964; <span class="foreign">time-limit</span> is from 1880. <span class="foreign">About time</span>, ironically for &quot;long past due time,&quot; is recorded from 1920.
