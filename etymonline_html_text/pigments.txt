<p>Many of the secondary senses are via the notion of &quot;intervening substance through which a force or quality is conveyed&quot; (1590s) and &quot;intermediate agency, channel of communication&quot; (c. 1600). From the latter comes the sense of &quot;a print publication&quot; (1795) which later grew into the meaning in <span class="crossreference">media</span>.</p>

<p>Figurative of heaviness at least since early 14c. American English slang <span class="foreign"><strong>lead balloon</strong></span> &quot;dismal failure&quot; attested by 1957, perhaps 1940s (as a type of something heavy that can be kept up only with effort, from 1904). <span class="foreign"><strong>Lead-footed</strong></span> &quot;slow&quot; is from 1896; opposite sense of &quot;fast&quot; emerged 1940s in trucker&#x27;s jargon, from notion of a foot heavy on the gas pedal.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;graphite in a pencil&quot; is from 1816 (see <span class="crossreference">pencil</span> (n.)). <span class="foreign"><strong>Black lead</strong></span> was an old name for &quot;graphite,&quot; hence <span class="foreign"><strong>lead pencil</strong></span> (1680s) and the colloquial figurative phrase to have <span class="foreign"><strong>lead in one&#x27;s pencil</strong></span> &quot;be possessed of (especially male sexual) vigor,&quot; attested by 1902. <span class="foreign"><strong>White lead</strong></span> (1560s) was an old name for &quot;tin.&quot;</p>
<p>As a name of a dull bluish-gray color, 1610s. From 1590s as figurative for &quot;bullets.&quot; Lead oxide was much used in glazing, mirror-making, and pigments.
