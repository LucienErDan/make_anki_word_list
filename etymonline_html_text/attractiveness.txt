<p>Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">wlite</span>. One guess [OED] leans toward an unrecorded Old English <span class="foreign">*gyrele</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gurwilon-</span>, diminutive of <span class="foreign">*gurwjoz</span> (apparently also represented by Low German <span class="foreign">gære</span> &quot;boy, girl,&quot; Norwegian dialectal <span class="foreign">gorre</span>, Swedish dialectal <span class="foreign">gurre</span> &quot;small child,&quot; though the exact relationship, if any, between all these is obscure), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ghwrgh-</span>, also found in Greek <span class="foreign">parthenos</span> &quot;virgin.&quot; But this involves some objectionable philology. Liberman (2008) writes:</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Girl</span> does not go back to any Old English or Old Germanic form. It is part of a large group of Germanic words whose root begins with a g or k and ends in r. The final consonant in <span class="foreign">girl</span> is a diminutive suffix. The <span class="foreign">g-r</span> words denote young animals, children, and all kinds of creatures considered immature, worthless, or past their prime.</blockquote>
<p>Another candidate is Old English <span class="foreign">gierela</span> &quot;garment&quot; (for possible sense evolution in this theory, compare <span class="crossreference">brat</span>). A former folk-etymology derivation from Latin <span class="foreign">garrulus</span> &quot;chattering, talkative&quot; is now discarded. Like <span class="crossreference">boy</span>, <span class="crossreference">lass</span>, <span class="crossreference">lad</span> it is of more or less obscure origin. &quot;Probably most of them arose as jocular transferred uses of words that had originally different meaning&quot; [OED]. Specific meaning of &quot;female child&quot; is late 14c. Applied to &quot;any young unmarried woman&quot; since mid-15c. Meaning &quot;sweetheart&quot; is from 1640s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Old girl</strong></span> in reference to a woman of any age is recorded from 1826. <span class="foreign"><strong>Girl next door</strong></span> as a type of unflashy attractiveness is recorded by 1953 (the title of a 20th Century Fox film starring June Haver).</p>
<blockquote>Doris [Day] was a big vocalist even before she hit the movies in 1948. There, as the latest movie colony &quot;girl next door,&quot; sunny-faced Doris soon became a leading movie attraction as well as the world&#x27;s top female recording star. &quot;She&#x27;s the girl next door, all right,&quot; said one Hollywood admirer. &quot;Next door to the bank.&quot; [&quot;Life&quot; magazine, Dec. 22, 1958]</blockquote>
