<p>In U.S. history, Alexander H. The image is older in U.S. political discourse and originally referred to the federal union.</p>

<p>As an adverb by c. 1300, &quot;near, close at hand.&quot; OED (2nd ed. print) has 38 distinct definitions of it as a preposition. Originally an adverbial particle of place, which sense survives in place names (<span class="foreign">Whitby</span>, <span class="foreign">Grimsby</span>, etc., also compare <span class="crossreference">rudesby</span>). Elliptical use for &quot;secondary course&quot; was in Old English (opposed to <span class="foreign">main</span>, as in <span class="crossreference">byway</span>, also compare <span class="foreign">by-blow</span> &quot;illegitimate child,&quot; 1590s, Middle English <span class="foreign">loteby</span> &quot;a concubine,&quot; from obsolete <span class="foreign">lote</span> &quot;to lurk, lie hidden&quot;). This also is the sense of the second <span class="foreign">by</span> in the phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>by the by</strong></span> (1610s). <span class="foreign">By the way</span> literally means &quot;along the way&quot; (c. 1200), hence &quot;in passing by,&quot; used figuratively to introduce a tangential observation (&quot;incidentally&quot;) by 1540s.</p>
