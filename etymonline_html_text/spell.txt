and meaning of spell" href="/word/spell#etymonline_v_23996"><em>spell</em> (v.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 14c., &quot;read letter by letter, write or say the letters of;&quot; c. 1400, &quot;form words by means of letters,&quot; apparently a French word that merged with or displaced a native Old English one; both are from the same Germanic root, but the French word had evolved a different sense. The native word is Old English <span class="foreign">spellian</span> &quot;to tell, speak, discourse, talk,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spellam</span> (source also of Old High German <span class="foreign">spellon</span> &quot;to tell,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">spjalla</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">spillon</span> &quot;to talk, tell&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*spel-</span> (2) &quot;to say aloud, recite.&quot;</p>
<p>But the current senses seem to come from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">espeller</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">espelir</span> &quot;mean, signify, explain, interpret,&quot; also &quot;spell out letters, pronounce, recite,&quot; from Frankish <span class="foreign">*spellon</span> &quot;to tell&quot; or some other Germanic source, ultimately identical with the native word.</p>
<p>Related: <span class="foreign">Spelled</span>; <span class="crossreference">spelling</span>. In early Middle English still &quot;to speak, preach, talk, tell,&quot; hence such expressions as <span class="foreign">hear spell</span> &quot;hear (something) told or talked about,&quot; <span class="foreign">spell the wind</span> &quot;talk in vain&quot; (both 15c.). Meaning &quot;form words with proper letters&quot; is from 1580s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Spell out</strong></span> &quot;explain step-by-step&quot; is first recorded 1940, American English. Shakespeare has <span class="foreign">spell (someone) backwards</span> &quot;reverse the character of, explain in a contrary sense, portray with determined negativity.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/spell#etymonline_v_23996"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of spell" href="/word/spell#etymonline_v_23997"><em>spell</em> (n.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">spell</span> &quot;story, saying, tale, history, narrative, fable; discourse, command,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spellam</span> (see <span class="crossreference">spell</span> (v.1)).
