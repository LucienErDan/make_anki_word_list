title="Origin and meaning of other" href="/word/other#etymonline_v_9948"><em>other</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">oþer</span> &quot;the second&quot; (adj.), also as a pronoun, &quot;one of the two, other,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*anthera-</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">athar</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">other</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">annarr</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">ander</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">andar</span>, German <span class="foreign">ander</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">anþar</span> &quot;second, other&quot;).</p>
<p>These are from PIE <span class="foreign">*an-tero- </span>(source of Lithuanian <span class="foreign">antras</span>, Old Prussian <span class="foreign">anters</span> &quot;other, second), which is perhaps a variant of <span class="foreign">*al-tero-</span> &quot;the other of two&quot; (source of Latin <span class="foreign">alter</span>), from root <span class="crossreference">*al-</span> &quot;beyond&quot; + adjectival comparative suffix <span class="foreign">*-tero-</span>.  Or the first element might be the pronoun <span class="foreign">*eno-</span>, <span class="foreign">*ono-</span> [Boutkan]. The Old English, Old Saxon, and Old Frisian forms show &quot;a normal loss of n before fricatives&quot; [Barnhart].</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;different&quot; is mid-13c. Sense of &quot;second&quot; was detached from this word in English (which uses <span class="crossreference">second</span>, from Latin) and German (<span class="foreign">zweiter</span>, from <span class="foreign">zwei</span> &quot;two&quot;) to avoid ambiguity. In Scandinavian, however, the second floor is still the &quot;other&quot; floor (Swedish <span class="foreign">andra</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">anden</span>). Also compare Old English <span class="foreign">oþergeara</span> &quot;next year.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign">The other woman</span> &quot;a woman with whom a man begins a love affair while he is already committed&quot; is from 1855. <span class="foreign">The other day</span> originally (mid-12c.) was &quot;the next day;&quot; later (c. 1300) &quot;yesterday;&quot; and now, loosely, &quot;a day or two ago&quot; (early 15c.). Phrase <span class="foreign">other half</span> in reference to either the poor or the rich, is recorded from c. 1600.</p>
