and meaning of crack" href="/word/crack#etymonline_v_284"><em>crack</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">cracian</span> &quot;make a sharp noise, give forth a loud, abrupt sound,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*krakojan</span> (source also of Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">craken</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kraken</span>, German <span class="foreign">krachen</span>); the whole group is probably ultimately imitative. Related: <span class="crossreference">Cracked</span>; <span class="crossreference">cracking</span>.</p>
<p>From c. 1300 as &quot;to burst, split open&quot; (intransitive), also transitive, &quot;to cause to break into chinks.&quot; From 1785 as &quot;break or crush into small pieces.&quot; Of the voice, &quot;change tone suddenly,&quot; as that of a youth passing into manhood, c. 1600. Meaning &quot;to open and drink&quot; (a bottle) is from 16c.</p>
<p>From early 14c. as &quot;to utter, say, speak, talk freely,&quot; especially &quot;speak loudly or boastingly&quot; (late 14c.). To <span class="foreign">crack a smile</span> is from 1835, American English; to <span class="foreign">crack a joke</span> is by 1732, probably from the &quot;speak, say&quot; sense. To <span class="foreign">crack the whip</span> in the figurative sense is from 1886. <span class="foreign">Get cracking</span> &quot;go to work, start doing what is to be done&quot; is by 1937.</p>
<blockquote>What is <span class="foreign">a crack</span> in English? A <span class="foreign">chat!</span> The synonym is as perfect as possible; yet the words are subtly distinguished by a whole hemisphere of feeling. A chat, by comparison &quot;wi&#x27; a <span class="foreign">crack</span>,&quot; is a poor, frivolous, shallow, altogether heartless business. A <span class="foreign">crack</span> is, indeed, only adequately to be defined as a <span class="foreign">chat</span> with a good, kindly, human heart in it .... [P.P. Meaning &quot;sharp, resounding blow&quot; is from 1836. Meaning &quot;rock cocaine&quot; is first attested 1985. The superstition that it is bad luck to step on sidewalk cracks has been traced to c. 1890. Meaning &quot;try, attempt&quot; first attested 1830, nautical, probably a hunting metaphor, from slang sense of &quot;fire a gun.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>At their head, apart from the rest, was a black bull, who appeared to be their leader; he came roaring along, his tail straight an [sic] end, and at times tossing up the earth with his horns. I never felt such a desire to have a crack at any thing in all my life. He drew nigh the place where I was standing; I raised my beautiful Betsey to my shoulder, took deliberate aim, blazed away, and he roared, and suddenly stopped. [&quot;A Narrative of the Life of David Crockett, Written by Himself,&quot; Philadelphia, 1834]</blockquote>
<p>Adjectival meaning &quot;top-notch, superior, excellent, first rate&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">a crack shot</span>) is slang from 1793, perhaps from earlier verbal sense of &quot;do any thing with quickness or smartness&quot; [Johnson], or from the verb in the sense of &quot;speak boastingly&quot; and suggesting &quot;having qualities to be proud of&quot; [Century Dictionary].
