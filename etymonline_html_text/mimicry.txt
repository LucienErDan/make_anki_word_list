from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*apan</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">apo</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">api</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">aap</span>, German <span class="foreign">affe</span>), probably a borrowed word, perhaps from Celtic (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">apa</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">epa</span>) or Slavic (compare Old Bohemian <span class="foreign">op</span>, Slovak <span class="foreign">opitza</span>), and the whole group probably is ultimately from an Eastern or non-Indo-European language.</p>

<p>The common word until the emergence of <span class="crossreference">monkey</span> in 16c. More technically, in zoology, &quot;a simian; tail-less, man-like monkey&quot; 1690s. The only native apes in Europe are the Barbary apes of Gibraltar, intelligent and docile, and these were the showman&#x27;s apes of the Middle Ages. Apes were noted in medieval times for mimicry of human action, hence, perhaps, the other figurative use of the word, to mean &quot;a fool&quot; (c. 1300).</p>

The first element is <span class="crossreference">Mag</span>, nickname for <span class="foreign">Margaret</span>, long used in proverbial and slang English for qualities associated generally with women, especially in this case &quot;idle chattering&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">Magge tales</span> &quot;tall tales, nonsense,&quot; early 15c.; also compare French <span class="foreign">margot</span> &quot;magpie,&quot; from <span class="foreign">Margot</span>, pet form of <span class="foreign">Marguerite</span>). The name <span class="foreign">Margaret</span>, and its reduced forms <span class="foreign">Mag</span>, <span class="foreign">Madge</span>, diminutive <span class="foreign">Maggie</span>, also has long been familiarly applied to birds.</p>
<p>The second element, <span class="foreign">pie</span>, is the earlier name of the bird, from Old French <span class="foreign">pie</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">pica</span> &quot;magpie,&quot; fem. of <span class="foreign">picus</span> &quot;woodpecker,&quot; from PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)peik-</span> &quot;woodpecker, magpie&quot; (source also of Umbrian <span class="foreign">peica</span> &quot;magpie,&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pikah</span> &quot;Indian cuckoo,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">spætr</span>, German <span class="foreign">Specht</span> &quot;woodpecker&quot;); possibly from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pi-</span>, denoting pointedness, of the beak, perhaps, but the magpie also has a long, pointed tail.</p>
<p>The birds are proverbial for pilfering and hoarding, can be taught to speak, and have been regarded since the Middle Ages as ill omens.</p>
<blockquote>Whan pyes chatter vpon a house it is a sygne of ryghte euyll tydynges. [1507]</blockquote>
<p>Divination by numbering magpies is attested from c. 1780 in Lincolnshire; the rhyme varies from place to place, the only consistency being that one is bad, two are good.</p>

<blockquote>The councils which magpies appear to hold together, at particular seasons, commonly called &quot;folkmotes,&quot; are associated in the minds of many with superstitious and ominous notions. The innocent objects of terror, while meeting together most probably for the purpose of choosing mates, are supposed to be conspiring and clubbing their wits, for the weal or woe of the inhabitants of the neighbouring village. If they are of an even number and carry on their cheerful, noisy chatter, it is supposed to betoken good to old and young--but if there is an odd magpie perched apart from the rest, silent, and disconsolate, the reverse of this is apprehended, and mischievous consequences are inevitably expected.
