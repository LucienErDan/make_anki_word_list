&quot;to give being to, give form or character to, bring into existence; construct, do, be the author of, produce; prepare, arrange, cause; behave, fare, transform,&quot; from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*makōjanan</span> &quot;to fashion, fit&quot; (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">makon</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">makia</span> &quot;to build, make,&quot; Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">maken</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">mahhon</span> &quot;to construct, make,&quot; German <span class="foreign">machen</span> &quot;to make&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*mag-</span> &quot;to knead, fashion, fit.&quot; If so, sense evolution perhaps is via prehistoric houses built of mud. It gradually replaced the main Old English word, <span class="foreign">gewyrcan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">work</span> (v.)).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to arrive at&quot; (a place), first attested 1620s, originally was nautical. Formerly used in many places where specific verbs now are used, such as <span class="foreign">to make Latin</span> (c. 1500) &quot;to write Latin compositions.&quot; This broader usage survives in some phrases, such as <span class="foreign"><strong>make water</strong></span> &quot;to urinate&quot; (c. 1400), <span class="foreign"><strong>make a book</strong></span> &quot;arrange a series of bets&quot; (1828), <span class="foreign"><strong>make hay</strong></span> &quot;to turn over mown grass to expose it to sun.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>Make the grade</strong></span> is 1912, perhaps from the notion of railway engines going up an incline.</p>
<blockquote>Read the valuable suggestions in Dr. C.V. Mosby&#x27;s book -- be prepared to surmount obstacles before you encounter them -- equipped with the power to &quot;make the grade&quot; in life&#x27;s climb. [advertisement for &quot;Making the Grade,&quot; December 1916]</blockquote>
<p>But the phrase also was in use in a schoolwork context at the time.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>make friends</strong></span> is from late 14c.; to <span class="foreign"><strong>make good</strong></span> &quot;make right&quot; is from early 15c.  To <span class="foreign"><strong>make do</strong></span> &quot;manage with what is available&quot; is attested by 1867; to <span class="foreign"><strong>make for</strong></span> &quot;direct one&#x27;s course to, proceed toward&quot; is from 1580s, but &quot;Not frequent before the 19th c.&quot; [OED]. To <span class="foreign"><strong>make off</strong></span> &quot;run away, depart suddenly&quot; is from 1709; to <span class="foreign"><strong>make off with</strong></span> &quot;run away with (something) in one&#x27;s possession&quot; is by 1820. To <span class="foreign"><strong>make way</strong></span> is from c. 1200 as &quot;cut a path,&quot; early 14c. as &quot;proceed, go.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Make time</strong></span> &quot;go fast&quot; is 1849; <span class="foreign"><strong>make tracks</strong></span> in this sense is from 1834.
