<p>First used in the literary sense in Latin in reference to a collection of poems in various meters on a variety of subjects by the late republican Roman poet Ennius. The matter of the little that survives of his verse does not seem to be particularly satiric, but in classical Latin the word came to mean especially a poem which assailed the prevailing vices, one after another. Altered in Latin by influence of Greek <span class="foreign">satyr</span>, on mistaken notion that the literary form is related to the Greek <span class="foreign">satyr</span> drama (see <span class="crossreference">satyr</span>). For nuances of usage, see <span class="crossreference">humor</span> (n.).</p>
<blockquote><strong>Satire</strong>, <span class="foreign">n</span>. <p>Also found in Old Norse as <span class="foreign">silki</span> but not elsewhere in Germanic. <p>According to some sources [Buck, OED], the use of <span class="foreign">-l-</span> instead of <span class="foreign">-r-</span> in the Balto-Slavic form of the word (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">šelku</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šilkai</span>) passed into English via the Baltic trade and may reflect a Chinese dialectal form, or a Slavic alteration of the Greek word.
