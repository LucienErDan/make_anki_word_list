from Middle French <span class="foreign">patriote</span> (15c.) and directly from Late Latin <span class="foreign">patriota</span> &quot;fellow-countryman&quot; (6c.), from Greek <span class="foreign">patriotes</span> &quot;fellow countryman,&quot; from <span class="foreign">patrios</span> &quot;of one&#x27;s fathers,&quot; <span class="foreign">patris</span> &quot;fatherland,&quot; from <span class="foreign">pater</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">patros</span>) &quot;father&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">father</span> (n.)); with <span class="foreign">-otes</span>, suffix expressing state or condition. Liddell &amp; Scott write that <span class="foreign">patriotes</span> was &quot;applied to barbarians who had only a common [patris], [politai] being used of Greeks who had a common [polis] (or <span class="foreign">free-state</span>).&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;loyal and disinterested supporter of one&#x27;s country&quot; is attested from c. 1600, but became an ironic term of ridicule or abuse from mid-18c. in England, so that Johnson, who at first defined it as &quot;one whose ruling passion is the love of his country,&quot; in his fourth edition added, &quot;It is sometimes used for a factious disturber of the government.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The name of patriot had become [c. 1744] a by-word of derision. Horace Walpole scarcely exaggerated when he said that ... the most popular declaration which a candidate could make on the hustings was that he had never been and never would be a patriot. [Macaulay, &quot;Horace Walpole,&quot; 1833]</blockquote>
<p>Somewhat revived in reference to resistance movements in overrun countries in World War II, it has usually had a positive sense in American English, where the phony and rascally variety has been consigned to the word <span class="foreign">patrioteer</span> (1928). Oriana Fallaci [&quot;The Rage and the Pride,&quot; 2002] marvels that Americans, so fond of <span class="crossreference">patriotic</span>, <span class="foreign">patriot</span>, and <span class="crossreference">patriotism</span>, lack the root noun and are content to express the idea of <span class="foreign">patria</span> by cumbersome compounds such as <span class="foreign">homeland</span>. (Joyce, Shaw, and H.G. Wells all used <span class="foreign">patria</span> as an English word early 20c., but it failed to stick.) <span class="foreign">Patriots&#x27; Day</span> (April 19, anniversary of the 1775 skirmishes at Lexington and Concord Bridge) was observed as a legal holiday in Maine and Massachusetts from 1894.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/patriot#etymonline_v_10166"><span>Relate entries &amp;
