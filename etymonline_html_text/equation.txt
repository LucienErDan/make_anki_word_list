A similar contraction of <span class="foreign">-ish</span> is in <span class="crossreference">Dutch</span>, <span class="crossreference">Scotch</span>, <span class="crossreference">Welsh</span>, suggesting the habit applies to the names of only the intimate neighbors.</p>
<p>In some provincial forms of English it could mean simply &quot;foreign.&quot; Used in many combination-words, often dealing with food or sex: <span class="foreign"><strong>French dressing</strong></span> (by 1860); <span class="foreign"><strong>French toast</strong></span> (1630s); <span class="foreign"><strong>French letter</strong></span> &quot;condom&quot; (c. 1856, perhaps on resemblance of sheepskin and parchment), <span class="crossreference">french</span> (v.) &quot;perform oral sex on,&quot; and <span class="foreign"><strong>French kiss</strong></span> (1923) all probably stem from the Anglo-Saxon equation of Gallic culture and sexual sophistication, a sense first recorded 1749 in the phrase <span class="foreign">French novel</span>. (In late 19c.-early 20c., a <span class="foreign">French kiss</span> was a kiss on each cheek.) <span class="foreign"><strong>French-Canadian</strong></span> is from 1774; <span class="foreign"><strong>French doors</strong></span> is by 1847. To take <span class="foreign"><strong>French leave</strong></span>, &quot;depart without telling the host,&quot; is 1771, from a social custom then prevalent. <blockquote>This manne can litle skyl ... to saue himself harmlesse from the perilous accidentes of this world, keping ye wulf from the doore (as they cal it). [&quot;The Institution of a Gentleman,&quot; 1555]</blockquote>
<p>Probably extinct in England from the end of the 15th century; in Scotland from the early 18th. Wolves as a symbol of lust are ancient, such as Roman slang <span class="foreign">lupa</span> &quot;whore,&quot; literally &quot;she-wolf&quot; (preserved in Spanish <span class="foreign">loba</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">lupa</span>, French <span class="foreign">louve</span>). The equation of &quot;wolf&quot; and &quot;prostitute, sexually voracious female&quot; persisted into 12c., but by Elizabethan times wolves had become primarily symbolic of male lust. The specific use of <span class="foreign">wolf</span> for &quot;sexually aggressive male&quot; first recorded 1847; <span class="foreign">wolf-whistle</span> attested by 1945, American English, at first associated with sailors. The image of a wolf in sheep&#x27;s skin is attested from c. 1400. See <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/wolf.php">here</a> for a discussion of &quot;wolf&quot; in Indo-European history.
