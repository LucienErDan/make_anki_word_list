title="Origin and meaning of crayon" href="/word/crayon#etymonline_v_315"><em>crayon</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;pencil-shaped piece of colored clay, chalk, etc., used for drawing on paper,&quot; 1640s, from French <span class="foreign">crayon</span> &quot;pencil&quot; (16c.), originally &quot;chalk pencil,&quot; from <span class="foreign">craie</span> &quot;chalk,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">creta</span> &quot;chalk, pipe-clay,&quot; which is of unknown origin. Not now considered to mean &quot;Cretan earth,&quot; as once was believed. It also formerly was regarded as possibly from <span class="foreign">(terra) creta</span> &quot;sifted (earth),&quot; but de Vaan finds this &quot;semantically uncompelling, since &#x27;clay&#x27; is not &#x27;sifted earth&#x27;, but at most &#x27;fine sand&#x27;,&quot; and he finds <span class="foreign">creta</span> more likely to be a cognate with, or a borrowing from, Celtic words for &quot;mud, clay&quot; (Old Irish <span class="foreign">cre</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">pridd</span>, Breton <span class="foreign">pri</span>).</p>

This is the neuter of the past-participle adjective <span class="foreign">enkaustos</span> &quot;burned in,&quot; from the stem of <span class="foreign">enkaiein</span> &quot;to burn in,&quot; from <span class="foreign">en-</span> &quot;in&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">en-</span> (1)) + <span class="foreign">kaiein</span> &quot;to burn&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">caustic</span>).</p>
<p>In Pliny the word is the name of a kind of painting executed by fire or heat. Later it was the name of the purple-red ink, the <span class="foreign">sacrum encaustum</span>, used by the Roman emperors to sign their documents; this was said to have been obtained from the ground remains of certain shellfish, formed into writing fluid by the application of fire or heat, which explained the name. In the Code of Justinian, the making of it for common uses, or by common persons, was prohibited under penalty of death and confiscation of goods.</p>
<blockquote>It denoted a kind of painting practised by the ancients, in which the crayon was dipped in wax of various colours. <span class="foreign">Encausto pingere</span> is to practise this art, paint in encaustic or enamel. <span class="foreign">Encaustum</span> afterwards came to signify an ink for the purpose of writing; and the &quot;sacred <span class="foreign">encaustum</span>&quot; of Justinian&#x27;s Code was an ink which the Roman Emperors used for imperial subscriptions. It was of the imperial colour, reddish purple, and was made of the purple dye, prepared in some way by the application of fire. (So that in this use of the word, the notion of burning which there is in the etymology, is still retained.) [from footnote in &quot;The Life, Letters, and Sermons of Bishop Herbert de Losinga,&quot; Oxford, 1878]</blockquote>
<p>The usual words for &quot;ink&quot; in Latin was <span class="foreign">atramentum</span> (source of Old French <span class="foreign">arrement</span>), literally &quot;anything that serves to dye black,&quot; from <span class="foreign">ater</span> &quot;black;&quot; the Greek word was <span class="foreign">melan</span>, neuter of <span class="foreign">melas</span> &quot;black.&quot; The Old English word for it was <span class="foreign">blæc</span>, literally &quot;black,&quot; and compare Swedish <span class="foreign">bläck</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">blæk</span> &quot;ink.&quot; Spanish and Portuguese (<span class="foreign">tinta</span>) and German (<span class="foreign">tinte</span>) get their &quot;ink&quot; words from Latin <span class="foreign">tinctus</span> &quot;a dyeing.&quot;</p>
<p>Donkin credits a Greek pronunciation, with the accent at the front of the word, for the French evolution; the same Latin word, behaving regularly, became <span class="foreign">inchiostro</span> (with unetymological <span class="foreign">-r-</span>) in Italian, <span class="foreign">encausto</span> in Spanish. As an adjective, <span class="foreign">inken</span> (c. 1600) occasionally has been used. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ink-slinger</strong></span>, contemptuous for &quot;journalist,&quot; is from 1870.
