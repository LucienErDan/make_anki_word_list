and meaning of man" href="/word/man#etymonline_v_6766"><em>man</em> (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;a featherless plantigrade biped mammal of the genus <span class="foreign">Homo</span>&quot; [Century Dictionary], Old English <span class="foreign">man</span>, <span class="foreign">mann</span> &quot;human being, person (male or female); brave man, hero;&quot; also &quot;servant, vassal, adult male considered as under the control of another person,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*mann-</span> (source also of Old Saxon, Swedish, Dutch, Old High German <span class="foreign">man</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mon</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mann</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">maðr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">mand</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">manna</span> &quot;man&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*man-</span> (1) &quot;man.&quot; For the plural, see <span class="crossreference">men</span>.</p>
<p>Sometimes connected to root <span class="crossreference">*men-</span> (1) &quot;to think,&quot; which would make the ground sense of <span class="foreign">man</span> &quot;one who has intelligence,&quot; but not all linguists accept this. Liberman, for instance, writes, &quot;Most probably <span class="foreign">man</span> &#x27;human being&#x27; is a secularized divine name&quot; from <span class="foreign">Mannus</span> [Tacitus, <span class="foreign">&quot;Germania,&quot;</span> chap. 2], &quot;believed to be the progenitor of the human race.&quot;</p>
<p>Specific sense of &quot;adult male of the human race&quot; (distinguished from a woman or boy) is by late Old English (c. 1000); Old English used <span class="foreign">wer</span> and <span class="foreign">wif</span> to distinguish the sexes, but <span class="foreign">wer</span> began to disappear late 13c. and was replaced by <span class="foreign">man</span>. Universal sense of the word remains in <span class="crossreference">mankind</span> and <span class="crossreference">manslaughter</span>. Similarly, Latin had <span class="foreign">homo</span> &quot;human being&quot; and <span class="foreign">vir</span> &quot;adult male human being,&quot; but they merged in Vulgar Latin, with <span class="foreign">homo</span> extended to both senses. A like evolution took place in Slavic languages, and in some of them the word has narrowed to mean &quot;husband.&quot; PIE had two other &quot;man&quot; roots: <span class="foreign">*uiHro</span> &quot;freeman&quot; (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vira-</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vyras</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">vir</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">fer</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">wair</span>; see <span class="crossreference">*wi-ro-</span>) and <span class="foreign">*hner</span> &quot;man,&quot; a title more of honor than <span class="foreign">*uiHro</span> (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">nar-</span>, Armenian <span class="foreign">ayr</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">ner</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">anēr</span>; see <span class="crossreference">*ner-</span> (2)).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Man</span> also was in Old English as an indefinite pronoun, &quot;one, people, they.&quot; It was used generically for &quot;the human race, mankind&quot; by c. 1200. As a word of familiar address, originally often implying impatience, c.1400; hence probably its use as an interjection of surprise or emphasis, since Middle English but especially popular from early 20c.</p>
<p>As &quot;a woman&#x27;s lover,&quot; by mid-14c. As &quot;adult male possessing manly qualities in an eminent degree,&quot; from 14c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Man&#x27;s man</strong></span><span class="foreign">,</span> one whose qualities are appreciated by other men, is by 1873. Colloquial use of <span class="foreign"><strong>the Man</strong></span> for &quot;the boss&quot; is by 1918. To be <span class="foreign"><strong>man or mouse</strong></span> &quot;be brave or be timid&quot; is from 1540s. Meaning &quot;piece with which a game (especially chess) is played&quot; is from c. 1400.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Man-about-town</strong></span> &quot;man of the leisure class who frequents clubs, theaters, and other social resorts&quot; is from 1734. <span class="foreign"><strong>Man of the world</strong></span> is from mid-14c. as &quot;secular man, layman;&quot; by early 15c. as &quot;man experienced in the ways of the world, one able to take things in stride.&quot; To do something <span class="foreign"><strong>as one man</strong></span> &quot;unanimously&quot; is from late 14c.</p>
