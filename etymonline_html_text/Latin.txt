title="Origin and meaning of Latin" href="/word/Latin#etymonline_v_2102"><em>Latin</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">latin</span> &quot;in Latin,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">Latinus</span> &quot;Latin, Roman, in Latin,&quot; literally &quot;belonging to <span class="foreign">Latium</span>,&quot; the region of Italy around Rome, a name of uncertain origin. Possibly from PIE root <span class="foreign">*stela-</span> &quot;to spread, extend,&quot; with a sense of &quot;flat country&quot; (as opposed to the mountainous district of the Sabines), or from a prehistoric non-IE language. Old folk etymology connected it with Latin <span class="foreign">latere</span> &quot;to lie hidden,&quot; and a fable of Saturn.</p>
<p>The Latin word also is the source of Spanish and Italian <span class="foreign">ladino</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">latijn</span>, German <span class="foreign">latein</span>, Irish Gaelic <span class="foreign">laidionn</span> (n.), Polish <span class="foreign">lacina</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">latuinŭ</span>. The more common form in Old English was <span class="foreign">læden</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Latin</span> (n.)).</p>
<p>In reference to the Roman Catholic Church, 1550s. Used as a designation for &quot;people whose languages descend from Latin&quot; (1856), hence <span class="foreign"><strong>Latin America</strong></span> (1862). The <span class="foreign"><strong>Latin Quarter</strong></span> (French <span class="foreign">Quartier latin</span>) of Paris, on the south (left) bank of the Seine, was the site of university buildings in the Middle Ages, hence it was the place where Latin was spoken. The surname <span class="crossreference">Latimer</span> means &quot;interpreter,&quot; literally &quot;a speaker of Latin.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Latin#etymonline_v_2102"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Latin" href="/word/Latin#etymonline_v_43157"><em>Latin</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;the language of the (ancient) Romans,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">latin</span> &quot;Latin, the language of the Romans; any foreign language,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">latinium</span> &quot;the Latin language,&quot; noun use of the adjective <span class="foreign">latinius</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Latin</span> (adj.)). The more common form in Old English was <span class="foreign">læden</span>, from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*ladinum</span>, which probably was deformed by influence of Old English <span class="foreign">leoden</span> &quot;language.&quot; For &quot;the Latin language&quot; Old English also had <span class="foreign">lædenspræc</span>.
<br/><br/>
In Old French the word was used very broadly, &quot;speech, language:&quot; &quot;What Latin was to the learned, that their tongue was to laymen; hence <span class="foreign">latino</span> was used for any dialect, even Arabic and the language of birds ....&quot; [Donkin, &quot;Etymological Dictionary of the Romance Languages,&quot; 1864].
<br/><br/>
Roughly speaking, <span class="foreign">Old Latin</span> is the Latin before the classical period including early authors and inscriptions. <span class="foreign">Classical Latin</span> flourished from about 75 B.C.E. to about 200 C.E., the Latin of Lucretius, Catullus, Caesar, Cicero, Virgil, Horace, Ovid, Livy, Seneca, etc.; it is the standard Latin of the grammars and dictionaries. <span class="foreign">Late Latin</span> followed the classical period to about 600 and includes the early church fathers. <span class="foreign">Medieval Latin</span> was the Latin of the Middle Ages, from about 600 to 1500. <span class="foreign">Modern Latin</span> is Latin as written from about 1500 on, largely by scientific writers in description and classification. <span class="foreign">Vulgar Latin</span> was the speech of the Roman home and marketplace, going on concurrently under Classical and Late Latin.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Latin#etymonline_v_43157"><span>Relate entries &amp; <blockquote>The animals play quite an important part in the naming [of children&#x27;s languages], as the hog, dog, fly, goose, pigeon, pig, all give names, with Mr. Hog leading. Among the names the Latins take the lead, and Hog Latin leads the list, being accredited as naming nearly as many languages as all the other names combined. Besides Hog Latin, there is Dog Latin, Pig Latin, Goose Latin, and Bum Latin. Then there is Greekish and Peddlers&#x27; French and Pigeon English. ... Very few can give any reason for the naming of the languages. In fact, no one can fully say where the great majority of names came from, for in most cases in the naming the following pretty well expresses the difficulty: &quot;It was born before I was. I can&#x27;t tell how young I was when I first heard of it.&quot; [&quot;The Secret Language of Children,&quot; in &quot;The North Western Monthly,&quot; October 1897]</blockquote>
