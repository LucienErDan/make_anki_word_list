class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;put an end to, do away with,&quot; mid-15c., from Old French <span class="foreign">aboliss-</span>, present participle stem of <span class="foreign">abolir</span> &quot;to abolish&quot; (15c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">abolere</span> &quot;destroy, efface, annihilate; cause to die out, retard the growth of,&quot; which is <p>But the Latin word rather could be from a root in common with Greek <span class="foreign">ollymi</span>, <span class="foreign">apollymi</span> &quot;destroy.&quot; Tucker writes that there has been a confusion of forms in Latin, based on similar roots, one meaning &quot;to grow,&quot; the other &quot;to destroy.&quot; Now generally used of institutions, customs, etc.; application to persons and concrete objects has long been obsolete. Related: <span class="foreign">Abolished</span>; <span class="foreign">abolishing</span>.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Abolish</span> is a strong word, and signifies a complete removal, generally but not always by a summary act.
