                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/band">band</a> 带子，乐队</span><p>来自PIE *bendh, 绑。同bend, bind, bond. 乐队义来自于旧时士兵绑一条带子在头上作为某种象征，而最早的乐队成员也有类似的动作。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/band">band</a></dt><dd><b>band: </b><span>There are two distinct words <i>band </i>in English, but neither of them goes back as far as Old English. The one meaning ‘group of people’ [15] comes from Old French <i>bande</i>, but is probably Germanic in ultimate origin; the specific sense ‘group of musicians’ developed in the 17th century. <i>Band </i>‘strip’ [13] comes from Germanic *<i>bindan</i>, source of English <i>bind</i>, but reached English in two quite separate phases.<br><br>It first came via Old Norse <i>band</i>, in the sense ‘something that ties or constrains’; this replaced Old English <i>bend</i>, also from Germanic *<i>bindan </i>(which now survives only as a heraldic term, as in <i>bend sinister</i>), but is now itself more or less obsolete, having been superseded by <i>bond</i>, a variant form. But then in the 15th century it arrived again, by a different route: Old French had <i>bande </i>‘strip, stripe’, which can be traced back, perhaps via a Vulgar Latin *<i>binda</i>, to the same ultimate source, Germanic *<i>bindan</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bend" target="_blank">bend</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bind" target="_blank">bind</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bond" target="_blank">bond</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bundle" target="_blank">bundle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ribbon" target="_blank">ribbon</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/band">band (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"a flat strip," also "something that binds," a merger of two words, ultimately from the same source. In the sense "that by which someone or something is bound," it is attested from early 12c., from Old Norse <span class="foreign">band</span> "thin strip that ties or constrains," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bindan</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*bendh-</span> "to bind" (cognates: Gothic <span class="foreign">bandi</span> "that which binds; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">bandhah</span> "a tying, bandage," source of <span class="foreign">bandana</span>; Middle Irish <span class="foreign">bainna</span> "bracelet;" see <a href="/etym/s/bend" target="_blank">bend</a> (v.), <a href="/etym/s/bind" target="_blank">bind</a> (v.)). Most of the figurative senses of this word have passed into <a href="/etym/s/bond" target="_blank">bond</a> (n.), which originally was a phonetic variant of this <span class="foreign">band</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The meaning "a flat strip" (late 14c.) is from Old French <span class="foreign">bande</span> "strip, edge, side," via Old North French <span class="foreign">bende</span>, from Old High German <span class="foreign">binda</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bindan</span> (see above). In Middle English, this was distinguished by the spelling <span class="foreign">bande</span>, but since the loss of the final <span class="foreign">-e</span> the words have fully merged. Meaning "broad stripe of color" is from late 15c.; the electronics sense of "range of frequencies or wavelengths" is from 1922. The Old North French form was retained in heraldic <a href="/etym/s/bend" target="_blank">bend</a>. <span class="foreign">Band saw</span> is recorded from 1864.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/band">band (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"an organized group," late 15c., from Middle French <span class="foreign">bande</span>, which is traceable to the Proto-Germanic root of <a href="/etym/s/band" target="_blank">band</a> (n.1), probably via a band of cloth worn as a mark of identification by a group of soldiers or others (compare Gothic <span class="foreign">bandwa</span> "a sign"). The extension to "group of musicians" is c. 1660, originally musicians attached to a regiment of the army. To <span class="foreign">beat the band</span> (1897) is to make enough noise to drown it out, hence to exceed everything.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/band">band (v.)</a></dt><dd>1520s, "to bind or fasten;" also "to join in a company," from <a href="/etym/s/band" target="_blank">band</a> (n.1) and (n.2) in various noun senses, and partly from French <span class="foreign">bander</span>. The meaning "to affix an ID band to (a wild animal, etc.)" is attested from 1914. Related: <span class="foreign">Banded</span>; <span class="foreign">banding</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
