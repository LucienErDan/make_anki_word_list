                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. prune <=====> plum (同源词，Change of pr- to pl- is peculiar to Germanic.)<br />
2. prune <=====> preen.<br />
3. 谐音“破芦嗯、破芦呢”-----剪破、修剪芦苇。<br />
4. perhaps from pro- + round => *pround => *prund => prun- + -e => prune "cut in a rounded shape in front".</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/prune">prune</a> 梅子干，李子干</span><p>来自拉丁语pruna,李子，来自希腊语proumnon,李子。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/prune">prune</a> 修剪树枝，裁剪，精减</span><p>来自古法语proignier,剪枝，给鸟修剪羽毛，来自pro-,向前，rotundus,圆的，词源同round,rotate. 即使圆润，剪掉枝桠，引申词义裁剪，精减等。脱落字母t,习惯性插入字母g.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/prune">prune</a></dt><dd><b>prune: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>prune</i>. The older, ‘dried plum’ [14], is ultimately the same word as <i>plum</i>, and indeed in the 16th and 17th century was often used for ‘plum’. It comes via Old French <i>prune </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>prūna</i>, which also gave English (through Germanic) <i>plum</i>. <i>Prune </i>‘cut off unneeded parts’ [15] denotes etymologically ‘cut in a rounded shape in front’. It comes via Old French <i>proignier </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>prōrotundiāre</i>, a compound verb formed from the Latin prefix <i>prō</i>- ‘in front’ and <i>rotundus </i>‘round’ (source of English <i>rotund </i>and <i>round</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/plum" target="_blank">plum</a>; <a href="/etym/s/rotund" target="_blank">rotund</a>, <a href="/etym/s/round" target="_blank">round</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/prune">prune (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "a plum," also "a dried plum" (c. 1200 in place name <span class="foreign">Prunhill</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">pronne</span> "plum" (13c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*pruna</span>, fem. singular formed from Latin <span class="foreign">pruna</span>, neuter plural of <span class="foreign">prunum</span> "a plum," by dissimilation from Greek <span class="foreign">proumnon</span>, from a language of Asia Minor. Slang meaning "disagreeable or disliked person" is from 1895. <span class="foreign">Prune juice</span> is from 1807.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/prune">prune (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 15c., <span class="foreign">prouyne</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">proignier</span> "cut back (vines), prune" (Modern French <span class="foreign">provigner</span>), of unknown origin. Perhaps [Watkins] from Gallo-Roman <span class="foreign">*pro-retundiare</span> "cut in a rounded shape in front," from <span class="foreign">pro-</span> "forth" (see <a href="/etym/s/pro-" target="_blank">pro-</a>) + <span class="foreign">*retundiare</span> "round off," from Latin <span class="foreign">rotundus</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/round" target="_blank">round</a> (adj.)). Klein suggests the Old French word is from <span class="foreign">provain</span> "layer of a vine," from Latin <span class="foreign">propago</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/prop" target="_blank">prop</a> (n.1)). &#13; <br /><br />&#13; Or the Middle English word might be identical with the falconry term <span class="foreign">proinen</span>, <span class="foreign">proynen</span> "trim the feather with the beak" (late 14c.), source of <a href="/etym/s/preen" target="_blank">preen</a> [Barnhart]. Related: <span class="foreign">Pruned</span>; <span class="foreign">pruning</span>. <span class="foreign">Pruning hook</span> is from 1610s; <span class="foreign">pruning knife</span> from 1580s.</dd></dl></div></div>
