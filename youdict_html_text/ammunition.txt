                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1、am- "这里可以理解为：to, 同ad-；其实并无实际意义，源于法语的错误拆分" + mun- "defend, fortify, protect" + -ition(复合后缀：-ite, -ion).<br />
2、字面含义：a defending, fortification, protecting, defensive walls.<br />
3、The mistake in the word perhaps was by influence of French a(d)monition "warning." The error was corrected in French (Modern French munition), but retained in English.<br />
4. 音似“俺没有你神”——你有“弹药和军火？”——那俺没有你神。</div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/ammunition">ammunition</a> 武器</span><p>来自法语短语la munition讹误为俗词源: am-同ad- . munition, 武器。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ammunition">ammunition</a></dt><dd><b>ammunition: </b><span>[17] <i>Ammunition </i>is one of many words which resulted from a mistaken analysis of ‘article’ plus ‘noun’ (compare ADDER). In this case, French <i>la munition </i>‘the munitions, the supplies’ was misapprehended as <i>l’ammunition</i>, and borrowed thus into English. At first it was used for military supplies in general, and it does not seem to have been until the beginning of the 18th century that its meaning became restricted to ‘bullets, shells, etc’.<br><br>The word <i>munition </i>itself was borrowed into English from French in the 16th century. It originally meant ‘fortification’, and came from the Latin noun <i>mūnītiō</i>; this was a derivative of the verb <i>munīre</i>, ‘defend, fortify’, which in turn was based on the noun <i>moenia </i>‘walls, ramparts’ (related to <i>mūrus </i>‘wall’, the source of English <i>mural</i>).<br><br>Also from <i>munīre</i>, via medieval Latin <i>mūnīmentum</i>, comes <i>muniment </i>[15], a legal term for ‘title deed’; the semantic connection is that a title deed is a means by which someone can ‘defend’ his or her legal right to property.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/muniment" target="_blank">muniment</a>, <a href="/etym/s/munition" target="_blank">munition</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mural" target="_blank">mural</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ammunition">ammunition (n.)</a></dt><dd>1620s, from French soldiers' faulty separation of Middle French <span class="foreign">la munition</span> into <span class="foreign">l'ammunition</span>; from Latin <span class="foreign">munitionem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">munitio</span>) "a fortifying" (see <a href="/etym/s/munition" target="_blank">munition</a>), and at first meaning all military supplies in general. The mistake in the word perhaps was by influence of French <span class="foreign">a(d)monition</span> "warning." The error was corrected in French (Modern French <span class="foreign">munition</span>), but retained in English.</dd></dl></div></div>
