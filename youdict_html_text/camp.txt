                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/camp">camp</a> 营地</span><p>来自PIE *kamp, 弯，围，即围起来的一块平地，原义也指战场。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/camp">camp</a></dt><dd><b>camp: </b><span>[16] Latin <i>campus </i>meant ‘open field’. It branched out into various more specialized meanings. One of them, for example, was ‘battle field’: this was borrowed into the Germanic languages as ‘battle’ (German has <i>kampf</i>, for instance, as in the title of Adolf Hitler’s book <i>Mein Kampf </i>‘My struggle’). Another was ‘place for military exercises’, and this seems to have developed, in the word’s passage via Italian <i>campo </i>and French <i>camp</i>, to ‘place where troops are housed’.<br><br>English got the word from French. <i>Camp </i>‘mannered, effeminate’ [20] is presumably a different word, but its origins are obscure. Latin <i>campus </i>itself was adopted in English in the 18th century for the ‘grounds of a college’. It was originally applied to Princeton university in the USA.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/campaign" target="_blank">campaign</a>, <a href="/etym/s/champion" target="_blank">champion</a>, <a href="/etym/s/decamp" target="_blank">decamp</a>, <a href="/etym/s/scamp" target="_blank">scamp</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/camp">camp (n.)</a></dt><dd>"place where an army lodges temporarily," 1520s, from French <span class="foreign">camp</span>, from Italian <span class="foreign">campo</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">campus</span> "open field, level space" (also source of French <span class="foreign">champ</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/campus" target="_blank">campus</a>), especially "open space for military exercise."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; A later reborrowing of the Latin word, which had been taken up in early West Germanic as <span class="foreign">*kampo-z</span> and appeared originally in Old English as <span class="foreign">camp</span> "contest, battle, fight, war." This was obsolete by mid-15c. Transferred to non-military senses 1550s. Meaning "body of adherents of a doctrine or cause" is 1871. <span class="foreign">Camp-follower</span> first attested 1810. <span class="foreign">Camp-meeting</span> is from 1809, originally usually in reference to Methodists. <span class="foreign">Camp-fever</span> (1758) is any epidemic fever incident to life in a camp, especially typhus or typhoid.&#13; &#13; &#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/camp">camp (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"tasteless," 1909, homosexual slang, of uncertain origin, perhaps from mid-17c. French <span class="foreign">camper</span> "to portray, pose" (as in <span class="foreign">se camper</span> "put oneself in a bold, provocative pose"); popularized 1964 by Susan Sontag's essay "Notes on Camp." <span class="foreign">Campy</span> is attested from 1959.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/camp">camp (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to encamp," 1540s, from <a href="/etym/s/camp" target="_blank">camp</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Camped</span>; <span class="foreign">camping</span>. <span class="foreign">Camping out</span> is attested from 1834, American English.</dd></dl></div></div>
