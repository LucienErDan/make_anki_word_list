                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/lurch">lurch</a> 困境，危难</span><p>来自中古英语lurch,一种棋盘游戏，来自luren,等待，潜藏，埋伏，词源同lour,lurk.比较poke.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/lurch">lurch</a> 倾斜，摇晃，蹒跚而行</span><p>词源不详，航海术语，用来指船在风浪中剧烈摇晃并倾斜，可能来自lurch,困境，危险。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lurch">lurch</a></dt><dd><b>lurch: </b><span>English has two words <i>lurch</i>, both with rather obscure histories. The verb, ‘stagger’ [19], appears to come from an earlier <i>lee-lurch</i>, which in turn may have been an alteration of an 18th-century nautical term <i>lee-latch</i>, denoting ‘drifting to leeward’. The <i>latch </i>element may have come from French <i>lâcher </i>‘let go’. The <i>lurch </i>of <i>leave someone in the lurch </i>[16] originated as a term in backgammon, denoting a ‘defeat’, ‘low score’, or ‘position of disadvantage’. It was borrowed from French <i>lourche</i>, which probably goes back to Middle High German <i>lurz </i>‘left’, hence ‘wrong’, ‘defeat’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lurch">lurch (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"sudden pitch to one side," 1784, from earlier <span class="foreign">lee-larches</span> (1765), a nautical term for "the sudden roll which a ship makes to lee-ward in a high sea, when a large wave strikes her, and bears her weather-side violently up, which depresses the other in proportion" ["Complete Dictionary of Arts and Sciences," London 1765]; perhaps from French <span class="foreign">lacher</span> "to let go," from Latin <span class="foreign">laxus</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/lax" target="_blank">lax</a>).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; When a Ship is brought by the Lee, it is commonly occaſsioned by a large Sea, and by the Neglect of the Helm's-man. When the Wind is two or three Points on the Quarter, the Ship taking a Lurch, brings the Wind on the other Side, and lays the Sails all dead to the Maſt; as the Yards are braced up, ſhe then having no Way, and the Helm being of no Service, I would therefore brace about the Head ſails ſharp the other Way .... [John Hamilton Moore, Practical Navigator, 8th ed., 1784]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lurch">lurch (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"predicament," 1580s, from Middle English <span class="foreign">lurch</span> (v.) "to beat in a game of skill (often by a great many points)," mid-14c., probably literally "to make a complete victory in <span class="foreign">lorche</span>," a game akin to backgammon, from Old French <span class="foreign">lourche</span>. The game name is perhaps related to Middle English <span class="foreign">lurken, lorken</span> "to lie hidden, lie in ambush," or it may be adopted into French from Middle High German <span class="foreign">lurz</span> "left," also "wrong."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lurch">lurch (v.)</a></dt><dd>1821, from <a href="/etym/s/lurch" target="_blank">lurch</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Lurched</span>; <span class="foreign">lurching</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
