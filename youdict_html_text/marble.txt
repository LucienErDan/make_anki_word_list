                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/marble">marble</a> 大理石，玻璃弹子</span><p>来自古法语marbre的拼写异化形式，来自拉丁语marmor,来自希腊语marmoros,大理石，明亮的石头，可能来自PIE*mer,发光，闪光，词源同mere,morning.后用于指用小石子磨成的弹子游戏，但后来随着玻璃技术的成熟，多用于指玻璃弹子。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marble">marble</a></dt><dd><b>marble: </b><span>[12] Greek <i>mármaros</i>, a word of unknown origin, denoted to begin with ‘any hard stone’, but association with the verb <i>marmaírein </i>‘shine’ led to a particular application to ‘marble’. Latin took it over as <i>marmor</i>, and it passed into Old French as <i>marbre</i>. Here, by a process known as dissimilation, in which one of two similar sounds is replaced by a different one, <i>marbre </i>became <i>marble </i>– whence English <i>marble</i>. The use of the word for the little ball with which the game of ‘marbles’ is played dates from the late 17th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marble">marble (n.)</a></dt><dd>type of stone much used in sculpture, monuments, etc., early 14c., by dissimilation from <span class="foreign">marbra</span> (mid-12c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">marbre</span> (which itself underwent dissimilation of 2nd <span class="foreign">-r-</span> to <span class="foreign">-l-</span> in 14c.; marbre persisted in English into early 15c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">marmor</span>, from or cognate with Greek <span class="foreign">marmaros</span> "marble, gleaming stone," of unknown origin, perhaps originally an adjective meaning "sparkling," which would connect it with <span class="foreign">marmairein</span> "to shine." The Latin word was taken directly into Old English as <span class="foreign">marma</span>. German <span class="foreign">Marmor</span> is restored Latin from Old High German <span class="foreign">marmul</span>. Meaning "little balls of marble used in a children's game" is attested from 1690s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marble">marble (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "of marble," from <a href="/etym/s/marble" target="_blank">marble</a> (n.). Meaning "mottled like marble" is mid-15c. <span class="foreign">Marble cake</span> is attested from 1864.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marble">marble (v.)</a></dt><dd>1590s (implied in <span class="foreign">marbled</span>), "to give (something) the appearance of marble," from <a href="/etym/s/marble" target="_blank">marble</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Marbling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
