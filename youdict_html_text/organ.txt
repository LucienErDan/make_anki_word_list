                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/organ">organ</a> 器官，管风琴，组织机构</span><p>来自希腊语organon,工具，乐器，身体器官，来自PIE*werg,工作，做工，词源同work,ergative.引申词义运行，组织，机构等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/organ">organ</a></dt><dd><b>organ: </b><span>[13] Greek <i>órganon </i>meant ‘tool, implement, instrument’. It was a descendant of the Indo-European base *<i>worg</i>- (source also of English <i>work</i>). Latin took the word over as <i>organum</i>, and in the post-classical period applied it to ‘musical instruments’. At first it was a very general term, but gradually it narrowed down to ‘wind instrument’, and in ecclesiastical Latin it came to be used for a musical instrument made from a number of pipes.<br><br>When English acquired it, via Old French <i>organe</i>, it was in the intermediate sense ‘wind instrument’ (in the 1611 translation of Psalm 150, ‘Praise him with stringed instruments and organs’, <i>organ </i>still means ‘pipe’), but by the end of the 17th century this had died out. The sense ‘functional part of the body’ goes right back to the word’s Greek source. The derivative <i>organize </i>[15] comes via Old French from medieval Latin <i>organizāre</i>.<br><br>This originally denoted literally ‘furnish with organs so as to form into a living being’, and hence ‘provide with a co-ordinated structure’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/organize" target="_blank">organize</a>, <a href="/etym/s/orgy" target="_blank">orgy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/work" target="_blank">work</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/organ">organ (n.)</a></dt><dd>fusion of late Old English <span class="foreign">organe</span>, and Old French <span class="foreign">orgene</span> (12c.), both meaning "musical instrument," both from Latin <span class="foreign">organa</span>, plural of <span class="foreign">organum</span> "a musical instrument," from Greek <span class="foreign">organon</span> "implement, tool for making or doing; musical instrument; organ of sense, organ of the body," literally "that with which one works," from PIE <span class="foreign">*werg-ano-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*werg-</span> "to do" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">ergon</span> "work," <span class="foreign">orgia</span> "religious performances;" Armenian <span class="foreign">gorc</span> "work;" Avestan <span class="foreign">vareza</span> "work, activity;" Gothic <span class="foreign">waurkjan</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">wyrcan</span> "to work," Old English <span class="foreign">weorc</span> "deed, action, something done;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">yrka</span> "work, take effect").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Applied vaguely in late Old English to musical instruments; sense narrowed by late 14c. to the musical instrument now known by that name (involving pipes supplied with wind by a bellows and worked by means of keys), though Augustine (c. 400) knew this as a specific sense of Latin <span class="foreign">organa</span>. The meaning "body part adapted to a certain function" is attested from late 14c., from a Medieval Latin sense of Latin <span class="foreign">organum</span>. <a href="/etym/s/Organist" target="_blank">Organist</a> is first recorded 1590s; <span class="foreign">organ-grinder</span> is attested from 1806.</dd></dl></div></div>
