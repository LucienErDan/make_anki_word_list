                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/glove">glove</a> 手套</span><p>来自德语。来自ge-, 强调，词源同game. *lap, 平的，手掌，可能同flat, plan.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/glove">glove</a></dt><dd><b>glove: </b><span>[OE] Not surprisingly, most words for ‘glove’ in European languages are related in some way to words for ‘hand’; German <i>handschuh </i>and Dutch <i>handschoen</i>, for example, mean literally ‘handshoe’; Greek <i>kheirís </i>was derived from <i>kheíris </i>‘hand’; and Romanian <i>manusa </i>was based on Latin <i>manus </i>‘hand’. And <i>glove </i>appears to be no exception; it probably goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>galōfō</i>, in which *<i>ga</i>- was a collective prefix and <i>lōfō </i>meant ‘hand’ (Swedish dialect <i>loof </i>‘palm of the hand’ comes from it).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/glove">glove (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">glof</span> "glove, covering for the hand having separate sheaths for the fingers," also "palm of the hand," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*galofo</span> "covering for the hand" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">glofi</span>), probably from <span class="foreign">*ga-</span> collective prefix + <span class="foreign">*lofi</span> "hand" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">lofi</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">love</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">lofa</span> "flat of the hand"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*lep-</span> (2) "be flat; palm, sole, shoulder blade" (cognates: Russian <span class="foreign">lopata</span> "shovel;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">lopa</span> "claw," <span class="foreign">lopeta</span> "shovel, spade").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; German <span class="foreign">Handschuh</span>, the usual word for "glove," literally "hand-shoe" (Old High German <span class="foreign">hantscuoh</span>; also Danish and Swedish <span class="foreign">hantsche</span>) is represented by Old English <span class="foreign">Handscio</span> (the name of one of Beowulf's companions, eaten by Grendel), but this is attested only as a proper name. Meaning "boxing glove" is from 1847. Figurative use of <span class="foreign">fit like a glove</span> is by 1771.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/glove">glove (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to cover or fit with a glove," c. 1400, from <a href="/etym/s/glove" target="_blank">glove</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Gloved</span>; <span class="foreign">gloving</span>. Old English had adjective <span class="foreign">glofed</span>. <span class="foreign">Glover</span> as a surname is from mid-13c.</dd></dl></div></div>
