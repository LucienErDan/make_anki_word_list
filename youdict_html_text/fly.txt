                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fly">fly</a> 飞行，昆虫</span><p>来自PIE*pleu, 流动，漂浮，词源同float, fleet. 由飘动引申词义在空中浮动，飞翔，飞行等。同时，用来指小昆虫等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fly">fly</a></dt><dd><b>fly: </b><span>[OE] Historically, ‘move through the air’ is something of a secondary semantic development for <i>fly</i>. Its distant Indo-European ancestor, *<i>pleu</i>-, denoted rapid motion in general, and in particular ‘flowing’ or ‘floating’, and it produced such offspring as Greek <i>pléo </i>‘sail, float’ and Sanskrit <i>plu</i>- ‘sail, swim’, as well as English <i>fleet</i>, <i>flood</i>, <i>flow</i>, <i>fowl</i>, <i>plover</i>, and <i>pluvial</i>.<br><br>An extension to that base, *<i>pleuk</i>-, gave rise to Lithuanian <i>plaukti </i>‘float, sail, swim’, and to prehistoric West and North Germanic *<i>fleugan</i>, source of German <i>fliegen</i>, Dutch <i>vliegen</i>, Swedish <i>flyga</i>, and English <i>fly</i>, all meaning ‘move with wings’. The insect-name <i>fly </i>is also of considerable antiquity, going back to a prehistoric Germanic derivative *<i>fleugōn </i>or *<i>fleugjōn</i>, but the origins of the adjective <i>fly </i>‘crafty, sharp’ [19] are not known.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fleet" target="_blank">fleet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/flood" target="_blank">flood</a>, <a href="/etym/s/flow" target="_blank">flow</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fowl" target="_blank">fowl</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plover" target="_blank">plover</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fly">fly (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fleoge</span> "a fly, winged insect," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fleugon</span> "flying insect" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fleiga</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fluga</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vlieghe</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vlieg</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">flioga</span>, German <span class="foreign">Fliege</span> "fly"); literally "the flying (insect)" (compare Old English <span class="foreign">fleogende</span> "flying"), from same source as <a href="/etym/s/fly" target="_blank">fly</a> (v.1).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Originally any winged insect (moths, gnats, beetles, locusts, hence <a href="/etym/s/butterfly" target="_blank">butterfly</a>, etc.) and long used by farmers and gardeners for any insect parasite. <span class="foreign">Flies</span> figuratively for "large numbers" of anything is from 1590s. Plural <span class="foreign">flien</span> (as in <span class="foreign">oxen</span>, etc.) gradually normalized 13c.-15c. to <span class="foreign">-s</span>. <span class="foreign">Fly in the ointment</span> is from Eccles. x:1. <span class="foreign">Fly on the wall</span> "unseen observer" first recorded 1881. <span class="foreign">No flies on _____</span> "no lack of activity or alertness on the part of," is attested by 1866. Meaning "fish-hook dressed to resemble an insect" is from 1580s; <span class="foreign">Fly-fishing</span> is from 1650s. <span class="foreign">Fly-catcher</span> "bird which eats insects on the wing" is from 1670s. The <span class="foreign">fly agaric</span> mushroom (1788) so called because it was used as a poison for flies.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sense of "a flight, flying" is from mid-15c. From the verb and the notion of "flapping as a wing does" comes the noun sense of "tent flap" (1810), which was extended to "strip of material sewn into a garment as a covering for buttons" or some other purpose (1844). Baseball <span class="foreign">fly ball</span> attested by 1866. To do something <span class="foreign">on the fly</span> is 1856, apparently from baseball.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; When the catcher sees several fielders running to catch a ball, he should name the one he thinks surest to take it, when the others should not strive to catch the ball on the fly, but only, in case of its being missed, take it on the bound. ["The American Boys Book of Sports and Games," New York, 1864]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fly">fly (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to soar through air; move through the air with wings," Old English <span class="foreign">fleogan</span> "to fly, take flight, rise into the air" (class II strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">fleag</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">flogen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fleugan</span> "to fly" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fliogan</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fliaga</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vlieghen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vliegen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fliogan</span>, German <span class="foreign">fliegen</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">flügja</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*pleuk-</span>, extended form of <span class="foreign">*pleu-</span> "to flow, float" (see <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "go at full speed" is from c. 1300. In reference to flags, 1650s. Transitive sense "cause to move or float in air" (as a flag, kite, etc.) is from 1739; sense of "convey through the air" ("Fly Me to the Moon") is from 1864. Related: <span class="foreign">Flew</span>; <span class="foreign">flied</span> (baseball); <a href="/etym/s/flown" target="_blank">flown</a>; <a href="/etym/s/flying" target="_blank">flying</a>. Slang phrase <span class="foreign">fly off the handle</span> "lose one's cool" dates from 1825.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fly">fly (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"run away," Old English <span class="foreign">fleon</span>, <span class="foreign">flion</span> "fly from, avoid, escape;" essentially a variant spelling of <a href="/etym/s/flee" target="_blank">flee</a> (q.v.). In Old English, this verb and <span class="foreign">fleogan</span> "soar through the air with wings" (modern <a href="/etym/s/fly" target="_blank">fly</a> (v.1)) differed only in their present tense forms and often were confused, then as now. In some Middle English dialects they seem to have merged completely. Distinguished from one another since 14c. in the past tense: <span class="foreign">flew</span> for <span class="foreign">fly</span> (v.1), <span class="foreign">fled</span> for <span class="foreign">fly</span> (v.2).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fly">fly (adj.)</a></dt><dd>slang, "clever, alert, wide awake," by 1811, perhaps from <a href="/etym/s/fly" target="_blank">fly</a> (n.) on the notion of the insect being hard to catch. Other theories, however, trace it to <a href="/etym/s/fledge" target="_blank">fledge</a> or <a href="/etym/s/flash" target="_blank">flash</a>. Slang use in 1990s might be a revival or a reinvention.</dd></dl></div></div>
