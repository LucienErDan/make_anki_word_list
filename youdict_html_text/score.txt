                                                                        <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/score">score</a></dt><dd><b>score: </b><span>[11] The etymological notion underlying <i>score </i>is of ‘cutting’ – for it is related to English <i>shear</i>. It was borrowed from Old Norse <i>skor</i>, which went back to the same prehistoric Germanic base – *<i>skur</i>-, *<i>sker</i>- ‘cut’ – that produced <i>shear </i>(not to mention <i>share</i>, <i>shore</i>, and <i>short</i>). It had a range of meanings, from ‘notch’ to ‘record kept by cutting notches’, but it was specifically the ‘number twenty’ (presumably originally ‘twenty recorded by cutting notches’) that English at first took over.<br><br>The other senses followed, perhaps as a result of reborrowing, in the 14th century, but the main modern meaning, ‘number of points made in a game’ (originally as recorded by cutting notches), is a purely English development of the 18th century. Roughly contemporary is ‘written music’, which is said to come from the linking together of related staves with a single common bar line or ‘score’ (in the sense ‘mark’).<br><br>The verb <i>score </i>‘mark with lines’ was borrowed in the 14th century from Old Norse <i>skora</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/share" target="_blank">share</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shear" target="_blank">shear</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shirt" target="_blank">shirt</a>, <a href="/etym/s/short" target="_blank">short</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skirt" target="_blank">skirt</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/score">score (n.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">scoru</span> "twenty," from Old Norse <span class="foreign">skor</span> "mark, notch, incision; a rift in rock," also, in Icelandic, "twenty," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skura-</span>, from PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)ker-</span> (1) "to cut" (see <a href="/etym/s/shear" target="_blank">shear</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The connecting notion probably is counting large numbers (of sheep, etc.) with a notch in a stick for each 20. That way of counting, called <span class="foreign">vigesimalism</span>, also exists in French: In Old French, "twenty" (<span class="foreign">vint</span>) or a multiple of it could be used as a base, as in <span class="foreign">vint et doze</span> ("32"), <span class="foreign">dous vinz et diz</span> ("50"). Vigesimalism was or is a feature of Welsh, Irish, Gaelic and Breton (as well as non-IE Basque), and it is speculated that the English and the French picked it up from the Celts. Compare <a href="/etym/s/tally" target="_blank">tally</a> (n.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The prehistoric sense of the Germanic word, then, likely was "straight mark like a scratch, line drawn by a sharp instrument," but in English this is attested only from c. 1400, along with the sense "mark made (on a chalkboard, etc.) to keep count of a customer's drinks in a tavern." This sense was extended by 1670s to "mark made for purpose of recording a point in a game or match," and thus "aggregate of points made by contestants in certain games and matches" (1742, originally in whist).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From the tavern-keeping sense comes the meaning "amount on an innkeeper's bill" (c. 1600) and thus the figurative verbal expression <span class="foreign">settle scores</span> (1775). Meaning "printed piece of music" first recorded 1701, said to be from the practice of connecting related staves by scores of lines. Especially "music composed for a film" (1927). Meaning "act of obtaining narcotic drugs" is by 1951.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Scoreboard</span> is from 1826; &#13; <span class="foreign">score-keeping-</span> from 1905; newspaper sports section <span class="foreign">score line</span> is from 1965; baseball <span class="foreign">score-card</span> is from 1877.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/score">score (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to cut with incisions or notches," c. 1400; "to record by means of notches" (late 14c.); see <a href="/etym/s/score" target="_blank">score</a> (n.). Meanings "to keep record of the scores in a game, etc." and "to make or add a point for one's side in a game, etc." both attested from 1742. The slang sense, in reference to men, "achieve intercourse" first recorded 1960. Meaning "to be scorekeeper, to keep the score in a game or contest" is from 1846. In the musical sense from 1839. Related: <span class="foreign">Scored</span>; <span class="foreign">scoring</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
