                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/toe">toe</a> 脚趾</span><p>来自古英语 ta,脚趾，缩写自 Proto-Germanic*taihwo,脚趾，来自 PIE*deik,指出，展示，词源 同 digit,手指，数字。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/toe">toe</a></dt><dd><b>toe: </b><span>[OE] Many European languages use the same word for ‘finger’ and ‘toe’ (Spanish <i>dedo</i>, for example, and Russian and Polish <i>palec</i>), and English <i>toe </i>may have originated in such a dualpurpose term. Its prehistoric Germanic ancestor was *<i>taikhwōn</i>, and it has been speculated that this may be related to Latin <i>digitus </i>‘finger, toe’ (source of English <i>digit</i>). Other descendants of the Germanic form include German <i>zehe</i>, Dutch <i>tee</i>, Swedish <i>tåa</i>, and Danish <i>taa</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/digit" target="_blank">digit</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/toe">toe (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ta</span> "toe" (plural <span class="foreign">tan</span>), contraction of <span class="foreign">*tahe</span> (Mercian <span class="foreign">tahæ</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*taihwo</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">ta</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">tane</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">te</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">teen</span> (perhaps originally a plural), Old High German <span class="foreign">zecha</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zehe</span> "toe"). Perhaps originally meaning "fingers" as well (many PIE languages still use one word to mean both fingers and toes), and thus from PIE root <span class="foreign">*deik-</span> "to show" (see <a href="/etym/s/diction" target="_blank">diction</a>).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Þo stode hii I-armed fram heued to þe ton. [Robert of Gloucester, "Chronicle," c. 1300]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The old plural survived regionally into Middle English as <span class="foreign">tan</span>, <span class="foreign">ton</span>. To be <span class="foreign">on (one's) toes</span> "alert, eager" is recorded from 1921. To <span class="foreign">step on (someone's) toes</span> in the figurative sense "give offense" is from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Toe-hold</span> "support for the toe of a boot in climbing" is from 1880.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/toe">toe (v.)</a></dt><dd>"touch or reach with the toes," 1813, from <a href="/etym/s/toe" target="_blank">toe</a> (n.). First recorded in expression <span class="foreign">toe the mark</span>, which seems to be nautical in origin.<blockquote>The chief mate ... marked a line on the deck, brought the two boys up to it, making them "toe the mark." [R.H. Dana, "Two Years Before the Mast," 1840]</blockquote>&#13; Related: <span class="foreign">Toed</span>; <span class="foreign">toeing</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
