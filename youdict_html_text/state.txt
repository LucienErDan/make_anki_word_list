                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/state">state</a> 状态，状况，情况，陈述，说明，规定</span><p>来自拉丁语 status,位置，地方，站立姿势，情况，状况，公共秩序，过去分词名词格于 stare, 站立，来自 PIE*sta,站立，词源同 stand.引申词义陈述，说明，规定等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/state">state</a></dt><dd><b>state: </b><span>[13] <i>State </i>comes, partly via Old French <i>estat </i>(source of English <i>estate</i>), from Latin <i>status </i>‘way of standing, condition, position’, which was formed from the same base as <i>stāre </i>‘stand’ (a distant relative of English <i>stand</i>). The word’s political sense, ‘body politic’, first recorded in the 16th century, comes from Latin expressions such as <i>status rei publicae </i>‘condition of the republic’ and <i>status civitatis </i>‘condition of the body politic’.<br><br>The verb <i>state </i>originally meant ‘put, place’; its modern meaning ‘declare’ arose from the notion of ‘placing’ something on record, setting it out in detail. English borrowed <i>status </i>itself in the 17th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/estate" target="_blank">estate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stand" target="_blank">stand</a>, <a href="/etym/s/station" target="_blank">station</a>, <a href="/etym/s/statistic" target="_blank">statistic</a>, <a href="/etym/s/statue" target="_blank">statue</a>; <a href="/etym/s/statute" target="_blank">statute</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/state">state (n.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "circumstances, position in society, temporary attributes of a person or thing, conditions," from Old French <span class="foreign">estat</span> "position, condition; status, stature, station," and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">status</span> "a station, position, place; way of standing, posture; order, arrangement, condition," figuratively "standing, rank; public order, community organization," noun of action from past participle stem of <span class="foreign">stare</span> "to stand" from PIE root <span class="foreign">*sta-</span> "to stand" (see <a href="/etym/s/stet" target="_blank">stet</a>). Some Middle English senses are via Old French <span class="foreign">estat</span> (French <span class="foreign">état</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/estate" target="_blank">estate</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Latin word was adopted into other modern Germanic languages (German, Dutch <span class="foreign">staat</span>) but chiefly in the political senses only. Meaning "physical condition as regards form or structure" is attested from late 13c. Meaning "mental or emotional condition" is attested from 1530s (phrase <span class="foreign">state of mind</span> first attested 1749); colloquial sense of "agitated or perturbed state" is from 1837. &#13; <blockquote>&#13; He [the President] shall from time to time give to the Congress Information of the State of the Union, and recommend to their Consideration such Measures as he shall judge necessary and expedient. [U.S. Constitution, Article II, Section iii]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/state">state (v.)</a></dt><dd>1590s, "to set in a position," from <a href="/etym/s/state" target="_blank">state</a> (n.1); the sense of "declare in words" is first attested 1640s, from the notion of "placing" something on the record. Related: <span class="foreign">Stated</span>; <span class="foreign">stating</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/state">state (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"political organization of a country, supreme civil power, government," c. 1300, from special use of <a href="/etym/s/state" target="_blank">state</a> (n.1); this sense grew out of the meaning "condition of a country" with regard to government, prosperity, etc. (late 13c.), from Latin phrases such as <span class="foreign">status rei publicæ</span> "condition (or existence) of the republic."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sense of "a semi-independent political entity under a federal authority, one of the bodies politic which together make up a federal republic" is from 1774. The British North American colonies occasionally were called <span class="foreign">states</span> as far back as 1630s; <span class="foreign">the States</span> has been short for "the United States of America" since 1777; also of the Netherlands. <span class="foreign">State rights</span> in U.S. political sense is attested from 1798; form <span class="foreign">states rights</span> is first recorded 1858. <span class="foreign">Church and state</span> have been contrasted from 1580s. <span class="foreign">State-socialism</span> attested from 1850.</dd></dl></div></div>
