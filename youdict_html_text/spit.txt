                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spit">spit</a> 吐，唾，怒斥</span><p>来自拟声词根辅音丛 sp-,吐，喷出，比较 spate,spew,spit,spout,sputter.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spit">spit</a> 烤肉扦</span><p>来自古英语 spitu,烤肉扦，来自 Proto-Germanic*spituz,尖刺，来自 PIE*spei,尖刺，尖点，词 源同 spike,spire.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spit">spit</a></dt><dd><b>spit: </b><span>English has two words <i>spit </i>in current usage. <i>Spit </i>‘eject saliva’ [OE] is one of a sizable group of English words beginning <i>sp</i>- which denote ‘ejecting or discharging liquid’. Others include <i>spew </i>[OE], <i>spout </i>[14], <i>spurt </i>[16], and <i>sputter </i>[16]. They all go back ultimately to an Indo- European base *<i>spyēu</i>-, *<i>spyū</i>-, etc, imitative of the sound of spitting, which also produced Latin <i>spuere </i>‘spit’ (source of English <i>cuspidor </i>[18] and <i>sputum </i>[17]).<br><br>The immediate source of <i>spit </i>itself was the prehistoric Germanic base *<i>spit</i>-, a variant of which, *<i>spāt</i>-, produced English <i>spittle </i>[15] (originally <i>spattle</i>, but changed through association with <i>spit</i>). <i>Spit </i>for roasting things on [OE] comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>spituz</i>, which also produced German <i>spiess </i>and Dutch <i>spit</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cuspidor" target="_blank">cuspidor</a>, <a href="/etym/s/spew" target="_blank">spew</a>, <a href="/etym/s/spout" target="_blank">spout</a>, <a href="/etym/s/spurt" target="_blank">spurt</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sputter" target="_blank">sputter</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sputum" target="_blank">sputum</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spit">spit (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"expel saliva," Old English <span class="foreign">spittan</span> (Anglian), <span class="foreign">spætan</span> (West Saxon), transitive and intransitive, past tense <span class="foreign">*spytte</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spitjan</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*sp(y)eu-</span>, of imitative origin (see <a href="/etym/s/spew" target="_blank">spew</a> (v.)). Not the usual Old English word for this; <span class="foreign">spætlan</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/spittle" target="_blank">spittle</a>) and <span class="foreign">spiwan</span> are more common; all are from the same root. To spit as a gesture of contempt (especially at someone) is in Old English. Related: <span class="foreign">Spat</span>; <span class="foreign">spitting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spit">spit (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"saliva," early 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/spit" target="_blank">spit</a> (v.1). Meaning "the very likeness" in modern use is attested from 1825 (as in <span class="foreign">spitting image</span>, attested from 1887); compare French <span class="foreign">craché</span> in same sense. <span class="foreign">Spit-curl</span> (1831) was originally considered colloquial or vulgar. Military phrase <span class="foreign">spit and polish</span> first recorded 1895.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spit">spit (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"sharp-pointed rod for roasting meat," late Old English <span class="foreign">spitu</span> "a spit," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spituz</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">spit</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">spett</span> (which perhaps is from Low German), Old High German <span class="foreign">spiz</span>, German <span class="foreign">Spieß</span> "roasting spit," German <span class="foreign">spitz</span> "pointed"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*spei-</span> "sharp point" (see <a href="/etym/s/spike" target="_blank">spike</a> (n.1)). This is also the source of the word meaning "sandy point" (1670s). Old French <span class="foreign">espois</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">espeto</span> "spit" are Germanic loan-words. The verb meaning "to put on a spit" is recorded from c. 1200.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spit">spit (v.2)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "put on a spit, thrust with a spit," from late Old English <span class="foreign">sputtian</span> "to spit" (for cooking), from <a href="/etym/s/spit" target="_blank">spit</a> (n.2). Meaning "pierce with a weapon, transfix, impale" is from early 15c. Related: <span class="foreign">Spitted</span>; <span class="foreign">spitting</span>. Nares' Glossary has <span class="foreign">spit-frog</span> "a small sword."</dd></dl></div></div>
