                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sheet">sheet</a> 床单，被单，纸张，薄片，薄板</span><p>来自古英语 scete,布，覆盖物，来自 Proto-Germanic*skauta,投掷，投射，来自 PIE*skeud,射 出，发射，词源同 shoot,shot,shut.引申诸相关词义。词义演变比较 pork,猪肉，原义猪，字面 意思为喜欢拱的动物，词源同 furrow,沟，垄。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sheet">sheet</a></dt><dd><b>sheet: </b><span><i>Sheet </i>‘cloth’ [OE] and <i>sheet </i>‘rope attached to a sail’ [OE] are distinct words, although they have a common ancestor. This was the Germanic base *<i>skaut</i>-, *<i>skut</i>- ‘project’, which also produced English <i>scot-free</i>, <i>scuttle </i>‘sink a ship’, <i>shoot</i>, <i>shot</i>, <i>shout</i>, <i>shut</i>, and <i>skit</i>. This produced two Old English nouns, <i>scēte </i>‘cloth’ and <i>scēata </i>‘sail-rope’, which have formally coalesced in modern English as <i>sheet</i>, but retained their distinctive meanings. (<i>Sheet </i>‘cloth’ was not used specifically for ‘bed sheet’ until the 13th century.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/scot-free" target="_blank">scot-free</a>, <a href="/etym/s/scuttle" target="_blank">scuttle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shoot" target="_blank">shoot</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shot" target="_blank">shot</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shout" target="_blank">shout</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shut" target="_blank">shut</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skit" target="_blank">skit</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sheet">sheet (n.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">sciete</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">scete</span> (Mercian) "cloth, covering, towel, shroud," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skautjon-</span>, from <span class="foreign">*skauta-</span> "project" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">skaut</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">skauts</span> "seam, hem of a garment;" Dutch <span class="foreign">schoot</span>; German <span class="foreign">Schoß</span> "bosom, lap"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*skeud-</span> "to shoot, chase, throw" (see <a href="/etym/s/shoot" target="_blank">shoot</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "piece of paper" first recorded c. 1500; that of "any broad, flat surface" (of metal, open water, etc.) is from 1590s. Of falling rain from 1690s. Meaning "a newspaper" is first recorded 1749. <span class="foreign">Sheet lightning</span> is attested from 1794; <span class="foreign">sheet music</span> is from 1857. <span class="foreign">Between the sheets</span> "in bed" (usually with sexual overtones) is attested from 1590s; to be <span class="foreign">white as a sheet</span> is from 1751. The first element in <span class="foreign">sheet-anchor</span> (late 15c.) appears to be a different word, of unknown origin.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sheet">sheet (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"rope that controls a sail," late 13c., shortened from Old English <span class="foreign">sceatline</span> "sheet-line," from <span class="foreign">sceata</span> "lower part of sail," originally "piece of cloth," from same root as <a href="/etym/s/sheet" target="_blank">sheet</a> (n.1). Compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">skaut</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">schoot</span>, German <span class="foreign">Schote</span> "rope fastened to a sail."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; This probably is the notion in phrase <span class="foreign">three sheets to the wind</span> "drunk and disorganized," first recorded 1812 (in form <span class="foreign">three sheets in the wind</span>), an image of a sloop-rigged sailboat whose three sheets have slipped through the blocks are lost to the wind, thus "out of control." Apparently there was an early 19c. informal drunkenness scale in use among sailors and involving one, two, and three sheets, three signifying the highest degree of inebriation; there is a <span class="foreign">two sheets in the wind</span> from 1813.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; It must not be wondered at that the poor, untutored, savage Kentuckyan got "more than two thirds drunk," that is, as the sailors term it, three sheets in the wind and the fourth shivering, before the dinner was ended. [Niles' Weekly Register, May 2, 1812] &#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
