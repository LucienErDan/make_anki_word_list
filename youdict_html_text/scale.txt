            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/scale">scale</a> 水垢，牙石，秤，天平，鳞片</span><p>来自古诺斯语 skel,贝壳，skal,碗，饮水容器，来自 Proto-Germanic*skaela,分开，劈开，来自 PIE*skel,砍，切，分开，词源同 shell,scallop.由贝壳引申词义碗，饮水器具，由贝壳的钙化 性质引申词义水垢，牙石，由碗，饮水容器引申词义秤，天平，即秤的原型，由该印欧语的 词根义引申词义鱼鳞，鳞片等。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/scale">scale</a> 梯子，攀登，攀爬，规模，范围，程度，等级，比例</span><p>来自拉丁语 scala,梯子，来自（缩写自）scandere,攀爬，攀登，来自 PIE*skand,爬，上升， 词源同 scan,ascend,descend.字母 d 脱落，类似拼写比较 rule,regulate.引申词义规模，范围， 程度，等级，比例等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale</a></dt><dd><b>scale: </b><span>English has three separate words <i>scale</i>. The oldest, ‘pan of a balance’ [13], was borrowed from Old Norse <i>skál </i>‘bowl, drinking cup’ (ancestor of Swedish <i>skåal</i>, from which English gets the toast <i>skol </i>[16]). This was descended from a Germanic base *<i>skal</i>-, *<i>skel</i>-, *<i>skul</i>-, denoting ‘split, divide, peel’, which also produced English <i>scalp</i>, <i>shell</i>, <i>shelter</i>, <i>shield</i>, <i>skill</i>, probably <i>skull</i>, and also <i>scale </i>‘external plate on fish, etc’ [14].<br><br>This second <i>scale </i>was borrowed from Old French <i>escale</i>, which itself was acquired from prehistoric Germanic *<i>skalō </i>– another derivative of *<i>skal</i>-. Its modern German descendant, <i>schale</i>, is the probable source of English <i>shale </i>[18]. The third <i>scale</i>, which originally meant ‘ladder’ [15], came from Latin <i>scāla </i>‘ladder’, a descendant of the same base as Latin <i>scandere </i>‘climb’, from which English gets <i>ascend</i>, <i>descend</i>, <i>scan</i>, and <i>scandal</i>. (In modern French <i>scāla </i>has evolved to <i>échelle</i>, whose derivative <i>échelon </i>has given English <i>echelon </i>[18].) The modern meanings of the word, variations on the theme ‘system of graduations used for measuring’, are metaphorical extensions of the original ‘ladder, steps’.<br><br>Its use as a verb, meaning ‘climb’, goes back to the medieval Latin derivative <i>scālāre</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/scalp" target="_blank">scalp</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shell" target="_blank">shell</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shelter" target="_blank">shelter</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shield" target="_blank">shield</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skill" target="_blank">skill</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skol" target="_blank">skol</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skull" target="_blank">skull</a>; <a href="/etym/s/shale" target="_blank">shale</a>; <a href="/etym/s/ascend" target="_blank">ascend</a>, <a href="/etym/s/descend" target="_blank">descend</a>, <a href="/etym/s/echelon" target="_blank">echelon</a>, <a href="/etym/s/scan" target="_blank">scan</a>, <a href="/etym/s/scandal" target="_blank">scandal</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"skin plates on fish or snakes," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">escale</span> "cup, scale, shell pod, husk" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">écale</span>) "scale, husk," from Frankish <span class="foreign">*skala</span> or some other Germanic source, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skælo</span> "split, divide" (cognates: Dutch <span class="foreign">schaal</span> "a scale, husk," Old High German <span class="foreign">scala</span> "shell," Gothic <span class="foreign">skalja</span> "tile," Old English <span class="foreign">scealu</span> "shell, husk"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)kel-</span> (1) "to cut, cleave, split" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">culter</span> "knife," <span class="foreign">scalpere</span> "to cut, scrape;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">skolika</span> "mussel, shell," Russian <span class="foreign">skala</span> "rind, bark," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">skelti</span> "split," Old English <span class="foreign">scell</span> "shell," <span class="foreign">scalu</span> "drinking cup, bowl, scale of a balance").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In reference to humans, as a condition of certain skin diseases, it is attested from c. 1400. As what falls from one's eye when blindness ends (usually figurative), it echoes Acts ix:18 (Latin <span class="foreign">tanquam squamæ</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">hosei lepides</span>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale (n.2)</a></dt><dd>weighing instrument, early 15c.; earlier "pan of a balance" (late 14c.); earlier still "drinking cup" (c. 1200), from Old Norse <span class="foreign">skal</span> "bowl, drinking cup," in plural, "weighing scale" from a noun derivative of Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skæla</span> "split, divide" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">skel</span> "shell," Old English <span class="foreign">scealu</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">skala</span> "a bowl (to drink from)," Old High German <span class="foreign">scala</span>, German <span class="foreign">Schale</span> "a bowl, dish, cup," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">scale</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">schaal</span> "drinking cup, bowl, shell, scale of a balance"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*skel-</span> (1) "to cut" (see <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a> (n.1)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The connecting sense seems to be of half of a bivalve ("split") shell used as a drinking cup or a pan for weighing. But according to Paulus Diaconus the "drinking cup" sense originated from a supposed custom of making goblets from skulls (see <a href="/etym/s/skull" target="_blank">skull</a>). Related: <span class="foreign">Scales</span>. This, as a name for the zodiac constellation Libra, is attested in English from 1630s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to climb by or as by a ladder," late 14c., from <span class="foreign">scale</span> (n.) "a ladder," from Latin <span class="foreign">scala</span> "ladder, flight of stairs," from <span class="foreign">*scansla</span>, from stem of <span class="foreign">scandere</span> "to climb" (see <a href="/etym/s/scan" target="_blank">scan</a> (v.)). Related: <span class="foreign">Scaled</span>; <span class="foreign">scaling</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"series of registering marks to measure by; marks laid down to determine distance along a line," late 14c., from Latin <span class="foreign">scala</span> "ladder, staircase" (see <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a> (v.1)). Meaning "succession or series of steps" is from c. 1600; that of "standard for estimation" (<span class="foreign">large scale</span>, <span class="foreign">small scale</span>, etc.) is from 1620s. Musical sense (1590s), and the meaning "proportion of a representation to the actual object" (1660s) are via Italian <span class="foreign">scala</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">scala</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"weigh in scales," 1690s, from <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a> (n.2). Earlier "to compare, estimate" (c. 1600). Meaning "measure or regulate by a scale" is from 1798, from <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a> (n.3); that of "weigh out in proper quantities" is from 1841. <span class="foreign">Scale down</span> "reduce proportionately" is attested from 1887. <span class="foreign">Scale factor</span> is from 1948. Related: <span class="foreign">Scaled</span>; <span class="foreign">scaling</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/scale">scale (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"remove the scales of (a fish, etc.)," c. 1400, from <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a> (n.1). Intransitive sense "to come off in scales" is from 1520s. Related: <span class="foreign">Scaled</span>; <span class="foreign">scaling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/scale">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
