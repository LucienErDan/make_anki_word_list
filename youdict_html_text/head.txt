                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/head">head</a> 头</span><p>来自PIE*caput,头，词源同captain,capital.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/head">head</a></dt><dd><b>head: </b><span>[OE] The word <i>head </i>can be traced back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>kauput</i>-, *<i>kaupet</i>-, which probably had connotations of ‘bowl’ (as in ‘skull’) as well as ‘head’, although which came first is not clear. From it was descended prehistoric Germanic *<i>khaubutham</i>, *<i>khaubitham</i>, which produced German <i>haupt</i>, Dutch <i>hoofd</i>, Swedish <i>huvud</i>, and English <i>head</i>.<br><br>A variant of the Indo-European ancestor, *<i>kaput</i>-, seems to have been responsible for the Latin word for ‘head’, <i>caput </i>(source of a wide range of English words, including <i>capillary</i>, <i>capital</i>, <i>captain</i>, and <i>chief</i>), and also for Sanskrit <i>kapucchala</i>- ‘hair at the back of the head’ and Danish <i>hoved </i>‘head’. And a further related form, *<i>keup</i>-, produced English <i>hive</i>, Latin <i>cūpa </i>‘barrel’, and medieval Latin <i>cuppa </i>(source of English <i>cup </i>and German <i>kopf </i>‘head’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/capital" target="_blank">capital</a>, <a href="/etym/s/captain" target="_blank">captain</a>, <a href="/etym/s/chief" target="_blank">chief</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cup" target="_blank">cup</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/head">head (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">heafod</span> "top of the body," also "upper end of a slope," also "chief person, leader, ruler; capital city," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haubudam</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">hobid</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hofuð</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">haved</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">hovet</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">hoofd</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">houbit</span>, German <span class="foreign">Haupt</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">haubiþ</span> "head"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*kaput-</span> "head" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kaput-</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">caput</span> "head").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Modern spelling is early 15c., representing what was then a long vowel (as in <span class="foreign">heat</span>) and remained after pronunciation shifted. Of rounded tops of plants from late 14c. Meaning "origin of a river" is mid-14c. Meaning "obverse of a coin" (the side with the portrait) is from 1680s; meaning "foam on a mug of beer" is first attested 1540s; meaning "toilet" is from 1748, based on location of crew toilet in the bow (or <span class="foreign">head</span>) of a ship.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Synechdochic use for "person" (as in <span class="foreign">head count</span>) is first attested late 13c.; of cattle, etc., in this sense from 1510s. As a height measure of persons, from c. 1300. Meaning "drug addict" (usually in a compound with the preferred drug as the first element) is from 1911.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To be <span class="foreign">over (one's) head</span> "beyond one's comprehension" is by 1620s. To <span class="foreign">give head</span> "perform fellatio" is from 1950s. Phrase <span class="foreign">heads will roll</span> "people will be punished" (1930) translates Adolf Hitler. <span class="foreign">Head case</span> "eccentric or insane person" is from 1979. <span class="foreign">Head game</span> "mental manipulation" attested by 1972.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/head">head (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to be at the head or in the lead," c. 1200, from <a href="/etym/s/head" target="_blank">head</a> (n.). Meaning "to direct the head (toward)" is from c. 1600. Related: <span class="foreign">headed</span>, <a href="/etym/s/heading" target="_blank">heading</a>. The earliest use of the word as a verb meant "behead" (Old English <span class="foreign">heafdian</span>). Verbal phrase <span class="foreign">head up</span> "supervise, direct" is attested by 1930.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/head">head (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"most important, principal, leading," c. 1200, from <a href="/etym/s/head" target="_blank">head</a> (n.). Old English <span class="foreign">heafod</span> was used in this sense in compounds.</dd></dl></div></div>
