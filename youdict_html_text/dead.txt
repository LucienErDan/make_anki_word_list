                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/dead">dead</a> 死的</span><p>来自PIE*dheu, 死，词源同die.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dead">dead</a></dt><dd><b>dead: </b><span>[OE] <i>Dead </i>is part of a Germanic family of adjectives (including also German <i>tot</i>, Dutch <i>dood</i>, Swedish <i>död</i>, and Gothic <i>dauths</i>) which come from a prehistoric Germanic adjective *<i>dauthaz</i>. This in turn came from an earlier *<i>dhautós</i>, which was the past participle of the verb base that eventually produced English <i>die </i>(thus etymologically <i>dead </i>is in effect a precursor of <i>died</i>). The word’s ultimate source was Indo- European *<i>dheu</i>-, which some have linked with Greek <i>thánatos </i>‘dead’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/die" target="_blank">die</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dead">dead (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">dead</span> "dead," also "torpid, dull;" of water, "still, standing," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*daudaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">dod</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">død</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">död</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">dad</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">doot</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">dood</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">tot</span>, German <span class="foreign">tot</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">dauðr</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">dauþs</span> "dead"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*dhou-toz-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*dheu-</span> (3) "to die" (see <a href="/etym/s/die" target="_blank">die</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "insensible" is first attested early 13c. Of places, "inactive, dull," from 1580s. Used from 16c. in adjectival sense of "utter, absolute, quite" (as in <span class="foreign">dead drunk</span>, first attested 1590s; <span class="foreign">dead heat</span>, 1796). As an adverb, from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Dead on</span> is 1889, from marksmanship. <span class="foreign">Dead duck</span> is from 1844. <span class="foreign">Dead letter</span> is from 1703, used of laws lacking force as well as uncollected mail. Phrase <span class="foreign">in the dead of the night</span> first recorded 1540s. <span class="foreign">Dead soldier</span> "emptied liquor bottle" is from 1913 in that form; the image is older.&#13; <blockquote>For but ich haue bote of mi bale I am ded as dorenail (c. 1350).</blockquote>&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
