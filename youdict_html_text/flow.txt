                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/flow">flow</a> 浮动</span><p>来自PIE*pleu, 浮动，流动，词源同float, fleet, pluvial.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flow">flow</a></dt><dd><b>flow: </b><span>[OE] The prehistoric Indo-European *<i>pleu</i>-, ancestor of a heterogeneous range of English vocabulary, from <i>fleet </i>to <i>plover</i>, denoted ‘flow, float’. It had a variant form *<i>plō</i>-, which passed into Germanic as *<i>flō</i>-. This formed the basis of the Old English verb <i>flōwan </i>(whence modern English <i>flow</i>) and also of the noun <i>flood</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fleet" target="_blank">fleet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/flood" target="_blank">flood</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fowl" target="_blank">fowl</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plover" target="_blank">plover</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flow">flow (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">flowan</span> "to flow, stream, issue; become liquid, melt; abound, overflow" (class VII strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">fleow</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">flowen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*flowan</span> "to flow" (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vloyen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vloeien</span>, <span class="foreign">vloeijen</span> "to flow," Old Norse <span class="foreign">floa</span> "to deluge," Old High German <span class="foreign">flouwen</span> "to rinse, wash"), probably from PIE <span class="foreign">*pleu-</span> "flow, float" (see <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a>). The weak form predominated from 14c., but strong past participle <span class="foreign">flown</span> is occasionally attested through 18c. Related: <span class="foreign">Flowed</span>; <span class="foreign">flowing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flow">flow (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-15c., "action of flowing," from <a href="/etym/s/flow" target="_blank">flow</a> (v.). Meaning "amount that flows" is from 1807. Sense of "any strong, progressive movement comparable to the flow of a river" is from 1640s. <span class="foreign">Flow chart</span> attested from 1920 (<span class="foreign">flow-sheet</span> in same sense from 1912). To <span class="foreign">go with the flow</span> is by 1977, apparently originally in skiing jargon.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Go with the flow, enjoy the forces, let ankles, knees, hips and waist move subtly to soak up potential disturbances of acceleration and deceleration. ["Ski" magazine, November 1980]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
