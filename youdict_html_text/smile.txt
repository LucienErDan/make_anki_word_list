                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/smile">smile</a> 笑，微笑</span><p>来自中古英语 smilen,来自丹麦语 smile,来自 Proto-Germanic*smilijana,来自 PIE*smei,笑，高 兴，惊叹，词源同 smirk,miracle,marvel.并取代古英语词 smearcian,即 smirk.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/smile">smile</a></dt><dd><b>smile: </b><span>[13] The Old English word for ‘smile’ was <i>smearcian</i>, ancestor of modern English <i>smirk</i>. This was descended ultimately from the Indo- European base *<i>smei</i>-, which also produced Greek <i>meidos </i>‘laugh’, Sanskrit <i>smeras </i>‘smiling’, Latvian <i>smaidīt </i>‘smile’, and Russian <i>smejat’ sja </i>‘laugh’. <i>Smile</i>, which from the 13th century began to push <i>smirk </i>towards the more specialized sense ‘smile in a self-satisfied way’, comes from the same base, and was probably borrowed from a Scandinavian source (Swedish has <i>smila </i>and Danish <i>smile</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/smirk" target="_blank">smirk</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/smile">smile (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, perhaps from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">*smilen</span> or a Scandinavian source (such as Danish <span class="foreign">smile</span> "smile," Swedish <span class="foreign">smila</span> "smile, smirk, simper, fawn"), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*smil-</span>, extended form of PIE root <span class="foreign">*smei-</span> "to laugh, smile" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">smerian</span> "to laugh at, scorn," Old High German <span class="foreign">smieron</span> "to smile," Latin <span class="foreign">mirus</span> "wonderful," <span class="foreign">mirari</span> "to wonder"). Related: <span class="foreign">Smiled</span>; <span class="foreign">smiling</span>. &#13; <br /><br />&#13; Gradually pushed the usual Old English word, <span class="foreign">smearcian</span> (modern <a href="/etym/s/smirk" target="_blank">smirk</a>), into a specific, unpleasant sense. Of the eyes, from 1759. Figuratively, as indicating favor or encouragement, from c. 1400. Romance, Celtic, and Slavic languages tend to use a diminutive of the word for "laugh" to mean "smile" (such as Latin <span class="foreign">ridere</span> "laugh;" <span class="foreign">subridere</span> "smile"), perhaps literally "small laugh" or "low laugh."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/smile">smile (n.)</a></dt><dd>1560s, from <a href="/etym/s/smile" target="_blank">smile</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
