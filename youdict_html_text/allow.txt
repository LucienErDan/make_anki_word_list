                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/allow">allow</a> 准许，发津贴</span><p>1.前缀al-同ad-, 去，往. 词根laud-, 表扬，词源同laudable，表扬，许可。引申词义准许。</p><p> 2.前缀al-同ad-, 去，往. 词根loc-, 放置，分配，词源同locate，该词字母c脱落。引申词义补助的津贴。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/allow">allow</a></dt><dd><b>allow: </b><span>[14] <i>Allow </i>comes ultimately from two completely different Latin verbs, <i>allaudāre </i>and <i>allocāre</i>, which became blended in Old French <i>alouer</i>. The first, <i>allaudāre</i>, was based on <i>laudāre </i>‘praise’ (source of English <i>laud</i>, <i>laudable</i>, and <i>laudatory</i>); the second, <i>allocāre </i>(source of English <i>allocate </i>[17]) on <i>locāre </i>‘place’.<br><br>The formal similarity of the Latin verbs gradually drew their meanings closer together. The notion of ‘placing’, and hence ‘allotting’ or ‘assigning’, developed via the now obsolete ‘place to somebody’s credit’ to ‘take into account, admit’. Meanwhile, the idea of ‘praising’ moved through ‘commending’ or ‘approving’ to ‘accepting as true or valid’, and ultimately to ‘permitting’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/allocate" target="_blank">allocate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/laudable" target="_blank">laudable</a>, <a href="/etym/s/location" target="_blank">location</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/allow">allow (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., <span class="foreign">allouen</span>, "to commend, praise; approve of, be pleased with; appreciate the value of;" also, "take into account or give credit for," also, in law and philosophy, "recognize, admit as valid" (a privilege, an excuse, a statement, etc.). From late 14c. as "sanction or permit; condone;" in business use from early 15c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Middle English word is from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">alouer</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">aloer</span>, <span class="foreign">alloiier</span> (13c.) "allot, apportion, bestow, assign," from Latin <span class="foreign">allocare</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/allocate" target="_blank">allocate</a>). This word in Old French was confused and ultimately merged with <span class="foreign">aloer</span>; <span class="foreign">alloer</span> "to praise, commend," from Latin <span class="foreign">allaudare</span>, <span class="foreign">adlaudare</span>, compound of <span class="foreign">ad-</span> "to" (see <a href="/etym/s/ad-" target="_blank">ad-</a>) + <span class="foreign">laudare</span> "to praise" (see <a href="/etym/s/laud" target="_blank">laud</a>). From the first word came the sense preserved in <a href="/etym/s/allowance" target="_blank">allowance</a> as "money granted;" from the second came its meaning "permission based on approval."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Between the two primary significations there naturally arose a variety of uses blending them in the general idea of <span class="foreign">assign with approval</span>, grant, concede a thing claimed or urged, admit a thing offered, permit, etc., etc. [OED].&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Related: <a href="/etym/s/Allowed" target="_blank">Allowed</a>; <span class="foreign">allowing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">

Police warned motorists to <b>allow</b> extra time to get to work. </dt> <dd>警方告诫驾车者出门上班要留出富余时间。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3.
