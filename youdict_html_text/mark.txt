                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mark">mark</a> 标记，记号，斑点</span><p>来自Proto-Germanic*marko,来自PIE*merg,边界，界限，词源同march,margin.后引申词义界标，记号，作标记等多种词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mark">mark</a></dt><dd><b>mark: </b><span>English has two words <i>mark</i>, although they may be ultimately related. <i>Mark </i>‘sign, trace’ [OE] goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>markō</i>. This seems originally to have denoted ‘boundary’ (that is what Old English <i>mearc </i>meant, and related forms such as <i>march </i>‘border’ and <i>margin </i>still bear witness to it), but the notion of a ‘sign denoting a boundary’ seems to have led early on to the development of the word’s main present-day sense. <i>Remark </i>is closely related, as are <i>marquis </i>and <i>marchioness</i>, and <i>marquetry </i>[16], borrowed from French <i>marqueterie</i>, a derivative of <i>marque </i>‘mark’, denotes etymologically work that is ‘marked’ with patterns. <i>Mark </i>‘coin’ [OE] comes from medieval Latin <i>marcus </i>or <i>marca</i>, which may well derive ultimately from the ancestor of <i>mark </i>‘sign, trace’ (its etymological meaning being ‘mark on a piece of metal, constituting a coin’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/demarcation" target="_blank">demarcation</a>, <a href="/etym/s/march" target="_blank">march</a>, <a href="/etym/s/margin" target="_blank">margin</a>, <a href="/etym/s/marquetry" target="_blank">marquetry</a>, <a href="/etym/s/marquis" target="_blank">marquis</a>, <a href="/etym/s/remark" target="_blank">remark</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mark">mark (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"trace, impression," Old English <span class="foreign">mearc</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">merc</span> (Mercian) "boundary, sign, limit, mark," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*marko</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">merki</span> "boundary, sign," <span class="foreign">mörk</span> "forest," which often marked a frontier; Old Frisian <span class="foreign">merke</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">marka</span> "boundary, frontier," Dutch <span class="foreign">merk</span> "mark, brand," German <span class="foreign">Mark</span> "boundary, boundary land"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*merg-</span> "edge, boundary, border" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">margo</span> "margin;" Avestan <span class="foreign">mareza-</span> "border," Old Irish <span class="foreign">mruig</span>, Irish <span class="foreign">bruig</span> "borderland," Welsh <span class="foreign">bro</span> "district").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The primary sense is probably "boundary," which had evolved by Old English through "sign of a boundary," through "sign in general," then to "impression or trace forming a sign." Meaning "any visible trace or impression" first recorded c. 1200. Sense of "line drawn to indicate starting point of a race" (as in <span class="foreign">on your marks ...</span>) first attested 1887. The Middle English sense of "target" (c. 1200) is the notion in <a href="/etym/s/marksman" target="_blank">marksman</a> and slang sense "victim of a swindle" (1883). The notion of "sign, token" is behind the meaning "numerical award given by a teacher" (1829). Influenced by Scandinavian cognates.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mark">mark (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to put a mark on," Old English <span class="foreign">mearcian</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">merciga</span> (Anglian) "to trace out boundaries," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*markojan</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">merkja</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">markon</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">merkia</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">marchon</span>, German <span class="foreign">merken</span> "to mark, note," Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">merken</span>), from the root of <a href="/etym/s/mark" target="_blank">mark</a> (n.1).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Influenced by Scandinavian cognates. Meaning "to have a mark" is from c. 1400; that of "to notice, observe" is late 14c. Meaning "to put a numerical price on an object for sale" led to verbal phrase <span class="foreign">mark down</span> (1859). <span class="foreign">Mark time</span> (1833) is from military drill. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Marked" target="_blank">Marked</a>; <a href="/etym/s/marking" target="_blank">marking</a>. Old French <span class="foreign">merchier</span> "to mark, note, stamp, brand" is a Germanic loan-word.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mark">mark (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"unit of money or weight," late Old English <span class="foreign">marc</span>, a unit of weight (chiefly for gold or silver) equal to about eight ounces, probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">mörk</span> "unit of weight," cognate with German <span class="foreign">Mark</span>, probably ultimately a derivative of <a href="/etym/s/mark" target="_blank">mark</a> (n.1), perhaps in sense of "imprinted weight or coin." Used from 18c. in reference to various continental coinages, especially. the silver coin of Germany first issued 1875.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Mark">Mark</a></dt><dd>masc. proper name, variant of <a href="/etym/s/Marcus" target="_blank">Marcus</a> (q.v.). Among the top 10 names given to boy babies born in the U.S. between 1955 and 1970.</dd></dl></div></div>
