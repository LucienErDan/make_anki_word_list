                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/rush">rush</a> 匆忙，急促，冲</span><p>可能来自中古英语 ruschen,冲，匆忙，来自古英语 hryscan,惊吓，摇晃，最终来自 PIE*kors, 跑，急促，词源同 course,horse.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rush">rush</a></dt><dd><b>rush: </b><span>English has two words <i>rush</i>. The plantname [OE] goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>rusk</i>-, which also produced German and Dutch <i>rusch</i>, and may be related to Latin <i>restis </i>‘rush’. <i>Rush </i>‘hurry’ [14] goes back ultimately to Old French <i>ruser </i>‘drive back, detour’, source of English <i>ruse</i>. It reached English via Anglo- Norman <i>russher</i>, where until the 17th century it was used in its original sense ‘drive back, repulse’. The sense ‘hurry’ developed in Anglo- Norman, presumably from some association of the sound of the word with ‘hurrying’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/ruse" target="_blank">ruse</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rush">rush (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"a hasty driving forward," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/rush" target="_blank">rush</a> (v.). Sense of "mass migration of people" (especially to a gold field) is from 1848, American English. Football/rugby sense from 1857. Meaning "surge of pleasure" is from 1960s. <span class="foreign">Rush hour</span> first recorded 1888. <span class="foreign">Rush order</span> from 1896.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rush">rush (v.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c. (implied in <span class="foreign">rushing</span>), "to drive back or down," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">russher</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">ruser</span> "to dodge, repel" (see <a href="/etym/s/ruse" target="_blank">ruse</a>). Meaning "to do something quickly" is from 1650s; transitive sense of "to hurry up (someone or something)" is from 1850. U.S. Football sense originally was in rugby (1857).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Fraternity/sorority sense is from 1896 (originally it was what the fraternity did to the student); from 1899 as a noun in this sense. Earlier it was a name on U.S. campuses for various tests of strength or athletic skill between freshmen and sophomores as classes (1860).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rush">rush (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"plant growing in marshy ground," Old English <span class="foreign">resc</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">risc</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*rusk-</span> (cognates: Middle Low German <span class="foreign">rusch</span>, Middle High German <span class="foreign">rusch</span>, German <span class="foreign">Rausch</span>, West Frisian <span class="foreign">risk</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">rusch</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*rezg-</span> "to plait, weave, wind" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">restis</span> "cord, rope").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Old French <span class="foreign">rusche</span> probably is from a Germanic source. Used for making torches and finger rings, also strewn on floors when visitors arrived; it was attested a type of "something of no value" from c. 1300. See OED for spelling variations.</dd></dl></div></div>
