                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/seven">seven</a> 七</span><p>来自古英语 seofon,来自 Proto-Germanic*sebun,来自 PIE*septm,七，词源同 September,septet.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/seven">seven</a></dt><dd><b>seven: </b><span>[OE] The Indo-European term for ‘seven’ was *<i>septm</i>. This evolved into Latin <i>septem </i>(source of English <i>September</i>), Greek <i>heptá </i>(source of English <i>heptathlon </i>[20]), and Germanic *<i>sebun</i>. And *<i>sebun </i>in turn has diversified into German <i>sieben</i>, Dutch <i>zeven</i>, Swedish <i>sju</i>, Danish <i>syv</i>, and English <i>seven</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/heptathlon" target="_blank">heptathlon</a>, <a href="/etym/s/september" target="_blank">september</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/seven">seven (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">seofon</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sebun</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sibun</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">sjau</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">sju</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">syv</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">sowen</span>, <span class="foreign">siugun</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">seven</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zeven</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sibun</span>, German <span class="foreign">sieben</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">sibun</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*septm</span> "seven" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sapta</span>, Avestan <span class="foreign">hapta</span>, Hittite <span class="foreign">shipta</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">hepta</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">septem</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">sedmi</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">septyni</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">secht</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">saith</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Long regarded as a number of perfection (<span class="foreign">seven wonders</span>; <span class="foreign">seven sleepers</span>, the latter translating Latin <span class="foreign">septem dormientes</span>; <span class="foreign">seven against Thebes</span>, etc.), but that notion is late in Old English and in German a nasty, troublesome woman could be <span class="foreign">eine böse Sieben</span> "an evil seven" (1662).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Magical power or healing skill associated since 16c. with the <span class="foreign">seventh son</span> ["The seuenth Male Chyld by iust order (neuer a Gyrle or Wench being borne betweene)," Thomas Lupton, "A Thousand Notable Things," 1579]. The typical number for "very great, strong," as in <span class="foreign">seven-league boots</span> in the fairy story of Hop o'my Thumb. The <span class="foreign">Seven Years' War</span> (1756-63) is also the Third Silesian War.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The <span class="foreign">Seven Stars</span> (Old English <span class="foreign">sibunsterri</span>), usually refers to the Pleiades, though in 15c. and after this name occasionally was given to the Big Dipper (which also has seven stars), or the seven planets of classical astronomy. Popular as a tavern sign, it might also (with six in a circle, one in the center) be a Masonic symbol.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; FOOL: ... The reason why the &#13; <br />seven stars are no more than seven is a pretty reason. &#13; <br />LEAR: Because they are not eight?&#13; <br />FOOL: Yes, indeed: thou wouldst make a good fool.&#13; <br />["King Lear," I.v.]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
