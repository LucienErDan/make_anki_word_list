                    <div class="row"><h3 id="yd-word">fallow</h3></div>

美 ['fælo]</div>
                                        <div class="row" id="yd-word-meaning"><ul>
<li>adj. 休耕的；不活跃的</li>
<li>n. 休耕地；休耕</li>
<li>vt. 使（土地）休闲；潜伏</li>
<li>n. (Fallow)人名；(英)法洛</li>
</ul></div>

                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. perhaps from a derivation of PIE root *pel- "turn, fold", *pel-有多重含义，其中之一就是此意.<br />
2. 跟汉语的“翻耕土地”的发音差不多，主要指首辅音的发音一样。<br />
3. Originally "plowed land," then "land plowed but not planted".<br />
4. 该词的颜色含义是因为翻耕过来的土地呈淡黄色、淡棕色。黄土地、黄土地、黄土高原就是这么叫起来的，世界上绝大部分土地都应该是呈淡黄色。<br />
5. 形近词：wallow, tallow, swallow, shallow, sallow, hallow, callow, fallow.<br />
6. Fallow: 肥喽！土地使用过度会失去肥力，所以能够保持肥沃的土地一定是----休闲的土地，休闲的。让土地休养生息自然就肥喽。<br />
7. f (谐音“否、弗”) + allow => fallow: 不允许翻耕土地。<br />
8. 谐音“乏耨、罚耨、否耨、弗耨”------不准耨地。<br />
9. fold => fallow.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fallow">fallow</a> 休耕</span><p>来自PIE*pel, 转，折叠，词源同fold, ply. 原指犁过的土地，后指犁过但未耕种的土地，最后指现在的词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fallow">fallow</a></dt><dd><b>fallow: </b><span>English has two words <i>fallow</i>, both of considerable antiquity. <i>Fallow </i>‘uncultivated’ [OE] originally meant ‘ploughed land’. Its present-day adjectival meaning ‘ploughed but not sown’ or, more broadly, just ‘uncultivated’, developed in the 15th century. <i>Fallow </i>‘pale yellowish-brown’ [OE] (now used only in <i>fallow deer</i>) comes via Germanic *<i>falwaz </i>from Indo- European *<i>polwos</i>, a derivative of the base *<i>pol</i>-, *<i>pel</i>-, which also produced English <i>appal </i>[14] (originally ‘grow pale’), <i>pale</i>, and <i>pallid</i>.<br><br>Its Germanic relatives include German <i>fahl </i>‘pale, fawn’ and <i>falb </i>‘pale yellow’. (Germanic *<i>falwaz</i>, incidentally, was the ancestor of French <i>fauve </i>‘wild animal’, source of the term <i>fauvism </i>[20] applied to an early 20th-century European art movement that favoured simplified forms and bold colours.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/appal" target="_blank">appal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pale" target="_blank">pale</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pallid" target="_blank">pallid</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fallow">fallow (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, from Old English <span class="foreign">fealh</span> "fallow land," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*falgo</span> (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">felga</span> "harrow," German <span class="foreign">Felge</span> "plowed-up fallow land," East Frisian <span class="foreign">falge</span> "fallow," <span class="foreign">falgen</span> "to break up ground"), perhaps from a derivation of PIE root <span class="foreign">*pel-</span> (3) "to turn, fold." Assimilated since Old English to <span class="foreign">fallow</span> (adj.), according to OED probably because of the color of plowed earth. Originally "plowed land," then "land plowed but not planted" (1520s). As an adjective, from late 14c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fallow">fallow (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"pale yellow, brownish yellow," Old English <span class="foreign">fealu</span> "reddish yellow, yellowish-brown, tawny, dusk-colored" (of flame, birds' feet, a horse, withered grass or leaves, waters, roads), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*falwa-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">falu</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fölr</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">valu</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vaal</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">falo</span>, German <span class="foreign">falb</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*pal-wo-</span> "dark-colored, gray" (cognates: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">plavu</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">palvas</span> "sallow;" Greek <span class="foreign">polios</span> "gray" (of hair, wolves, waves), Sanskrit <span class="foreign">palitah</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">llwyd</span> "gray;" Latin <span class="foreign">pallere</span> "to be pale"), suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*pel-</span> (2) "pale" (see <a href="/etym/s/pallor" target="_blank">pallor</a>). It also forms the root of words for "pigeon" in Greek (<span class="foreign">peleia</span>), Latin (<span class="foreign">palumbes</span>), and Old Prussian (<span class="foreign">poalis</span>). Related: <span class="foreign">Fallow-deer</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
