                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/one">one</a> 一</span><p>来自古英语an,来自Proto-Germanic*ainaz,来自PIE*aino,一，词源同an,inch,unit.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/one">one</a></dt><dd><b>one: </b><span>[OE] <i>One </i>is the English member of an ancient and widespread family of ‘one’-words that goes back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>oinos</i>. This also produced Latin <i>ūnus </i>(ancestor of French <i>un </i>and Italian and Spanish <i>uno </i>and source of English <i>ounce</i>, <i>union</i>, <i>unit</i>, etc), Welsh <i>un</i>, Lithuanian <i>víenas</i>, Czech and Polish <i>jeden</i>, and Russian <i>odin</i>, all meaning ‘one’.<br><br>Its Germanic descendant was *<i>ainaz</i>, which has fanned out into German <i>ein</i>, Dutch <i>een</i>, Swedish and Danish <i>en</i>, and English <i>one</i>. In many languages the word is used as the indefinite article, but in English the numeral <i>one </i>has become differentiated from the article <i>a</i>, <i>an</i>. <i>One </i>lies behind <i>alone</i>, <i>atone</i>, and <i>only </i>(all of which preserve its earlier diphthongal pronunciation) as well as <i>once</i>, and its negative form is <i>none</i>.<br><br>The use of the word as an indefinite pronoun, denoting ‘people in general’, dates from the late 15th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/alone" target="_blank">alone</a>, <a href="/etym/s/atone" target="_blank">atone</a>, <a href="/etym/s/eleven" target="_blank">eleven</a>, <a href="/etym/s/inch" target="_blank">inch</a>, <a href="/etym/s/lonely" target="_blank">lonely</a>, <a href="/etym/s/none" target="_blank">none</a>, <a href="/etym/s/once" target="_blank">once</a>, <a href="/etym/s/only" target="_blank">only</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ounce" target="_blank">ounce</a>, <a href="/etym/s/union" target="_blank">union</a>, <a href="/etym/s/unit" target="_blank">unit</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/one">one (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, from Old English <span class="foreign">an</span> (adjective, pronoun, noun) "one," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ainaz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">einn</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">een</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">an</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">een</span>, German <span class="foreign">ein</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">ains</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*oi-no-</span> "one, unique" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">oinos</span> "ace (on dice);" Latin <span class="foreign">unus</span> "one;" Old Persian <span class="foreign">aivam</span>; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">-inu</span>, <span class="foreign">ino-</span>; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vienas</span>; Old Irish <span class="foreign">oin</span>; Breton <span class="foreign">un</span> "one").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Originally pronounced as it still is in <span class="foreign">only</span>, and in dialectal <span class="foreign">good 'un</span>, <span class="foreign">young 'un</span>, etc.; the now-standard pronunciation "wun" began c. 14c. in southwest and west England (Tyndale, a Gloucester man, spells it <span class="foreign">won</span> in his Bible translation), and it began to be general 18c. Use as indefinite pronoun influenced by unrelated French <span class="foreign">on</span> and Latin <span class="foreign">homo</span>.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">One and only</span> "sweetheart" is from 1906. <span class="foreign">One of those things</span> "unpredictable occurrence" is from 1934. Slang <span class="foreign">one-arm bandit</span> "a type of slot machine" is recorded by 1938. <span class="foreign">One-night stand</span> is 1880 in performance sense; 1963 in sexual sense. <span class="foreign">One of the boys</span> "ordinary amiable fellow" is from 1893. <span class="foreign">One-track mind</span> is from 1927. Drinking expression <span class="foreign">one for the road</span> is from 1950 (as a song title).</dd></dl></div></div>
