                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/milk">milk</a> 牛奶，挤奶</span><p>来自PIE*melg,挤奶，奶，词源同emulsion.可能来自PIE*mel,磨，磨软，软的，词源同mild,mill.由磨引申词义抚摸，刮，挤奶，奶。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/milk">milk</a></dt><dd><b>milk: </b><span>[OE] Far back into prehistory, <i>milk </i>traces its ancestry to an Indo-European base *<i>melg</i>-, which denoted ‘wiping’ or ‘stroking’. The way of obtaining milk from animals is to pull one’s hand down their teats, and so *<i>melg</i>- came in due course to be used for ‘milk’. It passed into Germanic as *<i>melk</i>-, which formed the basis of the noun *<i>meluks</i>, and this over the centuries has become German <i>milch</i>, Dutch and Danish <i>melk</i>, Swedish <i>mjölk</i>, and English <i>milk</i>.<br><br>The now virtually obsolete adjective <i>milch </i>‘giving milk’ [OE] (as in <i>milch cow</i>) goes back to a Germanic derivative of *<i>meluks</i>. Another derivative of Indo-European *<i>melg</i>was the Latin verb <i>mulgēre </i>‘milk’, which has given English <i>emulsion </i>and <i>promulgate</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/emulsion" target="_blank">emulsion</a>, <a href="/etym/s/promulgate" target="_blank">promulgate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/milk">milk (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">meoluc</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">milc</span> (Anglian), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*meluks</span> "milk" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">mjolk</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">melok</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">miluk</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">melk</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">miluh</span>, German <span class="foreign">Milch</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">miluks</span>), from <span class="foreign">*melk-</span> "to milk," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*melg-</span> "to wipe, to rub off," also "to stroke; to milk," in reference to the hand motion involved in milking an animal (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">amelgein</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">mulgere</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">mlesti</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">melžu</span> "to milk," Old Irish <span class="foreign">melg</span> "milk," Sanskrit <span class="foreign">marjati</span> "wipes off"). Old Church Slavonic noun <span class="foreign">meleko</span> (Russian <span class="foreign">moloko</span>, Czech <span class="foreign">mleko</span>) is considered to be adopted from Germanic.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Of milk-like plant juices from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Milk chocolate</span> is first recorded 1723; <span class="foreign">milk shake</span> is first recorded 1889, for a variety of creations, but the modern version is only from the 1930s. <span class="foreign">Milk tooth</span> (1727) uses the word in its figurative sense "period of infancy," attested from 17c. To <span class="foreign">cry over spilt milk</span> is first attested 1836 in writing of Canadian humorist Thomas C. Haliburton. <span class="foreign">Milk and honey</span> is from the Old Testament phrase describing the richness of the Promised Land (Num. xvi:13, Old English <span class="foreign">meolc and hunie</span>). <span class="foreign">Milk of human kindness</span> is from "Macbeth" (1605).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/milk">milk (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">melcan</span>, <span class="foreign">milcian</span>, <span class="foreign">meolcian</span> "to milk, give milk, suckle," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*melk-</span> "to milk" (cognates: Dutch <span class="foreign">melken</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">melchan</span>, German <span class="foreign">melken</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*melg-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/milk" target="_blank">milk</a> (n.)). Figurative sense of "exploit for profit" is first found 1520s. Related: <span class="foreign">Milked</span>; <span class="foreign">milking</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
