                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/weave">weave</a> 编织</span><p>来自PIE*webh,转，编织，词源同web,weft.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/weave">weave</a></dt><dd><b>weave: </b><span>English has two distinct verbs <i>weave</i>, but they have grown to resemble each other closely over the centuries. <i>Weave </i>‘make cloth’ [OE] goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>weben</i>, which also produced German <i>weben</i>, Dutch <i>weven</i>, Swedish <i>väva</i>, and Danish <i>voeve</i>. It was formed from the base *<i>web</i>-, *<i>wab</i>- (source also of English <i>wafer</i>, <i>web</i>, and <i>weft </i>[OE]), which in turn went back to Indo-European *<i>webh</i>-, *<i>wobh</i>-, probable source of English <i>wasp</i>. <i>Weave </i>‘take a zigzag course’ [13] was probably borrowed from Old Norse <i>veifa</i>.<br><br>This was descended from prehistoric Germanic *<i>weibjan</i>, which came from the Indo-European base *<i>weib</i>-, *<i>wib</i>- ‘move quickly’ (source also of English <i>vibrate</i>, <i>whip</i>, and <i>wipe</i>). At first it meant ‘move about, travel’ (‘Then the evil ghost fares out of the man and weaves wide … seeking rest’, 11th-century English poem). The notion of ‘moving from side to side, threading one’s way’ did not emerge until the 16th century, presumably through the influence of the other verb <i>weave</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/wafer" target="_blank">wafer</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wasp" target="_blank">wasp</a>, <a href="/etym/s/web" target="_blank">web</a>, <a href="/etym/s/weft" target="_blank">weft</a>; <a href="/etym/s/vibrate" target="_blank">vibrate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/whip" target="_blank">whip</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wipe" target="_blank">wipe</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/weave">weave (v.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">wefan</span> "to weave, form by interlacing yarn," figuratively "devise, contrive, arrange" (class V strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">wæf</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">wefen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*weban</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">vefa</span>, Middle Low German, Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">weven</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">weban</span>, German <span class="foreign">weben</span> "to weave"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*webh-</span> "to weave;" also "to move quickly" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">ubhnati</span> "he laces together," Persian <span class="foreign">baftan</span> "to weave," Greek <span class="foreign">hyphe</span>, <span class="foreign">hyphos</span> "web," Old English <span class="foreign">webb</span> "web").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The form of the past tense altered in Middle English from <span class="foreign">wave</span> to <span class="foreign">wove</span>. Extended sense of "combine into a whole" is from late 14c.; meaning "go by twisting and turning" is from 1640s. Related: <span class="foreign">Wove</span>; <span class="foreign">woven</span>; <span class="foreign">weaving</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/weave">weave (n.)</a></dt><dd>1580s, "something woven," from <a href="/etym/s/weave" target="_blank">weave</a> (v.). Meaning "method or pattern of weaving" is from 1888.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/weave">weave (v.2)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "to move from one place to another," of uncertain origin, perhaps from <a href="/etym/s/weave" target="_blank">weave</a> (v.1). From early 14c. as "move to and fro;" 1590s as "move side to side." Use in boxing is from 1818. Related: <span class="foreign">Weaved</span>; <span class="foreign">weaving</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
