                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/back">back</a> 背，后面</span><p>词源不详，可能来自拉丁词bacilum, 杆，指脊柱，竖背。与ridge, 山脊，横背相对应。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/back">back</a></dt><dd><b>back: </b><span>[OE] <i>Back </i>goes back to a prehistoric West and North Germanic *<i>bakam</i>, which was represented in several pre-medieval and medieval Germanic languages: Old High German <i>bah</i>, for example, and Old Norse <i>bak</i>. In most of them, however, it has been ousted by relatives of English <i>ridge</i>, originally ‘spine’ (such as German <i>rücken </i>and Swedish <i>rygg</i>), and only English retains <i>back</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bacon" target="_blank">bacon</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/back">back (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">bæc</span> "back," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bakam</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bak</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">bek</span>), with no known connections outside Germanic. In other modern Germanic languages the cognates mostly have been ousted in this sense ib words akin to Modern English <a href="/etym/s/ridge" target="_blank">ridge</a> (cognates: Danish <span class="foreign">ryg</span>, German <span class="foreign">Rücken</span>). Many Indo-European languages show signs of once having distinguished the horizontal back of an animal (or a mountain range) from the upright back of a human. In other cases, a modern word for "back" may come from a word related to "spine" (Italian <span class="foreign">schiena</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">spina</span>) or "shoulder, shoulder blade" (Spanish <span class="foreign">espalda</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">plecy</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To <span class="foreign">turn (one's) back on (someone or something)</span> "ignore" is from early 14c. <span class="foreign">Behind (someone's) back</span> "clandestinely" is from late 14c. To <span class="foreign">know (something) like the back of one's hand</span>, implying familiarity, is first attested 1893. The first attested use of the phrase is from a dismissive speech made to a character in Robert Louis Stevenson's "Catriona":&#13; <blockquote>&#13; If I durst speak to herself, you may be certain I would never dream of trusting it to you; because I know you like the back of my hand, and all your blustering talk is that much wind to me.&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The story, a sequel to "Kidnapped," has a Scottish setting and context, and <span class="foreign">the back of my hand to you</span> was noted in the late 19th century as a Scottish expression meaning "I will have nothing to do with you" [see Longmuir's edition of Jamieson's Scottish dictionary]. In English generally, the <span class="foreign">back of (one's) hand</span> has been used to imply contempt and rejection since at least 1300. Perhaps the connection of a menacing dismissal is what made Stevenson choose that particular anatomical reference.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/back">back (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 15c., "to move (something) back," from <a href="/etym/s/back" target="_blank">back</a> (adv.). Meaning "to support" (as by a bet) is first attested 1540s. Related: <span class="foreign">Backed</span>; <span class="foreign">backing</span>.&#13; &#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/back">back (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Middle English, from <a href="/etym/s/back" target="_blank">back</a> (n.) and <a href="/etym/s/back" target="_blank">back</a> (adv.). Formerly with comparative <span class="foreign">backer</span> (c. 1400), also <span class="foreign">backermore</span>. To be <span class="foreign">on the back burner</span> in the figurative sense is from 1960, from the image of a cook keeping a pot there to simmer while he or she works on another concoction at the front of the stove.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/back">back (adv.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., shortened from <span class="foreign">abak</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">on bæc</span> "backwards, behind, aback" (see <a href="/etym/s/back" target="_blank">back</a> (n.)). Adverbial phrase <span class="foreign">back and forth</span> attested from 1814.</dd></dl></div></div>
