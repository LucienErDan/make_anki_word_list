                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/needle">needle</a> 针，指针</span><p>来自PIE*sne-tla,针，来自*sne,缝纫，编织，词源同net,nettle,*tla,工具格后缀。引申词义缝衣针，指针等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/needle">needle</a></dt><dd><b>needle: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, a <i>needle </i>is a ‘sewing’ implement. The word comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>nēthlō </i>(source also of German <i>nadel</i>, Dutch <i>naald</i>, Swedish <i>nål</i>, and Danish <i>naal</i>), which was derived from an Indo- European base *<i>nē</i>- ‘sew’ (represented also in English <i>nerve </i>and <i>neural</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/nerve" target="_blank">nerve</a>, <a href="/etym/s/neural" target="_blank">neural</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/needle">needle (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">nædl</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*næthlo</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">nathla</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">nal</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">nedle</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">nadala</span>, German <span class="foreign">Nadel</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">neþla</span> "needle"), literally "a tool for sewing," from PIE <span class="foreign">*net-la-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*(s)ne-</span> "to sew, to spin" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">snayati</span> "wraps up," Greek <span class="foreign">nein</span> "to spin," Latin <span class="foreign">nere</span> "to spin," German <span class="foreign">nähen</span> "to sew," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">niti</span> "thread," Old Irish <span class="foreign">snathat</span> "needle," Welsh <span class="foreign">nyddu</span> "to sew," <span class="foreign">nodwydd</span> "needle") + instrumental suffix <span class="foreign">*-tla.</span><blockquote>To seke out one lyne in all hys bookes wer to go looke a nedle in a meadow. [Thomas More, c. 1530]</blockquote>Meaning "piece of magnetized steel in a compass" is from late 14c. (on a dial or indicator from 1928); the surgical instrument so called from 1727; phonographic sense from 1902; sense of "leaf of a fir or pine tree" first attested 1797. <span class="foreign">Needledom</span> "the world of sewing" is from 1847. <span class="foreign">Needle's eye</span>, figurative of a minute opening, often is a reference to Matt. xix:24.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/needle">needle (v.)</a></dt><dd>1715, "to sew or pierce with a needle," from <a href="/etym/s/needle" target="_blank">needle</a> (n.). Meaning "goad, provoke" (1881) probably is from earlier meaning "haggle in making a bargain" (1812). Related: <span class="foreign">Needled</span>; <span class="foreign">needling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
