            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ingrain">ingrain</a></dt><dd><b>ingrain: </b><span>[17] <i>Ingrain </i>means literally ‘work into the grain’ (of fabric, originally) – whence the main metaphorical sense of <i>ingrained</i>, ‘deepseated’. But there is much more to the story of <i>ingrain </i>than that. Its ultimate source was <i>engrainer </i>‘dye’, an Old French verb based on <i>graine </i>‘cochineal dye’. English borrowed this in the 14th century as <i>engrain </i>‘dye crimson with cochineal’, which remained a live sense of the word into the 17th century.<br><br>Gradually awareness of the word’s original specific connections with the colour crimson died out, and the verb was virtually formed anew in the mid 17th century using the concept of the <i>grain </i>or ‘texture’ of cloth, but the spelling <i>engrain </i>remained, and remains as a secondary variant to this day, to remind us of the word’s origins.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/grain" target="_blank">grain</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ingrain">ingrain (v.)</a></dt><dd>1766, see <a href="/etym/s/engrain" target="_blank">engrain</a>. Figurative use, of qualities, habits, etc., attested from 1851 (in <span class="foreign">ingrained</span>). Of dyed carpets, etc., 1766, from <span class="foreign">in grain</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
