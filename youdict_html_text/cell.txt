                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cell">cell</a> 细胞，小室</span><p>来自PIE*kel, 隐藏，遮盖，词源同cellar, ceiling, hole. 即隐藏的小室，细胞义因其小室状结构而得名。比较cytology, 细胞学。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cell">cell</a></dt><dd><b>cell: </b><span>[12] <i>Cell </i>has branched out a lot over the centuries, but its original meaning seems to be ‘small secluded room’, for it comes ultimately from an Indo-European base *<i>kel</i>-, which is also the source of English <i>conceal</i>, <i>clandestine</i>, and <i>occult</i>. It came into English either via Old French <i>celle </i>or directly from Latin <i>cella </i>‘small room, storeroom, inner room of a temple’, and at first was used mainly in the sense ‘small subsidiary monastery’.<br><br>It is not until the 14th century that we find it being used for small individual apartments within a monastic building, and the development from this to ‘room in a prison’ came as late as the 18th century. In medieval biology the term was applied metaphorically to bodily cavities, and from the 17th century onwards it began to be used in the more modern sense ‘smallest structural unit of an organism’ (the botanist Nehemiah Grew was apparently the first so to use it, in the 1670s).<br><br>A late Latin derivative of <i>cella </i>was <i>cellārium </i>‘group of cells, storeroom’; this was the source of English <i>cellar </i>[13], via Anglo-Norman <i>celer</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/apocalypse" target="_blank">apocalypse</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cellar" target="_blank">cellar</a>, <a href="/etym/s/clandestine" target="_blank">clandestine</a>, <a href="/etym/s/conceal" target="_blank">conceal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hall" target="_blank">hall</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hell" target="_blank">hell</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hull" target="_blank">hull</a>, <a href="/etym/s/occult" target="_blank">occult</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cell">cell (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 12c., "small monastery, subordinate monastery" (from Medieval Latin in this sense), later "small room for a monk or a nun in a monastic establishment; a hermit's dwelling" (c. 1300), from Latin <span class="foreign">cella</span> "small room, store room, hut," related to Latin <span class="foreign">celare</span> "to hide, conceal."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Latin word represents PIE root <span class="foreign">*kel-</span> (2) "to cover, conceal" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">cala</span> "hut, house, hall;" Greek <span class="foreign">kalia</span> "hut, nest," <span class="foreign">kalyptein</span> "to cover," <span class="foreign">koleon</span> "sheath," <span class="foreign">kelyphos</span> "shell, husk;" Latin <span class="foreign">clam</span> "secret;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">cuile</span> "cellar," <span class="foreign">celim</span> "hide," Middle Irish <span class="foreign">cul</span> "defense, shelter;" Gothic <span class="foreign">hulistr</span> "covering," Old English <span class="foreign">heolstor</span> "lurking-hole, cave, covering," Gothic <span class="foreign">huljan</span> "cover over," <span class="foreign">hulundi</span> "hole," <span class="foreign">hilms</span> "helmet," <span class="foreign">halja</span> "hell," Old English <span class="foreign">hol</span> "cave," <span class="foreign">holu</span> "husk, pod").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of monastic rooms extended to prison rooms (1722). Used in 14c., figuratively, of brain "compartments;" used in biology by 17c. of various cavities (wood structure, segments of fruit, bee combs), gradually focusing to the modern sense of "basic structure of living organisms" (which OED dates to 1845).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Electric battery sense is from 1828, based on original form. Meaning "small group of people working within a larger organization" is from 1925. <span class="foreign">Cell body</span> is from 1851; <span class="foreign">cell division</span> from 1846; <span class="foreign">cell membrane</span> from 1837 (but <span class="foreign">cellular membrane</span> is 1732); <span class="foreign">cell wall</span> from 1842.</dd></dl></div></div>
