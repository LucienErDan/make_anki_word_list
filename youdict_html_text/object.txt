                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/object">object</a> 目标，物体，反对</span><p>ob-,向前，朝向，相对，对着的，-ject,扔，投掷，放置，词源同project.即放置在前面的目标或物体。也用于指对着扔的，引申词义反对。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/object">object</a></dt><dd><b>object: </b><span><i>Object </i>the noun [14] and <i>object </i>the verb [15] have diverged considerably over the centuries, but they come from the same ultimate source: Latin <i>obicere</i>. This was a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>ob</i>- ‘towards’ and <i>jacere </i>‘throw’ (source of English <i>ejaculate</i>, <i>inject</i>, <i>subject</i>, etc), and hence originally meant literally ‘throw towards’, but by classical times it had been extended metaphorically to ‘place a hindrance in the way of, oppose’.<br><br>This was the strand of the word’s meaning taken up by English in the verb <i>object</i>, and also originally in the noun (‘how Christ answered to objects [that is, objections] of false Jews’, John Wycliffe 1380). The standard present-day meaning of the noun, however, comes from a post-classical meaning of Latin <i>objectum </i>(the noun formed from the past participle of <i>obicere</i>): ‘something put in someone’s way so that it can be seen’, hence a ‘visible object’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/ejaculate" target="_blank">ejaculate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/inject" target="_blank">inject</a>, <a href="/etym/s/jet" target="_blank">jet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/subject" target="_blank">subject</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/object">object (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "tangible thing, something perceived or presented to the senses," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">objectum</span> "thing put before" (the mind or sight), noun use of neuter of Latin <span class="foreign">obiectus</span> "lying before, opposite" (as a noun in classical Latin, "charges, accusations"), past participle of <span class="foreign">obicere</span> "to present, oppose, cast in the way of," from <span class="foreign">ob</span> "against" (see <a href="/etym/s/ob-" target="_blank">ob-</a>) + <span class="foreign">iacere</span> "to throw" (see <a href="/etym/s/jet" target="_blank">jet</a> (v.)). Sense of "thing aimed at" is late 14c. <span class="foreign">No object</span> "not a thing regarded as important" is from 1782. As an adjective, "presented to the senses," from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Object lesson</span> "instruction conveyed by examination of a material object" is from 1831.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/object">object (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "to bring forward in opposition," from Old French <span class="foreign">objecter</span> and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">obiectus</span>, past participle of <span class="foreign">obiectare</span> "to cite as grounds for disapproval, set against, oppose," literally "to put or throw before or against," frequentative of <span class="foreign">obicere</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/object" target="_blank">object</a> (n.)). Related: <span class="foreign">Objected</span>; <span class="foreign">objecting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
