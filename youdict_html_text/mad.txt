                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mad">mad</a> 发疯的</span><p>来自Proto-Germanic*maid,变坏，恶化，来自PIE*mei,改变，词源同mutual,miss.后引申词义心理失常的，发疯的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mad">mad</a></dt><dd><b>mad: </b><span>[13] The underlying etymological meaning of <i>mad </i>is ‘changed’. It goes back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>moitó</i>-, a past participial form based on *<i>moi</i>-, *<i>mei</i>-, ‘change’ (source also of Latin <i>mūtāre </i>‘change’, from which English gets <i>mutate</i>). Prehistoric Germanic inherited it, adding the collective prefix *<i>ga</i>- to form *<i>gamaithaz</i>, which passed into Old English as <i>gemād </i>‘insane’. From this was derived the verb <i>gemǣdan </i>‘madden’, whose past participle <i>gemǣded </i>eventually became a new adjective <i>gemǣdd</i>. By the Middle English period this had become <i>amadd</i>, and the reduced prefix <i>a</i>eventually disappeared, leaving <i>mad</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/mutate" target="_blank">mutate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mad">mad (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 13c., from Old English <span class="foreign">gemædde</span> (plural) "out of one's mind" (usually implying also violent excitement), also "foolish, extremely stupid," earlier <span class="foreign">gemæded</span> "rendered insane," past participle of a lost verb <span class="foreign">*gemædan</span> "to make insane or foolish," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ga-maid-jan</span>, demonstrative form of <span class="foreign">*ga-maid-az</span> "changed (for the worse), abnormal" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gimed</span> "foolish," Old High German <span class="foreign">gimeit</span> "foolish, vain, boastful," Gothic <span class="foreign">gamaiþs</span> "crippled, wounded," Old Norse <span class="foreign">meiða</span> "to hurt, maim"), from intensive prefix <span class="foreign">*ga-</span> + PIE <span class="foreign">*moito-</span>, past participle of root <span class="foreign">*mei-</span> (1) "to change" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">mutare</span> "to change," <span class="foreign">mutuus</span> "done in exchange," <span class="foreign">migrare</span> "to change one's place of residence;" see <a href="/etym/s/mutable" target="_blank">mutable</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Emerged in Middle English to replace the more usual Old English word, <span class="foreign">wod</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/wood" target="_blank">wood</a> (adj.)). Sense of "beside oneself with excitement or enthusiasm" is from early 14c. Meaning "beside oneself with anger" is attested from early 14c., but deplored by Rev. John Witherspoon (1781) as an Americanism. It now competes in American English with <a href="/etym/s/angry" target="_blank">angry</a> for this sense. Of animals, "affected with rabies," from late 13c. Phrase <span class="foreign">mad as a March hare</span> is attested from 1520s, via notion of breeding season; <span class="foreign">mad as a hatter</span> is from 1829 as "demented," 1837 as "enraged," according to a modern theory supposedly from erratic behavior caused by prolonged exposure to poison mercuric nitrate, used in making felt hats. For <span class="foreign">mad as a wet hen</span> see <a href="/etym/s/hen" target="_blank">hen</a>. <span class="foreign">Mad money</span> is attested from 1922; <span class="foreign">mad scientist</span> is from 1891.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mad">mad (adv.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/mad" target="_blank">mad</a> (adj.).</dd></dl></div></div>
