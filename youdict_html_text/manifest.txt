                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/manifest">manifest</a> 显现，展现</span><p>来自拉丁语manifestus,清晰的，明显的，抓个正着的，来自manus,手，词源同manual,-fest,击，打，抓住，词源同infest,offend.引申词义显现，展现。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/manifest">manifest</a></dt><dd><b>manifest: </b><span>[14] That which is <i>manifest </i>is etymologically ‘grasped by the hand’ – that is, ‘palpable, obvious’. The word comes via Old French from Latin <i>manifestus</i>. This was a later form of <i>manufestus</i>, a compound formed from <i>manus </i>‘hand’ and *<i>festus </i>‘gripped’. <i>Manifesto </i>[17] is a borrowing from Italian; it originally meant ‘evidence, proof’, and only gradually developed to the present-day ‘political statement’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/manifesto" target="_blank">manifesto</a>, <a href="/etym/s/manual" target="_blank">manual</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/manifest">manifest (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "clearly revealed," from Old French <span class="foreign">manifest</span> "evident, palpable," (12c.), or directly from Latin <span class="foreign">manifestus</span> "plainly apprehensible, clear, apparent, evident;" of offenses, "proved by direct evidence;" of offenders, "caught in the act," probably from <span class="foreign">manus</span> "hand" (see <a href="/etym/s/manual" target="_blank">manual</a>) + <span class="foreign">-festus</span> "struck" (compare second element of <a href="/etym/s/infest" target="_blank">infest</a>).&#13; <blockquote>Other nations have tried to check ... the fulfillment of our manifest destiny to overspread the Continent allotted by Providence for the free development of our yearly multiplying millions. [John O'Sullivan (1813-1895), "U.S. Magazine &amp; Democratic Review," July 1845]</blockquote>&#13; The phrase apparently is O'Sullivan's coinage; the notion is as old as the republic.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/manifest">manifest (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to spread" (one's fame), "to show plainly," from <a href="/etym/s/manifest" target="_blank">manifest</a> (adj.) or else from Latin <span class="foreign">manifestare</span> "to discover, disclose, betray" (see <a href="/etym/s/manifest" target="_blank">manifest</a> (adj.)). Meaning "to display by actions" is from 1560s; reflexive sense, of diseases, etc., "to reveal as in operation" is from 1808. Related: <span class="foreign">Manifested</span>; <span class="foreign">manifesting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/manifest">manifest (n.)</a></dt><dd>"ship's cargo," 1706; see <a href="/etym/s/manifest" target="_blank">manifest</a> (adj.). Earlier, "a public declaration" (c. 1600; compare <a href="/etym/s/manifesto" target="_blank">manifesto</a>), from French <span class="foreign">manifeste</span>, verbal noun from <span class="foreign">manifester</span>. Earlier still in English as "a manifestation" (1560s).</dd></dl></div></div>
