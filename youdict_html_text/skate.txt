                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/skate">skate</a> 溜冰，滑冰</span><p>来自荷兰语 schaats,溜冰，滑冰，可能借自古英语 sceacan,摇摆，滑动，词源同 shake.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/skate">skate</a></dt><dd><b>skate: </b><span>English has two words <i>skate</i>. The older is the fish-name [14], which was borrowed from Old Norse <i>skata</i>. <i>Skate </i>used for gliding over ice [17] comes from an Old French word for ‘stilt’ – <i>eschasse</i>. Its northern dialect form was <i>escase</i>. This was borrowed into English in the 16th century as the now obsolete <i>scatch </i>‘stilt’, and into Middle Dutch as <i>schaetse</i>, its meaning unaccountably changed to ‘skate’.<br><br>Its modern Dutch descendant <i>schaats </i>was borrowed into English as <i>scates</i>, which soon came to be regarded as a plural, and was ‘singularized’ to <i>skate</i>. <i>Eschasse </i>itself came from a Frankish *<i>skakkja</i>, a derivative of the verb *<i>skakan </i>‘run fast’, which in turn was descended from prehistoric Germanic *<i>skakan </i>(source of English <i>shake</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/shake" target="_blank">shake</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/skate">skate (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"type of flat, cartilaginous fish, a kind of ray," mid-14c., from a Scandinavian source such as Old Norse <span class="foreign">skata</span> "skate," Danish <span class="foreign">skade</span>, Faeroese <span class="foreign">skøta</span>, of unknown origin.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/skate">skate (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"ice skate," 1660s, <span class="foreign">skeates</span> "ice skates," from Dutch <span class="foreign">schaats</span> (plural <span class="foreign">schaatsen</span>), a singular mistaken in English for plural, from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">schaetse</span>. The word and the custom were brought to England after the Restoration by exiled followers of Charles II who had taken refuge in Holland.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Dutch word is from Old North French <span class="foreign">escache</span> "a stilt, trestle," related to Old French <span class="foreign">eschace</span> "stilt" (French <span class="foreign">échasse</span>), from Frankish <span class="foreign">*skakkja</span> "stilt" or a similar Germanic source (compare Frisian <span class="foreign">skatja</span> "stilt"), perhaps literally "thing that shakes or moves fast" and related to root of Old English <span class="foreign">sceacan</span> "to vibrate" (see <a href="/etym/s/shake" target="_blank">shake</a> (v.)). Or perhaps [Klein] the Dutch word is connected to Middle Low German <span class="foreign">schenke</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">scanca</span> "leg" (see <a href="/etym/s/shank" target="_blank">shank</a>). Sense alteration in Dutch from "stilt" to "skate" is not clearly traced. Sense in English extended to roller-skates by 1876. Meaning "an act of skating" is from 1853.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/skate">skate (v.)</a></dt><dd>1690s, "to ice-skate," from <a href="/etym/s/skate" target="_blank">skate</a> (n.2). U.S. slang sense of "to get away with something" is attested from 1945. Related: <span class="foreign">Skated</span>; <span class="foreign">skating</span>. A modern Latinate word for an ice-skating rink is <span class="foreign">glaciarium</span> (1876).</dd></dl></div></div>
