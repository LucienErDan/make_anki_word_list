                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/point">point</a> 点，论点，观点，目标，指向，用灰泥抹砖缝</span><p>来自古法语point,点，刺点，斑点，勾缝，来自拉丁语punctum,刺点，来自拉丁语pungere,刺，-ct,过去分词格，来自PIE*peug,刺，词源同pugnacious,puncture.并引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/point">point</a></dt><dd><b>point: </b><span>[13] ‘Sharp end’ is the etymological notion underlying <i>point</i>. For it comes ultimately from Latin <i>pungere </i>‘prick, pierce’ (source also of English <i>expunge</i>, <i>poignant </i>and <i>pungent</i>). The neuter form of its past participle, <i>punctum</i>, was used as a noun, meaning ‘small hole made by pricking, dot, particle, etc’ (it is the source of English <i>punctual</i>, <i>punctuation</i>, etc), which passed into Old French as <i>point</i>.<br><br>Then in the post-classical period a further noun was created, from the feminine past participle <i>puncta</i>, meaning ‘sharp tip’, and this gave Old French <i>pointe</i>. The two have remained separate in French, but in English they have coalesced in <i>point</i>. The Spanish descendant of Latin <i>punctum</i>, <i>punta</i>, has given English <i>punt </i>‘bet’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/compunction" target="_blank">compunction</a>, <a href="/etym/s/expunge" target="_blank">expunge</a>, <a href="/etym/s/poignant" target="_blank">poignant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/punctual" target="_blank">punctual</a>, <a href="/etym/s/punctuation" target="_blank">punctuation</a>, <a href="/etym/s/punt" target="_blank">punt</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/point">point (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "minute amount, single item in a whole; sharp end of a sword, etc.," a merger of two words, both ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">pungere</span> "prick, pierce, puncture" (see <a href="/etym/s/pungent" target="_blank">pungent</a>). The Latin neuter past participle <span class="foreign">punctum</span> was used as a noun, meaning "small hole made by pricking," subsequently extended to anything that looked like one, hence, "dot, particle," etc. This yielded Old French <span class="foreign">point</span> "dot; smallest amount," which was borrowed in Middle English by c. 1300.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meanwhile the Latin fem. past participle of <span class="foreign">pungere</span> was <span class="foreign">puncta</span>, which was used in Medieval Latin to mean "sharp tip," and became Old French <span class="foreign">pointe</span> "point of a weapon, vanguard of an army," which also passed into English, early 14c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The senses have merged in English, but remain distinct in French. Extended senses are from the notion of "minute, single, or separate items in an extended whole." Meaning "small mark, dot" in English is mid-14c. Meaning "distinguishing feature" is recorded from late 15c. Meaning "a unit of score in a game" is first recorded 1746. As a typeface unit (in Britain and U.S., one twelfth of a pica), it went into use in U.S. 1883. As a measure of weight for precious stones (one one-hundredth of a carat) it is recorded from 1931.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">The point</span> "the matter being discussed" is attested from late 14c.; meaning "sense, purpose, advantage" (usually in the negative, as in <span class="foreign">what's the point?</span>) is first recorded 1903. <span class="foreign">Point of honor</span> (1610s) translates French <span class="foreign">point d'honneur</span>. <span class="foreign">Point of no return</span> (1941) is originally aviators' term for the point in a flight "before which any engine failure requires an immediate turn around and return to the point of departure, and beyond which such return is no longer practical."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/point">point (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "indicate with the finger;" c. 1400, "wound by stabbing; make pauses in reading a text; seal or fill openings or joints or between tiles," partly from Old French <span class="foreign">pointoier</span> "to prick, stab, jab, mark," and also from <a href="/etym/s/point" target="_blank">point</a> (n.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Mid-15c. as "to stitch, mend." From late 15c. as "stitch, mend;" also "furnish (a garment) with tags or laces for fastening;" from late 15c. as "aim (something)." Related: <a href="/etym/s/Pointed" target="_blank">Pointed</a>; <span class="foreign">pointing</span>. To <span class="foreign">point up</span> "emphasize" is from 1934; to <span class="foreign">point out</span> is from 1570s.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. The turning <b>point</b> in the process of growing up is when you discover the core of strength within you that survives all hurt. </dt> <dd>当你从内心深处找到一种可以忍受一切痛苦的坚强力量时，你的成长历程就会出现飞跃。</dd> <p>来自金山词霸 每日一句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2.
