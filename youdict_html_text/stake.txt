                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stake">stake</a> 桩，木桩，木柱，刑柱，用桩划界，界分</span><p>来自古英语 staca,桩，钉，销子，来自 Proto-Germanic*stakon,棍，棒，柱子，来自 PIE*steg, 棍，棒，柱子，词源同 stack,stick.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stake">stake</a></dt><dd><b>stake: </b><span>[OE] <i>Stake </i>‘post’ comes ultimately from the prehistoric Germanic base *<i>stak</i>-, *<i>stek</i>-, *<i>stik</i>- ‘pierce, prick’, which also produced English <i>attach</i>, <i>stick</i>, <i>stockade</i>, etc. It may be that <i>stake </i>‘wager’ [16] is the same word, alluding to a supposed former practice of putting the object wagered (such as one’s shirt) on a post before the start of the contest.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/attach" target="_blank">attach</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stick" target="_blank">stick</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stockade" target="_blank">stockade</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stake">stake (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"pointed stick or post," Old English <span class="foreign">staca</span> "pin, stake," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stakon</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">stiaki</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">stage</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">stake</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">stake</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">staak</span>, German <span class="foreign">stake</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*steg-</span> (1) "pole, stick." The Germanic word has been borrowed in Spanish (<span class="foreign">estaca</span>), Old French (<span class="foreign">estaque</span>), and Italian <span class="foreign">stacca</span>) and was borrowed back as <a href="/etym/s/attach" target="_blank">attach</a>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "post upon which persons were bound for death by burning" is recorded from c. 1200. Meaning "vertical bar affixed to the edge of a platform of s truck, rail car, etc., to hold boards to keep the load from falling off" is from 1875; hence <span class="foreign">stake-body</span> as a type of truck (1907). In <span class="foreign">pull up stakes</span>, "The allusion is to pulling up the stakes of a tent" [Bartlett].</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stake">stake (v.1)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "to mark (land) with stakes," from <a href="/etym/s/stake" target="_blank">stake</a> (n.1). Hence, <span class="foreign">to stake a claim</span> (1857). Meaning "to maintain surveilance" (usually <span class="foreign">stake out</span>) is first recorded 1942, American English colloquial, probably form earlier sense of "mark off territory." Related: <span class="foreign">Staked</span>; <span class="foreign">staking</span>. Old English had <span class="foreign">stacung</span> "piercing of an effigy by a pin or stake" (in witchcraft); <span class="foreign">staccan</span> "pierce with a stake, spit."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stake">stake (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to risk, wager," 1520s, perhaps from notion of "post on which a gambling wager was placed" (see <a href="/etym/s/stake" target="_blank">stake</a> (n.2)), though Weekley suggests "there is a tinge of the burning or baiting metaphor" in this usage. Meaning "to maintain surveillance" (usually <span class="foreign">stake out</span>) is first recorded 1942, American English colloquial, probably form earlier sense of "mark off territory." Related: <span class="foreign">Staked</span>; <span class="foreign">staking</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stake">stake (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"that which is placed at hazard," 1530s, from <a href="/etym/s/stake" target="_blank">stake</a> (v.). Perhaps literally "that which is put up," from notion of "post on which a gambling wager was placed," though OED points out there is "no evidence of the existence of such a custom." Weekley suggests "there is a tinge of the burning or baiting metaphor" in this usage. Hence, "an interest, something to gain or lose" (1784). Plural <span class="foreign">stakes</span>, "sum of money to be won in a (horse) race," first recorded 1690s (compare <a href="/etym/s/sweepstakes" target="_blank">sweepstakes</a>). To <span class="foreign">have a stake in</span> is recorded from 1784.</dd></dl></div></div>
