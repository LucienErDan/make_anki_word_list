                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spell">spell</a> 拼写，拼读，符咒，咒语，魔力</span><p>来自古英语 spell,故事，叙述，寓言，来自 Proto-Germanic*spellam,说，告知，来自 PIE*spel, 说，念，吟诵，背诵。引申词义符咒，咒语，魔力等。词义拼写来自古法语同源词 espelir, 拼写，拼读，发音。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spell">spell</a></dt><dd><b>spell: </b><span>English has three distinct words <i>spell</i>, although two of them come from the same ultimate source. <i>Spell </i>‘name the letters of a word’ [13] was adapted from Old French <i>espeler </i>‘read out’. This was descended from an earlier *<i>espeldre</i>, which was borrowed from prehistoric Germanic *<i>spellōn</i>. And it was a noun relative of this, *<i>spellam</i>, which gave English <i>spell </i>‘magic formula’ [OE]. <i>Spell </i>‘period of time’ [16] may go back ultimately to Old English <i>spelian </i>‘substitute’; its original meaning was ‘replace someone else at a job’, and the main modern sense ‘period of time’ did not emerge, via ‘period of work’, until the 18th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spell">spell (v.1)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "read letter by letter, write or say the letters of;" c. 1400, "form words by means of letters," apparently a French word that merged with or displaced a native Old English one; both are from the same Germanic root, but the French word had evolved a different sense. The native word is Old English <span class="foreign">spellian</span> "to tell, speak, discourse, talk," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spellam</span> (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">spellon</span> "to tell," Old Norse <span class="foreign">spjalla</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">spillon</span> "to talk, tell"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*spel-</span> (2) "to say aloud, recite."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; But the current senses seem to come from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">espeller</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">espelir</span> "mean, signify, explain, interpret," also "spell out letters, pronounce, recite," from Frankish <span class="foreign">*spellon</span> "to tell" or some other Germanic source, ultimately identical with the native word.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Related: <span class="foreign">Spelled</span>; <a href="/etym/s/spelling" target="_blank">spelling</a>. In early Middle English still "to speak, preach, talk, tell," hence such expressions as <span class="foreign">hear spell</span> "hear (something) told or talked about," <span class="foreign">spell the wind</span> "talk in vain" (both 15c.). Meaning "form words with proper letters" is from 1580s. <span class="foreign">Spell out</span> "explain step-by-step" is first recorded 1940, American English. Shakespeare has <span class="foreign">spell (someone) backwards</span> "reverse the character of, explain in a contrary sense, portray with determined negativity."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spell">spell (n.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">spell</span> "story, saying, tale, history, narrative, fable; discourse, command," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spellam</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/spell" target="_blank">spell</a> (v.1)). Compare Old Saxon <span class="foreign">spel</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">spjall</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">spel</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">spill</span> "report, discourse, tale, fable, myth;" German <span class="foreign">Beispiel</span> "example." From c. 1200 as "an utterance, something said, a statement, remark;" meaning "set of words with supposed magical or occult powers, incantation, charm" first recorded 1570s; hence any means or cause of enchantment.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The term 'spell' is generally used for magical procedures which cause harm, or force people to do something against their will -- unlike charms for healing, protection, etc. ["Oxford Dictionary of English Folklore"]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Also in Old English, "doctrine; a sermon; religious instruction or teaching; the gospel; a book of the Bible;" compare <a href="/etym/s/gospel" target="_blank">gospel</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spell">spell (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"work in place of (another)," 1590s, earlier <span class="foreign">spele</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">spelian</span> "to take the place of, be substitute for, represent," related to <span class="foreign">gespelia</span> "substitute," of uncertain origin. Perhaps related to <span class="foreign">spilian</span> "to play" (see <a href="/etym/s/spiel" target="_blank">spiel</a>). Related: <span class="foreign">Spelled</span>; <span class="foreign">spelling</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spell">spell (n.2)</a></dt><dd>1620s, "a turn of work in place of another," from <a href="/etym/s/spell" target="_blank">spell</a> (v.2); compare Old English <span class="foreign">gespelia</span> "a substitute." Meaning shifted toward "continuous course of work" (1706), probably via notion of shift work (as at sea) where one man or crew regularly "spelled" another. Hence "continuous stretch" of something (weather, etc.), recorded by 1728. Hence also, via the notion in <span class="foreign">give a spell</span> (1750) "relieve another by taking a turn of work" came the sense "interval of rest or relaxation" (1845), which took the word to a sense opposite what it had at the start.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/spell">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. Drug experts say it could <b>spell</b> the end of the crack epidemic. </dt> <dd>禁毒专家说它可能会结束强效可卡因吸食泛滥的局面。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. A <b>spell</b> of poor health took the edge off her performance. </dt> <dd>一度身体欠佳令她表现有失水准。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. For many years sundials have cast their <b>spell</b> over scientists and mathematicians. </dt> <dd>多年来，日晷一直吸引着科学家和数学家们。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. How many times do I have to <b>spell</b> it out? </dt> <dd>我要仔仔细细讲多少遍？</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. Jacqueline is not relishing the prospect of another <b>spell</b> in prison. </dt> <dd>杰奎琳可不想再蹲一阵子监狱。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/spell">spell 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
