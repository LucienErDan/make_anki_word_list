                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sack">sack</a> 麻袋，购物袋，解雇，抢劫</span><p>来自拉丁语 saccus,袋子，来自希腊语 sakkos,袋子，来自某闪族语词，比较希伯来语 saq,袋 子。通常指比较大的袋子，引申词义麻袋，购物袋等，后引申比喻义抢劫及现代词义解雇， 开除，卷包袱走人。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack</a></dt><dd><b>sack: </b><span>English has three separate words <i>sack</i>, one of them now a historical relic and the other two ultimately related. <i>Sack </i>‘large bag’ [OE] was borrowed from Latin <i>saccus </i>(source also of English <i>sac</i>, <i>sachet</i>, and <i>satchel</i>). This in turn came from Greek <i>sákkos </i>‘rough cloth used for packing’, which was of Semitic origin (Hebrew has <i>saq </i>meaning both ‘sack’ and ‘sackcloth’).<br><br>The colloquial sense ‘dismissal from work’ (as in <i>get the sack</i>) arose in the early 19th century, perhaps from the notion of a dismissed worker going away with his tools or clothing in his bag. <i>Sack </i>‘plunder’ [16] came via French <i>sac </i>from <i>sacco </i>‘bag’, the Italian descendant of Latin <i>saccus</i>. This was used in expressions like <i>mettere a sacco</i>, literally ‘put in a bag’, which denoted figuratively ‘plunder, pillage’ (no doubt inspired by the notion of ‘putting one’s loot in a bag’). <i>Sack </i>‘sherry-like wine’ [16] (Sir John Falstaff’s favourite tipple) was an alteration of <i>seck</i>.<br><br>This was short for <i>wine sec</i>, a partial translation of French <i>vin sec </i>‘dry wine’ (French <i>sec </i>came from Latin <i>siccus </i>‘dry’, source of English <i>desiccate </i>[16]).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/sac" target="_blank">sac</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sachet" target="_blank">sachet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/satchel" target="_blank">satchel</a>; <a href="/etym/s/desiccate" target="_blank">desiccate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sec" target="_blank">sec</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"large bag," Old English <span class="foreign">sacc</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">sec</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">sæc</span> (Old Kentish) "large cloth bag," also "sackcloth," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sakkiz</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sak</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sac</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">sekkr</span>, but Gothic <span class="foreign">sakkus</span> probably is directly from Greek), an early borrowing from Latin <span class="foreign">saccus</span> (also source of Old French <span class="foreign">sac</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">saco</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">sacco</span>), from Greek <span class="foreign">sakkos,</span> from Semitic (compare Hebrew <span class="foreign">saq</span> "sack").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The wide spread of the word is probably due to the Biblical story of Joseph, in which a sack of corn figures (Gen. xliv). Baseball slang sense of "a base" is attested from 1913. Slang meaning "bunk, bed" is from 1825, originally nautical. The verb meaning "go to bed" is recorded from 1946. <span class="foreign">Sack race</span> attested from 1805.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"a dismissal from work," 1825, from <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (n.1), perhaps from the notion of the worker going off with his tools in a bag; the original formula was <span class="foreign">to give (someone) the sack.</span> It is attested earlier in French (<span class="foreign">on luy a donné son sac</span>, 17c.) and Dutch (<span class="foreign">iemand de zak geven</span>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (n.4)</a></dt><dd>"sherry," 1530s, alteration of French <span class="foreign">vin sec</span> "dry wine," from Latin <span class="foreign">siccus</span> "dry" (see <a href="/etym/s/siccative" target="_blank">siccative</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to plunder," 1540s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">sac</span>, in the phrase <span class="foreign">mettre à sac</span> "put it in a bag," a military leader's command to his troops to plunder a city (parallel to Italian <span class="foreign">sacco</span>, with the same range of meaning), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*saccare</span> "to plunder," originally "to put plundered things into a sack," from Latin <span class="foreign">saccus</span> "bag" (see <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (n.1)). The notion is probably of putting booty in a bag.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"plunder; act of plundering, the plundering of a city or town after storming and capture," 1540s, from French <span class="foreign">sac</span> "pillage, plunder," from Italian <span class="foreign">sacco</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (v.1)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"put in a bag," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Sacked</span>; <span class="foreign">sacking</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (v.4)</a></dt><dd>type of U.S. football play, 1969, from <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (v.1) in the sense of "to plunder" or <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (v.2) on the notion of "put in a bag." As a noun from 1972.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sack">sack (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"dismiss from work," 1841, from <a href="/etym/s/sack" target="_blank">sack</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Sacked</span>; <span class="foreign">sacking</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
