                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/blue">blue</a> 蓝色的</span><p>来自PIE * bhel, 燃烧，发光。词源同blank, black. 在古英语里这一PIE词表示各种颜色。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blue">blue</a></dt><dd><b>blue: </b><span>[13] Colour terms are notoriously slippery things, and <i>blue </i>is a prime example. Its ultimate ancestor, Indo-European *<i>bhlēwos</i>, seems originally to have meant ‘yellow’ (it is the source of Latin <i>flāvus </i>‘yellow’, from which English gets <i>flavine </i>‘yellow dye’ [19]). But it later evolved via ‘white’ (Greek <i>phalós </i>‘white’ is related) and ‘pale’ to ‘livid, the colour of bruised skin’ (Old Norse has <i>blá </i>‘livid’).<br><br>English had the related <i>blāw</i>, but it did not survive, and the modern English word was borrowed from Old French <i>bleu</i>. This was descended from a Common Romance *<i>blāvus</i>, which in turn was acquired from prehistoric Germanic *<i>blǣwaz </i>(source also of German <i>blau </i>‘blue’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/flavine" target="_blank">flavine</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blue">blue (1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">bleu</span>, <span class="foreign">blwe</span>, etc., from Old French <span class="foreign">blo</span> "pale, pallid, wan, light-colored; blond; discolored; blue, blue-gray," from Frankish <span class="foreign">*blao</span> or some other Germanic source, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*blæwaz</span> (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">blaw</span>, Old Saxon and Old High German <span class="foreign">blao</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">blaa</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">blå</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">blau</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bla</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">blauw</span>, German <span class="foreign">blau</span> "blue"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhle-was</span> "light-colored, blue, blond, yellow," from PIE root <span class="foreign">bhel-</span> (1) "to shine, flash" (see <a href="/etym/s/bleach" target="_blank">bleach</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The same PIE root yielded Latin <span class="foreign">flavus</span> "yellow," Old Spanish <span class="foreign">blavo</span> "yellowish-gray," Greek <span class="foreign">phalos</span> "white," Welsh <span class="foreign">blawr</span> "gray," Old Norse <span class="foreign">bla</span> "livid" (the meaning in <span class="foreign">black and blue</span>), showing the usual slippery definition of color words in Indo-European The present spelling is since 16c., from French influence (Modern French <span class="foreign">bleu</span>).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The exact color to which the Gmc. term applies varies in the older dialects; M.H.G. <span class="foreign">bla</span> is also 'yellow,' whereas the Scandinavian words may refer esp. to a deep, swarthy black, e.g. O.N. <span class="foreign">blamaðr</span>, N.Icel. <span class="foreign">blamaður</span> 'Negro' [Buck]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; Few words enter more largely into the composition of slang, and colloquialisms bordering on slang, than does the word BLUE. Expressive alike of the utmost contempt, as of all that men hold dearest and love best, its manifold combinations, in ever varying shades of meaning, greet the philologist at every turn. [John S. Farmer, "Slang and Its Analogues Past and Present," 1890, p.252]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The color of constancy since Chaucer at least, but apparently for no deeper reason than the rhyme in <span class="foreign">true blue</span> (c. 1500). From early times blue was the distinctive color of the dress of servants, which may be the reason police uniforms are blue, a tradition Farmer dates to Elizabethan times. For <span class="foreign">blue ribbon</span> see <span class="foreign">cordon bleu</span> under <a href="/etym/s/cordon" target="_blank">cordon</a>. <span class="foreign">Blue whale</span> attested from 1851, so called for its color. The flower name <span class="foreign">blue bell</span> is recorded by 1570s. <span class="foreign">Blue streak</span>, of something resembling a bolt of lightning (for quickness, intensity, etc.) is from 1830, U.S. Western slang.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Many Indo-European languages seem to have had a word to describe the color of the sea, encompasing blue and green and gray; such as Irish <span class="foreign">glass</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/Chloe" target="_blank">Chloe</a>); Old English <span class="foreign">hæwen</span> "blue, gray," related to <span class="foreign">har</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/hoar" target="_blank">hoar</a>); Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">sinji</span> "gray-blue, sea-green;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šyvas</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">sivyj</span> "gray."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blue">blue (2)</a></dt><dd>"lewd, indecent" recorded from 1840 (in form <span class="foreign">blueness</span>, in an essay of Carlyle's); the sense connection is unclear, and is opposite to that in <a href="/etym/s/blue_laws" target="_blank">blue laws</a> (q.v.). John Mactaggart's "Scottish Gallovidian Encyclopedia" (1824) containing odd words he had learned while growing up in Galloway and elsewhere in Scotland, has an entry for <span class="foreign">Thread o'Blue</span>, "any little smutty touch in song-singing, chatting, or piece of writing." Farmer ["Slang and Its Analogues Past and Present," 1890] offers the theory that this meaning derives from the blue dress uniforms issued to harlots in houses of correction, but he writes that the earlier slang authority John Camden Hotten "suggests it as coming from the French <span class="foreign">Bibliothèque Bleu</span>, a series of books of very questionable character," and adds, from Hotten, that, "Books or conversation of an entirely opposite nature are said to be Brown or Quakerish, i.e., serious, grave, decent."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blue">blue (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to make blue," c. 1600, from <a href="/etym/s/blue" target="_blank">blue</a> (1).</dd></dl></div></div>
