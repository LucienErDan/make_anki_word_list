                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/flux">flux</a> 变迁</span><p>来自拉丁语fluere, 流动，词源同fluent. 引申持续流动，改变，变迁。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flux">flux</a></dt><dd><b>flux: </b><span>[14] <i>Flux </i>denotes generally ‘flowing’, and comes from Latin <i>fluxus</i>, a derivative of the past participle of <i>fluere </i>‘flow’. This verb, similar in form and meaning to English <i>flow </i>but in fact unrelated to it, is responsible for a very wide range of English words: its past participle has given us <i>fluctuate </i>[17], its present participle <i>fluent </i>[16] and a spectrum of derived forms, such as <i>affluent</i>, <i>effluent </i>[18], and <i>influence</i>, and other descendants include <i>fluid </i>[15] (literally ‘flowing’, from Latin <i>fluidus</i>), <i>mellifluous </i>(literally ‘flowing with honey’), <i>superfluous </i>[15], and <i>fluvial </i>[14] (from Latin <i>fluvius </i>‘river’, a derivative of <i>fluere</i>).<br><br>Latin <i>fluxus </i>also produced the card-playing term <i>flush </i>[16].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/affluent" target="_blank">affluent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/effluent" target="_blank">effluent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fluctuate" target="_blank">fluctuate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fluent" target="_blank">fluent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fluid" target="_blank">fluid</a>, <a href="/etym/s/influence" target="_blank">influence</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mellifluous" target="_blank">mellifluous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/superfluous" target="_blank">superfluous</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flux">flux (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "abnormally copious flow," from Old French <span class="foreign">flus</span> "a flowing, a rolling; a bleeding" (Modern French <span class="foreign">flux</span>), or directly from Latin <span class="foreign">fluxus</span> (adj.) "flowing, loose, slack," past participle of <span class="foreign">fluere</span> "to flow" (see <a href="/etym/s/fluent" target="_blank">fluent</a>). Originally "excessive flow" (of blood or excrement), it also was an early name for "dysentery;" sense of "continuous succession of changes" is first recorded 1620s. The verb is early 15c., from the noun.</dd></dl></div></div>
