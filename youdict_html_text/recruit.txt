                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/recruit">recruit</a></dt><dd><b>recruit: </b><span>[17] Etymologically, a <i>recruit </i>is something that ‘grows again’. The word’s ultimate ancestor is Latin <i>recrēscere </i>‘regrow’, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>re</i>- ‘again’ and <i>crēscere </i>‘grow’ (source of English <i>crescent</i>, <i>increase</i>, etc). This passed into French as <i>recroître</i>, whose feminine past participle in the standard language was <i>recrue</i>. In the dialect of northeastern France, however, it was <i>recrute</i>, and it was this, used as a noun meaning ‘new growth’, hence ‘reinforcement of troops’, that gave English <i>recruit</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/crescent" target="_blank">crescent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/croissant" target="_blank">croissant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/increase" target="_blank">increase</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/recruit">recruit (v.)</a></dt><dd>1630s, "to strengthen, reinforce," from French <span class="foreign">recruter</span> (17c.), from <span class="foreign">recrute</span> "a levy, a recruit" (see <a href="/etym/s/recruit" target="_blank">recruit</a> (n.)). Sense of "to enlist new soldiers" is attested from 1650s; of student athletes, from 1913. Related: <span class="foreign">Recruited</span>; <span class="foreign">recruiting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/recruit">recruit (n.)</a></dt><dd>"military reinforcement, one of a newly raised body of troops," 1640s, from <span class="foreign">recruit</span> (v)., replacing earlier <span class="foreign">recrew</span>, <span class="foreign">recrue</span>; or from obsolete French <span class="foreign">recrute</span>, alteration of <span class="foreign">recreue</span> "a supply," <span class="foreign">recrue</span> "a levy of troops" (late 16c.), Picardy or Hainault dialect variant of <span class="foreign">recrue</span> "a levy, a recruit," literally "new growth," from Old French <span class="foreign">recreu</span> (12c.), past participle of <span class="foreign">recreistre</span> "grow or increase again," from <span class="foreign">re-</span> "again" (see <a href="/etym/s/re-" target="_blank">re-</a>) + <span class="foreign">creistre</span> "to grow," from Latin <span class="foreign">crescere</span> "to grow" (see <a href="/etym/s/crescent" target="_blank">crescent</a>). "The French word first appeared in literary use in gazettes published in Holland, and was disapproved of by French writers in the latter part of the 17th c." [OED]. The French word also is the source of Dutch <span class="foreign">recruut</span>, German <span class="foreign">Recrut</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">rekryt</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
