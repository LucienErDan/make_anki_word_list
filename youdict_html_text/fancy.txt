                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fancy">fancy</a> 幻想，想象</span><p>缩写自fantasy, 幻想，空想。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fancy">fancy</a></dt><dd><b>fancy: </b><span>[15] Ultimately, <i>fancy </i>is the same word as <i>fantasy </i>[15], from which it emerged by a process of contraction and gradually became differentiated in meaning. Both go back originally to the Greek verb <i>phaínein </i>‘show’ (source also of English <i>diaphanous </i>and <i>phenomenon</i>). From it was derived <i>phantázein </i>‘make visible’, which produced the noun <i>phantasíā </i>‘appearance, perception, imagination’ and its associated adjective <i>phantastikós </i>‘able to make visible’ (and also incidentally <i>phántasma</i>, from which English gets <i>phantasm </i>and <i>phantom</i>).<br><br>The noun passed into English via Latin <i>phantasia </i>and Old French <i>fantasie</i>, bringing with it the original Greek senses and also some others which it had picked up on the way, including ‘caprice’. The semantic split between <i>fantasy</i>, which has basically taken the road of ‘imagination’, and <i>fancy</i>, which has tended more to ‘capricious preference’, was more or less complete by about 1600.<br><br>The quasi- Greek spelling <i>phantasy </i>was introduced in the 16th century, and has persisted for the noun, although the contemporary <i>phantastic </i>for the adjective has now died out. The Italian form <i>fantasia </i>was borrowed in the 18th century for a fanciful musical composition. (<i>Fancy </i>and <i>fantasy </i>have no etymological connection with the superficially similar <i>fanatic</i>, incidentally, which comes ultimately from Latin <i>fānum </i>‘temple’.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/diaphanous" target="_blank">diaphanous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fantasy" target="_blank">fantasy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pant" target="_blank">pant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/phantom" target="_blank">phantom</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fancy">fancy (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-15c., <span class="foreign">fantsy</span> "inclination, liking," contraction of <a href="/etym/s/fantasy" target="_blank">fantasy</a>. It took the older and longer word's sense of "inclination, whim, desire." Meaning "the productive imagination" is from 1580s. That of "a fanciful image or conception" is from 1660s. Meaning "fans of an amusement or sport, collectively" is attested by 1735, especially (though not originally) of the prize ring. The adjective is recorded from 1751 in the sense "fine, elegant, ornamental" (opposed to <span class="foreign">plain</span>); later as "involving fancy, of a fanciful nature" (1800). <span class="foreign">Fancy man</span> attested by 1811.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fancy">fancy (v.)</a></dt><dd>"take a liking to," 1540s, a contraction of <span class="foreign">fantasien</span> "to fantasize (about)," from <a href="/etym/s/fantasy" target="_blank">fantasy</a> (n.). Meaning "imagine" is from 1550s. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Fancied" target="_blank">Fancied</a>; <span class="foreign">fancies</span>; <span class="foreign">fancying</span>. Colloquial use in <span class="foreign">fancy that</span>, etc. is recorded by 1813.</dd></dl></div></div>
