                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sap">sap</a> 植物的汁液</span><p>来自古英语 saepm,来自 Proto-Germanic*sapam,来自 PIE*sab,液体，汁液，来自 PIE*sap,品尝， 品味，词源同 savor,insipid.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sap">sap</a> 挖地道，挖坑道，削弱，破坏</span><p>来自中古法语 saper,挖地道，来自拉丁语 sappa,铲子，词源可能同 spade,铲子。其比喻义削 弱，破坏可能受上一词义影响。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sap">sap</a></dt><dd><b>sap: </b><span>English has three distinct words <i>sap</i>. The oldest, ‘plant-juice’ [OE], goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>sappam</i>, which also produced German <i>saft </i>‘juice’. This in turn was a descendant of Indo-European *<i>sapon</i>-, from which came Latin <i>sapa </i>‘new wine’. <i>Sap </i>‘undermine’ [16] was borrowed via French <i>saper </i>from Italian <i>zappare</i>, which may have been ultimately of Arabic origin.<br><br>Its original literal sense ‘dig a trench or tunnel underneath in order to attack’ has now been largely superseded by the metaphorical ‘weaken’, which has been heavily influenced by <i>sap </i>‘plant-juice’ (from the notion of ‘draining sap from a plant’). The colloquial <i>sap </i>‘fool’ [19] may be short for an earlier <i>sapskull</i>, a compound formed from <i>sap </i>in the now seldom heard sense ‘sapwood’ – hence ‘wooden head’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sap">sap (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"liquid in a plant," Old English <span class="foreign">sæp</span>m from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sapam</span> (cognates: Middle Low German, Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">sap</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">saf</span>, German <span class="foreign">Saft</span> "juice"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*sab-</span> "juice, fluid" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sabar-</span> "sap, milk, nectar," Latin <span class="foreign">sapere</span> "to taste," Irish <span class="foreign">sug</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">soku</span> "sap," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">sakas</span> "tree-gum"). As a verb meaning "To drain the sap from," 1725.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sap">sap (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"simpleton," 1815, originally especially in Scottish and English schoolboy slang, probably from earlier <span class="foreign">sapskull</span> (1735), <span class="foreign">saphead</span> (1798), from <span class="foreign">sap</span> as a shortened form of <span class="foreign">sapwood</span> "soft wood between the inner bark and the heartwood" (late 14c.), from <a href="/etym/s/sap" target="_blank">sap</a> (n.1) + <a href="/etym/s/wood" target="_blank">wood</a> (n.); so called because it conducts the sap; compare <a href="/etym/s/sappy" target="_blank">sappy</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sap">sap (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"dig a trench toward the enemy's position," 1590s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">saper</span>, from <span class="foreign">sappe</span> "spade," from Late Latin <span class="foreign">sappa</span> "spade" (source also of Italian <span class="foreign">zappa</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">zapa</span> "spade"). Extended sense "weaken or destroy insidiously" is from 1755, probably influenced by the verb form of <a href="/etym/s/sap" target="_blank">sap</a> (n.1), on the notion of "draining the vital sap from." Related: <span class="foreign">Sapped</span>; <span class="foreign">sapping</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sap">sap (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"hit with a sap," 1926, from <a href="/etym/s/sap" target="_blank">sap</a> (n.3). Related: <span class="foreign">Sapped</span>; <span class="foreign">sapping</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sap">sap (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"club, stick for hitting," 1899, from shortening of <span class="foreign">sapwood</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/sap" target="_blank">sap</a> (n.2)) or <a href="/etym/s/sapling" target="_blank">sapling</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
