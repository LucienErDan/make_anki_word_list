                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/under">under</a> 在下面</span><p>来自PIE*ndher,在下面，来自PIE*ni的扩大格，词源同nether, beneath.其中*dher为PIE*ther,*ter的变体形式，印欧语比较级后缀，词源同farther, other, interior, ulterior.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/under">under</a></dt><dd><b>under: </b><span>[OE] <i>Under </i>originated as a comparative form. It has been traced back to a prehistoric Indo-European *<i>ndhero</i>-, which meant ‘lower’, and is also the ultimate source of English <i>inferior </i>[15]. This passed into Germanic as *<i>unther</i>-, which has evolved into German <i>unter</i>, Dutch <i>onder</i>, and Swedish, Danish, and English <i>under</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/inferior" target="_blank">inferior</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/under">under (prep., adv.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">under</span> (prep.) "beneath, among, before, in the presence of, in subjection to, under the rule of, by means of," also, as an adverb, "beneath, below, underneath," expressing position with reference to that which is above, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*under-</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">under</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">onder</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">untar</span>, German <span class="foreign">unter</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">undir</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">undar</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ndher-</span> "under" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">adhah</span> "below;" Avestan <span class="foreign">athara-</span> "lower;" Latin <span class="foreign">infernus</span> "lower," <span class="foreign">infra</span> "below").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Productive as a prefix in Old English, as in German and Scandinavian (often forming words modeled on Latin ones in <span class="foreign">sub-</span>). Notion of "inferior in rank, position, etc." was present in Old English. With reference to standards, "less than in age, price, value," etc., late 14c. As an adjective, "lower in position; lower in rank or degree" from 13c. Also used in Old English as a preposition meaning "between, among," as still in <span class="foreign">under these circumstances</span>, etc. (though this may be an entirely separate root; see <a href="/etym/s/understand" target="_blank">understand</a>).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Under the weather</span> "indisposed" is from 1810. <span class="foreign">Under the table</span> is from 1921 in the sense of "very drunk," 1940s in sense of "illegal." To get something <span class="foreign">under (one's) belt</span> is from 1954; to keep something <span class="foreign">under (one's) hat</span> "secret" is from 1885; to have something <span class="foreign">under (one's) nose</span> "in plain sight" is from 1540s; to speak <span class="foreign">under (one's) breath</span> "in a low voice" is attested from 1832. To be <span class="foreign">under (someone's) thumb</span> "entirely controlled" (by that person) is recorded from 1754.</dd></dl></div></div>
