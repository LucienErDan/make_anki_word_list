                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/French">French</a> 法国的</span><p>来自Frank, 法兰克人。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/french">french</a> 口交</span><p>来自英国人对法国人开涮或其它原因。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/French">French (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">frensh</span>, <span class="foreign">frenche</span>, "pertaining to France or the French," from Old English <span class="foreign">frencisc</span> "French," originally "of the Franks," from <span class="foreign">franca</span>, the people name (see <a href="/etym/s/Frank" target="_blank">Frank</a>). A similar contraction of <span class="foreign">-ish</span> is in <a href="/etym/s/Dutch" target="_blank">Dutch</a>, <a href="/etym/s/Scotch" target="_blank">Scotch</a>, <a href="/etym/s/Welsh" target="_blank">Welsh</a>, suggesting the habit applies to the names of only the intimate neighbors.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In some provincial forms of English it could mean simply "foreign." Used in many combination-words, often dealing with food or sex: <span class="foreign">French dressing</span> (by 1860); <span class="foreign">French toast</span> (1630s); <span class="foreign">French letter</span> "condom" (c. 1856, perhaps on resemblance of sheepskin and parchment), <a href="/etym/s/french" target="_blank">french</a> (v.) "perform oral sex on," and <span class="foreign">French kiss</span> (1923) all probably stem from the Anglo-Saxon equation of Gallic culture and sexual sophistication, a sense first recorded 1749 in the phrase <span class="foreign">French novel</span>. (In late 19c.-early 20c., a <span class="foreign">French kiss</span> was a kiss on each cheek.) <span class="foreign">French-Canadian</span> is from 1774; <span class="foreign">french doors</span> is by 1847. To take <span class="foreign">French leave</span>, "depart without telling the host," is 1771, from a social custom then prevalent. However, this is said to be called in France <span class="foreign">filer à l'anglaise</span>, literally "to take English leave."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/french">french (v.)</a></dt><dd>"perform oral sex on," c. 1917, from <a href="/etym/s/French" target="_blank">French</a> (adj.), reflecting Anglo-Saxon equation of Gallic culture and sexual sophistication. Related: <span class="foreign">Frenched</span>; <span class="foreign">frenching</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/French">French (n.)</a></dt><dd>from Old English <span class="foreign">frencisc</span> (early Middle English <span class="foreign">frencisc</span>, <span class="foreign">frenscen</span>) "French person; the French nation," from the adjective (see <a href="/etym/s/French" target="_blank">French</a> (adj.)). From c. 1300 as "the French language." Euphemistic meaning "bad language" (<span class="foreign">pardon my French</span>) is from 1895. French <span class="foreign">Français</span> is from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">*francencis</span>, from <span class="foreign">francus</span> "a Frank" + nationality suffix <span class="foreign">-ensis</span> "belonging to" (see <a href="/etym/s/-ese" target="_blank">-ese</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
