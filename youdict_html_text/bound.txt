            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bound">bound</a> 边界，束缚，有义务，弹</span><p>1.弹，跳，拟声词，同bounce.</p><p> 2.边界，束缚，词源同bind.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound</a></dt><dd><b>bound: </b><span>English has no fewer than four separate words <i>bound</i>. The only one which goes back to Old English is the adjective, meaning ‘obliged’ or ‘destined’, which comes from the past participle of <i>bind </i>(in Old English this was <i>bunden</i>, which survives partially in ‘bounden duty’). Next oldest is the adjective meaning ‘going or intending to go’ [13]. Originally meaning ‘ready’, this was borrowed from Old Norse <i>búinn</i>, the past participle of <i>búa </i>‘prepare’, which derived from the same ultimate source (the Germanic base *<i>bū</i>- ‘dwell, cultivate’) as <i>be</i>, <i>boor</i>, <i>booth</i>, <i>bower</i>, <i>build</i>, <i>burly</i>, <i>bye-law</i>, and <i>byre</i>.<br><br>The final -<i>d </i>of <i>bound</i>, which appeared in the 16th century, is probably due to association with <i>bound </i>‘obliged’. Virtually contemporary is the noun <i>bound </i>‘border, limit’ [13]. It originally meant ‘landmark’, and came via Anglo-Norman <i>bounde </i>from early Old French <i>bodne </i>(source also of Old French <i>borne</i>, from which English got <i>bourn</i>, as in Hamlet’s ‘undiscovered country from whose bourn no traveller returns’).<br><br>Its ultimate source was medieval Latin <i>bodina</i>, perhaps from a prehistoric Gaulish *<i>bodina</i>. <i>Boundary </i>[17] seems to have been formed from the dialectal <i>bounder</i>, an agent noun derived from the verb <i>bound </i>‘form the edge or limit of’. Finally, <i>bound </i>‘leap’ [16] comes from Old French <i>bondir</i>. It originally meant ‘rebound’ in English (<i>rebound </i>[14] began as an Old French derivative of <i>bondir</i>), but this physical sense was a metaphorical transference from an earlier sense related to sound.<br><br>Old French <i>bondir </i>‘resound’ came from Vulgar Latin *<i>bombitīre </i>‘hum’, which itself was a derivative of Latin <i>bombus </i>‘booming sound’ (source of English <i>bomb</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/band" target="_blank">band</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bend" target="_blank">bend</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bind" target="_blank">bind</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bond" target="_blank">bond</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bundle" target="_blank">bundle</a>; <a href="/etym/s/be" target="_blank">be</a>, <a href="/etym/s/boor" target="_blank">boor</a>, <a href="/etym/s/booth" target="_blank">booth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bower" target="_blank">bower</a>, <a href="/etym/s/build" target="_blank">build</a>, <a href="/etym/s/burly" target="_blank">burly</a>, <a href="/etym/s/byre" target="_blank">byre</a>, <a href="/etym/s/neighbour" target="_blank">neighbour</a>; <a href="/etym/s/boundary" target="_blank">boundary</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bourn" target="_blank">bourn</a>; <a href="/etym/s/bomb" target="_blank">bomb</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rebound" target="_blank">rebound</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to form the boundary of," also "to set the boundaries of," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/bound" target="_blank">bound</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Bounded</span>; <span class="foreign">bounding</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to leap," 1580s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">bondir</span> "to rebound, resound, echo," from Old French <span class="foreign">bondir</span> "to leap, jump, rebound; make a noise, sound (a horn), beat (a drum)," 13c., ultimately "to echo back," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*bombitire</span> "to buzz, hum" (see <a href="/etym/s/bomb" target="_blank">bomb</a> (n.)), perhaps on model of Old French <span class="foreign">tentir</span>, from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*tinnitire</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound (adj.1)</a></dt><dd>"fastened," mid-14c., in figurative sense of "compelled," from <span class="foreign">bounden,</span> past participle of <a href="/etym/s/bind" target="_blank">bind</a> (v.). Meaning "under obligation" is from late 15c.; the literal sense "made fast by tying" is the latest recorded (1550s).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound (adj.2)</a></dt><dd>"ready to go," c. 1200, <span class="foreign">boun</span>, from Old Norse <span class="foreign">buinn</span> past participle of <span class="foreign">bua</span> "to prepare," also "to dwell, to live," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bowan</span> (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">buan</span> "to dwell," Old Danish <span class="foreign">both</span> "dwelling, stall"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bheue-</span> "to be, exist, dwell" (see <a href="/etym/s/be" target="_blank">be</a>). Final <span class="foreign">-d</span> is presumably through association with <a href="/etym/s/bound" target="_blank">bound</a> (adj.1).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"limit," c. 1200, from Anglo-Latin <span class="foreign">bunda</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">bonde</span> "limit, boundary, boundary stone" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">borne</span>), variant of <span class="foreign">bodne</span>, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">bodina</span>, perhaps from Gaulish. Now chiefly in <span class="foreign">out of bounds</span>, which originally referred to limits imposed on students at schools.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bound">bound (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"a leap, a springing," 1580s, from <a href="/etym/s/bound" target="_blank">bound</a> (v.2).</dd></dl></div></div>
