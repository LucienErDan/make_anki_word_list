                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. cat- + egor- "declaim (in the assembly)" + -y.<br />
2. 其本意为：speak against, accuse, assert, predicate.<br />
3. Original sense of "accuse" weakened to "assert, name" by the time Aristotle applied kategoria to his 10 classes of things that can be named.<br />
4. 由此进而引申为如今的：种类、范畴、类别。<br />
5. 该词的最初含义在categorical\categorically中还看得到、还保留着。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/category">category</a> 类别</span><p>cata-, 向下，相对。-agora, 广场，公开说，公开演讲，见allegory, 寓言。原指在广场发表演讲，叙述。后古希腊哲学家亚里士多德用该词描述类别，种类，使之固定为现在的词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/category">category</a></dt><dd><b>category: </b><span>[15] The word <i>category </i>has a rather complicated semantic history. It comes ultimately from Greek <i>katēgorein </i>‘accuse’, a compound formed from the prefix <i>katá</i>- ‘against’ and <i>agorá </i>‘public assembly’ (source of English <i>agoraphobia </i>and related to <i>gregarious</i>) – hence ‘speak against publicly’. ‘Accuse’ gradually became weakened in meaning to ‘assert, name’, and the derived noun <i>katēgoríā </i>was applied by Aristotle to the enumeration of all classes of things that can be named – hence ‘category’. The word reached English via late Latin <i>catēgoria </i>or French <i>catégorie</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/agoraphobia" target="_blank">agoraphobia</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gregarious" target="_blank">gregarious</a>, <a href="/etym/s/panegyric" target="_blank">panegyric</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/category">category (n.)</a></dt><dd>1580s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">catégorie</span>, from Late Latin <span class="foreign">categoria</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">kategoria</span> "accusation, prediction, category," verbal noun from <span class="foreign">kategorein</span> "to speak against; to accuse, assert, predicate," from <span class="foreign">kata</span> "down to" (or perhaps "against;" see <a href="/etym/s/cata-" target="_blank">cata-</a>) + <span class="foreign">agoreuein</span> "to harangue, to declaim (in the assembly)," from <span class="foreign">agora</span> "public assembly" (see <a href="/etym/s/agora" target="_blank">agora</a>). Original sense of "accuse" weakened to "assert, name" by the time Aristotle applied <span class="foreign">kategoria</span> to his 10 classes of things that can be named.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; <b>category</b> should be used by no-one who is not prepared to state (1) that he does not mean <span class="foreign">class</span>, &amp; (2) that he knows the difference between the two .... [Fowler]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
