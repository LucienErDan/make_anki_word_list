                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/diet">diet</a> 饮食</span><p>来自拉丁语dieta, 一日的工作，饮食，作息。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/diet">diet</a></dt><dd><b>diet: </b><span>[13] <i>Diet </i>comes, via Old French <i>diete </i>and Latin <i>diaeta</i>, from Greek <i>díaita </i>‘mode of life’. This was used by medical writers, such as Hippocrates, in the specific sense ‘prescribed mode of life’, and hence ‘prescribed regimen of food’. It has been speculated that Latin <i>diaeta</i>, presumably in the yet further restricted sense ‘day’s allowance of food’, came to be associated with Latin <i>diēs </i>‘day’.<br><br>This gave rise to medieval Latin <i>diēta </i>‘day’s journey’, ‘day’s work’, etc, hence ‘day appointed for a meeting’, and thus ‘meeting (of legislators)’. English acquired this word (coming orthographically full circle as <i>diet</i>) in the 15th century, but it is now mainly used for referring to various foreign legislatures.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/diet">diet (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"regular food," early 13c., from Old French <span class="foreign">diete</span> (13c.) "diet, pittance, fare," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">dieta</span> "parliamentary assembly," also "a day's work, diet, daily food allowance," from Latin <span class="foreign">diaeta</span> "prescribed way of life," from Greek <span class="foreign">diaita</span>, originally "way of life, regimen, dwelling," related to <span class="foreign">diaitasthai</span> "lead one's life," and from <span class="foreign">diaitan</span>, originally "separate, select" (food and drink), frequentative of <span class="foreign">*diainysthai</span> "take apart," from <span class="foreign">dia-</span> "apart" + <span class="foreign">ainysthai</span> "take," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ai-</span> (1) "to give, allot." Often with a sense of restriction since 14c.; hence <span class="foreign">put (someone) on a diet</span> (mid-15c.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/diet">diet (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"assembly," mid-15c., from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">dieta</span>, variant of <span class="foreign">diaeta</span> "daily office (of the Church), daily duty, assembly, meeting of counselors," from Greek <span class="foreign">diaita</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/diet" target="_blank">diet</a> (n.1)), but associated with Latin <span class="foreign">dies</span> "day" (see <a href="/etym/s/diurnal" target="_blank">diurnal</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/diet">diet (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to regulate one's diet for the sake of health," from Old French <span class="foreign">dieter</span>, from <span class="foreign">diete</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/diet" target="_blank">diet</a> (n.1)); meaning "to regulate oneself as to food" (especially against fatness) is from 1650s. Related: <span class="foreign">Dieted</span>; <span class="foreign">dieting</span>. An obsolete word for this is <a href="/etym/s/banting" target="_blank">banting</a>. The adjective in this sense (<span class="foreign">Diet Coke</span>, etc.) is from 1963, originally American English.</dd></dl></div></div>
