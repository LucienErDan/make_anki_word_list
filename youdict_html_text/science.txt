                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/science">science</a> 知识，科学</span><p>来自古法语 science,知识，学习，习得，来自拉丁语 scientia,知识，学识，专长，来自 scire, 知道，了解，字面意思即区分，鉴别，来自 PIE*skei,砍，切，劈，分开，词源同 shed,shin,scythe. 词义演变比较 intelligence,智能，智商，原义为选择和区分的能力。后词义专门化为科学。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/science">science</a></dt><dd><b>science: </b><span>[14] Etymologically, <i>science </i>simply means ‘knowledge’, for it comes via Old French <i>science </i>from Latin <i>scientia</i>, a noun formed from the present participle of the verb <i>scīre </i>‘know’. It early on passed via ‘knowledge gained by study’ to a ‘particular branch of study’, but its modern connotations of technical, mathematical, or broadly ‘non-arts’ studies did not begin to emerge until the 18th century. The derivative <i>scientist </i>was coined in 1840 by William Whewell: ‘We need very much a name to describe a cultivator of science in general. I should incline to call him a Scientist’, <i>Philosophy of the Inductive Sciences </i>1840.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/conscious" target="_blank">conscious</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/science">science (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "what is known, knowledge (of something) acquired by study; information;" also "assurance of knowledge, certitude, certainty," from Old French <span class="foreign">science</span> "knowledge, learning, application; corpus of human knowledge" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">scientia</span> "knowledge, a knowing; expertness," from <span class="foreign">sciens</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">scientis</span>) "intelligent, skilled," present participle of <span class="foreign">scire</span> "to know," probably originally "to separate one thing from another, to distinguish," related to <span class="foreign">scindere</span> "to cut, divide," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*skei-</span> "to cut, to split" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">skhizein</span> "to split, rend, cleave," Gothic <span class="foreign">skaidan,</span> Old English <span class="foreign">sceadan</span> "to divide, separate;" see <a href="/etym/s/shed" target="_blank">shed</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From late 14c. in English as "book-learning," also "a particular branch of knowledge or of learning;" also "skillfulness, cleverness; craftiness." From c. 1400 as "experiential knowledge;" also "a skill, handicraft; a trade." From late 14c. as "collective human knowledge" (especially "that gained by systematic observation, experiment, and reasoning). Modern (restricted) sense of "body of regular or methodical observations or propositions concerning a particular subject or speculation" is attested from 1725; in 17c.-18c. this concept commonly was called <span class="foreign">philosophy</span>. Sense of "non-arts studies" is attested from 1670s.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Science, since people must do it, is a socially embedded activity. It progresses by hunch, vision, and intuition. Much of its change through time does not record a closer approach to absolute truth, but the alteration of cultural contexts that influence it so strongly. Facts are not pure and unsullied bits of information; culture also influences what we see and how we see it. Theories, moreover, are not inexorable inductions from facts. The most creative theories are often imaginative visions imposed upon facts; the source of imagination is also strongly cultural. [Stephen Jay Gould, introduction to "The Mismeasure of Man," 1981]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; In <span class="foreign">science</span> you must not talk before you know. In art you must not talk before you do. In literature you must not talk before you think. [John Ruskin, "The Eagle's Nest," 1872]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The distinction is commonly understood as between theoretical truth (Greek <span class="foreign">episteme</span>) and methods for effecting practical results (<span class="foreign">tekhne</span>), but <span class="foreign">science</span> sometimes is used for practical applications and <span class="foreign">art</span> for applications of skill. To <span class="foreign">blind (someone) with science</span> "confuse by the use of big words or complex explanations" is attested from 1937, originally noted as a phrase from Australia and New Zealand.</dd></dl></div></div>
