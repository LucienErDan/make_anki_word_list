                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/odd">odd</a> 奇数的，怪异的，偶然发生的</span><p>来自古诺斯语oddi,第三或多出的，来自Proto-Germanic*uzdaz,向上刺的点，顶角，来自PIE*uzdho,刺，刺点。后引申词义奇数的，怪异的，偶然发生的等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/odd">odd</a></dt><dd><b>odd: </b><span>[14] The etymological idea underlying <i>odd </i>is of ‘pointing upwards’. Its ultimate ancestor is a prehistoric Indo-European *<i>uzdho</i>-, a compound formed from *<i>uz</i>- ‘up’ and *<i>dho</i>- ‘put, place’ (source of English <i>do</i>). From the notion of a ‘pointed vertical object’ developed ‘triangle’, which in turn introduced the idea of ‘three’ and ‘one left over from two’, hence ‘indivisible by two’. This is the meaning <i>odd </i>had when English borrowed it from Old Norse <i>oddi</i>, and the modern sense ‘peculiar’ (as if the ‘odd one out’) did not emerge until the late 16th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/do" target="_blank">do</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/odd">odd (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "constituting a unit in excess of an even number," from Old Norse <span class="foreign">oddi</span> "third or additional number," as in <span class="foreign">odda-maðr</span> "third man, odd man (who gives the casting vote)," <span class="foreign">odda-tala</span> "odd number." The literal meaning of Old Norse <span class="foreign">oddi</span> is "point of land, angle" (related via notion of "triangle" to <span class="foreign">oddr</span> "point of a weapon"); from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*uzdaz</span> "pointed upward" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">ord</span> "point of a weapon, spear, source, beginning," Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ord</span> "point, place," Dutch <span class="foreign">oord</span> "place, region," Old High German <span class="foreign">ort</span> "point, angle," German <span class="foreign">Ort</span> "place"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*uzdho-</span> (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">us-nis</span> "thistle"). None of the other languages, however, shows the Old Norse development from "point" to "third number." Used from late 14c. to indicate a surplus over any given sum.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "strange, peculiar" first attested 1580s from notion of "odd one out, unpaired one of three" (attested earlier, c. 1400, as "singular" in a positive sense of "renowned, rare, choice"). <span class="foreign">Odd job</span> (c. 1770) is so called from notion of "not regular." <span class="foreign">Odd lot</span> "incomplete or random set" is from 1897. The international order of <span class="foreign">Odd Fellows</span> began as local social clubs in England, late 18c., with Masonic-type trappings; formally organized 1813 in Manchester.</dd></dl></div></div>
