                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/beat">beat</a> 击打</span><p>来自PIE *bhau, bhat, 击，打。同bat, batter, butt.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/beat">beat</a></dt><dd><b>beat: </b><span>[OE] Old English <i>bēatan </i>and the related Old Norse <i>bauta </i>may be traced back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>bautan</i>. It has been conjectured that this could be connected with *<i>fu</i>-, the base of Latin <i>confūtāre </i>and <i>refūtāre </i>(source respectively of English <i>confute </i>[16] and <i>refute </i>[16]) and of Latin <i>fustis </i>‘club’ (from which English gets <i>fusty </i>[14]).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/beetle" target="_blank">beetle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/confute" target="_blank">confute</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fusty" target="_blank">fusty</a>, <a href="/etym/s/refute" target="_blank">refute</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/beat">beat (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">beatan</span> "inflict blows on, thrash" (class VII strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">beot</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">beaten</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bautan</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">bauta</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">bozan</span> "to beat"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bhau-</span> "to strike" (see <a href="/etym/s/batter" target="_blank">batter</a> (v.)). Of the heart, c. 1200, from notion of it striking against the breast. Meaning "to overcome in a contest" is from 1610s (the source of the sense of "legally avoid, escape" in <span class="foreign">beat the charges</span>, etc., attested from c. 1920 in underworld slang).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Past tense <span class="foreign">beat</span> is from c. 1500, probably not from Old English but a shortening of Middle English <span class="foreign">beted</span>. <span class="foreign">Dead-beat</span> (originally "tired-out") preserves the old past participle. Meaning "strike cover to rouse or drive game" (c. 1400) is source of <span class="foreign">beat around the bush</span> (1570s), the metaphoric sense of which has shifted from "make preliminary motions" to "avoid, evade." Command <span class="foreign">beat it</span> "go away" first recorded 1906 (though "action of feet upon the ground" was a sense of Old English <span class="foreign">betan</span>). To <span class="foreign">beat off</span> "masturbate" is recorded by 1960s. For <span class="foreign">beat generation</span> see <a href="/etym/s/beatnik" target="_blank">beatnik</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/beat">beat (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "a beating, whipping; the beating of a drum," from <a href="/etym/s/beat" target="_blank">beat</a> (v.). As "throb of the heart" from 1755. Meaning "regular route travelled by someone" is attested from 1731, also "a track made by animals" (1736), from the sense of the "beat" of the feet on the ground (late Old English), or perhaps that in <span class="foreign">beat the bushes</span> to flush game (c. 1400), or <span class="foreign">beat the bounds</span> (1560s). Extended to journalism by 1875. Musical sense is by 1842, perhaps from the motion of the conductor and the notion of "beating the time":&#13; <blockquote>&#13; It is usual, in beating the time of a piece of music, to mark or signalize the commencement of every measure by a downward movement or beat of the hand, or of any other article that may be used for the purpose .... ["Godfrey Weber's General Music Teacher," 1842]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Earlier in music it meant a sort of grace note:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; BEAT, in music, a transient grace note, struck immediately before the note it is intended to ornament. The beat always lies half a note beneath its principal, and should be heard so closely upon it, that they may almost seem to be struck together. ["The British Encyclopedia," London, 1809]&#13; </blockquote>&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/beat">beat (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"defeated, overcome by effort," c. 1400, from past tense of <a href="/etym/s/beat" target="_blank">beat</a> (v.). Meaning "tired, exhausted," is by 1905, American English.</dd></dl></div></div>
