                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bull">bull</a> 公牛，教皇训谕</span><p>1.公牛，来自词根bov, 牛，见bovine. 或来自PIE * bhel, 膨胀，见ball. </p><p> 2.教皇训谕，来自用封蜡（形似小球）密封的教皇下发给下属教会的文件，词源同bill.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bull">bull</a></dt><dd><b>bull: </b><span>There are three distinct words <i>bull </i>in English. The oldest is the animal name, which first appears in late Old English as <i>bula</i>. Related forms occur in other Germanic languages, including German <i>bulle </i>and Dutch <i>bul</i>. The diminutive <i>bullock </i>is also recorded in late Old English. The second <i>bull </i>is ‘edict’ [13], as in ‘papal bull’. This comes from medieval Latin <i>bulla </i>‘sealed document’, a development of an earlier sense ‘seal’, which can be traced back to classical Latin <i>bulla </i>‘bubble’ (source also of English <i>bowl</i>, as in the game of bowls; of <i>boil </i>‘heat liquid’; of <i>budge </i>[16], via Old French <i>bouger </i>and Vulgar Latin *<i>bullicāre </i>‘bubble up, boil’; and probably of <i>bill </i>‘statement of charges’).<br><br>And finally there is ‘ludicrous or selfcontradictory statement’ [17], usually now in the phrase <i>Irish bull</i>, whose origins are mysterious; there may be a connection with the Middle English noun <i>bul </i>‘falsehood’ and the 15th-to 17th-century verb <i>bull </i>‘mock, cheat’, which has been linked with Old French <i>boler </i>or <i>bouller </i>‘deceive’. The source of the modern colloquial senses ‘nonsense’ and ‘excessive discipline’ is not clear.<br><br>Both are early 20th-century, and closely associated with the synonymous and contemporary <i>bullshit</i>, suggesting a conscious link with <i>bull </i>the animal. In meaning, however, the first at least is closer to <i>bull </i>‘ludicrous statement’. <i>Bull’s-eye </i>‘centre of a target’ and ‘large sweet’ are both early 19th-century. <i>Bulldoze </i>is from 1870s America, and was apparently originally applied to the punishment of recalcitrant black slaves; it has been conjectured that the underlying connotation was of ‘giving someone a dose fit for a bull’.<br><br>The term <i>bulldozer </i>was applied to the vehicle in the 1930s.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/phallic" target="_blank">phallic</a>; <a href="/etym/s/bill" target="_blank">bill</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bowl" target="_blank">bowl</a>, <a href="/etym/s/budge" target="_blank">budge</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bull">bull (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"bovine male animal," from Old English <span class="foreign">bula</span> "a bull, a steer," or Old Norse <span class="foreign">boli</span> "bull," both from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bullon-</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bulle</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">bul</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bulle</span>), perhaps from a Germanic verbal stem meaning "to roar," which survives in some German dialects and perhaps in the first element of <a href="/etym/s/boulder" target="_blank">boulder</a> (q.v.). The other possibility [Watkins] is that the Germanic root is from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhln-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*bhel-</span> (2) "to blow, inflate, swell" (see <a href="/etym/s/bole" target="_blank">bole</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; An uncastrated male, reared for breeding, as opposed to a <span class="foreign">bullock</span> or <span class="foreign">steer</span>. Extended after 1610s to males of other large animals (elephant, alligator, whale, etc.). Stock market sense is from 1714 (see <a href="/etym/s/bear" target="_blank">bear</a> (n.)). Meaning "policeman" attested by 1859. Figurative phrase <span class="foreign">to take the bull by the horns</span> first recorded 1711. To be a <span class="foreign">bull in a china shop</span>, figurative of careless and inappropriate use of force, attested from 1812 and was the title of a popular humorous song in 1820s England. <span class="foreign">Bull-baiting</span> attested from 1570s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bull">bull (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"papal edict," c. 1300, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">bulla</span> "sealed document" (source of Old French <span class="foreign">bulle</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">bulla</span>), originally the word for the seal itself, from Latin <span class="foreign">bulla</span> "round swelling, knob," said ultimately to be from Gaulish, from PIE <span class="foreign">*beu-</span>, a root supposed to have formed words associated with swelling (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">bule</span> "buttocks," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">puyl</span> "bag," also possibly Latin <span class="foreign">bucca</span> "cheek").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bull">bull (v.)</a></dt><dd>"push through roughly," 1884, from <a href="/etym/s/bull" target="_blank">bull</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Bulled</span>; <span class="foreign">bulling</span>.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bull">bull (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"false talk, fraud," Middle English, apparently from Old French <span class="foreign">bole</span> "deception, trick, scheming, intrigue," and perhaps connected to modern Icelandic <span class="foreign">bull</span> "nonsense."&#13; <blockquote>Sais christ to ypocrites ... yee ar ... all ful with wickednes, tresun and bull. ["Cursor Mundi," early 14c.]</blockquote>&#13; There also was a verb <span class="foreign">bull</span> meaning "to mock, cheat," which dates from 1530s.</dd></dl></div></div>
