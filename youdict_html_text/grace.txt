                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/grace">grace</a> 优雅，恩宠</span><p>来自PIE*gwere, 恩惠，眷顾，进一步来自PIE*gher, 神恩，鼓励，词源同charisma, exhort. 原指神的恩宠，好感，感谢，神赐予的美德，美丽，后词义通用化。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grace">grace</a></dt><dd><b>grace: </b><span>[12] Latin <i>grātus </i>meant ‘pleasing’. Its most obvious English descendants are <i>grateful</i>, <i>gratify</i>, <i>gratuity</i>, etc, but it is also responsible for <i>grace </i>(not to mention the even better disguised <i>agree</i>). Its derived noun <i>grātia </i>‘pleasure, favour, thanks’ passed into English via Old French <i>grace</i>. <i>Gracious </i>[13] comes ultimately from Latin <i>grātiōsus</i>; <i>grateful </i>[15] is an English formation. (The apparently similar <i>gracile </i>‘slender’ [17], incidentally, is not etymologically related; it comes from Latin <i>gracilis </i>‘slender’.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/agree" target="_blank">agree</a>, <a href="/etym/s/grateful" target="_blank">grateful</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Grace">Grace</a></dt><dd>fem. proper name, literally "favor, grace;" see <a href="/etym/s/grace" target="_blank">grace</a> (n.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grace">grace (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 12c., "God's unmerited favor, love, or help," from Old French <span class="foreign">grace</span> "pardon, divine grace, mercy; favor, thanks; elegance, virtue" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">grâce</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">gratia</span> "favor, esteem, regard; pleasing quality, good will, gratitude" (source of Italian <span class="foreign">grazia</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">gracia</span>; in Church use translating Greek <span class="foreign">kharisma</span>), from <span class="foreign">gratus</span> "pleasing, agreeable," from PIE <span class="foreign">*gwreto-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*gwere-</span> (3) "to favor" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">grnati</span> "sings, praises, announces," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">giriu</span> "to praise, celebrate," Avestan <span class="foreign">gar-</span> "to praise").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "virtue" is early 14c., that of "beauty of form or movement, pleasing quality" is mid-14c. In classical sense, "one of the three sister goddesses (Latin <span class="foreign">Gratiæ</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">Kharites</span>), bestowers of beauty and charm," it is first recorded in English 1579 in Spenser. In music, "an embellishment not essential to the melody or harmony," 1650s. As the name of the short prayer that is said before or after a meal (early 13c.; until 16c. usually <span class="foreign">graces</span>) has a sense of "gratitude." As a title of honor, c. 1500.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grace">grace (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "to thank," from Old French <span class="foreign">graciier</span> "thank, give thanks to; praise," from <span class="foreign">grace</span> "mercy, favor, thanks, virtue" (see <a href="/etym/s/grace" target="_blank">grace</a> (n.)). Meaning "to show favor" (mid-15c.) led to that of "to lend or add grace to something" (1580s, as in <span class="foreign">grace us with your presence</span>), which is the root of the musical sense in <span class="foreign">grace</span> notes (1650s). Related: <span class="foreign">Graced</span>; <span class="foreign">gracing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
