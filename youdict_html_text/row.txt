                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/row">row</a> 排，行，划船</span><p>1.来自古英语 raew,排，行，系列，词源不确定，可能最终来自 PIE*rei,刮，砍，劈，词源同 rift,river. 2.划船，来自古英语 rowan,划船，来自 Proto-Germanic*ro,划船，来自 PIE*ere,划船，词源同 rudder.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/row">row</a></dt><dd><b>row: </b><span>There are three distinct words <i>row </i>in English. The one meaning ‘use oars’ [OE] goes back to a prehistoric Germanic base *<i>rō</i>- ‘steer’, which also produced Dutch <i>roeijen </i>and Swedish <i>ro</i>, not to mention English <i>rudder</i>. <i>Row </i>‘orderly line’ [OE] comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>raigwa</i>, and is probably related to German <i>reihe </i>‘row’. <i>Row </i>‘noisy quarrel’ [18] seems to have originated in the late 18th century as a piece of Cambridge University slang, but where it came from is not known.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/rudder" target="_blank">rudder</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/row">row (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"line of people or things," Old English <span class="foreign">ræw</span> "a row, line; succession, hedge-row," probably from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*rai(h)waz</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">rie</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">rij</span> "row;" Old High German <span class="foreign">rihan</span> "to thread," <span class="foreign">riga</span> "line;" German <span class="foreign">Reihe</span> "row, line, series;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">rega</span> "string"), possibly from PIE root <span class="foreign">*rei-</span> "to scratch, tear, cut" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">rikhati</span> "scratches," <span class="foreign">rekha</span> "line"). Meaning "a number of houses in a line" is attested from mid-15c., originally chiefly Scottish and northern English. Phrase <span class="foreign">a hard row to hoe</span> attested from 1823, American English.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/row">row (v.)</a></dt><dd>"propel with oars," Old English <span class="foreign">rowan</span> "go by water, row" (class VII strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">reow</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">rowen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ro-</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">roa</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">roeien</span>, West Frisian <span class="foreign">roeije</span>, Middle High German <span class="foreign">rüejen</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ere-</span> (1) "to row" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">aritrah</span> "oar;" Greek <span class="foreign">eressein</span> "to row," <span class="foreign">eretmon</span> "oar," <span class="foreign">trieres</span> "trireme;" Latin <span class="foreign">remus</span> "oar;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">iriu</span> "to row," <span class="foreign">irklas</span> "oar;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">rome</span> "oar," Old English <span class="foreign">roðor</span> "rudder").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/row">row (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"noisy commotion," 1746, Cambridge University slang, of uncertain origin, perhaps related to <span class="foreign">rousel</span> "drinking bout" (c. 1600), a shortened form of <a href="/etym/s/carousal" target="_blank">carousal</a>. Klein suggests a back-formation from <a href="/etym/s/rouse" target="_blank">rouse</a> (n.), mistaken as a plural (compare <span class="foreign">pea</span> from <span class="foreign">pease</span>).</dd></dl></div></div>
