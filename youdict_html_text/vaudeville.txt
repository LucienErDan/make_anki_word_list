                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/vaudeville">vaudeville</a> 歌舞杂耍表演</span><p>词源有争议，通常认为来自法语voix de ville, 即village voice，原指村庄或山谷里面的娱乐表演，引申词义歌舞杂耍表演等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/vaudeville">vaudeville</a></dt><dd><b>vaudeville: </b><span>[18] In 15th-century France there was a fashion for songs from the valley of the Vire, in the Calvados region of Normandy (particularly popular, apparently, were the satirical songs composed by a local fuller, Olivier Basselin). They were known as <i>chansons du Vau de Vire </i>‘songs of the valley of the Vire’, which became shortened to <i>vaudevire</i>, and this was later altered to <i>vaudeville</i>. It was originally used in English for a ‘popular song’; the application to ‘light variety entertainment’ did not emerge until the early 19th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/vaudeville">vaudeville (n.)</a></dt><dd>1735, "a country song," especially one for the stage, from French <span class="foreign">vaudeville</span> (16c.), alteration (by influence of <span class="foreign">ville</span> "town") of Middle French <span class="foreign">vaudevire</span>, said to be from <span class="foreign">(chanson du) Vau de Vire</span> "(song of the) valley of Vire," in the Calvados region of Normandy, first applied to the popular satirical songs of Olivier Basselin, a 15c. poet who lived in Vire. The alternative explanation is that <span class="foreign">vaudevire</span> derives from Middle French dialectal <span class="foreign">vauder</span> "to go" + <span class="foreign">virer</span> "to turn." From the popularity of the songs in France grew a form of theatrical entertainment based on parodies of popular opera and drama, interspersed with songs.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The <span class="foreign">Théatre du Vaudeville</span> is rich in parodies, which follow rapidly upon every new piece given at the Opera, or at the <span class="foreign">Théatre Français</span>. Their parody upon Hamlet is too ludicrous for description, but irresistibly laughable; and the elegaut light ballet of <span class="foreign">La Colombe Retrouvée</span> [The Dove found again], I saw parodied at the <span class="foreign">Vaudeville</span> as <span class="foreign">"La Maison Retrouvée"</span> [The House found again], with a breadth of farce quite beyond the genius of Sadler's Wells. Some of the acting here, particularly that of the men, is exquisite; and the orchestra like all the orchestras in Paris is full and excellent. ["France in 1816," by Lady Morgan]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; As a sort of popular stage variety entertainment show suitable for families, from c. 1881 in U.S., displaced by movies after c. 1914, considered dead from 1932.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>

    document.getElementById('yd-search').onsubmit = function() {
        var word = this.word.value.replace(/\u3000/g, ' ').replace(/(^\s+|\s+$)/g, '');
        if (word == '') {
            return false;
        } else if (word.search(/^[\w \-]+$/) == 0) {
            top.location.href = '/w/' + encodeURIComponent(word.replace(/ /g, '_'));
            return false;
        } else {
            this.word.value = word.replace(/[\uff01-\uffe1]/g, function(match) {
                var char_code = match.charCodeAt(0) - 0xfee0;
                return String.fromCharCode(char_code);
            });
        }
    }
