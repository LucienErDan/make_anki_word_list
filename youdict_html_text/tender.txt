                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. tender (adj.): tend- + -er. 应该是由于很柔软、很细嫩、很纤弱的东西很容易拉伸，由此引申形容那些很容易拉伸、很容易因拉伸而受伤、一拉就痛的的东西。<br />
2. 即：the things that can be stretched easily, or it could be easily painful by stretching.<br />
3. 用一个不存在但很贴切的词形容就是：非常的*stretchable.<br />
4. tender (v.): tend- + -er. => stretch, stretch out, hold out, offer.<br />
5. tender (n.): tend (a shortening form of attend) + -er => tender.</div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tender">tender</a> 投标，提议，建议</span><p>tend-,延伸，伸长，词源同 tend,extend.引申词义投标，建议。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tender">tender</a> 嫩的，柔软的，亲切的，和善的</span><p>来自 tend-,变细，细长的，词源同 tenuous,thin.引申词义嫩的，柔软的，后用于指亲切的，和 善的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tender">tender</a></dt><dd><b>tender: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>tender</i>, both of which go back ultimately to the Indo- European base *<i>ten</i>- ‘stretch’. The adjective, ‘delicate, fragile’ [13], comes via Old French <i>tendre </i>from Latin <i>tener </i>‘delicate’, a descendant of *<i>ten</i>- and source also of English <i>tendril </i>[16] (etymologically a ‘tender’ shoot). The verb, ‘offer’ [16], comes from another Old French <i>tendre</i>, which went back to Latin <i>tendere </i>‘stretch, hold out’ (source of English <i>tend</i>, <i>tendency</i>, etc).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/tenant" target="_blank">tenant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tend" target="_blank">tend</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tendril" target="_blank">tendril</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tender">tender (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"soft, easily injured," early 13c., from Old French <span class="foreign">tendre</span> "soft, delicate; young" (11c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">tenerem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">tener</span>) "soft, delicate; of tender age, youthful," from a derivative of PIE root <span class="foreign">*ten-</span> "stretch" (see <a href="/etym/s/tenet" target="_blank">tenet</a>), on the notion of "stretched," hence "thin," hence "weak" or "young." Compare Sanskrit <span class="foreign">tarunah</span> "young, tender," Greek <span class="foreign">teren</span> "tender, delicate," Armenian <span class="foreign">t'arm</span> "young, fresh, green." &#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "kind, affectionate, loving" first recorded early 14c. Meaning "having the delicacy of youth, immature" is attested in English from early 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Tenderly</span>; <span class="foreign">tenderness</span>. <span class="foreign">Tender-hearted</span> first recorded 1530s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tender">tender (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to offer formally," 1540s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">tendre</span> "to offer, hold forth" (11c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">tendere</span> "to stretch, extend" (see <a href="/etym/s/tenet" target="_blank">tenet</a>). The retention of the ending of the French infinitive is unusual (see <a href="/etym/s/render" target="_blank">render</a> (v.) for another example). The noun meaning "formal offer for acceptance" is from 1540s; specific sense of "money that may be legally offered as payment" is from 1740; hence <span class="foreign">legal tender</span> "currency."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tender">tender (n.)</a></dt><dd>"person who tends another," late 15c., probably an agent noun formed from Middle English <span class="foreign">tenden</span> "attend to" (see <a href="/etym/s/tend" target="_blank">tend</a> (v.2)); later extended to locomotive engineers (1825) and barmen (1883). The meaning "small boat used to attend larger ones" first recorded 1670s.</dd></dl></div></div>
