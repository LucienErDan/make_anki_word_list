                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/free">free</a> 自由的，空闲的</span><p>来自PIE*pri, 爱，友爱，词源同friend, Frigga. 词义由爱引申为亲人，朋友，和平，自由，免费。类似词义演变比较kind.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/free">free</a></dt><dd><b>free: </b><span>[OE] The prehistoric ancestor of <i>free </i>was a term of affection uniting the members of a family in a common bond, and implicitly excluding their servants or slaves – those who were not ‘free’. It comes ultimately from Indo- European *<i>prijos</i>, whose signification ‘dear, beloved’ is revealed in such collateral descendants as Sanskrit <i>priyás </i>‘dear’, Russian <i>prijatel</i>’ ‘friend’, and indeed English <i>friend</i>.<br><br>Its Germanic offspring, *<i>frijaz</i>, displays the shift from ‘affection’ to ‘liberty’, as shown in German <i>frei</i>, Dutch <i>vrij</i>, Swedish and Danish <i>fri</i>, and English <i>free</i>. Welsh <i>rhydd </i>‘free’ comes from the same Indo-European source.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/friday" target="_blank">friday</a>, <a href="/etym/s/friend" target="_blank">friend</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/free">free (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">freo</span> "free, exempt from, not in bondage, acting of one's own will," also "noble; joyful," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*frija-</span> "beloved; not in bondage" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fri</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">vri</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">vri</span>, German <span class="foreign">frei</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vrij</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">freis</span> "free"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*priy-a-</span> "dear, beloved," from root <span class="foreign">*pri-</span> "to love" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">priyah</span> "own, dear, beloved," <span class="foreign">priyate</span> "loves;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">prijati</span> "to help," <span class="foreign">prijatelji</span> "friend;" Welsh <span class="foreign">rhydd</span> "free").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The primary Germanic sense seems to have been "beloved, friend, to love;" which in some languages (notably Germanic and Celtic) developed also a sense of "free," perhaps from the terms "beloved" or "friend" being applied to the free members of one's clan (as opposed to slaves; compare Latin <span class="foreign">liberi</span>, meaning both "free persons" and "children of a family"). For the older sense in Germanic, compare Gothic <span class="foreign">frijon</span> "to love;" Old English <span class="foreign">freod</span> "affection, friendship, peace," <span class="foreign">friga</span> "love," <span class="foreign">friðu</span> "peace;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">friðr</span> "peace, personal security; love, friendship," German <span class="foreign">Friede</span> "peace;" Old English <span class="foreign">freo</span> "wife;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">Frigg</span> "wife of Odin," literally "beloved" or "loving;" Middle Low German <span class="foreign">vrien</span> "to take to wife," Dutch <span class="foreign">vrijen</span>, German <span class="foreign">freien</span> "to woo."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "clear of obstruction" is from mid-13c.; sense of "unrestrained in movement" is from c. 1300; of animals, "loose, at liberty, wild," late 14c. Meaning "liberal, not parsimonious" is from c. 1300. Sense of "characterized by liberty of action or expression" is from 1630s; of art, etc., "not holding strictly to rule or form," from 1813. Of nations, "not subject to foreign rule or to despotism," recorded in English from late 14c. (<span class="foreign">Free world</span> "non-communist nations" attested from 1950 on notion of "based on principles of civil liberty.") Sense of "given without cost" is 1580s, from notion of "free of cost."&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Free lunch</span>, originally offered in bars to draw in customers, by 1850, American English. <span class="foreign">Free pass</span> on railways, etc., attested by 1850. <span class="foreign">Free speech</span> in Britain was used of a privilege in Parliament since the time of Henry VIII. In U.S., in reference to a civil right to expression, it became a prominent phrase in the debates over the Gag Rule (1836). <span class="foreign">Free enterprise</span> recorded from 1832; <span class="foreign">free trade</span> is from 1823; <span class="foreign">free market</span> from 1630s. <span class="foreign">Free will</span> is from early 13c. <span class="foreign">Free school</span> is from late 15c. <span class="foreign">Free association</span> in psychology is from 1899. <span class="foreign">Free love</span> "sexual liberation" attested from 1822 (the doctrine itself is much older), American English. <span class="foreign">Free and easy</span> "unrestrained" is from 1690s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/free">free (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">freogan</span> "to free, liberate, manumit," also "to love, think of lovingly, honor;" also "to rid (of something)," from <span class="foreign">freo</span> "not in bondage" (see <a href="/etym/s/free" target="_blank">free</a> (adj.)). The forking sense in the Germanic adjective is reflected in the verbs that grew from it in the daughter languages. Compare Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fria</span> "to make free;" Old Saxon <span class="foreign">friohan</span> "to court, woo;" German <span class="foreign">befreien</span> "to free," <span class="foreign">freien</span> "to woo;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">frja</span> "to love;" Gothic <span class="foreign">frijon</span> "to love." Related: <span class="foreign">Freed</span>; <span class="foreign">freeing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
