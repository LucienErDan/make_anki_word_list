                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/port">port</a> 港口，端口，船的左舷</span><p>来自拉丁语portus,港口，来自PIE*per,向前，通过，词源同ford,forth.用于航海术语左舷，即从后面掌舵位往前看朝向港口的位置，比较starboard,右舷，掌舵位。并引申计算机端口等词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/port">port</a></dt><dd><b>port: </b><span>English has no fewer than five distinct words <i>port</i>, all of them going back to the Latin stem <i>port</i>-, a descendant of the Indo-European base *<i>por</i>- ‘going, passage’ (from which English also gets <i>fare</i>, <i>ford</i>, etc). Based on this stem was <i>portus </i>‘harbour’ (etymologically a ‘place by which one enters’), which was borrowed into English as <i>port </i>‘harbour’ [OE].<br><br>It is thought that the nautical <i>port </i>‘left’ [17] originally denoted the side of the vessel facing harbour. And <i>port </i>the drink [17] gets its name from <i>Oporto </i>(literally ‘the port’), the town at the mouth of the river Douro in Portugal through which port is shipped. From Latin <i>portus </i>was derived the verb <i>portāre</i>, which presumably originally meant ‘bring into port’, but by classical times had broadened out to simply ‘carry’.<br><br>This gave English the military verb <i>port </i>‘carry’ [16], and also underlies <i>deport </i>[15], <i>export </i>[15], <i>import</i>, <i>important</i>, <i>portable </i>[14], <i>portfolio </i>[18] (etymologically a ‘carrier of leaves’ or papers), <i>portly </i>[16], <i>portmanteau</i>, <i>report</i>, and <i>transport</i>. Also from <i>portus </i>comes English <i>opportunity</i>. From the same stem came Latin <i>porta </i>‘gate, door’, which reached English via Old French <i>porte </i>as <i>port </i>‘gate’ [13].<br><br>It came to be applied in the 14th century to an ‘opening in the side of a ship’, and it is now most commonly encountered in the compound <i>porthole </i>[16]. <i>Portal </i>[14] and <i>portcullis </i>are among its descendants.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fare" target="_blank">fare</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ferry" target="_blank">ferry</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fiord" target="_blank">fiord</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ford" target="_blank">ford</a>; <a href="/etym/s/deport" target="_blank">deport</a>, <a href="/etym/s/export" target="_blank">export</a>, <a href="/etym/s/import" target="_blank">import</a>, <a href="/etym/s/important" target="_blank">important</a>, <a href="/etym/s/opportunity" target="_blank">opportunity</a>, <a href="/etym/s/portable" target="_blank">portable</a>, <a href="/etym/s/portly" target="_blank">portly</a>, <a href="/etym/s/report" target="_blank">report</a>, <a href="/etym/s/transport" target="_blank">transport</a>; <a href="/etym/s/porch" target="_blank">porch</a>, <a href="/etym/s/portal" target="_blank">portal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/portcullis" target="_blank">portcullis</a>, <a href="/etym/s/porthole" target="_blank">porthole</a>, <a href="/etym/s/portico" target="_blank">portico</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/port">port (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"harbor," Old English <span class="foreign">port</span> "harbor, haven," reinforced by Old French <span class="foreign">port</span> "harbor, port; mountain pass;" Old English and Old French words both from Latin <span class="foreign">portus</span> "port, harbor," originally "entrance, passage," figuratively "place of refuge, asylum," from PIE <span class="foreign">*prtu-</span> "a going, a passage," from root <span class="foreign">*per-</span> (2) "to lead, pass over" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">parayati</span> "carries over;" Greek <span class="foreign">poros</span> "journey, passage, way," <span class="foreign">peirein</span> "to pierce, run through;" Latin <span class="foreign">porta</span> "gate, door," <span class="foreign">portare</span> "passage," <span class="foreign">peritus</span> "experienced;" Avestan <span class="foreign">peretush</span> "passage, ford, bridge;" Armenian <span class="foreign">hordan</span> "go forward;" Welsh <span class="foreign">rhyd</span> "ford;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pariti</span> "to fly;" Old English <span class="foreign">faran</span> "to go, journey," Old Norse <span class="foreign">fjörðr</span> "inlet, estuary").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "left side of a ship" (looking forward from the stern) is attested from 1540s, from notion of "the side facing the harbor" (when a ship is docked). It replaced <a href="/etym/s/larboard" target="_blank">larboard</a> in common usage to avoid confusion with <a href="/etym/s/starboard" target="_blank">starboard</a>; officially so by Admiralty order of 1844 and U.S. Navy Department notice of 1846. Figurative sense "place of refuge" is attested from early 15c.; phrase <span class="foreign">any port in a storm</span> first recorded 1749. A <span class="foreign">port of call</span> (1810) is one paid a scheduled visit by a ship.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/port">port (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"gateway," Old English <span class="foreign">port</span> "portal, door, gate, entrance," from Old French <span class="foreign">porte</span> "gate, entrance," from Latin <span class="foreign">porta</span> "city gate, gate; door, entrance," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*per-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/port" target="_blank">port</a> (n.1)). Specific meaning "porthole, opening in the side of a ship" is attested from c. 1300.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/port">port (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"bearing, mien," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">port</span>, from <span class="foreign">porter</span> "to carry," from Latin <span class="foreign">portare</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/port" target="_blank">port</a> (n.1)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/port">port (n.4)</a></dt><dd>type of sweet dark-red wine, 1690s, shortened from <span class="foreign">Oporto</span>, city in northwest Portugal from which the wine originally was shipped to England; from <span class="foreign">O Porto</span> "the port;" (see <a href="/etym/s/port" target="_blank">port</a> (n.1)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/port">port (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to carry," from Middle French <span class="foreign">porter</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">portare</span> "to carry" (see <a href="/etym/s/port" target="_blank">port</a> (n.1)).
