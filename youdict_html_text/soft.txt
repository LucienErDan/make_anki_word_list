            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/soft">soft</a> 柔软的，柔和的</span><p>来自古英语 softe,柔软的，舒服的，来自 Proto-Germanic*samftijaz,平的，均匀的，来自*somiz, 一致的，来自 PIE*sem,一，一起，词源同 same,some,simple.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>

            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/soft">soft</a></dt><dd><b>soft: </b><span>[OE] <i>Soft </i>goes back to a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>samft</i>-, which also produced German <i>sanft </i>‘gentle, easy, smooth’ and Dutch <i>zacht </i>‘soft’. It may go back ultimately to the prehistoric base *<i>sōrn</i>- ‘fitting, agreeable’, source of English <i>seem </i>and <i>seemly</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/seem" target="_blank">seem</a>, <a href="/etym/s/seemly" target="_blank">seemly</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/soft">soft (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">softe</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">sefte</span>, "gentle, mild-natured; easeful, comfortable, calm, undisturbed; luxurious," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*samfti</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*samftijaz</span> "level, even, smooth, gentle, soft" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">safti</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">semfti</span>, German <span class="foreign">sanft</span>; and from a variant form with <span class="foreign">-ch-</span> for <span class="foreign">-f-</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sachte</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zacht</span>, German <span class="foreign">sacht</span>), from root <span class="foreign">*som-</span> "fitting, agreeable."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From c. 1200 of material things, "not stiff, not coarse, fine; yielding to weight." From late 14c. of wind, rain, etc. Of sounds, "quiet, not loud," from early 13c. Of words, "mild, restrained; courteous" mid-14c. From late 14c. as "indulgent," also "physically feeble; easily overcome, lacking manly courage." From 1755 of water ("relatively free from mineral salts"), from 1789 of coal. Meaning "foolish, simple, silly" is attested from 1620s; earlier "easily moved or swayed; soft-hearted, sympathetic; docile" (early 13c.). In reference to drinks, "non-alcoholic" from 1880. As an adverb, Old English <span class="foreign">softe</span> "gently;" late 13c. as "quietly." As an interjection from 1540s.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Soft landing</span> is from 1958 and the U.S. space program. Adjective <span class="foreign">soft-core</span> (in reference to pornography) is from 1966 (see <a href="/etym/s/hardcore" target="_blank">hardcore</a>). <span class="foreign">Soft rock</span> as a music style is attested from 1969. <span class="foreign">Soft sell</span> is from 1955. <span class="foreign">Soft-shoe</span> as a dancing style is attested from 1927. <span class="foreign">Soft-boiled</span> is from 1757 of eggs; of persons, ideas, etc., 1930 (compare <span class="foreign">half-baked</span>). <span class="foreign">Soft-focus</span> (adj.) of camera shots is from 1917. <span class="foreign">The softer sex</span> "women collectively" is from 1640s.</dd></dl></div></div>
