                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/home">home</a> 家，住所</span><p>来自古英语ham,村庄，屋子，居住地，来自Proto-Germanic*haimaz,词源同hamlet,haunt.来自PIE*kei,聚居，定居，安家，词源同city,civil.现词义局限为家，住所。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/home">home</a></dt><dd><b>home: </b><span>[OE] Old English <i>hām </i>meant ‘place where one lives, house, village’. The last of these survives only in place-names (such as <i>Birmingham</i>, <i>Fulham</i>), and it is the ‘house, abode’ sense that has come through into modern English <i>home</i>. Its ancestor was prehistoric Germanic *<i>khaim</i>-, which also produced German <i>heim</i>, Dutch <i>heem</i>, Swedish <i>hem</i>, and Danish <i>hjem</i>. It is not clear where this came from, although some have connected it with Latin <i>civis </i>‘citizen’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/home">home (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ham</span> "dwelling, house, estate, village," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haimaz</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hem</span> "home, village," Old Norse <span class="foreign">heimr</span> "residence, world," <span class="foreign">heima</span> "home," Danish <span class="foreign">hjem</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">heem</span>, German <span class="foreign">heim</span> "home," Gothic <span class="foreign">haims</span> "village"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*tkei-</span> "to settle, dwell, be home" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kseti</span> "abides, dwells," Armenian <span class="foreign">shen</span> "inhabited," Greek <span class="foreign">kome</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kaimas</span> "village;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">semija</span> "domestic servants").&#13; <blockquote>'Home' in the full range and feeling of [Modern English] <span class="foreign">home</span> is a conception that belongs distinctively to the word <span class="foreign">home</span> and some of its Gmc. cognates and is not covered by any single word in most of the IE languages. [Buck]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Home stretch</span> (1841) is originally a reference from horse racing. <span class="foreign">Home base</span> in baseball attested by 1859 (<span class="foreign">home plate</span> by 1867; <span class="foreign">home</span> as the goal in a sport or game is from 1778). <span class="foreign">Home economics</span> first attested 1899. Slang phrase <span class="foreign">make (oneself) at home</span> "become comfortable in a place one does not live" dates from 1892. To <span class="foreign">keep the home fires burning</span> is from a song title from 1914. To be <span class="foreign">nothing to write home about</span> "unremarkable" is from 1907. <span class="foreign">Home movie</span> is from 1919; <span class="foreign">home computer</span> is from 1967.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/home">home (v.)</a></dt><dd>1765, "to go home," from <a href="/etym/s/home" target="_blank">home</a> (n.). Meaning "be guided to a destination by radio signals, etc. (of missiles, aircraft, etc.) is from 1920; it had been used earlier in reference to pigeons (1862). Related: <span class="foreign">Homed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/homing" target="_blank">homing</a>. Old English had <span class="foreign">hamian</span> "to establish in a home."</dd></dl></div></div>
