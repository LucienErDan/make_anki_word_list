            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/round">round</a> 圆的，圆形</span><p>来自拉丁语 rotundus,圆的，词源同 rotund,rotate.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/round">round</a></dt><dd><b>round: </b><span>[13] <i>Round </i>goes back ultimately to Latin <i>rotundus </i>‘round’, source of English <i>rotund</i>. In Vulgar Latin this became *<i>retundus</i>, which passed into Old French as <i>reont</i>, later <i>ront</i>. Its stem form <i>rond</i>- gave English <i>round</i>. Derivatives to have reached English include <i>prune </i>‘cut branches’, <i>rondo </i>[18], <i>roundel </i>[13], and <i>roundelay </i>[16]; but <i>surround</i>, despite the similarity, is not related.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/rota" target="_blank">rota</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rotund" target="_blank">rotund</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/round">round (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 13c., from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">rounde</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">roont</span> (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">rond</span>), probably originally <span class="foreign">*redond</span>, from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*retundus</span> (source also of Provençal <span class="foreign">redon</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">redondo</span>, Old Italian <span class="foreign">ritondo</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">rotundus</span> "like a wheel, circular, round," related to <span class="foreign">rota</span> "wheel" (see <a href="/etym/s/rotary" target="_blank">rotary</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As an adverb from c. 1300; as a preposition from c. 1600. In many uses it is a shortened form of <a href="/etym/s/around" target="_blank">around</a>. The French word is the source of Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">ront</span> (Dutch <span class="foreign">rond</span>), Middle High German <span class="foreign">runt</span> (German <span class="foreign">rund</span>) and similar Germanic words.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Of numbers from mid-14c., from earlier sense "full, complete, brought to completion" (mid-14c., notion of symmetry extended to that of completeness). First record of <span class="foreign">round trip</span> is from 1844, originally of railways. <span class="foreign">Round heels</span> attested from 1926, in reference to incompetent boxers, 1927 in reference to loose women, in either case implying an inability to avoid ending up flat on one's back.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/round">round (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "a spherical body," from <a href="/etym/s/round" target="_blank">round</a> (adj.) and Old French <span class="foreign">roond</span>. Compare Dutch <span class="foreign">rond</span>, Danish and Swedish <span class="foreign">rund</span>, German <span class="foreign">runde</span>, all nouns from adjectives. Meaning "large round piece of beef" is recorded from 1650s. Theatrical sense (in phrase <span class="foreign">in the round</span>) is recorded from 1944. Sense of "circuit performed by a sentinel" is from 1590s; that of "recurring course of time" is from 1710. Meaning "song sung by two or more, beginning at different times" is from 1520s. Golfing sense attested from 1775. Meaning "quantity of liquor served to a company at one time" is from 1630s; that of "single bout in a fight or boxing match" is from 1812; "single discharge of a firearm" is from 1725. Sense of "recurring session of meetings or negotiations" is from 1964.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/round">round (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to make round," from <a href="/etym/s/round" target="_blank">round</a> (adj.). Sense of "make a circuit round" is from 1590s. Sense of "bring to completeness" is from c. 1600; meaning "to approximate (a number)" is from 1934. Meaning "turn round and face, turn on and assault" is from 1882. <span class="foreign">Round out</span> "fill up" is from 1856. Related: <span class="foreign">Rounded</span>; <span class="foreign">rounding</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
