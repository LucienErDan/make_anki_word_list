                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/button">button</a> 纽扣</span><p>butt, 撞，同beat. -on, 小词后缀。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/button">button</a></dt><dd><b>button: </b><span>[14] <i>Button </i>comes via Old French <i>bouton </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>botōne</i>, a word connected with the verb *<i>hottāre </i>‘thrust’ (from which ultimately English gets <i>butt </i>‘hit with the head’). The underlying notion contained in <i>button </i>is thus of something which pushes up, thrusts itself outwards, rather like a bud growing on a plant; the fact that the resulting round knob is used for fastening is, from the point of view of the word’s semantic history, secondary. (Inconclusive attempts have in fact been made to link <i>bud </i>with Old French <i>boter</i>, a descendant of Vulgar Latin *<i>bottāre</i>, and from the 15th century the word <i>button </i>has been applied in English to ‘buds’.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/butt" target="_blank">butt</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/button">button (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300 (surname <span class="foreign">Botouner</span> "button-maker" attested from mid-13c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">boton</span> "a button," originally "a bud" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">bouton</span>), from <span class="foreign">bouter</span>, <span class="foreign">boter</span> "to thrust," common Romanic (Spanish <span class="foreign">boton</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">bottone</span>), ultimately from Germanic (see <a href="/etym/s/butt" target="_blank">butt</a> (v.)). Thus a <span class="foreign">button</span> is, etymologically, something that pushes up, or thrusts out.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "point of the chin" is pugilistic slang, by 1921. A button as something you push to create an effect by closing an (electrical) circuit is attested from 1840s. <span class="foreign">Button-pusher</span> as "deliberately annoying or provocative person" is attested by 1990 (in reference to Bill Gates, in "InfoWorld" magazine, Nov. 19). In the 1980s it meant "photographer."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/button">button (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to furnish with buttons;" early 15c., "to fasten with buttons" (of a garment,) from <a href="/etym/s/button" target="_blank">button</a> (n.) or from Old French <span class="foreign">botoner</span> (Modern French <span class="foreign">boutonner</span>), from <span class="foreign">boton</span> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Buttoned</span>; <span class="foreign">buttoning</span>. <span class="foreign">Button-down</span> (adj.) in reference to shirt collars is from 1916.</dd></dl></div></div>
