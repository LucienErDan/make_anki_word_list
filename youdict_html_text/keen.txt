                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/keen">keen</a> 渴望，热切</span><p>来自古英语cene,勇敢的，来自Proto-Germnaic*kan,有能力的，来自PIE*gno,知道，了解，词源同can,know.引申词义渴望的，热切的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/keen">keen</a></dt><dd><b>keen: </b><span>[OE] The ancestral meaning of <i>keen </i>is ‘brave’. That is what its German and Dutch relatives, <i>kühn </i>and <i>koen</i>, mean, and that is what <i>keen </i>itself meant in the Old English period. But this sense had died out by the 17th century, having been replaced by the meanings familiar today, such as ‘eager’ and ‘sharp’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/keen">keen (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, from Old English <span class="foreign">cene</span> "bold brave," later "clever, wise," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kan-</span> "be able to" (see <a href="/etym/s/can" target="_blank">can</a>). Original prehistoric senses seem to have been both "brave" and "skilled;" cognate with Old Norse <span class="foreign">kænn</span> "skillful, wise," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">coene</span> "bold," Dutch <span class="foreign">koen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kuon</span> "pugnacious, strong," German <span class="foreign">kühn</span> "bold, daring." Sense of "eager" is from mid-14c. The meaning "sharp" is peculiar to English: of blades and edges early 13c., of sounds c. 1400, of eyesight c. 1720. A popular word of approval in teenager and student slang from c. 1900.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/keen">keen (v.)</a></dt><dd>"lament," 1811, from Irish <span class="foreign">caoinim</span> "I weep, wail, lament," from Old Irish <span class="foreign">coinim</span> "I wail." Related: <span class="foreign">Keened</span>; <span class="foreign">keening</span>. As a noun from 1830.</dd></dl></div></div>
