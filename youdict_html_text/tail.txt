                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tail">tail</a> 尾巴，尾部</span><p>来自古英语 taegl,尾巴，来自 Proto-Germanic*tagla,尾巴，尾毛，来自 PIE*dok,尾巴上的毛， 来自 PIE*dek,撕，扯，分开。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tail">tail</a></dt><dd><b>tail: </b><span>[OE] <i>Tail </i>comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>taglaz</i>, whose other modern descendants include German <i>zagel </i>‘penis’ and Swedish <i>tagel </i>‘horsehair’. This in turn went back to an Indo- European *<i>doklos</i>, which had the general meaning ‘something long and thin’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tail">tail (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"hindmost part of an animal," Old English <span class="foreign">tægl</span>, <span class="foreign">tægel</span> "a tail," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*tagla-</span> (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">zagal</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zagel</span> "tail," dialectal German <span class="foreign">Zagel</span> "penis," Old Norse <span class="foreign">tagl</span> "horse's tail," Gothic <span class="foreign">tagl</span> "hair"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*doklos</span>, from suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*dek-</span> (2) "something long and thin" (referring to such things as fringe, lock of hair, horsetail; cognates: Old Irish <span class="foreign">dual</span> "lock of hair," Sanskrit <span class="foreign">dasah</span> "fringe, wick"). According to OED, the primary sense, at least in Germanic, seems to have been "hairy tail," or just "tuft of hair," but already in Old English the word was applied to the hairless "tails" of worms, bees, etc. But Buck writes that the common notion is of "long, slender shape." As an adjective from 1670s.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "reverse side of a coin" (opposite the side with the head) is from 1680s; that of "backside of a person, buttocks" is recorded from c. 1300; slang sense of "pudenda" is from mid-14c.; that of "woman as sex object" is from 1933, earlier "act of copulation" with a prostitute (1846). Of descending strokes of letters, from 1590s.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Tails</span> "coat with tails" is from 1857. The <span class="foreign">tail-race</span> (1776) is the part of a mill race below the wheel. To <span class="foreign">turn tail</span> "take flight" (1580s) originally was a term in falconry. The image of the <span class="foreign">tail wagging the dog</span> is attested from 1907. Another Old English word for "tail" was <span class="foreign">steort</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/stark" target="_blank">stark</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tail">tail (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"limitation of ownership," a legal term, early 14c. in Anglo-French; late 13c. in Anglo-Latin, in most cases a shortened form of <a href="/etym/s/entail" target="_blank">entail</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tail">tail (v.)</a></dt><dd>1520s, "attach to the tail," from <a href="/etym/s/tail" target="_blank">tail</a> (n.1). Meaning "move or extend in a way suggestive of a tail" is from 1781. Meaning "follow secretly" is U.S. colloquial, 1907, from earlier sense of "follow or drive cattle." Related: <span class="foreign">Tailed</span>; <span class="foreign">tailing</span>. <span class="foreign">Tail off</span> "diminish" is attested from 1854.</dd></dl></div></div>
