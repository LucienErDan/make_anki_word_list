2. pose (v.1): put, place. => propose, suggest.<br />
3. pose (v.2): put, place. => suppose, assume. => earlier "question, interrogate". => puzzle, confuse, perplex.<br />

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pose">pose</a> 摆姿势，佯装，冒充</span><p>来自古法语poser,放置，提议，来自拉丁语pausare,停止，暂停，停歇，休息，词源同pause.同时，词义与另一个拉丁词ponere的过去分词positus相混淆并大量吸收了该词词义，来自ponere,放置，摆放，词源同position,component.引申词义摆出姿势，佯装，冒充等。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pose">pose</a> 提出，提问，造成威胁</span><p>缩写自oppose,对着放，反对。引申词义提问，造成威胁等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pose">pose</a></dt><dd><b>pose: </b><span>[16] <i>Pose </i>and <i>pause </i>come ultimately from the same source. This was late Latin <i>pausāre </i>‘stop, pause’. In Vulgar Latin it came to be associated with <i>pōnere </i>‘put’, and particularly, owing to the similarity of form, with its past participle <i>positum </i>(source of English <i>position</i>), and gradually started to take over its meaning. Hence Old French <i>poser</i>, source of the English word, meant ‘put, place’. The noun <i>pose </i>is a modern acquisition from French, dating from the early 19th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pause" target="_blank">pause</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pose">pose (v.1)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., <span class="foreign">posen</span>, "suggest (something is so), suppose, assume; grant, concede," from Old French <span class="foreign">poser</span> "put, place, propose," a term in debating, from Late Latin <span class="foreign">pausare</span> "to halt, rest, cease, pause" (source also of Italian <span class="foreign">posare</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">posar</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/pause" target="_blank">pause</a> (v.)). The Late Latin verb also had a transitive sense, "cause to pause or rest," and hence the Old French verb (in common with cognates in Spanish, Italian, Portuguese) acquired the sense of Latin <span class="foreign">ponere</span> (past participle <span class="foreign">positus</span>) "to put, place," by confusion of the similar stems. Meaning "put in a certain position" in English is from early 15c. Sense of "assume a certain attitude" is from 1840; the transitive sense (as an artist's model, etc.) is from 1859. Related: <span class="foreign">Posed</span>; <span class="foreign">posing</span>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; One of the most remarkable facts in F[rench] etymology is the extraordinary substitution whereby the Low Lat. <span class="foreign">pausare</span> came to mean 'to make to rest, to set,' and so usurped the place of the Lat. <span class="foreign">ponere</span>, to place, set, with which it has no etymological connection. And this it did so effectually as to restrict the F. <span class="foreign">pondre</span>, the true equivalent of Lat. <span class="foreign">ponere</span>, to the sense of 'laying eggs;' whilst in all compounds it completely thrust it aside, so that <span class="foreign">compausare</span> (i.e. F. <span class="foreign">composer</span>) took the place of Lat. <span class="foreign">componere</span>, and so on throughout. Hence the extraordinary result, that whilst the E. verbs <span class="foreign">compose</span>, <span class="foreign">depose</span>, <span class="foreign">impose</span>, <span class="foreign">propose</span>, &amp;c. exactly represent <span class="foreign">in sense</span> the Lat. <span class="foreign">componere</span>, <span class="foreign">deponere</span>, <span class="foreign">imponere</span>, <span class="foreign">proponere</span>, &amp;c., we cannot <span class="foreign">derive</span> the E. verbs from the Lat. ones since they have (as was said) no real etymological connection. [W.W. Skeat, "Etymological Dictionary of the English Language," 1898]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pose">pose (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to puzzle, confuse, perplex," 1590s, earlier "question, interrogate" (1520s), probably from Middle French <span class="foreign">poser</span> "suppose, assume," from Old French <span class="foreign">poser</span> "to put, place, set" (see <a href="/etym/s/pose" target="_blank">pose</a> (v.1)). Also in some cases a shortening of English <span class="foreign">appose</span> "examine closely," and <span class="foreign">oppose</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Posed</span>; <span class="foreign">posing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pose">pose (n.)</a></dt><dd>"act of posing the body," 1818, from <a href="/etym/s/pose" target="_blank">pose</a> (v.1), in a sense developed in the French cognate. Figuratively from 1884.</dd></dl></div></div>
