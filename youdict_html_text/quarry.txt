                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/quarry">quarry</a> 采石场</span><p>来自拉丁语quadrare,给石头打磨，使成（四）方形，词源同four,square,quarter.引申词义石头打磨声，采石场。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/quarry">quarry</a> 猎物</span><p>来自古法语coree,内脏，肠，来自拉丁语cor,心，心脏，词源同heart,cordial.原指给猎鹰猎捕 到猎物后的奖赏，后引申词义猎物。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quarry">quarry</a></dt><dd><b>quarry: </b><span><i>Quarry </i>from which stone is extracted [15] and <i>quarry </i>which one hunts [14] are quite different words. The former was borrowed from Old French <i>quarriere</i>, a derivative of *<i>quarre </i>‘square stone’. This went back to Latin <i>quadrum </i>‘square’, which was based on the stem <i>quadr</i>- ‘four’, source of English <i>quadrangle</i>, <i>quadrant</i>, <i>quadruped</i>, etc.<br><br>The sort of <i>quarry </i>that is pursued came from Anglo-Norman *<i>quire </i>or *<i>quere</i>, which denoted ‘entrails of a killed deer given to the hounds to eat’. This went back to Old French <i>cuiree</i>, which was an alteration of an earlier <i>couree </i>or <i>coree</i>. And this in turn was descended from Vulgar Latin *<i>corāta </i>‘entrails’, a derivative of Latin <i>cor </i>‘heart’.<br><br>The present-day sense of the English word emerged in the 15th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/quarter" target="_blank">quarter</a>; <a href="/etym/s/cordial" target="_blank">cordial</a>, <a href="/etym/s/courage" target="_blank">courage</a>, <a href="/etym/s/record" target="_blank">record</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quarry">quarry (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"what is hunted," early 14c., <span class="foreign">quirre</span> "entrails of deer placed on the hide and given to dogs of the chase as a reward," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">quirreie</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">cuiriee</span> "the spoil, quarry" (Modern French <span class="foreign">curée</span>), altered (by influence of Old French <span class="foreign">cuir</span> "skin," from Latin <span class="foreign">corium</span> "hide"), from Old French <span class="foreign">corée</span> "viscera, entrails," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*corata</span> "entrails," from Latin <span class="foreign">cor</span> "heart," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kerd-</span> (1) "heart" (see <a href="/etym/s/heart" target="_blank">heart</a> (n.)). Sense of "anything chased in hunt" is first recorded 1610s; earlier "bird targeted by a hawk or other raptor" (late 15c.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quarry">quarry (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"open place where rocks are excavated," c. 1400 (mid-13c. as a place name), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">quareia</span>, dissimilated from <span class="foreign">quarreria</span> (mid-13c.), literally "place where stones are squared," from Latin <span class="foreign">quadrare</span> "to square" (see <a href="/etym/s/quadrant" target="_blank">quadrant</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quarry">quarry (v.)</a></dt><dd>1774, from <a href="/etym/s/quarry" target="_blank">quarry</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Quarried</span>; <span class="foreign">quarrying</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
