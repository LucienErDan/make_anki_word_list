            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/heat">heat</a> 热，温度</span><p>来自hot的名词形式。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heat">heat</a></dt><dd><b>heat: </b><span>[OE] From an etymological point of view, <i>heat </i>is simply ‘hotness’ – that is, the adjective <i>hot </i>with an abstract noun suffix added to it. But the addition took place a long time ago, in the prehistoric ancestor of Old English. The suffix *-<i>īn </i>‘state, condition’ was tacked on to the adjective *<i>khaitaz </i>‘hot’ to produce *<i>khaitīn</i>, which eventually became modern English <i>heat</i>. The verb <i>heat </i>is equally ancient, and was independently formed from *<i>khiataz </i>(<i>het</i>, as in ‘het up’, comes from a dialectal form of its past participle).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/het" target="_blank">het</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hot" target="_blank">hot</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heat">heat (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hætu</span>, <span class="foreign">hæto</span> "heat, warmth, quality of being hot; fervor, ardor," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haita-</span> "heat" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">hittia</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hiti</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hete</span>, German <span class="foreign">hitze</span> "heat," Gothic <span class="foreign">heito</span> "fever"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*kaid-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*kai-</span> "heat." The same root is the source of Old English <span class="foreign">hat</span> "hot" and <span class="foreign">hæða</span> "hot weather."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "a single course in a race," especially a horse race, is from 1660s, perhaps from earlier figurative sense of "violent action; a single intense effort" (late 14c.), or the meaning "run given to a horse to prepare for a race" (1570s). The latter word over time was extended to "division of a race or contest when there are too many contestants to run at once," the winners of each heat then competing in a final race. &#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "sexual excitement in animals" is from 1768, especially of females, corresponding to <span class="foreign">rut</span> in males. Meaning "trouble with the police" attested by 1920. <span class="foreign">Heat wave</span> "period of excessive hot weather" first attested 1890; earlier in reference to solar cycles. <span class="foreign">Heat-stroke</span> is from 1874. <span class="foreign">Heat-seeking</span> (adj.) of missiles, etc., is from 1956. <span class="foreign">Red heat</span>, <span class="foreign">white heat</span> are in reference to the color of heated metals, especially iron.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heat">heat (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hætan</span> "to make hot; to become hot," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haita-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/heat" target="_blank">heat</a> (n.)). Related: <a href="/etym/s/Heated" target="_blank">Heated</a> (with many variants in Middle English); <a href="/etym/s/heating" target="_blank">heating</a>. Compare Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">heeten</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">heten</span>, German <span class="foreign">heizen</span> "to heat."</dd></dl></div></div>
