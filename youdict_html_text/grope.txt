2. g- (瓜[gua]的首字母) + rope (绳、线 => 藤) => 顺藤摸瓜(地去摸索、探索)，是不是很形象啊？<br />
3. 绳子前的哥哥在摸索，顺着绳子去摸索嘛！<br />

            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grope">grope</a></dt><dd><b>grope: </b><span>see <a href="/etym/s/grip" target="_blank">grip</a></span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grope">grope (v.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">grapian</span> "to feel about (as one blind or in darkness)," also "take hold of, seize, touch, attain," related to <span class="foreign">gripan</span> "grasp at" (see <a href="/etym/s/gripe" target="_blank">gripe</a> (v.)). Transitive sense "search out by sense of touch alone" was in late Old English. Figurative sense is from early 14c. Indecent sense "touch (someone) amorously, play with, fondle" (marked as "obsolete" in OED 2nd edition) is from c. 1200. Related: <span class="foreign">Groped</span>; <span class="foreign">groping</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grope">grope (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1500, "act of groping," from <a href="/etym/s/grope" target="_blank">grope</a> (v.). Old English had <span class="foreign">grap</span> "a grasp."</dd></dl></div></div>

        if (word == '') {
            return false;
