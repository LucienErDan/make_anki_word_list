                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plane">plane</a> 飞机</span><p>缩写自airplane,飞机。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plane">plane</a> 水平，平面，刨平</span><p>来自拉丁语planum,平面，来自PIE*pele,展开，放平，词源同plain,plan.引申词义水平，平面，刨平，刨子等诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane</a></dt><dd><b>plane: </b><span>English has five distinct <i>planes</i>, four of which are essentially the same word as <i>plain</i>. These come ultimately from Latin <i>plānus</i>, but preserve its ‘flat’ meanings rather than (like <i>plain</i>) its ‘clear’ meanings. <i>Plane </i>‘flat surface’ [17] comes from Latin <i>plānum</i>, a noun use of the neuter form of the adjective; it is the <i>plane </i>from which <i>aeroplane</i>, and hence its abbreviation <i>plane</i>, were formed. <i>Plane </i>‘carpenter’s smoothing tool’ [14] comes via Old French <i>plane </i>from late Latin <i>plāna</i>, a derivative of the verb <i>plānāre </i>‘make level’, itself a derivative of <i>plānus</i>. <i>Plane </i>‘flat’ [17] is an alteration of <i>plain</i>, on the model of French <i>plan </i>‘flat’.<br><br>And <i>plane </i>‘glide, soar’ [17] comes from French <i>planer</i>, a derivative of <i>plan </i>‘level surface’ (the underlying notion being of a bird soaring with level wings). The odd man out is <i>plane </i>the tree-name [14], which comes via Old French <i>plane </i>and Latin <i>platanus </i>from Greek <i>plátanos</i>, a derivative of <i>platús </i>‘broad’ (source of English <i>place</i>, <i>plaice</i>, and <i>platypus</i>) – the reference being to its broad leaves. <i>Platanus </i>probably also underlies English <i>plantain</i>, as applied to the banana-like vegetable.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/piano" target="_blank">piano</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plain" target="_blank">plain</a>; <a href="/etym/s/place" target="_blank">place</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plaice" target="_blank">plaice</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plantain" target="_blank">plantain</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plate" target="_blank">plate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/platypus" target="_blank">platypus</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"flat surface," c. 1600, from Latin <span class="foreign">planum</span> "flat surface, plane, level, plain," noun use of neuter of adjective <span class="foreign">planus</span> "flat, level, even, plain, clear," from PIE <span class="foreign">*pla-no-</span> (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">plonas</span> "thin;" Celtic <span class="foreign">*lanon</span> "plain;" perhaps also Greek <span class="foreign">pelanos</span> "sacrificial cake, a mixture offered to the gods, offering (of meal, honey, and oil) poured or spread"), suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*pele-</span> (2) "to spread out; broad, flat" (cognates: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">polje</span> "flat land, field," Russian <span class="foreign">polyi</span> "open;" Old English and Old High German <span class="foreign">feld</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">veld</span> "field"). Introduced (perhaps by influence of French <span class="foreign">plan</span> in this sense) to differentiate the geometrical senses from <span class="foreign">plain</span>, which in mid-16c. English also meant "geometric plane." Figurative sense is attested from 1850. As an adjective from 1660s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane (n.2)</a></dt><dd>1908, short for <span class="foreign">aeroplane</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/airplane" target="_blank">airplane</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"tool for smoothing surfaces," mid-14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">plane</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">plaine</span> (14c.), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">plana</span>, back-formation from <span class="foreign">planare</span> "make level," from Latin <span class="foreign">planus</span> "level, flat" (see <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a> (n.1)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"soar, glide on motionless wings," early 15c., from Old French <span class="foreign">planer</span> "to hover (as a bird), to lie flat," from <span class="foreign">plan</span> (n.) "plane," from Latin <span class="foreign">planum</span> "flat surface" (see <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a> (n.1)), on notion of bird gliding with flattened wings. Of boats, etc., "to skim over the surface of water," it is first found 1913. Related: <span class="foreign">Planed</span>; <span class="foreign">planing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane (n.4)</a></dt><dd>"tree of the genus <span class="foreign">Platanus</span>," late 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">plane</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">plasne</span> (14c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">platanus</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">platanos</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">platanistos</span> "plane tree," a species from Asia Minor, associated with <span class="foreign">platys</span> "broad" (see <a href="/etym/s/plaice" target="_blank">plaice</a> (n.)), in reference to its leaves. Applied since 1778 in Scotland and northern England to the sycamore, whose leaves somewhat resemble those of the true plane tree.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plane">plane (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to make smooth," early 14c., "to gloss over, explain away;" mid-14c. as "to make smooth or even," from Old French <span class="foreign">planer</span> "to smooth, level off; wipe away, erase" (12c.), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">planare</span> "make level," from Latin <span class="foreign">planus</span> "level, flat" (see <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a> (n.1)). In early use in English often <span class="foreign">plain</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Planed</span>; <span class="foreign">planing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
