                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/nick">nick</a> 刻痕，缺口，监狱，警察局</span><p>词源不详，可能来自nock拼写变体，刻痕，缺口。俚语义监狱，警察局。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/nick">nick (n.)</a></dt><dd>"notch, groove, slit," late 15c., <span class="foreign">nyke</span>, of unknown origin, possibly influenced by Middle French <span class="foreign">niche</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/niche" target="_blank">niche</a>), or from it. <span class="foreign">Nick of time</span> is first attested 1640s (<span class="foreign">nick of opportunity</span> is 1610s), possibly from an old custom of recording time as it passed by making notches on a tally stick, though <span class="foreign">nick</span> in the general sense of "critical moment" is older (1570s, Hanmer, who adds "as commonly we say") than the phrase.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/nick">nick (v.)</a></dt><dd>1520s, "to make a notch in," from <a href="/etym/s/nick" target="_blank">nick</a> (n.). Sense of "to steal" is from 1869, probably from earlier slang sense of "to catch, take unawares, arrest" (1620s). The precise sense connection is unclear. Related: <span class="foreign">Nicked</span>; <span class="foreign">nicking</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Nick">Nick</a></dt><dd>masc. proper name, familiar form of <a href="/etym/s/Nicholas" target="_blank">Nicholas</a>. As "the devil" by 1640s, but the reason for it is obscure.</dd></dl></div></div>
