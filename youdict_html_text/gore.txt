                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gore">gore</a> 戳伤</span><p>词源同gar, 矛。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gore">gore</a></dt><dd><b>gore: </b><span>English has three separate words <i>gore</i>, two of them perhaps ultimately related. <i>Gore </i>‘blood’ [OE] originally meant ‘dung, shit’, or more generally ‘filth, dirt, slime’, and related words in other languages, such as Dutch <i>goor </i>‘mud, filth’, Old Norse <i>gor </i>‘slime’, and Welsh <i>gôr </i>‘pus’, round out a semantic picture of ‘unpleasant semi-liquid material’, with frequent specific application to ‘bodily excretions’.<br><br>It was from this background that the sense ‘blood’, and particularly ‘coagulated blood’, emerged in the mid-16th century. <i>Gore </i>‘triangular piece of cloth, as let into a skirt’ [OE] comes from Old English <i>gāra </i>‘triangular piece of land’ (a sense preserved in the London street-name <i>Kensington Gore</i>). This was related to Old English <i>gār </i>‘spear’ (as in <i>garlic</i>; see GOAD), the semantic connection being that a spearhead is roughly triangular. <i>Gore </i>‘wound with horns’ [14] originally meant simply ‘stab, pierce’; it too may come ultimately from <i>gār </i>‘spear’, although there is some doubt about this.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/garlic" target="_blank">garlic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gore">gore (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"triangular piece of ground," Old English <span class="foreign">gara</span> "corner, point of land, cape, promontory," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gaizon-</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">gare</span> "a gore of cloth; a garment," Dutch <span class="foreign">geer</span>, German <span class="foreign">gehre</span> "a wedge, a gore"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ghaiso-</span> "a stick, spear" (see <a href="/etym/s/gar" target="_blank">gar</a>). The connecting sense is "triangularity." Hence also the senses "front of a skirt" (mid-13c.), and "triangular piece of cloth" (early 14c.). In New England, the word applied to a strip of land left out of any property by an error when tracts are surveyed (1640s).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gore">gore (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"thick, clotted blood," Old English <span class="foreign">gor</span> "dirt, dung, filth, shit," a Germanic word (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">goor</span> "filth, mud;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">gor</span> "cud;" Old High German <span class="foreign">gor</span> "animal dung"), of uncertain origin. Sense of "clotted blood" (especially shed in battle) developed by 1560s (<span class="foreign">gore-blood</span> is from 1550s).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gore">gore (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to pierce, stab," c. 1400, from Middle English <span class="foreign">gore</span> (n.) "spear," from Old English <span class="foreign">gar</span> "spear" (see <a href="/etym/s/gar" target="_blank">gar</a>, also <a href="/etym/s/gore" target="_blank">gore</a> (n.2) "triangular piece of ground"). Related: <span class="foreign">Gored</span>; <span class="foreign">goring</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
