                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. brachio- / brachi- "arm" => brace.<br />
2. 双臂举起支撑某物。 => 撑牢，支柱，支撑；加固，使固定，使稳定。<br />
3. 双臂紧抱或双臂向外拉紧某物。=> 拉紧；使绷紧，使变紧；使紧张。<br />
4. 双臂紧抱或双臂向外拉紧某物。=> 缚牢，系牢，系紧，束紧。<br />
5. 双臂抱住。 => 很像一对大括号。=> 大括号。<br />
6. 通过肩部拉紧裤子的带子。 => 背带。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/brace">brace</a> 箍子，大括号</span><p>来自拉丁词bracchia, 手臂。词源同brief, 短的。原指两手合抱，表支持义，后指箍子，括号等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/brace">brace</a></dt><dd><b>brace: </b><span>[14] English borrowed <i>brace </i>from Old French <i>brace</i>, which meant simply ‘(the length measured by) two arms’. It came from Latin <i>bracchia</i>, the plural of <i>bracchium </i>‘arm’ (source of French <i>bras </i>‘arm’, and also of various English technical terms, such as <i>brachiopod </i>[19], a type of shellfish, literally ‘arm-foot’). The word’s ultimate source was Greek <i>brakhíōn </i>‘arm’, originally ‘upper arm’, which was formed from the comparative of <i>brakhús </i>‘short’, a relative of English <i>brief </i>(the sense development is probably that the upper arm was named from being ‘shorter’ than the forearm).<br><br>Of the rather diverse range of meanings the word has in modern English, ‘pair’ derives from the original notion of ‘twoness’, while ‘strengthening or supporting structure’ owes much to the idea of ‘clasping’, mainly contained originally in the verb <i>brace </i>[14], from Old French <i>bracier </i>‘put one’s arms around’ (a derivative of Old French <i>brace</i>). In English it now only means ‘support, strengthen’, the sense ‘clasp with the arms’ being reserved to <i>embrace </i>[14], from Old French <i>embracer</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/brief" target="_blank">brief</a>, <a href="/etym/s/embrace" target="_blank">embrace</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/brace">brace (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "piece of armor for the arms," also "thong, strap for fastening," from Old French <span class="foreign">brace</span>, <span class="foreign">braz</span> "arms," also "length measured by two arms" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">bras</span> "arm, power;" <span class="foreign">brasse</span> "fathom, armful, breaststroke"), from Latin <span class="foreign">bracchia</span>, plural of <span class="foreign">bracchium</span> "an arm, a forearm," from Greek <span class="foreign">brakhion</span> "an arm" (see <a href="/etym/s/brachio-" target="_blank">brachio-</a>). Applied to various devices for fastening and tightening on notion of clasping arms. Of dogs, "a couple, a pair" from c. 1400.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/brace">brace (v.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "to seize, grasp," also "wrap, enshroud; tie up, fetter," from Old French <span class="foreign">bracier</span> "to embrace," from <span class="foreign">brace</span> "arms" (see <a href="/etym/s/brace" target="_blank">brace</a> (n.)). Meaning "to render firm or steady by tensing" is mid-15c., earlier in figurative sense "strengthen or comfort" (someone), early 15c., with later extension to tonics, etc. that "brace" the nerves (compare <a href="/etym/s/bracer" target="_blank">bracer</a> "stiff drink"). Related: <span class="foreign">Braced</span>; <span class="foreign">bracing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
