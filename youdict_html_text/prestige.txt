                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/prestige">prestige</a> 威望，名望</span><p>来自法语prestige,欺骗，骗术，幻术，变戏法，来自拉丁语praestigium,幻术，变戏法，来自（异化自）praestringere,使眼花缭乱，使眩晕，来自prae,在前面，stringere,拉，拉绳子，捆绑，词源同string.该词原用于形容法国皇帝拿破仑像个街头玩变戏法的，含贬义和讽刺意味，后随着越来越多的人崇拜拿破仑，词义由贬义转褒义，而原词义则不再使用。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/prestige">prestige</a></dt><dd><b>prestige: </b><span>[17] As opponents of semantic change are fond of pointing out, <i>prestige </i>once meant ‘trick, illusion’, and its use until the 19th century was usually derogatory. It comes via French <i>prestige </i>from Latin <i>praestigiae </i>‘illusions produced by a conjurer or juggler’, an alteration of an unrecorded *<i>praestrigiae</i>. This would have been a derivative of <i>praestringere </i>‘blindfold’, hence ‘confuse the sight, dazzle’, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>prae</i>- ‘before’ and <i>stringere </i>‘bind’ (source of English <i>strict</i>). The modern approbatory meaning appears to have been reintroduced from French.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/strict" target="_blank">strict</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/prestige">prestige (n.)</a></dt><dd>1650s, "trick," from French <span class="foreign">prestige</span> (16c.) "deceit, imposture, illusion" (in Modern French, "illusion, magic, glamour"), from Latin <span class="foreign">praestigium</span> "delusion, illusion" (see <a href="/etym/s/prestigious" target="_blank">prestigious</a>). Derogatory until 19c.; sense of "dazzling influence" first applied 1815, to Napoleon.</dd></dl></div></div>
