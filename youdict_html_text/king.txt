                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/king">king</a> 国王，君主，重要的，大的</span><p>缩写自古英语cyning,国王，君主，统治者，来自Proto-Germanic*kuningaz,对应于kin,家族，种族，氏族，-ing,集合名词后缀。即一个家族或氏族的统领，后用于指国王，君主。引申词义关键的，重要的，大的等。比较其与emperor的区别。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/king">king</a></dt><dd><b>king: </b><span>[OE] The prehistoric Germanic ancestor of <i>king </i>(as of German <i>könig</i>, Dutch <i>koning</i>, Swedish <i>konung</i>, and Danish <i>konge</i>) was *<i>kuninggaz</i>. This seems to have been a derivative of *<i>kunjam </i>‘race, people’ (source of English <i>kin</i>). If it was, <i>king </i>means etymologically ‘descendant of the race, offspring of the people’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/kin" target="_blank">kin</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/king">king (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">cyning</span> "king, ruler," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kuningaz</span> (cognates: Dutch <span class="foreign">koning</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">konungr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">konge</span>, Old Saxon and Old High German <span class="foreign">kuning</span>, Middle High German <span class="foreign">künic</span>, German <span class="foreign">König</span>). Possibly related to Old English <span class="foreign">cynn</span> "family, race" (see <a href="/etym/s/kin" target="_blank">kin</a>), making a <span class="foreign">king</span> originally a "leader of the people;" or from a related root suggesting "noble birth," making a <span class="foreign">king</span> originally "one who descended from noble birth." The sociological and ideological implications render this a topic of much debate.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Finnish <span class="foreign">kuningas</span> "king," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kunegu</span> "prince" (Russian <span class="foreign">knyaz</span>, Bohemian <span class="foreign">knez</span>), Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kunigas</span> "clergyman" are loans from Germanic.&#13; <blockquote>As leon is the king of bestes. [John Gower, <span class="foreign">"Confessio Amantis,"</span> 1390]</blockquote>&#13; In Old English, used for names of chiefs of Anglian and Saxon tribes or clans, then of the states they founded. Also extended to British and Danish chiefs they fought. The chess piece so called from early 15c.; the playing card from 1560s; use in checkers/draughts first recorded 1820. Applied in nature to species deemed remarkably big or dominant (such as <span class="foreign">king crab</span>, 1690s). In marketing, <span class="foreign">king-size</span> is from 1939, originally of cigarettes.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; [I]t was [Eugene] Field who haunted the declining years of Creston Clarke with his review of that actor's Lear. ... Said he, "Mr. Clarke played the King all the evening as though under constant fear that someone else was about to play the Ace." ["Theatre Magazine," January 1922]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
