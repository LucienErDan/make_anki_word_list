                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gum">gum</a> 牙龈，树胶，口香糖</span><p>1.树胶，来自埃及语。</p><p> 2.牙龈，来自PIE*gheu, 打呵欠，张嘴，词源同chaos, gap.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gum">gum</a></dt><dd><b>gum: </b><span>English has three words <i>gum</i>. The oldest, ‘tissue surrounding the teeth’ [OE], originally meant ‘mucous lining of the mouth and throat’; its present-day meaning did not emerge until the 14th century. It is not clear where it came from, although it is related to German <i>gaumen </i>‘roof of the mouth’, and perhaps to Lithuanian <i>gomurys </i>‘gum’ and even Latin <i>fauces </i>‘throat’ (source of English <i>suffocate</i>). <i>Gum </i>‘sticky material’ [14] comes ultimately from Egyptian <i>kemai</i>, which passed into English via Greek <i>kómmi</i>, Latin <i>cummi </i>or <i>gummi</i>, Vulgar Latin *<i>gumma</i>, and Old French <i>gomme</i>.<br><br>And <i>gum </i>in the exclamation <i>by gum </i>[19] is a euphemistic alteration of <i>god</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gum">gum (n.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "resin from dried sap of plants," from Old French <span class="foreign">gome</span> "(medicinal) gum, resin," from Late Latin <span class="foreign">gumma</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">gummi</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">kommi</span> "gum," from Egyptian <span class="foreign">kemai</span>. As the name of a hardened, sweetened gelatine mixture as a candy, 1827. As a shortened form of <span class="foreign">chewing gum</span>, first attested 1842 in American English. The <span class="foreign">gum tree</span> (1670s) was so called for the resin it exudes. Latin <span class="foreign">gummi</span> also is the source of German <span class="foreign">Gummi</span> (13c.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gum">gum (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"soft tissues of the mouth," Old English <span class="foreign">goma</span> "palate, side of the mouth" (single or plural), from a Germanic source represented by Old Norse <span class="foreign">gomi</span> "palate," Old High German <span class="foreign">goumo</span>; related to Lithuanian <span class="foreign">gomurys</span> "palate," and perhaps from PIE root <span class="foreign">*gheu-</span> "to yawn" (source also of Old English <span class="foreign">ginian</span> "to yawn;" see <a href="/etym/s/yawn" target="_blank">yawn</a> (v.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gum">gum (v.1)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., <span class="foreign">gommen</span>, "treat with (medicinal or aromatic) gums," from <a href="/etym/s/gum" target="_blank">gum</a> (n.1). In the transferred or figurative sense of "spoil, ruin" (usually with <span class="foreign">up</span>), as if by some gummy substance, it is first recorded 1901, probably from the notion of machinery becoming clogged. Related: <span class="foreign">Gummed</span>; <span class="foreign">gumming</span>.&#13; &#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gum">gum (v.2)</a></dt><dd>of infants, toothless adults, etc., "to chew or gnaw (something) with the gums," by 1907, from <a href="/etym/s/gum" target="_blank">gum</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Gummed</span>; <span class="foreign">gumming</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
