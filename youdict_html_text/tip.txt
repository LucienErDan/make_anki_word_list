                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. “贴士”是英语“Tips”的音译词，用作名词，是指“供参考的资料”或者“提醒、提示别人的信息”，如：考试、赌博或游戏的提示。<br />
2. “贴士”一词最初由香港地区按照粤语的译音而来，主要在香港、广东一带使用。后来，使用的地域范围逐渐扩大。<br />
3. “贴士”多见于报刊、网络等传媒的书面用语，如“旅游小贴士”、“家居小贴士”、“足球贴士”等，读起来让人有一种温馨、亲切的感觉。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tip">tip</a> 告密，指点，内幕消息，给小费</span><p>可能来自 tap,轻拍，轻扣。后用于指暗示，指点，告密等，同时，轻拍某人肩膀常用于表示 鼓励，友善，后引申词义小费，并有人据此逆构出 TIP,缩写自 to insure promptitude,为保证服 务速度（而给小费）。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tip">tip</a> 尖端，尖点，倾斜，倾覆，倾倒</span><p>词源同 top,顶部，顶端。引申词义尖端，尖点，后用于指倾斜，倾覆，倾倒等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip</a></dt><dd><b>tip: </b><span>English has three distinct words <i>tip</i>, two of them possibly related. <i>Tip </i>‘extremity’ [15] was probably borrowed from Old Norse <i>typpi</i>. This was descended from prehistoric Germanic *<i>tupp</i>- ‘upper extremity’ (source also of English <i>top </i>and <i>toupee</i>). <i>Tip </i>‘touch lightly’ [13] (as in ‘tip-and-run cricket’) may have been borrowed from Low German <i>tippen</i>, although it could be the same word as <i>tip </i>‘extremity’ (from the notion of ‘just touching something with the tip of something else’).<br><br>It was used in 17th-century underworld argot for ‘give’ (as in ‘tip someone the wink’), and this evolved in the 18th century to ‘give a gratuity’. The antecedents of <i>tip </i>‘overturn’ [14] (originally <i>tipe</i>) are not known, although the fact that it first appeared in northern dialects suggests that it may have been borrowed from a Scandinavian language. The derived <i>tipsy </i>[16] denotes etymologically ‘liable to fall over’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/top" target="_blank">top</a>, <a href="/etym/s/toupee" target="_blank">toupee</a>; <a href="/etym/s/tipsy" target="_blank">tipsy</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip (v.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "to knock down, overturn, topple, knock askew" (transitive), of uncertain origin, possibly from Scandinavian (compare Swedish <span class="foreign">tippa</span> "to tip, dump"), or from a special use of <a href="/etym/s/tip" target="_blank">tip</a> (n.). Intransitive sense of "to fall over, be overturned" is from mid-15c. Related: <span class="foreign">Tipped</span>; <span class="foreign">tipping</span>. To <span class="foreign">tip the scales at</span> "weigh (so much" is from 1879. <span class="foreign">Tipping point</span> attested by 1972. To <span class="foreign">tip (one's) hand</span> "reveal one's intentions" is from 1907, an image from poker-playing.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip (n.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "extreme end of something, top of something round or pointed, metal attachment to the end of something," from Middle Low German or Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">tip</span> "utmost point, extremity, tip" (compare German <span class="foreign">zipfel</span>, a diminutive formation); or from a Scandinavian source (compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">typpi</span>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"give a small present of money to," c. 1600, originally "to give, hand, pass," thieves' cant, perhaps from <a href="/etym/s/tip" target="_blank">tip</a> (v.3) "to tap." The meaning "give a gratuity to" is first attested 1706. The noun in this sense is from 1755; the noun meaning "piece of confidential information" is from 1845; and the verb in the sense "give private information to" is from 1883.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The popularity of the tale of the word's supposed origin as an acronym in mid-18th century English taverns seems to be no older than Frederick W. Hackwood's 1909 book "Inns, Ales and Drinking Customs of Old England," where it was said to stand for <span class="foreign">To insure promptitude</span> (in the form <span class="foreign">to insure promptness</span> the anecdote is told from 1946). A reviewer of the book in the "Athenaeum" of Oct. 2, 1909, wrote, "We deprecate the careless repetition of popular etymologies such as the notion that "tip" originated from an abbreviated inscription on a box placed on the sideboard in old coaching-inns, the full meaning of which was "To Insure Promptitude." Also <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/baloney.php"><b>see here</b></a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip (v.3)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "to strike, occur suddenly," of uncertain origin, possibly from Low German <span class="foreign">tippen</span> "to poke, touch lightly," related to Middle Low German <span class="foreign">tip</span> "end, point," and thus connected to <a href="/etym/s/tip" target="_blank">tip</a> (n.); or else related to <a href="/etym/s/tap" target="_blank">tap</a> (v.1). Meaning "strike sharply but lightly" is from 1560s. Sports sense is from 1816, originally in cricket. Related: <span class="foreign">Tipped</span>; <span class="foreign">tipping</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"a light, sharp blow or tap," mid-15c., from <a href="/etym/s/tip" target="_blank">tip</a> (v.3).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tip">tip (v.4)</a></dt><dd>"put a tip on, adorn with a tip," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/tip" target="_blank">tip</a> (n.) or Old Norse <span class="foreign">typpa</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Tipped</span>; <span class="foreign">tipping</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
