                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/slut">slut</a> 邋遢女人，荡妇，泼妇</span><p>来自辅音丛 sl-,邋遢的，懒的，比较 slattern,slut,sloven,slug.原不分男女，后固定为邋遢的女 人，词义进一步贬义化为荡妇，泼妇，妓女。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/slut">slut (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "a dirty, slovenly, or untidy woman," according to OED "Of doubtful origin," but probably cognate with dialectal German <span class="foreign">Schlutt</span> "slovenly woman," dialectal Swedish <span class="foreign">slata</span> "idle woman, slut," and Dutch <span class="foreign">slodde</span> "slut," <span class="foreign">slodder</span> "a careless man," but the exact relationship of all these is obscure. Chaucer uses <a href="/etym/s/sluttish" target="_blank">sluttish</a> (late 14c.) in reference to the appearance of an untidy man. Also "a kitchen maid, a drudge" (mid-15c.; hard pieces in a bread loaf from imperfect kneading were called <span class="foreign">slut's pennies</span>, 18c.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Specific modern sense of "woman who enjoys sex in a degree considered shamefully excessive" is by 1966. Meaning "woman of loose character, bold hussy" is attested from mid-15c., but the primary association through 18c. was untidiness. Johnson has it (second definition) as "A word of slight contempt to a woman" but sexual activity does not seem to figure into his examples. Playful use of the word, without implication of messiness or loose morals, is attested by 1660s:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; My wife called up the people to washing by four o'clock in the morning; and our little girl Susan is a most admirable slut, and pleases us mightily, doing more service than both the others, and deserves wages better. [Pepys, diary, Feb. 21, 1664]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Compare playful use of <span class="foreign">scamp</span>, etc., for boys. Sometimes used 19c. as a euphemism for <span class="foreign">bitch</span> to describe a female dog.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; There is a group of North Sea Germanic words in <span class="foreign">sl-</span> that mean "sloppy," and also "slovenly woman" and, less often, "slovenly man," and that tend to evolve toward "woman of loose morals." Compare <a href="/etym/s/slattern" target="_blank">slattern</a>, also English dialectal <span class="foreign">slummock</span> "a dirty, untidy, or slovenly person" (1861), variant of <span class="foreign">slammacks</span> "slatternly woman," said to be from <span class="foreign">slam</span> "ill-shaped, shambling fellow." Also <span class="foreign">slammakin</span> (from 1756 as a type of loose gown; 1785 as "slovenly female," 1727 as a character name in Gay's "Beggar's Opera"), with variants <span class="foreign">slamkin</span>, <span class="foreign">slammerkin</span>. Also possibly related are Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">slore</span> "a sluttish woman," Dutch <span class="foreign">slomp</span>, German <span class="foreign">schlampe</span> "a slattern."</dd></dl></div></div>

A basin of cold water has cured the <b>slut</b> . " </dt> <dd>一盆冷水 就医 好了!</dd> <p>来自汉英文学 - 现代散文</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. Go on with your work, you impudent <b>slut</b>! </dt> <dd>干你的活去, 你这不要脸的邋遢女人!</dd> <p>来自辞典例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4.     document.getElementById('yd-search').onsubmit = function() {

        if (word == '') {
            return false;
        } else if (word.search(/^[\w \-]+$/) == 0) {
            top.location.href = '/w/' + encodeURIComponent(word.replace(/ /g, '_'));
            return false;

                var char_code = match.charCodeAt(0) - 0xfee0;
                return String.fromCharCode(char_code);
            });
        }
