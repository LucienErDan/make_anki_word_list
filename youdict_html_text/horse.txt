                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/horse">horse</a> 马</span><p>来自古英语hors,马，来自Proto-Germanic*hursa,跑动，来自PIE*kurs,跑，词源同car,current.引申词义马。比较equine.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/horse">horse</a></dt><dd><b>horse: </b><span>[OE] The Germanic languages have gone their own way as far as the horse is concerned. The prehistoric Indo-European term for the animal was *<i>ekwos</i>, which produced Latin <i>equus </i>(source of English <i>equestrian </i>and <i>equine</i>), Greek <i>híppos </i>(whence English <i>hippodrome </i>and <i>hippopotamus</i>), Sanskrit <i>açvás</i>, and Old English <i>eoh</i>. Remarkably, though, this has virtually died out as the day-to-day word for ‘horse’ in the modern European branches of the Indo- European languages.<br><br>In the case of English, it has been replaced by a descendant of prehistoric Germanic *<i>khorsam </i>or *<i>khorsaz</i>: <i>horse </i>(German <i>ross</i>, now mainly a literary term equivalent to English <i>steed</i>, is related). Its source is not known, although some have linked it with Latin <i>currere </i>‘run’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/horse">horse (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hors</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hursa-</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">hross</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hors</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">ors</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">ros</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hros</span>, German <span class="foreign">Roß</span> "horse"), of unknown origin, connected by some with PIE root <span class="foreign">*kurs-</span>, source of Latin <span class="foreign">currere</span> "to run" (see <a href="/etym/s/current" target="_blank">current</a> (adj.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The usual Indo-European word is represented by Old English <span class="foreign">eoh</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*ekwo-</span> "horse" (see <a href="/etym/s/equine" target="_blank">equine</a>). In many other languages, as in English, this root has been lost in favor of synonyms, probably via superstitious taboo on uttering the name of an animal so important in Indo-European religion.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Used since at least late 14c. of various devices or appliances which suggest a horse (as in <span class="foreign">sawhorse</span>). To <span class="foreign">ride a horse that was foaled of an acorn</span> (1670s) was through early 19c. a way to say "be hanged from the gallows." Slang for <span class="foreign">heroin</span> is first attested 1950. <span class="foreign">Horse latitudes</span> first attested 1777, the name of unknown origin, despite much speculation. <span class="foreign">Dead horse</span> as a figure for "something that has ceased to be useful" is attested from 1630s.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; HORSEGODMOTHER, a large masculine wench; one whom it is difficult to rank among the purest and gentlest portion of the community. [John Trotter Brockett, "A Glossary of North Country Words," 1829]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">The horse's mouth</span> as a source of reliable information is from 1921, perhaps originally of racetrack tips, from the fact that a horse's age can be determined accurately by looking at its teeth. To <span class="foreign">swap horses while crossing the river</span> (a bad idea) is from the American Civil War and appears to have been originally one of Abe Lincoln's stories. <span class="foreign">Horse and buggy</span> meaning "old-fashioned" is recorded from 1926 slang, originally in reference to a "young lady out of date, with long hair."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/horse">horse (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">horsian</span> "to provide with a horse or horses," from <a href="/etym/s/horse" target="_blank">horse</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Horsed</span>; <span class="foreign">horsing</span>. Sense of "to play excessive jokes on" is by 1893, mostly in formation <span class="foreign">horse around</span> (1928), perhaps from <a href="/etym/s/horseplay" target="_blank">horseplay</a>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; [A] favorite pastime for many men is to "horse" or guy a friend who has shown himself susceptible to ridicule or fun making. "Horsing" is extremely wholesome mental discipline for over sensitive or super-conceited young men. "Horsing" always implies a joke at another's expense. As to how it came into use there is no satisfactory theory to offer. ["Yale Literary Magazine," December 1893]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
