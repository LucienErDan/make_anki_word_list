                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/grave">grave</a> 坟墓</span><p>来自PIE*ghrebh, 刮，挖，词源同carve, graph. 后该词主要指坟墓。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/grave">grave</a> 重音符</span><p>来自PIE*gwere, 重，词源同barometer, guru, gravity. 用于音乐术语。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grave">grave</a></dt><dd><b>grave: </b><span>Modern English has essentially two words <i>grave</i>. <i>Grave </i>‘burial place’ goes back ultimately to prehistoric Indo-European *<i>ghrebh</i>- ‘dig’, which also produced Latvian <i>grebt </i>‘hollow out’ and Old Church Slavonic <i>pogreti </i>‘bury’. Its Germanic descendant had variants *<i>grōb</i>- (source of <i>groove</i>), *<i>grub</i>- (whence <i>grub</i>), and *<i>grab</i>-.<br><br>This last formed the basis of *<i>graban</i>, from which have come the verbs for ‘dig’ in most Germanic languages, including German <i>graben</i>, Dutch <i>graven</i>, Swedish <i>gräva</i>, and Danish <i>grave</i>. The English member of the family, <i>grave</i>, is now virtually obsolete as a verb (although its derivative <i>engrave </i>[16] survives); but its nominal relative <i>grave</i>, also formed from *<i>grab</i>-, is still very much with us. <i>Grave </i>‘serious’ [16] comes via Old French <i>grave </i>from Latin <i>gravis </i>‘heavy, important’, source also of English <i>gravity </i>and <i>grief</i>.<br><br>Its application to a backward-leaning accent (as in <i>è</i>) comes from the original use of such an accent-mark to indicate low or deep intonation.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/engrave" target="_blank">engrave</a>, <a href="/etym/s/groove" target="_blank">groove</a>, <a href="/etym/s/grub" target="_blank">grub</a>; <a href="/etym/s/gravity" target="_blank">gravity</a>, <a href="/etym/s/grief" target="_blank">grief</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grave">grave (n.)</a></dt><dd>"excavation in earth for reception of a dead body," Old English <span class="foreign">græf</span> "grave; ditch, trench; cave," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*graban</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">graf</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">gref</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">grab</span> "grave, tomb;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">gröf</span> "cave," Gothic <span class="foreign">graba</span> "ditch"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ghrebh-</span> (2) "to dig, to scratch, to scrape" (source also of Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">grobu</span> "grave, tomb"); related to Old English <span class="foreign">grafan</span> "to dig" (see <a href="/etym/s/grave" target="_blank">grave</a> (v.)).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; "The normal mod. representation of OE. <span class="foreign">græf</span> would be <span class="foreign">graff</span>; the ME. disyllable <span class="foreign">grave</span>, from which the standard mod. form descends, was prob. due to the especially frequent occurrence of the word in the dat. (locative) case. [OED]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; From Middle Ages to 17c., they were temporary, crudely marked repositories from which the bones were removed to ossuaries after some years and the grave used for a fresh burial. "Perpetual graves" became common from c. 1650. <span class="foreign">Grave-side</span> (n.) is from 1744. <span class="foreign">Grave-robber</span> attested from 1757. To <span class="foreign">make (someone) turn in his grave</span> "behave in some way that would have offended the dead person" is first recorded 1888.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grave">grave (adj.)</a></dt><dd>1540s, "influential, respected; marked by weighty dignity," from Middle French <span class="foreign">grave</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">greve</span> "terrible, dreadful," 14c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">gravis</span>, "heavy, ponderous, burdensome, loaded; pregnant;" of matters, "weighty, important;" of sounds, "deep, low, bass;" figuratively "oppressive, hard to bear, troublesome, grievous," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*gwere-</span> (2) "heavy" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">guruh</span> "heavy, weighty, venerable;" Greek <span class="foreign">baros</span> "weight," <span class="foreign">barys</span> "heavy in weight," often with the notion of "strength, force;" Old English <span class="foreign">cweorn</span> "quern;" Gothic <span class="foreign">kaurus</span> "heavy;" Lettish <span class="foreign">gruts</span> "heavy"). In English, the sense "solemn, sober" is from 1580s; of immaterial things, "important, serious" 1590s. Greek <span class="foreign">barys</span> (opposed to <span class="foreign">kouphos</span>) also was used figuratively, of suffering, sorrow, sobbing, and could mean "oppressive, burdensome, grave, dignified, impressive." The noun meaning "accent mark over a vowel" is c. 1600, from French.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grave">grave (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to engrave," Old English <span class="foreign">grafan</span> "to dig, dig up; engrave, carve, chisel" (medial <span class="foreign">-f-</span> pronounced as "v" in Old English; past tense <span class="foreign">grof</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">grafen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*grabanan</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">grafa</span> "to dig; engrave; inquire into," Old Frisian <span class="foreign">greva</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">graven</span> "to dig, delve," Old High German <span class="foreign">graban</span>, German <span class="foreign">graben</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">graban</span> "to dig, carve"), from the same source as <a href="/etym/s/grave" target="_blank">grave</a> (n.). Its Middle English strong past participle, <a href="/etym/s/graven" target="_blank">graven</a>, is the only part still active, the rest of the word supplanted by its derivative, <a href="/etym/s/engrave" target="_blank">engrave</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
