            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/minister">minister</a> 部长，大臣</span><p>来自古法语menistre,仆人，奴才，管家，臣子，来自拉丁语minister,仆人，下属，下等人，来自minus,下等的，次等的，-ter,比较级后缀。后用于指现代政府的部长，大臣。比较marshall.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/minister">minister</a> 部长，大臣</span><p>来自古法语menistre,仆人，奴才，管家，臣子，来自拉丁语minister,仆人，下属，下等人，来自minus,下等的，次等的，-ter,比较级后缀。后用于指现代政府的部长，大臣。比较marshall.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/minister">minister</a></dt><dd><b>minister: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, a <i>minister </i>is a person of ‘lower’ status, a ‘servant’. The word goes back via Old French <i>ministre </i>to Latin <i>minister </i>‘servant, attendant’, which was derived from <i>minus </i>‘less’. It retained this meaning when it arrived in English, and indeed it still survives in the verb <i>minister</i>. But already by the Middle Ages a specialized application to a ‘church functionary’ had developed, and in the 16th century this hardened into the present-day ‘clergyman’.<br><br>The political sense of the word developed in the 17th century, from the notion of a ‘servant’ of the crown. Derivatives from other languages to have established themselves in English include <i>métier </i>[18], which came via French from Vulgar Latin *<i>misterium</i>, an alteration of Latin <i>ministerium </i>‘service’ (source of English <i>ministry </i>[14]), and <i>minstrel</i>.<br><br>And etymologically, <i>minister </i>is the antonym of <i>master</i>, whose Latin ancestor was based on <i>magis </i>‘more’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/m%C3%A9tier" target="_blank">métier</a>, <a href="/etym/s/minstrel" target="_blank">minstrel</a>, <a href="/etym/s/minus" target="_blank">minus</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/minister">minister (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "to perform religious rites, provide religious services;" mid-14c., "to serve (food or drink);" late 14c. "render service or aid," from Old French <span class="foreign">menistrer</span> "to serve, be of service, administer, attend, wait on," and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">ministrare</span> "to serve, attend, wait upon" (see <a href="/etym/s/minister" target="_blank">minister</a> (n.)). Related: <span class="foreign">Ministered</span>; <span class="foreign">ministering</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/minister">minister (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "one who acts upon the authority of another," from Old French <span class="foreign">menistre</span> "servant, valet, member of a household staff, administrator, musician, minstrel" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">minister</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">ministri</span>) "inferior, servant, priest's assistant" (in Medieval Latin, "priest"), from <span class="foreign">minus</span>, <span class="foreign">minor</span> "less," hence "subordinate," (see <a href="/etym/s/minus" target="_blank">minus</a>) + comparative suffix <span class="foreign">*-teros</span>. Formed on model of <span class="foreign">magister</span>. Meaning "priest" is attested in English from early 14c. Political sense of "high officer of the state" is attested from 1620s, from notion of "service to the crown."</dd></dl></div></div>
