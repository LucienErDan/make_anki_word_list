            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/elder">elder</a> 年长的</span><p>来自old的比照级。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/elder">elder</a></dt><dd><b>elder: </b><span><i>Elder </i>‘older’ [OE] is not, of course, the same word as <i>elder </i>the tree-name [OE]. The former began life in prehistoric Germanic as *<i>althizon</i>, the comparative form of *<i>althaz </i>‘old’. Gradually, the vowel <i>i </i>had an effect on the preceding vowel <i>a</i>, and by Old English times the word had become <i>eldra </i>– hence modern English <i>elder</i>. The regularized form <i>older </i>appeared in the 16th century. The derivative <i>elderly </i>dates from the 17th century. The tree-name comes from Old English <i>ellærn</i>, a word whose origin is not known for certain (although it may perhaps be related to English <i>alder</i>). The intrusive <i>d </i>began to appear in the 14th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/old" target="_blank">old</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/elder">elder (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"more old," Old English (Mercian) <span class="foreign">eldra</span>, comparative of <span class="foreign">eald</span>, <span class="foreign">ald</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/old" target="_blank">old</a>); only English survival of umlaut in comparison. Superseded by <span class="foreign">older</span> since 16c. <span class="foreign">Elder statesman</span> (1921) originally was a translation of Japanese <span class="foreign">genro</span> (plural).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/elder">elder (n.2)</a></dt><dd>type of berry tree, c. 1400, with excrescent <span class="foreign">-d-</span> from earlier <span class="foreign">ellen</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">ellæn</span>, <span class="foreign">ellærn</span> "elderberry tree," origin unknown, perhaps related to <a href="/etym/s/alder" target="_blank">alder</a>, which at any rate might be the source of the <span class="foreign">-d-</span>. Common Germanic, cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">elora</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">elre</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">elira</span>, German <span class="foreign">Eller</span>, <span class="foreign">Erle</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Elderberry</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/elder">elder (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"elderly person, senior citizen," c. 1200, from Old English <span class="foreign">eldra</span> "older person, parent; ancestor; chief, prince" (used in biblical translation for Greek <span class="foreign">presbyter</span>); see <a href="/etym/s/elder" target="_blank">elder</a> (adj.). Compare German <span class="foreign">Eltern</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">forældre</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">föräldrar</span> "parents." The Old English for "grandfather" was <span class="foreign">ealdfæder</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
