                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/temper">temper</a> 脾性，性情，脾气，火气，怒火</span><p>来自 temper,调和。在中世纪医学理论特指人体内的四种体液的组合或融合，即血液，黏液， 胆汁液，黑胆汁，这四种体液的组合决定了人的脾性和性情，因而引申诸相关词义。词义火 气缩写自 bad temper.更多参照 humor 词源。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/temper">temper</a> 回火，管控，调和，调节</span><p>来自拉丁语 temperare,调节，管控，掌握火候，把控时间，来自 tempus,时间，词源同 temporal. 比喻用法，即掌握好时间。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/temper">temper</a></dt><dd><b>temper: </b><span>[OE] The verb <i>temper </i>was borrowed into Old English from Latin <i>temperāre </i>‘mix, blend’. This seems originally to have meant ‘mix in due proportion’, and so may have been derived from Latin <i>tempus </i>‘time, due time’ (source of English <i>temporary</i>). The noun <i>temper </i>was derived from the verb in the 14th century in the sense ‘mixture of elements’, and this led on in the 17th century to ‘set of mental traits’ (a meaning that has now largely passed to the derivative <i>temperament </i>[15]).<br><br>The modern sense ‘ill humour’ emerged from this in the 19th century. Another meaning of Latin <i>temperāre </i>was ‘restrain oneself’, which has come through into the derivatives <i>temperance </i>[14] and <i>temperate </i>[14]. Other relatives include <i>distemper </i>and <i>temperature</i>. <i>Tamper </i>probably originated as an alteration of <i>temper</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/distemper" target="_blank">distemper</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tamper" target="_blank">tamper</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tempera" target="_blank">tempera</a>, <a href="/etym/s/temperature" target="_blank">temperature</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/temper">temper (v.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">temprian</span> "to moderate, bring to a proper or suitable state, to modify some excessive quality, to restrain within due limits," from Latin <span class="foreign">temperare</span> "observe proper measure, be moderate, restrain oneself," also transitive, "mix correctly, mix in due proportion; regulate, rule, govern, manage," usually described as from <span class="foreign">tempus</span> "time, season" (see <a href="/etym/s/temporal" target="_blank">temporal</a>), with a sense of "proper time or season." Meaning "to make (steel) hard and elastic" is from late 14c. Sense of "to tune the pitch of a musical instrument" is recorded from c. 1300. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Tempered" target="_blank">Tempered</a>; <span class="foreign">tempering</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/temper">temper (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "due proportion of elements or qualities," from <a href="/etym/s/temper" target="_blank">temper</a> (v.). The sense of "characteristic state of mind, inclination, disposition" is first recorded 1590s; that of "calm state of mind, tranquility" in c. 1600; and that of "angry state of mind" (for <span class="foreign">bad temper</span>) in 1828. Meaning "degree of hardness and resiliency in steel" is from late 15c.</dd></dl></div></div>
