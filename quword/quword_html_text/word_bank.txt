                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bank">bank</a> 银行，堤岸</span><p>词源同bench，长凳，原指土凳，堤岸。银行义来自14世纪意大利威尼斯商人在市场摆一条凳子从事货币存储及放贷业务, 也即现代银行的起源。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bank">bank</a></dt><dd><b>bank: </b><span>[12] The various disparate meanings of modern English <i>bank </i>all come ultimately from the same source, Germanic *<i>bangk</i>-, but they have taken different routes to reach us. Earliest to arrive was ‘ridge, mound, bordering slope’, which came via a hypothetical Old Norse *<i>banki</i>. Then came ‘bench’ [13] (now obsolete except in the sense ‘series of rows or tiers’ – as in a typewriter’s <i>bank </i>of keys); this arrived from Old French <i>banc</i>, which was originally borrowed from Germanic *<i>bangk</i>- (also the source of English <i>bench</i>).<br><br>Finally came ‘moneylender’s counter’ [15], whose source was either French <i>banque </i>or Italian <i>banca </i>– both in any case deriving ultimately once again from Germanic *<i>bangk</i>-. The current sense, ‘place where money is kept’, developed in the 17th century. The derived <i>bankrupt </i>[16] comes originally from Italian <i>banca rotta</i>, literally ‘broken counter’ (<i>rotta </i>is related to English <i>bereave </i>and <i>rupture</i>); in early times a broken counter or bench was symbolic of an insolvent moneylender.<br><br>The diminutive of Old French <i>banc </i>was <i>banquet </i>‘little bench’ (perhaps modelled on Italian <i>banchetto</i>), from which English gets <i>banquet </i>[15]. It has undergone a complete reversal in meaning over the centuries; originally it signified a ‘small snack eaten while seated on a bench (rather than at table)’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bench" target="_blank">bench</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bank">bank (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"financial institution," late 15c., from either Old Italian <span class="foreign">banca</span> or Middle French <span class="foreign">banque</span> (itself from the Italian word), both meaning "table" (the notion is of the moneylender's exchange table), from a Germanic source (compare Old High German <span class="foreign">bank</span> "bench"); see <a href="/etym/s/bank" target="_blank">bank</a> (n.2).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Bank holiday</span> is from 1871, though the tradition is as old as the Bank of England. To <span class="foreign">cry all the way to the bank</span> was coined 1956 by flamboyant pianist Liberace, after a Madison Square Garden concert that was packed with patrons but panned by critics.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bank">bank (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"earthen incline, edge of a river," c. 1200, probably in Old English but not attested in surviving documents, from a Scandinavian source such as Old Norse <span class="foreign">banki</span>, Old Danish <span class="foreign">banke</span> "sandbank," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bangkon</span> "slope," cognate with <span class="foreign">*bankiz</span> "shelf" (see <a href="/etym/s/bench" target="_blank">bench</a> (n.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bank">bank (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to act as a banker," 1727, from <a href="/etym/s/bank" target="_blank">bank</a> (n.1). As "to deposit in a bank" from 1833. Figurative sense of "to rely on" (i.e. "to put money on") is from 1884, U.S. colloquial. Meaning "to ascend," as of an incline, is from 1892. In aeronautics, from 1911. Related: <span class="foreign">Banked</span>; <span class="foreign">banking</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
