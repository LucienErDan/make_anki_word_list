                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/buck">buck</a> 一美元，雄鹿</span><p>来自PIE *bhug, 公羊，同时指公鹿，公兔等其它雄性动物，后主要指雄鹿。美元义来自于美国西部大开发中鹿皮做为土著印地安人和欧洲殖民者间的货币媒介。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/Buck">Buck</a> ’s Fizz 巴克泡腾酒</span><p>因在伦敦著名的Buck’s Club首次调制出而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buck">buck</a></dt><dd><b>buck: </b><span>[OE] Old English had two related words which have coalesced into modern English <i>buck</i>: <i>bucca </i>‘male goat’ and <i>buc </i>‘male deer’. Both go back to a prehistoric Germanic stem *<i>buk</i>-, and beyond that probably to an Indo-European source. The 18th-century meaning ‘dashing fellow’ probably comes ultimately from the related Old Norse <i>bokki</i>, a friendly term for a male colleague, which was originally adopted in English in the 14th century meaning simply ‘fellow’. The colloquial American sense ‘dollar’ comes from an abbreviation of <i>buckskin</i>, which was used as a unit of trade with the Native Americans in Frontier days.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/butcher" target="_blank">butcher</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buck">buck (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"male deer," c. 1300, earlier "male goat;" from Old English <span class="foreign">bucca</span> "male goat," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bukkon</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">buck</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">boc</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">bok</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">boc</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bock</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">bokkr</span>), perhaps from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*bhugo</span> (cognates: Avestan <span class="foreign">buza</span> "buck, goat," Armenian <span class="foreign">buc</span> "lamb"), but some speculate that it is from a lost pre-Germanic language. Barnhart says Old English <span class="foreign">buc</span> "male deer," listed in some sources, is a "ghost word or scribal error."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "dollar" is 1856, American English, perhaps an abbreviation of <a href="/etym/s/buckskin" target="_blank">buckskin</a>, a unit of trade among Indians and Europeans in frontier days, attested in this sense from 1748. <span class="foreign">Pass the buck</span> is first recorded in the literal sense 1865, American English:<blockquote>The 'buck' is any inanimate object, usually knife or pencil, which is thrown into a jack pot and temporarily taken by the winner of the pot. Whenever the deal reaches the holder of the 'buck', a new jack pot must be made. [J.W. Keller, "Draw Poker," 1887]</blockquote>Perhaps originally especially a buck-handled knife. The figurative sense of "shift responsibility" is first recorded 1912. <span class="foreign">Buck private</span> is recorded by 1870s, of uncertain signification.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buck">buck (v.)</a></dt><dd>1848, apparently with a sense of "jump like a buck," from <a href="/etym/s/buck" target="_blank">buck</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Bucked</span>; <span class="foreign">bucking</span>. <span class="foreign">Buck up</span> "cheer up" is from 1844.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buck">buck (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"sawhorse," 1817, American English, apparently from Dutch <span class="foreign">bok</span> "trestle."</dd></dl></div></div>
