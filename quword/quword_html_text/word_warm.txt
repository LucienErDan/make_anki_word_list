            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/warm">warm</a> 温暖的</span><p>词源有争议，来自PIE*gwher,燃烧，热的，词源同burn,furnace,thermal.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/warm">warm</a></dt><dd><b>warm: </b><span>[OE] English, German, and Dutch <i>warm </i>and Swedish and Danish <i>varm </i>go back to a common prehistoric source, *<i>warmaz</i>. This in turn was descended from Indo-European *<i>ghworm</i>-, *<i>ghwerm</i>-, which also produced Greek <i>thermós </i>‘hot’ (source of English <i>thermal</i>, <i>thermometer</i>, etc), Latin <i>formus </i>‘warm’ and <i>fornus </i>‘oven’ (source of English <i>fornication </i>and <i>furnace</i>), and Armenian <i>jerm </i>‘warm’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fornication" target="_blank">fornication</a>, <a href="/etym/s/furnace" target="_blank">furnace</a>, <a href="/etym/s/thermal" target="_blank">thermal</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/warm">warm (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">wearm</span> "warm," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*warmaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Old Frisian, Middle Dutch, Old High German, German <span class="foreign">warm</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">varmr</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">warmjan</span> "to warm"), of uncertain origin. On one guess it is from PIE <span class="foreign">*gwher-</span> (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">gharmah</span> "heat;" Old Persian <span class="foreign">Garmapada-</span>, name of the fourth month, corresponding to June/July, from <span class="foreign">garma-</span> "heat;" Armenian <span class="foreign">jerm</span> "warm;" Greek <span class="foreign">thermos</span> "warm;" Latin <span class="foreign">formus</span> "warm," <span class="foreign">fornax</span> "oven;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">fogeir</span> "heated;" Hittite <span class="foreign">war-</span> "to burn"). On another guess it is connected to the source of Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">goriti</span> "to burn," <span class="foreign">varŭ</span> "heat," <span class="foreign">variti</span> "to cook, boil;" and Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vérdu</span> "to seethe."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The use of distinct words, based on degree of heat, for <span class="foreign">warm</span> and <span class="foreign">hot</span> is general in Balto-Slavic and Germanic, but in other languages one word often covers both (Greek <span class="foreign">thermos</span>; Latin <span class="foreign">calidus</span>, French <span class="foreign">chaud</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">caliente</span>). In reference to feelings, etc., attested from late 15c. Of colors from 1764. Sense in guessing games first recorded 1860, from earlier hunting use in reference to scent or trail (1713). <span class="foreign">Warm-blooded</span> in reference to mammals is recorded from 1793. <span class="foreign">Warm-hearted</span> first recorded c. 1500.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/warm">warm (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">wyrman</span> "make warm" and <span class="foreign">wearmian</span> "become warm;" from the root of <a href="/etym/s/warm" target="_blank">warm</a> (adj.). Phrase <span class="foreign">warm the bench</span> is sports jargon first recorded 1907. Related: <span class="foreign">Warmed</span>; <span class="foreign">warming</span>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; SCOTCH WARMING PAN. A wench. [Grose, "Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue," 1785]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
