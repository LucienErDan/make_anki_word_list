                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/thumb">thumb</a> 拇指</span><p>来自古英语 thuma,拇指，来自 Proto-Germanic*thumon,拇指，来自 PIE*tum,鼓起，来自 PIE*teue, 鼓起，词源同 tumid,thigh.插入字母 b,比较 number,numerate.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/thumb">thumb</a></dt><dd><b>thumb: </b><span>[OE] The <i>thumb </i>is etymologically the ‘swollen’ part – an allusion to its greater thickness than the other fingers. Along with its relatives German <i>daumen </i>and Dutch <i>duim</i>, it goes back to a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>thūmon</i>. This in turn can be traced to Indo- European *<i>tum</i>- ‘swell’, which also produced English <i>tumour </i>and <i>tumult</i>. The <i>b </i>in <i>thumb </i>appeared in the early Middle English period, when it was still a two-syllable word (<i>thumbe</i>), and at first was pronounced, but it has fallen silent over the centuries.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/thigh" target="_blank">thigh</a>, <a href="/etym/s/thimble" target="_blank">thimble</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tumour" target="_blank">tumour</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tumult" target="_blank">tumult</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/thumb">thumb (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">þuma</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*thumon-</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">thuma</span>, Old Saxon, Old High German <span class="foreign">thumo</span>, German <span class="foreign">Daumen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">duim</span> "thumb," Old Norse <span class="foreign">þumall</span> "thumb of a glove"), literally "the stout or thick (finger)," from PIE <span class="foreign">*tum-</span> "swell," from root <span class="foreign">*teue-</span> (2) "to swell" (see <a href="/etym/s/thigh" target="_blank">thigh</a>). For spelling with <span class="foreign">-b</span> (attested from late 13c.), see <a href="/etym/s/limb" target="_blank">limb</a>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; In some of the IE languages there is a single word for "thumb," which is called the "big finger," like NE <span class="foreign">big toe</span>. Many of the single words are of similar semantic origin, based on the notion of "stout, thick." [Buck]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Compare Greek <span class="foreign">megas daktylos</span> "thumb," but Greek also had <span class="foreign">antikheir</span>, literally "what is opposite the fingers." Italian <span class="foreign">pollice</span>, French <span class="foreign">pouce</span> are from Latin <span class="foreign">pollex</span>, perhaps formed (on analogy of <span class="foreign">index</span>) from <span class="foreign">pollere</span> "to be strong."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To be <span class="foreign">under (someone's) thumb</span> "be totally controlled by that person" is recorded from 1580s. <span class="foreign">Thumbs up</span> (1887) and <span class="foreign">thumbs down</span> (1906) were said to be from expressions of approval or the opposite in ancient amphitheaters, especially gladiator shows, where the gesture decided whether a defeated combatant was spared or slain. But the Roman gesture was merely one of hiding the thumb in the hand or extending it. Perhaps the modern gesture is from the usual coachmen's way of greeting while the hands are occupied with the reins.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/thumb">thumb (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to go through" (especially of printed material), 1930, from <a href="/etym/s/thumb" target="_blank">thumb</a> (n.), though the related sense of "soil or wear by handling" dates from 1640s. Earlier as a verb it meant "to play (a musical instrument) with the thumb" (1590s). Meaning "to hitchhike" is 1939; originally the thumb pointed in the direction one wished to travel. Related: <span class="foreign">Thumbed</span>; <span class="foreign">thumbing</span>. To <span class="foreign">thumb (one's) nose</span> as an expression of derision is recorded from 1903.</dd></dl></div></div>
