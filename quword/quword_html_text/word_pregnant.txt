                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pregnant">pregnant</a></dt><dd><b>pregnant: </b><span>[16] Latin <i>praegnās </i>‘pregnant’ probably originated as a compound formed from <i>prae </i>‘before’ and <i>gnascī </i>or <i>nascī </i>‘be born’ (source of English <i>nation</i>, <i>native</i>, etc). It was altered to <i>praegnāns</i>, bringing it in line with present participial adjectives ending in -<i>āns</i>, and was borrowed into English in its stem form as <i>pregnant</i>. From it was derived the verb <i>impregnāre</i>, from which English gets <i>impregnate </i>[16], but English <i>impregnable </i>is quite unrelated, despite its similarity – it comes from Latin <i>prehendere </i>‘seize’ (source also of English <i>apprehend</i>, <i>comprehend</i>, etc).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/impregnate" target="_blank">impregnate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/nation" target="_blank">nation</a>, <a href="/etym/s/native" target="_blank">native</a>, <a href="/etym/s/nature" target="_blank">nature</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pregnant">pregnant (adj.2)</a></dt><dd>"convincing, weighty, pithy," late 14c., "cogent, convincing, compelling" (of evidence, an argument, etc.); sense of "full of meaning" is from c. 1400. According to OED from Old French <span class="foreign">preignant</span>, present participle of <span class="foreign">preindre</span> "press, squeeze, stamp, crush," from earlier <span class="foreign">priembre</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">premere</span> "to press" (see <a href="/etym/s/press" target="_blank">press</a> (v.1)). But Watkins has it from Latin <span class="foreign">praehendere</span> "to grasp, seize," and in Barnhart it is from Latin <span class="foreign">praegnans</span> "with child," literally "before birth" and thus identical with <a href="/etym/s/pregnant" target="_blank">pregnant</a> (adj.1).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pregnant">pregnant (adj.1)</a></dt><dd>"with child," early 15c., from Latin <span class="foreign">praegnantem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">praegnans</span>, originally <span class="foreign">praegnas</span>) "with child," literally "before birth," probably from <span class="foreign">prae-</span> "before" (see <a href="/etym/s/pre-" target="_blank">pre-</a>) + root of <span class="foreign">gnasci</span> "be born" (see <a href="/etym/s/genus" target="_blank">genus</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Retained its status as a taboo word until c. 1950; modern euphemisms include <span class="foreign">anticipating, enceinte, expecting, in a family way, in a delicate (or interesting) condition</span>. Old English terms included <span class="foreign">mid-bearne</span>, literally "with child;" <span class="foreign">bearn-eaca</span>, literally "child-adding" or "child-increasing;" and <span class="foreign">geacnod</span> "increased." Among c. 1800 slang terms for "pregnant" was <span class="foreign">poisoned</span> (in reference to the swelling).</dd></dl></div></div>
