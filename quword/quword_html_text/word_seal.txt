                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/seal">seal</a> 封蜡，封印，印章，图章，玺，密封</span><p>来自古法语 seel,封印，图章，来自拉丁语 sigillum,小图片，小印，封印，来自 signum,标记， 记号，词源同 sign.原指书信上的封蜡标记，引申诸相关词义。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/seal">seal</a> 海豹</span><p>来自中古英语 sele,来自古英语 seolh,来自 Proto-Germanic*selhaz,海豹，来自 PIE*selk,拉，划， 词源同 sulkus,垄沟，裂缝。比喻用法，由动作代指动物本身，因海豹两条前肢较后腿为短， 在陆地上活动时，总是拖着累赘的后肢，将身体弯曲爬行，并在地面上留下一行形如沟垄的 痕迹。参照百度百科。比较 pork,猪肉，原义猪，字面意思为喜欢拱的动物，词源同 furrow, 沟，垄。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/seal">seal</a></dt><dd><b>seal: </b><span><i>Seal </i>the animal [OE] and <i>seal </i>‘impressed mark, closure’ [13] are of course different words. The former goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>selkhaz</i>, a word of unknown origin which also produced Swedish <i>säl </i>and Danish <i>sæl</i>. The latter was borrowed from Anglo- Norman <i>seal</i>, a descendant of Latin <i>sigillum</i>. This, a diminutive form of <i>signum </i>(from which English gets <i>sign</i>), meant ‘little mark or picture’, and came to be used for such a mark distinctive of a particular person.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/sign" target="_blank">sign</a>, <a href="/etym/s/signal" target="_blank">signal</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/seal">seal (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"design stamped on wax," especially one attached to a document as evidence of authenticity, c. 1200, from Old French <span class="foreign">seel</span> "seal on a letter" (Modern French <span class="foreign">sceau</span>), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*sigellum</span> (source of Italian <span class="foreign">suggello</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">sello</span>; also Old Frisian and Middle High German <span class="foreign">sigel</span>, German <span class="foreign">Siegel</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">sigillum</span> "small picture, engraved figure, seal," diminutive of <span class="foreign">signum</span> "mark, token" (see <a href="/etym/s/sign" target="_blank">sign</a> (n.)). An earlier borrowing directly from Latin is represented by Old English <span class="foreign">insigel</span>. Technical use, "what prevents the escape of a gas or liquid" is from 1853.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/seal">seal (n.2)</a></dt><dd>fish-eating mammal with flippers, Old English <span class="foreign">seolh</span> "seal," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*selkhaz</span> (compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">selr</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">sjöl</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">sæl</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">sel</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">seel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">selah</span>), of unknown origin, perhaps a borrowing from Finnic. <span class="foreign">Seal point</span> "dark brown marking on a Siamese cat" is recorded from 1934, from the dark brown color of seal fur; compare <span class="foreign">seal brown</span> "rich, dark brown color," by 1875. Old English <span class="foreign">seolhbæð</span>, literally "seal's bath," was an Anglo-Saxon kenning for "the sea."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/seal">seal (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to fasten with (or as with) a seal," c. 1200, from <a href="/etym/s/seal" target="_blank">seal</a> (n.1). Meaning "to place a seal on (a document)" is recorded from mid-14c.; hence "to conclude, ratify, render official" (late 15c.). Sense of "to close up with wax, lead, cement, etc." is attested from 1660s, from the notion of wax seals on envelopes. In reference to the actions of wood-coatings, 1940. Related: <span class="foreign">Sealed</span>; <span class="foreign">sealing</span>. <span class="foreign">Sealing-wax</span> is attested from c. 1300. To <span class="foreign">seal (one's) fate</span> (1799) probably reflects the notion of a seal on an execution warrant.</dd></dl></div></div>
