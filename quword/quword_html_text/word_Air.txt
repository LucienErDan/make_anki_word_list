                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/air">air</a> 空气</span><p>来自拉丁词aerem, 举起，空气。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/air">air</a></dt><dd><b>air: </b><span>[13] Modern English <i>air </i>is a blend of three strands of meaning from, ultimately, two completely separate sources. In the sense of the gas we breathe it goes back via Old French <i>air </i>and Latin <i>āēr </i>to Greek <i>áēr </i>‘air’ (whence the <i>aero</i>-compounds of English; see AEROPLANE). Related words in Greek were <i>áērni </i>‘I blow’ and <i>aúrā </i>‘breeze’ (from which English acquired <i>aura </i>in the 18th century), and cognates in other Indo-European languages include Latin <i>ventus </i>‘wind’, English <i>wind</i>, and <i>nirvana </i>‘extinction of existence’, which in Sanskrit meant literally ‘blown out’.<br><br>In the 16th century a completely new set of meanings of <i>air </i>arrived in English: ‘appearance’ or ‘demeanour’. The first known instance comes in Shakespeare’s <i>1 Henry IV</i>, IV, i: ‘The quality and air of our attempt brooks no division’ (1596). This <i>air </i>was borrowed from French, where it probably represents an earlier, Old French, <i>aire </i>‘nature, quality’, whose original literal meaning ‘place of origin’ (reflected in another derivative, <i>eyrie</i>) takes it back to Latin <i>ager </i>‘place, field’, source of English <i>agriculture </i>and related to <i>acre</i>. (The final syllable of English <i>debonair </i>[13] came from Old French <i>aire</i>, incidentally; the phrase <i>de bon aire </i>meant ‘of good disposition’.) The final strand in modern English <i>air </i>comes via the Italian descendant of Latin <i>āēr, aria</i>.<br><br>This had absorbed the ‘nature, quality’ meanings of Old French <i>aire</i>, and developed them further to ‘melody’ (perhaps on the model of German <i>weise</i>, which means both ‘way, manner’ and ‘tune’ – its English cognate <i>wise</i>, as in ‘in no wise’, meant ‘song’ from the 11th to the 13th centuries). It seems likely that English <i>air </i>in the sense ‘tune’ is a direct translation of the Italian.<br><br>Here again, Shakespeare got in with it first – in <i>A Midsummer Night’s Dream</i>, I, i: ‘Your tongue’s sweet air more tunable than lark to shepherd’s ear’ (1590). (<i>Aria </i>itself became an English word in the 18th century.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/acre" target="_blank">acre</a>, <a href="/etym/s/aeroplane" target="_blank">aeroplane</a>, <a href="/etym/s/agriculture" target="_blank">agriculture</a>, <a href="/etym/s/aria" target="_blank">aria</a>, <a href="/etym/s/aura" target="_blank">aura</a>, <a href="/etym/s/eyrie" target="_blank">eyrie</a>, <a href="/etym/s/malaria" target="_blank">malaria</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wind" target="_blank">wind</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/air">air (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to expose to open air," 1520s, from <a href="/etym/s/air" target="_blank">air</a> (n.1). Figurative sense of "to expose, make public" is from 1610s of objects, 1862 of opinions, grievances, etc. Meaning "to broadcast" (originally on radio) is from 1933. Related: <span class="foreign">Aired</span>; <a href="/etym/s/airing" target="_blank">airing</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/air">air (n.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "invisible gases that make up the atmosphere," from Old French <span class="foreign">air</span> "atmosphere, breeze, weather" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">aerem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">aer</span>) "air, lower atmosphere, sky," from Greek <span class="foreign">aer</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">aeros</span>) "air" (related to <span class="foreign">aenai</span> "to blow, breathe"), which is of unknown origin, possibly from a base <span class="foreign">*awer-</span> and thus related to <span class="foreign">aeirein</span> "to raise" and <span class="foreign">arteria</span> "windpipe, artery" (see <a href="/etym/s/aorta" target="_blank">aorta</a>) on notion of "lifting, that which rises." In Homer mostly "thick air, mist;" later "air" as one of the four elements.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Words for "air" in Indo-European languages tend to be associated with wind, brightness, sky. In English, <span class="foreign">air</span> replaced native <span class="foreign">lyft</span>, <span class="foreign">luft</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/loft" target="_blank">loft</a> (n.)). To be <span class="foreign">in the air</span> "in general awareness" is from 1875; <span class="foreign">up in the air</span> "uncertain, doubtful" is from 1752. To build <span class="foreign">castles in the air</span> is from 1590s (in 17c. English had <span class="foreign">airmonger</span> "one preoccupied with visionary projects"). Broadcasting sense (as in <span class="foreign">on the air</span>) first recorded 1927. To <span class="foreign">give (someone) the air</span> "dismiss" is from 1900. <span class="foreign">Air pollution</span> is attested by 1870.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/air">air (n.2)</a></dt><dd>1590s, "manner, appearance" (as in <span class="foreign">an air of mystery</span>); 1650s, "assumed manner, affected appearance" (especially in phrase <span class="foreign">put on airs</span>, 1781), from French <span class="foreign">air</span> "look, appearance, mien, bearing, tone" (Old French <span class="foreign">aire</span> "reality, essence, nature, descent, extraction," 12c.; compare <a href="/etym/s/debonair" target="_blank">debonair</a>), from Latin <span class="foreign">ager</span> "place, field" (see <a href="/etym/s/acre" target="_blank">acre</a>) on notion of "place of origin."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; But some French sources connect this Old French word with the source of <a href="/etym/s/air" target="_blank">air</a> (n.1), and it also is possible these senses in English developed from or were influenced by <span class="foreign">air</span> (n.1); compare sense development of <a href="/etym/s/atmosphere" target="_blank">atmosphere</a> and Latin <span class="foreign">spiritus</span> "breath, breeze," also "high spirit, pride," and the extended senses of <a href="/etym/s/anima" target="_blank">anima</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/air">air (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"melody, tune," 1580s, from Italian <span class="foreign">aria</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/aria" target="_blank">aria</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/air">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
