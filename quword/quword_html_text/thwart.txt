                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/thwart">thwart</a> 横跨，横过，横着的，阻止，阻挠</span><p>原义为副词，来自古诺斯语 thvert,穿过，横过，来自 Proto-Germanic*thwerh,扭转的，交叉的， 置换自 PIE*terkw,扭动，转动，来自 PIE*ter,*tere,弯，转，穿过，词源同 turn,through,trans-. 后引申动词词义相反，阻止，阻挠等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/thwart">thwart</a></dt><dd><b>thwart: </b><span>[13] <i>Thwart </i>was originally an adverb and adjective, meaning ‘across, crosswise’. It was however used as a verb, meaning ‘obstruct’ (from the metaphorical notion of ‘crossing’ someone) as early as the 13th century. It was borrowed from Old Norse <i>thvert</i>, the neuter form of <i>thverr </i>‘transverse’. This went back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>thwerkhwaz </i>(possible source also of English <i>queer</i>), which in turn was descended from Indo-European *<i>twork</i>-, *<i>twerk</i>- ‘twist’ (source also of English <i>torch</i>, <i>torment</i>, <i>torture</i>, etc).<br><br>How the noun <i>thwart </i>‘seat across a boat’ [18] fits into the picture is not altogether clear. Its modern meaning clearly connects it with <i>thwart </i>‘across’, but the notion of ‘crosswise’ may have been a secondary development. For an earlier noun <i>thought </i>‘seat in a boat’ existed, which came ultimately from Old English <i>thofta </i>‘rower’s bench’, and it could be that <i>thwart </i>the modern English noun represents a blending, both formal and semantic, of <i>thwart </i>‘across’ with the now obsolete <i>thought</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/queer" target="_blank">queer</a>, <a href="/etym/s/torch" target="_blank">torch</a>, <a href="/etym/s/torment" target="_blank">torment</a>, <a href="/etym/s/torture" target="_blank">torture</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/thwart">thwart (adv.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, from a Scandinavian source, probably Old Norse <span class="foreign">þvert</span> "across," originally neuter of <span class="foreign">thverr</span> (adj.) "transverse, across," cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">þweorh</span> "transverse, perverse, angry, cross," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*thwerh-</span> "twisted, oblique" (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">dwers</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">dwars</span> "cross-grained, contrary," Old High German <span class="foreign">twerh</span>, German <span class="foreign">quer</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">þwairhs</span> "angry"), altered (by influence of <span class="foreign">*thwer-</span> "to turn") from <span class="foreign">*therkh-</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*terkw-</span> "to twist" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">torquere</span> "to twist," Sanskrit <span class="foreign">tarkuh</span> "spindle," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">traku</span> "band, girdle," Old High German <span class="foreign">drahsil</span> "turner," German <span class="foreign">drechseln</span> "to turn on a lathe"), possibly a variant of <span class="foreign">*twerk-</span> "to cut." From mid-13c. as an adjective.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/thwart">thwart (v.)</a></dt><dd>"oppose, hinder," mid-13c., from <a href="/etym/s/thwart" target="_blank">thwart</a> (adv.). Related: <span class="foreign">Thwarted</span>; <span class="foreign">thwarting</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
