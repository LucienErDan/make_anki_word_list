                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/poison">poison</a> 毒物，毒药</span><p>来自古法语poison,饮料，尤指药饮，来自拉丁语potare,饮水，饮用，词源同potion,symposium.后词义过渡为毒物，毒药。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/poison">poison</a></dt><dd><b>poison: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, <i>poison </i>is simply something you ‘drink’. The word comes via Old French <i>poison </i>from Latin <i>pōtiō </i>‘drink’ (source also of English <i>potion</i>), a derivative of the verb <i>pōtāre </i>‘drink’ (from which English gets <i>potable</i>). The specialization in meaning from ‘drink’ to ‘poisonous drink’ took place in classical Latin, but the further progression to ‘any poisonous substance’ is a later development. Another probable relative is <i>pot</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/potable" target="_blank">potable</a>, <a href="/etym/s/potion" target="_blank">potion</a>, <a href="/etym/s/symposium" target="_blank">symposium</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/poison">poison (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "a deadly potion or substance," also figuratively, from Old French <span class="foreign">poison</span>, <span class="foreign">puison</span> (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">poison</span>) "a drink," especially a medical drink, later "a (magic) potion, poisonous drink" (14c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">potionem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">potio</span>) "a drinking, a drink," also "poisonous drink" (Cicero), from <span class="foreign">potare</span> "to drink" (see <a href="/etym/s/potion" target="_blank">potion</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; For form evolution from Latin to French, compare <span class="foreign">raison</span> from <span class="foreign">rationem</span>. The Latin word also is the source of Old Spanish <span class="foreign">pozon</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">pozione</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">pocion</span>. The more usual Indo-European word for this is represented in English by <a href="/etym/s/virus" target="_blank">virus</a>. The Old English word was <span class="foreign">ator</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/attercop" target="_blank">attercop</a>) or <span class="foreign">lybb</span>. Slang sense of "alcoholic drink" first attested 1805, American English.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; For sense evolution, compare Old French <span class="foreign">enerber</span>, <span class="foreign">enherber</span> "to kill with poisonous plants." In many Germanic languages "poison" is named by a word equivalent to English <a href="/etym/s/gift" target="_blank">gift</a> (such as Old High German <span class="foreign">gift</span>, German <span class="foreign">Gift</span>, Danish and Swedish <span class="foreign">gift</span>; Dutch <span class="foreign">gift</span>, <span class="foreign">vergift</span>). This shift might have been partly euphemistic, partly by influence of Greek <span class="foreign">dosis</span> "a portion prescribed," literally "a giving," used by Galen and other Greek physicians to mean an amount of medicine (see <a href="/etym/s/dose" target="_blank">dose</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Figuratively from late 15c.; of persons by 1910. As an adjective from 1520s; with plant names from 18c. <span class="foreign">Poison ivy</span> first recorded 1784; <span class="foreign">poison oak</span> is from 1743. <span class="foreign">Poison gas</span> first recorded 1915. <span class="foreign">Poison-pen</span> (letter) popularized 1913 by a notorious criminal case in Pennsylvania, U.S.; the phrase dates to 1898.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/poison">poison (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to give poison to; kill with poison," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">poisonner</span> "to give to drink," and directly from <a href="/etym/s/poison" target="_blank">poison</a> (n.). Figuratively from late 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Poisoned</span>; <span class="foreign">poisoning</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
