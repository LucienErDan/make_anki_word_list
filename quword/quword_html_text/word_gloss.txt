                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. gloss English has two words gloss. The one meaning 'shining surface' is of unknown origin, although no doubt it belongs ultimately to the general nexus of words beginning gl- which mean broadly 'bright, shining'.<br />
2. gloss 'explanation, definition' goes back to Greek glossa 'tongue' => 'mouthpiece, obscure word, language'. This developed the secondary sense 'language' (as English tongue itself has done), and was borrowed by Latin as glossa meaning 'obsolete or obscure or foreign word needing an explanation', and eventually the 'explanation' itself.<br />
3. 谐音“光裸色”------由光形成的裸色，很有光泽感。<br />
4. glossary => gloss.<br />
5. gl- "光"----与“光”有关的词根、单词。<br />
6. words beginning gl- which mean broadly 'bright, shining'.<br />
7. glow => gloss.<br />
8. 谐音“割了舌”-----gloss-, glott- "舌头"。<br />
9. 谐音“葛老师、哥乐释”-----葛老师经常给我们解释、作注解，哥哥很乐意给我解释、作注解。<br />
10. 谐音“光loss”----光、光泽减损、耗损了。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gloss">gloss</a> 光泽，注释</span><p>1.光泽，词源同glass, glow. </p><p> 2.注释，来自拉丁语glossa, 舌头，外来词，注释。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gloss">gloss</a></dt><dd><b>gloss: </b><span>English has two words <i>gloss</i>. The one meaning ‘shining surface’ [16] is of unknown origin, although no doubt it belongs ultimately to the general nexus of words beginning <i>gl</i>- which mean broadly ‘bright, shining’. Forms such as Icelandic <i>glossi </i>‘spark’ and Swedish dialect <i>glossa </i>‘glow’ suggest a Scandinavian origin. <i>Gloss </i>‘explanation, definition’ [16] goes back to Greek <i>glossa </i>‘tongue’, source also of English <i>epiglottis </i>[17].<br><br>This developed the secondary sense ‘language’ (as English <i>tongue </i>itself has done), and was borrowed by Latin as <i>glōssa </i>meaning ‘foreign word needing an explanation’, and eventually the ‘explanation’ itself. It passed into English via medieval Latin <i>glōsa </i>and Old French <i>glose </i>as <i>gloze </i>in the 14th century, and was reformulated as <i>gloss </i>on the basis of classical Latin <i>glōssa </i>in the 16th century. <i>Glossary </i>[14] comes from the Latin derivative <i>glossārium</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/epiglottis" target="_blank">epiglottis</a>, <a href="/etym/s/glossary" target="_blank">glossary</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gloss">gloss (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"glistening smoothness, luster," 1530s, probably from Scandinavian (compare Icelandic <span class="foreign">glossi</span> "flame," related to <span class="foreign">glossa</span> "to flame"), or obsolete Dutch <span class="foreign">gloos</span> "a glowing," from Middle High German <span class="foreign">glos</span>; probably ultimately from the same source as English <a href="/etym/s/glow" target="_blank">glow</a> (v.). Superficial lustrous smoothness due to the nature of the material (unlike <span class="foreign">polish</span>, which is artificial).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gloss">gloss (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"word inserted as an explanation, translation, or definition," c. 1300, <span class="foreign">glose</span> (modern form from 1540s; earlier also <span class="foreign">gloze</span>), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">glossa</span> "obsolete or foreign word," one that requires explanation; later extended to the explanation itself, from Greek <span class="foreign">glossa</span> (Ionic), <span class="foreign">glotta</span> (Attic) "language, a tongue; word of mouth, hearsay," also "obscure or foreign word, language," also "mouthpiece," literally "the tongue" (as the organ of speech), from PIE <span class="foreign">*glogh-</span> "thorn, point, that which is projected" (source also of Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">glogu</span> "thorn," Greek <span class="foreign">glokhis</span> "barb of an arrow").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Glosses were common in the Middle Ages, usually rendering Hebrew, Greek, or Latin words into vernacular Germanic, Celtic, or Romanic. Originally written between the lines, later in the margins. By early 14c. in a bad sense, "deceitful explanation, commentary that disguises or shifts meaning." This sense probably has been colored by <a href="/etym/s/gloss" target="_blank">gloss</a> (n.1). Both <span class="foreign">glossology</span> (1716) and <span class="foreign">glottology</span> (1841) have been used in the sense "science of language."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gloss">gloss (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">glosen</span> "use fair words; speak smoothly, cajole, flatter;" late 14c. as "comment on (a text), insert a word as an explanation, interpret," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">glossare</span> and Old French <span class="foreign">gloser</span>, from Late Latin <span class="foreign">glossa</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/gloss" target="_blank">gloss</a> (n.2)). Modern spelling from 16c.; formerly also <span class="foreign">gloze</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The other verb, meaning "to add luster, make smooth and shining," is from 1650s, from <a href="/etym/s/gloss" target="_blank">gloss</a> (n.1). Figurative sense of "smooth over, hide" is from 1729, mostly from the first verb, in its extended sense of "explain away, veil or shift the meaning of," but showing influence of the second. Related: <span class="foreign">Glossed</span>; <span class="foreign">glossing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
