            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hide">hide</a> 隐藏，隐蔽，兽皮</span><p>来自PIE*skeu,隐藏，遮盖，词源同house,cuticle,sky.引申词义兽皮。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hide">hide</a></dt><dd><b>hide: </b><span>English has two words <i>hide </i>in current usage, probably from an identical Indo-European source. The verb, ‘conceal’ [OE], which has no living relatives among the Germanic languages, comes from a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>khūdjan</i>. This was derived from a base which probably also produced English <i>hoard</i>, <i>huddle</i>, and <i>hut</i>, and goes back to Indo-European *<i>keudh</i>-, source also of Greek <i>keúthein </i>‘cover, hide’, Welsh <i>cuddio </i>‘hide’, and Breton <i>kuzat </i>‘hide’. <i>Hide </i>‘skin’ [OE] and its Germanic relatives, German <i>haut</i>, Dutch <i>huid</i>, and Swedish and Danish <i>hud</i>, come ultimately from Indo-European *<i>keut</i>-, which also produced Latin <i>cutis </i>‘skin’ (source of English <i>cuticle </i>[17] and <i>cutaneous </i>[16]) and Welsh <i>cwd </i>‘scrotum’.<br><br>The semantic link between the two <i>hides </i>is ‘covering’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/hoard" target="_blank">hoard</a>, <a href="/etym/s/huddle" target="_blank">huddle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hut" target="_blank">hut</a>; <a href="/etym/s/cutaneous" target="_blank">cutaneous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cuticle" target="_blank">cuticle</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hide">hide (v.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hydan</span> "to hide, conceal; preserve; hide oneself; bury a corpse," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*hudjan</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">huden</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*keudh-</span> (source also of Greek <span class="foreign">keuthein</span> "to hide, conceal"), from root <span class="foreign">*(s)keu-</span> "to cover, conceal" (see <a href="/etym/s/hide" target="_blank">hide</a> (n.1)). <span class="foreign">Hide and seek</span> (by 1670s), children's game, replaced earlier <span class="foreign">all hid</span> (1580s).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hide">hide (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"skin of a large animal," Old English <span class="foreign">hyd</span> "hide, skin," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hudiz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">huð</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hed</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">huut</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">huid</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hut</span>, German <span class="foreign">Haut</span> "skin"), related to Old English verb <span class="foreign">hydan</span> "to hide," the common notion being of "covering."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; All of this is from PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)keu-</span> "to cover, conceal" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kostha</span> "enclosing wall," <span class="foreign">skunati</span> "covers;" Armenian <span class="foreign">ciw</span> "roof;" Latin <span class="foreign">cutis</span> "skin," <span class="foreign">scutum</span> "shield," <span class="foreign">ob-scurus</span> "dark;" Greek <span class="foreign">kytos</span> "a hollow, vessel," <span class="foreign">keutho</span> "to cover, to hide," <span class="foreign">skynia</span> "eyebrows;" Russian <span class="foreign">kishka</span> "gut," literally "sheath;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kiautas</span> "husk," <span class="foreign">kutis</span> "stall;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">sky</span> "cloud;" Old English <span class="foreign">sceo</span> "cloud;" Middle High German <span class="foreign">hode</span> "scrotum;" Old High German <span class="foreign">scura</span>, German <span class="foreign">Scheuer</span> "barn;" Welsh <span class="foreign">cuddio</span> "to hide").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The alliterative pairing of <span class="foreign">hide</span> and <span class="foreign">hair</span> (often negative, <span class="foreign">hide nor hair</span>) was in Middle English (early 15c.), but earlier and more common was <span class="foreign">hide ne hewe</span>, literally "skin and complexion ('hue')" (c. 1200).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hide">hide (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"measure of land" (obsolete), Old English <span class="foreign">hid</span> "hide of land," earlier <span class="foreign">higid</span>, from <span class="foreign">hiw-</span> "family" (related to <span class="foreign">hiwan</span> "household," <span class="foreign">hiwo</span> "a husband, master of a household"), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hiwido-</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*keiwo-</span> (source also of Latin <span class="foreign">civis</span> "citizen"), from root <span class="foreign">*kei-</span> "to lie; bed, couch; beloved, dear" (see <a href="/etym/s/cemetery" target="_blank">cemetery</a>, and compare <a href="/etym/s/city" target="_blank">city</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The notion was of "amount of land needed to feed one free family and dependents," usually 100 or 120 acres, but the amount could be as little as 60, depending on the quality of the land. Often also defined as "as much land as could be tilled by one plow in a year." Translated in Latin as <span class="foreign">familia</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
