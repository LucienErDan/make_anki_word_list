                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bug">bug</a> 小昆虫，故障</span><p>来自古英语词bugge, 妖怪，令人害怕之物。后指昆虫。故障义据说来自大发明家爱迪生，在检察某机器故障时发现是由于里面死了一个小虫。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bug">bug</a></dt><dd><b>bug: </b><span>[14] Originally, <i>bug </i>meant ‘something frightening’ – and in fact one of the earliest known uses of the word was for what we would now call a ‘scare-crow’. It is one of a set of words (others are <i>bogle </i>and perhaps <i>bugaboo</i>) for alarming or annoying phenomena, usually supernatural, whose interrelationship and ultimate source have never been adequately explained (see BOGEY). <i>Bug </i>‘insect’ [16] is probably the same word, although it has also been connected with Old English <i>budd </i>‘beetle’. The meanings ‘defect’ (from the 19th century) and ‘germ’ and ‘hidden microphone’ (both 20th-century) all developed from ‘insect’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bug">bug (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to annoy, irritate," 1949, probably from <a href="/etym/s/bug" target="_blank">bug</a> (n.) and a reference to insect pests. Sense of "equip with a concealed microphone" is from 1919. Related: <span class="foreign">Bugged</span>; <span class="foreign">bugging</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bug">bug (n.)</a></dt><dd>"insect," 1620s (earliest reference is to bedbugs), of unknown origin, probably but not certainly from or influenced by Middle English <span class="foreign">bugge</span> "something frightening, scarecrow" (late 14c.), a meaning obsolete since the "insect" sense arose except in <a href="/etym/s/bugbear" target="_blank">bugbear</a> (1570s) and <a href="/etym/s/bugaboo" target="_blank">bugaboo</a> (q.v.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Probably connected with Scottish <span class="foreign">bogill</span> "goblin, bugbear," or obsolete Welsh <span class="foreign">bwg</span> "ghost, goblin" (compare Welsh <span class="foreign">bwgwl</span> "threat," earlier "fear," Middle Irish <span class="foreign">bocanách</span> "supernatural being"). Some speculate that these words are from a root meaning "goat" (see <a href="/etym/s/buck" target="_blank">buck</a> (n.1)) and represent originally a goat-like spectre. Compare also <a href="/etym/s/bogey" target="_blank">bogey</a> (n.1) and German <span class="foreign">bögge, böggel-mann</span> "goblin." Perhaps influenced in meaning by Old English <span class="foreign">-budda</span> used in compounds for "beetle" (compare Low German <span class="foreign">budde</span> "louse, grub," Middle Low German <span class="foreign">buddech</span> "thick, swollen"). &#13; <blockquote>&#13; In the United States bug is not confined, as in England, to the domestic pest, but is applied to all insects of the Coleoptera order, which includes what in this country are generally called beetles. [Farmer &amp; Henley, "Dictionary of Slang and Colloquial English," 1912 abridged edition]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Meaning "defect in a machine" (1889) may have been coined c. 1878 by Thomas Edison (perhaps with the notion of an insect getting into the works). Meaning "person obsessed by an idea" (such as <a href="/etym/s/firebug" target="_blank">firebug</a>) is from 1841, perhaps from notion of persistence. Sense of "microbe, germ" is from 1919. <span class="foreign">Bugs</span> "crazy" is from c. 1900. <span class="foreign">Bug juice</span> as a slang name for drink is from 1869, originally "bad whiskey." The 1811 slang dictionary has <span class="foreign">bug-hunter</span> "an upholsterer." <span class="foreign">Bug-word</span> "word or words meant to irritate and vex" is from 1560s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bug">bug (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to bulge, protrude," 1872, originally of eyes, perhaps from a humorous or dialect mispronunciation of <span class="foreign">bulge</span> (v.). Related: <span class="foreign">Bugged</span>; <span class="foreign">bugging</span>. As an adjective, <span class="foreign">bug-eyed</span> recorded from 1872; so commonly used of space creatures in mid-20c. science fiction that the initialism (acronym) <span class="foreign">BEM</span> for <span class="foreign">bug-eyed monster</span> was current by 1953.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bug">bug (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"to scram, skedaddle," 1953, of uncertain origin, perhaps related to <a href="/etym/s/bug" target="_blank">bug</a> (v.2), and compare <a href="/etym/s/bug_off" target="_blank">bug off</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. He heard that they were planning to <b>bug</b> his office. </dt> <dd>他得知他们打算在他办公室安装窃听器。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. There was a <b>bug</b> going around at the club. </dt> <dd>这个俱乐部流行着一种轻微的传染病。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4.
