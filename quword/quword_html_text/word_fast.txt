                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fast">fast</a> 稳固的，快的，绝食</span><p>来自PIE*past,固定，稳固，进一步来自PIE*pag, 固定，连接，词源同fang, pact.用于宗教术语斋戒，绝食。同时由稳固的衍生词义快速的。类似词义演变参照very, 原义真实的，fair, 原义美丽的，或汉语成语欲速则不达。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fast">fast</a></dt><dd><b>fast: </b><span>[OE] Widely dissimilar as they now seem, <i>fast </i>‘quick’ and <i>fast </i>‘abstain from food’ in fact come from the same ultimate source. This was Germanic *<i>fastuz</i>, which denoted ‘firm’. That underlying sense persists in various contexts, such as ‘hold fast’ and ‘fast friend’. The verbal application to ‘eating no food’ originated in the notion of ‘holding fast to a particular observance’ – specifically, abstinence from food.<br><br>The use of <i>fast </i>for ‘quick’ is a much later development, dating from the 13th century. It probably comes from a perception of <i>fast </i>‘firm’ containing an underlying connotation of ‘extremity’ or ‘severity’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fast">fast (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fæst</span> "firmly fixed, steadfast, constant; secure; enclosed, watertight; strong, fortified," probably from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fastu-</span> "firm, fast" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fest</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fastr</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vast</span>, German <span class="foreign">fest</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*past-</span> "firm, solid" (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pastyam</span> "dwelling place").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "rapid, quick" is from 1550s, from the adverb (q.v.). Of colors, from 1650s; of clocks, from 1840. The sense of "living an unrestrained life, eager in pursuit of pleasure" (usually of women) is from 1746 (<span class="foreign">fast living</span> is from 1745). <span class="foreign">Fast buck</span> recorded from 1947; <span class="foreign">fast food</span> is first attested 1951. <span class="foreign">Fast lane</span> is by 1966; the <span class="foreign">fast track</span> originally was in horse-racing (1934), one that permits maximum speed; figurative sense by 1960s. <span class="foreign">Fast-forward</span> is by 1948, originally of audio tape.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fast">fast (v.)</a></dt><dd>"abstain from food," Old English <span class="foreign">fæstan</span> "to fast" (as a religious duty), also "to make firm; establish, confirm, pledge," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fastan</span> "to hold fast, observe abstinence" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">festia</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fasten</span>, German <span class="foreign">fasten</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fasta</span> "abstain from food"), from the same root as <a href="/etym/s/fast" target="_blank">fast</a> (adj.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The original meaning in prehistoric Germanic was "hold firmly," and the sense evolved via "have firm control of oneself," to "hold oneself to observance" (compare Gothic <span class="foreign">fastan</span> "to keep, observe," also "to fast"). Perhaps the Germanic sense shifted through use of the native words to translate Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">observare</span> in its sense "to fast." The verb in the sense "to make fast" continued in Middle English, but was superseded by <span class="foreign">fasten</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Fasted</span>; <span class="foreign">fasting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fast">fast (n.)</a></dt><dd>"act of fasting," late Old English <span class="foreign">fæsten</span> "voluntary abstinence from food and drink or from certain kinds of food," especially, but not necessarily, as a religious duty; either from the verb in Old English or from Old Norse <span class="foreign">fasta</span> "a fast, fasting, season for fasting," from a Proto-Germanic noun formed from the verbal root of <a href="/etym/s/fast" target="_blank">fast</a> (v.). In earlier Old English <span class="foreign">fæsten</span> meant "fortress, cloister, enclosure, prison."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fast">fast (adv.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fæste</span> "firmly, securely; strictly;" also, perhaps, "speedily," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fasto</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fasto</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">feste</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vast</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fasto</span>, German <span class="foreign">fast</span> "firmly, immovably, strongly, very"), from <span class="foreign">*fastu-</span> (adj.) "firm, fast" (see <a href="/etym/s/fast" target="_blank">fast</a> (adj.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The meaning "quickly, swiftly, rapidly" was perhaps in Old English, certainly by c. 1200, probably from or developed under influence of Old Norse <span class="foreign">fast</span> "firmly, fast." This sense developed, apparently in Scandinavian, from that of "firmly, strongly, vigorously" (<span class="foreign">to run hard</span> means the same as <span class="foreign">to run fast</span>; also compare <span class="foreign">fast asleep</span>, also compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">drekka fast</span> "to drink hard," <span class="foreign">telja fast</span> "to give (someone) a severe lesson"). Or perhaps from the notion of a runner who "sticks" close to whatever he is chasing (compare Old Danish <span class="foreign">fast</span> "much, swiftly, at once, near to, almost," and sense evolution of German <span class="foreign">fix</span> "fast, fixed; fast, quick, nimble," from Latin <span class="foreign">fixus</span>). The expression <span class="foreign">fast by</span> "near, close, beside" also is said to be from Scandinavian. To <span class="foreign">fast talk</span> someone (v.) is recorded by 1946.</dd></dl></div></div>
