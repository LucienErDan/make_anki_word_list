                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/buff">buff</a> 爱好者，浅黄褐色</span><p>来自buffalo leather, 水牛皮，黄褐色。爱好者义来自美国历史上1820年纽约志愿者救火队的制服颜色，这群志愿者在当时享有非常大的声誉。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buff">buff</a></dt><dd><b>buff: </b><span>[16] <i>Buff </i>originally meant ‘buffalo’; it was presumably an alteration of the French word <i>buffe </i>‘buffalo’. That sense had died out by the early 18th century, but since then the word has undergone a bizarre series of semantic changes. First, it came to mean ‘leather’, originally from buffalo hides, but later from ox hides. This was commonly used in the 16th and 17th centuries for making military uniforms, so <i>be in buff </i>came to mean ‘be in the army’.<br><br>Then in the 17th century the associations of ‘hide’ and ‘skin’ led to the expression <i>in the buff </i>‘naked’. The colour of buff leather, a sort of dull yellowish-brown, led to the word’s adoption in the 18th century as a colour term. In the 19th century, soft buff or suede leather was used for the small pads or wheels used by silversmiths, watchmakers, etc for polishing: hence the verb <i>buff </i>‘polish’.<br><br>And finally, in the 1820s New York City volunteer firemen were known as ‘buffs’, from the colour of their uniforms; thus anyone who was a volunteer or enthusiastic for something became known as a <i>buff </i>(as in ‘film buff’). The <i>buff </i>of <i>blind-man’s buff </i>is a different word. It meant ‘blow, punch’, and was borrowed in the 15th century from Old French <i>buffe</i>, source also of English <i>buffet </i>‘blow’ [13].<br><br>The term <i>blind-man’s buff </i>is first recorded around 1600, some what later than its now obsolete synonym <i>hoodman blind</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/buffalo" target="_blank">buffalo</a>, <a href="/etym/s/buffet" target="_blank">buffet</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buff">buff (n.)</a></dt><dd>1570s, <span class="foreign">buffe leather</span> "leather made of buffalo hide," from Middle French <span class="foreign">buffle</span> "buffalo" (15c., via Italian, from Latin <span class="foreign">bufalus</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/buffalo" target="_blank">buffalo</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The color term comes from the hue of buffalo hides (later ox hides). Association of "hide" and "skin" led c. 1600 to <span class="foreign">in the buff</span>. Buff-colored uniforms of New York City volunteer firefighters since 1820s led to meaning "enthusiast" (1903).<blockquote>The Buffs are men and boys whose love of fires, fire-fighting and firemen is a predominant characteristic. [N.Y. "Sun," Feb. 4, 1903]</blockquote>&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buff">buff (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"well-built, hunky," 1980s, from <a href="/etym/s/buff" target="_blank">buff</a> (v.) "polish, make attractive."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/buff">buff (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to polish, make attractive," 1885, in reference to the treatment of buff leather or else to the use of buff cloth in polishing metals, from <a href="/etym/s/buff" target="_blank">buff</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Buffed</span>; <span class="foreign">buffing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
