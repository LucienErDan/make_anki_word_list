                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/wit">wit</a> 才思，智慧</span><p>来自PIE*weid,看，知道，词源同visit,wise。引申词义见多思广，智慧。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wit">wit</a></dt><dd><b>wit: </b><span>Both the noun <i>wit </i>[OE] and the verb [OE] go back ultimately to the Indo-European base *<i>woid</i>-, *<i>weid</i>-, *<i>wid</i>-. This originally meant ‘see’, in which sense it has given English <i>visible</i>, <i>vision</i>, etc, but it developed metaphorically to ‘know’, and it is this sense that lies behind English <i>wit</i>. The noun to begin with denoted ‘mind, understanding, judgement, sense’ (a meaning preserved in expressions such as ‘keep one’s wits about one’ and ‘slow-witted’), and the modern sense ‘clever humorousness’ did not begin to emerge until the 16th century.<br><br>The verb has now virtually died out, except in the expression <i>to wit</i>. <i>Witness </i>is etymologically the state of ‘knowing’. Other English words that come from the same Indo-European base or its Germanic descendant include <i>guide</i>, <i>history</i>, <i>idea</i>, <i>story</i>, and <i>twit</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/guide" target="_blank">guide</a>, <a href="/etym/s/guise" target="_blank">guise</a>, <a href="/etym/s/history" target="_blank">history</a>, <a href="/etym/s/idea" target="_blank">idea</a>, <a href="/etym/s/story" target="_blank">story</a>, <a href="/etym/s/twit" target="_blank">twit</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vision" target="_blank">vision</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wise" target="_blank">wise</a>, <a href="/etym/s/witness" target="_blank">witness</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wit">wit (n.)</a></dt><dd>"mental capacity," Old English <span class="foreign">wit</span>, <span class="foreign">witt</span>, more commonly <span class="foreign">gewit</span> "understanding, intellect, sense; knowledge, consciousness, conscience," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wit-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">wit</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vit</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">vid</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">vett</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">wit</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">wizzi</span> "knowledge, understanding, intelligence, mind," German <span class="foreign">Witz</span> "wit, witticism, joke," Gothic <span class="foreign">unwiti</span> "ignorance"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*weid-</span> "to see," metaphorically "to know" (see <a href="/etym/s/vision" target="_blank">vision</a>). Related to Old English <span class="foreign">witan</span> "to know" (source of <a href="/etym/s/wit" target="_blank">wit</a> (v.)). Meaning "ability to connect ideas and express them in an amusing way" is first recorded 1540s; that of "person of wit or learning" is from late 15c. For nuances of usage, see <a href="/etym/s/humor" target="_blank">humor</a>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; A witty saying proves nothing. [Voltaire, <span class="foreign">Diner du Comte de Boulainvilliers</span>]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; Wit ought to be five or six degrees above the ideas that form the intelligence of an audience. [Stendhal, "Life of Henry Brulard"]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Witjar</span> was old slang (18c.) for "head, skull." <span class="foreign">Witling</span> (1690s) was "a pretender to wit."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wit">wit (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to know" (archaic), Old English <span class="foreign">witan</span> (past tense <span class="foreign">wast</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">witen</span>) "to know, beware of or conscious of, understand, observe, ascertain, learn," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*witan</span> "to have seen," hence "to know" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">witan</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vita</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">wita</span>, Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">weten</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">wizzan</span>, German <span class="foreign">wissen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">witan</span> "to know"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*weid-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/wit" target="_blank">wit</a> (n.)). The phrase <span class="foreign">to wit</span>, almost the only surviving use of the verb, is first recorded 1570s, from earlier <span class="foreign">that is to wit</span> (mid-14c.), probably a loan-translation of Anglo-French <span class="foreign">cestasavoir</span>, used to render Latin <span class="foreign">videlicet</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/viz." target="_blank">viz.</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
