                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. 谐音“鞋(四川方言发音：hai)哦、后哦”------鞋后哦-----脚后哦-----脚后跟哦。<br />
2. high heel 高跟鞋.<br />
3. heels 高跟鞋.<br />
4. 阿喀琉斯之踵（Achilles' Heel），原指阿喀琉斯的脚跟，因是其唯一一个没有浸泡到神水的地方，是他唯一的弱点。后来在特洛伊战争中被人射中致命，现在一般是指致命的弱点，要害。<br />
<br />
阿喀琉斯，是凡人珀琉斯和美貌仙女忒提斯的宝贝儿子。忒提斯为了让儿子炼成“金钟罩”，在他刚出生时就将其倒提着浸进冥河，遗憾的是，乖儿被母亲捏住的脚后跟却不慎露在水外，全身留下了惟一一处“死穴”。后来，阿喀琉斯被太阳神阿波罗一箭射中了脚踝而死去。后人常以“阿喀琉斯之踵”譬喻这样一个道理：即使是再强大的英雄，他也有致命的死穴或软肋。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/heel">heel</a> 脚跟</span><p>来自PIE*kenk,弯，转，膝弯，脚弯，词源同hock,kink,-el,小词后缀。引申词义脚跟。拼写比较nail,tile.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heel">heel</a></dt><dd><b>heel: </b><span>English has two separate words <i>heel</i>. The one that names the rear part of the foot [OE] comes ultimately from Germanic *<i>khangkh</i>-, which also produced English <i>hock </i>‘quadruped’s joint corresponding to the human ankle’. From it was derived *<i>khākhil</i>-, source of Dutch <i>hiel</i>, Swedish <i>häl</i>, Danish <i>hæl</i>, and English <i>heel</i>. <i>Heel </i>‘tilt, list’ [16] is probably descended from the Old English verb <i>hieldan </i>‘incline’ (which survived dialectally into the 19th century), its -<i>d </i>mistaken as a past tense or past participle ending and removed to form a new infinitive. <i>Hieldan </i>itself came ultimately from the prehistoric Germanic adjective *<i>khalthaz </i>‘inclined’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/hock" target="_blank">hock</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heel">heel (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"back of the foot," Old English <span class="foreign">hela</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hanhilon</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">hæll</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hel</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">hiel</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*kenk-</span> (3) "heel, bend of the knee" (source also of Old English <span class="foreign">hoh</span> "hock").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "back of a shoe or boot" is c. 1400. <span class="foreign">Down at heels</span> (1732) refers to heels of boots or shoes worn down and the owner too poor to replace them. For <span class="foreign">Achilles' heel</span> "only vulnerable spot" see <a href="/etym/s/Achilles" target="_blank">Achilles</a>. To "fight with (one's) heels" (<span class="foreign">fighten with heles</span>) in Middle English meant "to run away."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heel">heel (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to lean to one side," in reference to a ship, Old English <span class="foreign">hieldan</span> "incline, lean, slope," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*helthijan</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">helden</span> "to lean," Dutch <span class="foreign">hellen</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hallr</span> "inclined," Old High German <span class="foreign">halda</span>, German <span class="foreign">halde</span> "slope, declivity"). Re-spelled 16c. from Middle English <span class="foreign">hield</span>, probably by misinterpretation of <span class="foreign">-d</span> as a past tense suffix.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heel">heel (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"contemptible person," 1914 in U.S. underworld slang, originally "incompetent or worthless criminal," perhaps from a sense of "person in the lowest position" and thus from <a href="/etym/s/heel" target="_blank">heel</a> (n.1).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/heel">heel (v.1)</a></dt><dd>of a dog, "to follow or stop at a person's heels," 1810, from <a href="/etym/s/heel" target="_blank">heel</a> (n.1). Also see <a href="/etym/s/heeled" target="_blank">heeled</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
