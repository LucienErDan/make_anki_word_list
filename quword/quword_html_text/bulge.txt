                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. bulge 与 bugle 的区别。<br />
2. The Bulge => 二战时德国与盟军进行的一场著名的《突出部战役》，该战役被拍成了电影，其中文译名为《坦克大决战》。<br />
3. bugle => bug- (from Latin buculus "heifer, young ox", diminutive of bos (genitive bovis) "ox, cow") + -le (diminutive). => 原意为：young bull；后来引申为：wild ox, buffalo；再后来引申为：用牛角做的号角，于是便有了今天的含义。<br />
4. bugle => abbreviation of buglehorn "musical horn, hunting horn"；原意为牛角，由此引申为号角，也就是用牛角做的号角。<br />
5. bulge 与 budget 同源，都源于拉丁语 bulga (皮包).<br />
6. bulge：饱之。让他吃饱，吃饱了肚子就胀起来了——膨胀，鼓起。<br />
7. bulge “包”→凸出部分</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bulge">bulge</a></dt><dd><b>bulge: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, <i>bulge </i>and <i>budget </i>are the same word, and indeed when English first acquired <i>bulge </i>it was as a noun, with, like <i>budget</i>, the sense ‘pouch’. It came from Old French <i>bouge </i>‘leather bag’, a descendant of Latin <i>bulga</i>, which may have been of Gaulish origin (medieval Irish <i>bolg </i>‘bag’ has been compared). The word’s present-day connotations of ‘swelling’ and ‘protruding’ presumably go back to an early association of ‘pouches’ with ‘swelling up when filled’ (compare the case of <i>bellows </i>and <i>belly</i>, which originally meant ‘bag’, and came from a source which meant ‘swell’), but curiously, apart from an isolated instance around 1400 when <i>bulge </i>is used for a ‘hump on someone’s back’, there is no evidence for this meaning in English before the 17th century.<br><br>Additionally, from the 17th to the 19th centuries <i>bulge </i>was used for the ‘bottom of a ship’s hull’; it has now been superseded in this sense by <i>bilge </i>[15], which may well be a variant form.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bilge" target="_blank">bilge</a>, <a href="/etym/s/budget" target="_blank">budget</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bulge">bulge (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "wallet, leather bag," from Old French <span class="foreign">bouge</span>, <span class="foreign">boulge</span> "wallet, pouch, leather bag," or directly from Latin <span class="foreign">bulga</span> "leather sack" (see <a href="/etym/s/budget" target="_blank">budget</a> (n.)). Sense of "a swelling" is first recorded 1620s. <a href="/etym/s/Bilge" target="_blank">Bilge</a> (q.v.) might be a nautical variant.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bulge">bulge (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to protrude, swell out," 1670s, from <a href="/etym/s/bulge" target="_blank">bulge</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Bulged</span>; <span class="foreign">bulging</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
