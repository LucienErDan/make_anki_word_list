                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/Mass">Mass</a> 弥撒</span><p>来自拉丁语missa,解散，遣散，词源同mission,emit.宗教词义弥撒来自弥撒仪式后的解散语”Ite,missa est”,即走吧，解散了，ite,走，离开，词源同exit,missa,解散，词源同emit,est,是，词源同is,essence.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mass">mass</a> 团，块，堆，物质质量</span><p>来自拉丁语massa,大块，面团，来自PIE*mag,揉，捏，词源同make,massage.引申词义团，块，堆，以及用于物理学术语物质质量，原子质量等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mass">mass</a></dt><dd><b>mass: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>mass</i>. The one meaning ‘Eucharist’ [OE] comes from late Latin <i>missa</i>, a noun use of the feminine past participle of <i>mittere </i>‘send’ (source of English <i>admit</i>, <i>commit</i>, <i>dismiss</i>, <i>mission</i>, etc) possibly arising from <i>Ite</i>, <i>missa est </i>‘Go, it is the dismissal’, the last words of the Latin Eucharist service. <i>Mass </i>‘amount of matter’ [14] comes via Old French <i>masse </i>and Latin <i>massa </i>from Greek <i>maza </i>‘barley cake’, hence ‘lump, mass’.<br><br>The derivative <i>massive </i>[15] goes back ultimately to Vulgar Latin *<i>massīceus</i>. A possible relative is <i>massage </i>[19], a borrowing from French. It was a derivative of <i>masser </i>‘massage’, which may have been acquired from Portuguese <i>amassar </i>‘knead’, a verb based on <i>massa </i>‘mass, dough’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/admit" target="_blank">admit</a>, <a href="/etym/s/commit" target="_blank">commit</a>, <a href="/etym/s/dismiss" target="_blank">dismiss</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mission" target="_blank">mission</a>, <a href="/etym/s/transmit" target="_blank">transmit</a>; <a href="/etym/s/massage" target="_blank">massage</a>, <a href="/etym/s/massive" target="_blank">massive</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mass">mass (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to gather in a mass" (intransitive), 1560s, from <a href="/etym/s/mass" target="_blank">mass</a> (n.1) or from French <span class="foreign">masser</span>. Transitive sense by c. 1600. Related: <span class="foreign">Massed</span>; <span class="foreign">massing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mass">mass (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"lump, quantity, size," late 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">masse</span> "lump, heap, pile; crowd, large amount; ingot, bar" (11c.), and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">massa</span> "kneaded dough, lump, that which adheres together like dough," probably from Greek <span class="foreign">maza</span> "barley cake, lump, mass, ball," related to <span class="foreign">massein</span> "to knead," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*mag-</span> "to knead" (source of Lithuanian <span class="foreign">minkyti</span> "to knead," see <a href="/etym/s/macerate" target="_blank">macerate</a>). Sense extended in English 1580s to "a large quantity, amount, or number." Strict sense in physics is from 1704.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As an adjective from 1733, first attested in <span class="foreign">mass meeting</span> in American English. <span class="foreign">mass culture</span> is from 1916 in sociology (earlier in biology); <span class="foreign">mass hysteria</span> is from 1914; <span class="foreign">mass media</span> is from 1923; <span class="foreign">mass movement</span> is from 1897; <span class="foreign">mass production</span> is from 1920; <span class="foreign">mass grave</span> is from 1918; <span class="foreign">mass murder</span> from 1880.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mass">mass (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"Eucharistic service," Old English <span class="foreign">mæsse</span>, from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*messa</span> "eucharistic service," literally "dismissal," from Late Latin <span class="foreign">missa</span> "dismissal," fem. past participle of <span class="foreign">mittere</span> "to let go, send" (see <a href="/etym/s/mission" target="_blank">mission</a>); probably so called from the concluding words of the service, <span class="foreign">Ite, missa est</span>, "Go, (the prayer) has been sent," or "Go, it is the dismissal." Sometimes glossed in Old English as <span class="foreign">sendnes</span> "send-ness."</dd></dl></div></div>
