                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mean">mean</a> 意思是，意味着</span><p>来自古英语maenan,意思是，打算，来自PIE*meino,意见，意图，来自*men,思考，思想，词源同mind,mania.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mean">mean</a> 普通的，平庸的，吝啬的，小气的，刻薄的</span><p>来自古英语gemaene,普通的，公共的，一般的，来自Proto-Germanic*ga-mainiz,共有的，来自PIE*ko-moin,共有，公共，*ko,一起，词源同com-,*moin,交换，互通，词源同common,mutable.引申词义平庸的，后词义进一步贬义化，用于指吝啬的，小气的，刻薄的。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mean">mean</a> 中间的，中间值</span><p>来自拉丁语medianus,中间的，来自medius,在中间，词源同middle,medial.用于数学术语中间值等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mean">mean</a></dt><dd><b>mean: </b><span>English has three distinct words <i>mean</i>. The oldest, ‘intend’ [OE], goes back via a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>mainjan </i>to the Indo-European base *<i>men</i>- ‘think’ (source also of English <i>memory</i>, <i>mention</i>, <i>mind</i>, etc). The adjective ‘petty, stingy’ [12] originally meant ‘common, shared by all’. It comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>gamainiz </i>(source also of German <i>gemein </i>‘common, shared’), which was formed from the collective prefix *<i>ga</i>- and *<i>mainiz</i>.<br><br>This went back to an Indo-European base *<i>moi</i>-, *<i>mei</i>- ‘change, exchange’, which also lies behind English <i>mad, moult, mutate, mutual</i>, and the second syllable of <i>common</i>. <i>Mean</i>’s semantic history can be traced from ‘common to all’ via ‘inferior’ and ‘low, ignoble’ to ‘petty’. The adjective ‘intermediate, average’ [14] came via Anglo-Norman <i>meen </i>and Old French <i>meien </i>from Latin <i>mediānus </i>(source of English <i>median</i>), a derivative of <i>medius </i>‘middle’ (source of English <i>medium</i>).<br><br>It forms the basis of the plural noun <i>means </i>‘method’ [14], and of the compound adverb <i>meanwhile </i>[15].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/memory" target="_blank">memory</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mention" target="_blank">mention</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mind" target="_blank">mind</a>; <a href="/etym/s/common" target="_blank">common</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mad" target="_blank">mad</a>, <a href="/etym/s/moult" target="_blank">moult</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mutate" target="_blank">mutate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mutual" target="_blank">mutual</a>; <a href="/etym/s/median" target="_blank">median</a>, <a href="/etym/s/medium" target="_blank">medium</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mean">mean (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"intend, have in mind," Old English <span class="foreign">mænan</span> "to mean, intend, signify; tell, say; complain, lament," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*mainijan</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mena</span> "to signify," Old Saxon <span class="foreign">menian</span> "to intend, signify, make known," Dutch <span class="foreign">menen</span>, German <span class="foreign">meinen</span> "think, suppose, be of the opinion"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*meino-</span> "opinion, intent" (cognates: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">meniti</span> "to think, have an opinion," Old Irish <span class="foreign">mian</span> "wish, desire," Welsh <span class="foreign">mwyn</span> "enjoyment"), perhaps from root <span class="foreign">*men-</span> "think" (see <a href="/etym/s/mind" target="_blank">mind</a> (n.)). Conversational question <span class="foreign">you know what I mean?</span> attested by 1834.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mean">mean (adj.1)</a></dt><dd>"low-quality," c. 1200, "shared by all," from <span class="foreign">imene</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">gemæne</span> "common, public, general, universal, shared by all," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ga-mainiz</span> "possessed jointly" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mene</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gimeni</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">gemeine</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gemene</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">gemeen</span>, German <span class="foreign">gemein</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">gamains</span> "common"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ko-moin-i-</span> "held in common," a compound adjective formed from collective prefix <span class="foreign">*ko-</span> "together" (Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ga-</span>) + <span class="foreign">*moi-n-</span>, suffixed form of PIE root <span class="foreign">*mei-</span> (1) "to change, exchange" (see <a href="/etym/s/mutable" target="_blank">mutable</a>). Compare second element in <a href="/etym/s/common" target="_blank">common</a> (adj.), a word with a sense evolution parallel to that of this word.&#13; <br /><br />Of things, "inferior, second-rate," from late 14c. (a secondary sense in Old English was "false, wicked"). Notion of "so-so, mediocre" led to confusion with <a href="/etym/s/mean" target="_blank">mean</a> (n.). Meaning "inferior in rank or status" (of persons) emerged early 14c.; that of "ordinary" from late 14c.; that of "stingy, nasty" first recorded 1660s; weaker sense of "disobliging, pettily offensive" is from 1839, originally American English slang. Inverted sense of "remarkably good" (i.e. <span class="foreign">plays a mean saxophone</span>) first recorded c. 1900, perhaps from phrase <span class="foreign">no mean _______</span> "not inferior" (1590s, also, "not average," reflecting further confusion with <span class="foreign">mean</span> (n.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mean">mean (n.)</a></dt><dd>"that which is halfway between extremes," early 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">meien</span> "middle, means, intermediary," noun use of adjective from Latin <span class="foreign">medianus</span> "of or that is in the middle" (see <a href="/etym/s/mean" target="_blank">mean</a> (adj.2)). Oldest sense is musical; mathematical sense is from c. 1500. Some senes reflect confusion with <span class="foreign">mean</span> (adj.1). This is the <span class="foreign">mean</span> in <span class="foreign">by no means</span> (late 15c.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mean">mean (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"calculate an arithmetical mean," 1882, from <a href="/etym/s/mean" target="_blank">mean</a> (n.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mean">mean (adj.2)</a></dt><dd>"occupying a middle or intermediate place," mid-14c., from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">meines</span> (plural), Old French <span class="foreign">meien</span>, variant of <span class="foreign">moiien</span> "mid-, medium, common, middle-class" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">moyen</span>), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">medianus</span> "of the middle," from Latin <span class="foreign">medius</span> "in the middle" (see <a href="/etym/s/medial" target="_blank">medial</a> (adj.)). Meaning "intermediate in time" is from mid-15c. Mathematical sense is from late 14c.</dd></dl></div></div>
