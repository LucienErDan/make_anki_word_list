                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">“har”简写自“hear-听”，“rang”演变自“ring-一圈”，ue-后缀——听众站成一圈——高谈阔论。<br />
2. 长篇指责性演说，象“海浪”一样，滔滔不绝。<br />
3. 谐音“好long”-----讲了好长时间的话。<br />
4. har- "crowd, army" + ring => harang- + -ue => harangue (literally, "a large group of people crowded round").<br />
5. ring => *hring => *haring- => *harang- + -ue => harangue.<br />
6. with the idea of 'addressing' them only developing later.<br />
7. harbour, harbinger, harness <====> harangue.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/harangue">harangue</a> 长篇演说，呵斥，大声谴责</span><p>来自中古法语harangue,来自意大利语aringo,公共广场，词源同ring,环，圈。由广场引申词义慷慨陈词，长篇演讲，后用于指广场批斗，呵斥，大声谴责。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harangue">harangue</a></dt><dd><b>harangue: </b><span>[15] The original notion underlying <i>harangue </i>may have been of a large group of people crowded round, with the idea of ‘addressing’ them only developing later. The word comes via Old French <i>harangue </i>from medieval Latin <i>harenga</i>, and it has been speculated that this was perhaps acquired from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>kharikhring</i>- ‘assembly’, a compound of *<i>kharjaz </i>‘crowd’ (source of English <i>harbinger</i>, <i>harbour</i>, <i>harry</i>, and <i>herald </i>and related to <i>harness</i>) and *<i>khringaz </i>‘ring’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/harbinger" target="_blank">harbinger</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harbour" target="_blank">harbour</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harness" target="_blank">harness</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harry" target="_blank">harry</a>, <a href="/etym/s/herald" target="_blank">herald</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harangue">harangue (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-15c., <span class="foreign">arang</span>, Scottish (in English from c. 1600), from Middle French <span class="foreign">harangue</span> "a public address" (14c.), from Old Italian <span class="foreign">aringo</span> "public square, platform; pulpit; arena," from a Germanic source such as Old High German <span class="foreign">hring</span> "circle" (see <a href="/etym/s/ring" target="_blank">ring</a> (n.1)) on the notion of "circular gathering," with an <span class="foreign">-a-</span> inserted to ease Romanic pronunciation of Germanic <span class="foreign">hr-</span> (compare <a href="/etym/s/hamper" target="_blank">hamper</a> (n.1)). But Watkins and Barnhart suggest a Germanic compound, <span class="foreign">*harihring</span> "circular gathering, assembly," literally "host-ring, army-ring," with first element <span class="foreign">*hari-</span> "war-band, host" (see <a href="/etym/s/harry" target="_blank">harry</a> (v.)). From the same Germanic "ring" root via Romanic come <a href="/etym/s/rank" target="_blank">rank</a> (n.), <a href="/etym/s/range" target="_blank">range</a> (v.), <a href="/etym/s/arrange" target="_blank">arrange</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harangue">harangue (v.)</a></dt><dd>1650s, from French <span class="foreign">haranguer</span> (15c.), from Middle French <span class="foreign">harangue</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/harangue" target="_blank">harangue</a> (n.)). Related: <span class="foreign">Harangued</span>; <span class="foreign">haranguing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
