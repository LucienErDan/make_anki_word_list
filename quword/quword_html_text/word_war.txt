                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/war">war</a> 战争</span><p>来自PIE*wers,搅拌，混乱，来自PIE*wer的扩大格，转，弯，词源同wrestle,versus。引申词义战争。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/war">war</a></dt><dd><b>war: </b><span>[12] The word <i>war </i>was acquired from <i>werre</i>, the northern dialect form of Old French <i>guerre</i>. This in turn came from prehistoric Germanic *<i>werra </i>‘strife’, which was formed from the base *<i>wers</i>- (source also of English <i>worse </i>and German <i>wirren </i>‘confuse’). <i>Warrior </i>[13] is from the Old Northern French derivative <i>werreieor</i>. The diminutive of <i>guerra</i>, the Spanish equivalent of French <i>guerre</i>, gave English <i>guerilla</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/guerilla" target="_blank">guerilla</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/war">war (n.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">wyrre</span>, <span class="foreign">werre</span> "large-scale military conflict," from Old North French <span class="foreign">werre</span> "war" (Old French <span class="foreign">guerre</span> "difficulty, dispute; hostility; fight, combat, war;" Modern French <span class="foreign">guerre</span>), from Frankish <span class="foreign">*werra</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*werz-a-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">werran</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">werran</span>, German <span class="foreign">verwirren</span> "to confuse, perplex"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*wers-</span> (1) "to confuse, mix up". Cognates suggest the original sense was "to bring into confusion."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Spanish, Portuguese, and Italian <span class="foreign">guerra</span> also are from Germanic; Romanic peoples turned to Germanic for a "war" word possibly to avoid Latin <span class="foreign">bellum</span> because its form tended to merge with <span class="foreign">bello-</span> "beautiful." There was no common Germanic word for "war" at the dawn of historical times. Old English had many poetic words for "war" (<span class="foreign">wig</span>, <span class="foreign">guð</span>, <span class="foreign">heaðo</span>, <span class="foreign">hild</span>, all common in personal names), but the usual one to translate Latin <span class="foreign">bellum</span> was <span class="foreign">gewin</span> "struggle, strife" (related to <a href="/etym/s/win" target="_blank">win</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; First record of <span class="foreign">war time</span> is late 14c. <a href="/etym/s/Warpath" target="_blank">Warpath</a> (1775) is originally in reference to North American Indians, as are <span class="foreign">war-whoop</span> (1761), <span class="foreign">war-paint</span> (1826), and <span class="foreign">war-dance</span> (1757). <span class="foreign">War crime</span> first attested 1906 (in Oppenheim's "International Law"). <span class="foreign">War chest</span> is attested from 1901; now usually figurative. <span class="foreign">War games</span> translates German <span class="foreign">Kriegspiel</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/kriegspiel" target="_blank">kriegspiel</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/war">war (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to make war on," mid-12c.; see <a href="/etym/s/war" target="_blank">war</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Warred</span>; <span class="foreign">warring</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
