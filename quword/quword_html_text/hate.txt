                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hate">hate</a> 厌恶，讨厌</span><p>来自古英语hatian,讨厌，厌恶，来自PIE*kad,憎恶，词源同heinous.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hate">hate</a></dt><dd><b>hate: </b><span>[OE] There are indications that the ancestral meaning of <i>hate </i>may have been a neutral ‘strong feeling’ rather than the positive ‘dislike’. It has been traced to a prehistoric Indo-European *<i>k</i>ə <i>des</i>-, amongst whose other descendants were Greek <i>kedos</i>, which meant ‘care, anxiety, grief’, and Old Irish <i>caiss</i>, which meant ‘love’ as well as ‘hate’.<br><br>It is clear, though, that the notion of strong dislike became established fairly early, and that it was certainly the sense transmitted via Germanic *<i>khatis</i>-, source of German <i>hass</i>, Dutch <i>haat</i>, Swedish <i>hat</i>, Danish <i>had</i>, and English <i>hate</i>. The derivative <i>hatred </i>was formed from the verb in the 13th century with the suffix -<i>red </i>‘condition’, as in <i>kindred</i>.<br><br>Old French borrowed the Germanic verb *<i>khatjan </i>‘hate’ as <i>haïr</i>, and derived from it the adjective <i>haïneus</i>, acquired by English as <i>heinous </i>[14].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/heinous" target="_blank">heinous</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hate">hate (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hatian</span> "regard with extreme ill-will, have a passionate aversion to, treat as an enemy," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haton</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">haton</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hata</span>, German <span class="foreign">hassen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">hatan</span> "to hate"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kad-</span> "sorrow, hatred" (cognates: Avestan <span class="foreign">sadra-</span> "grief, sorrow, calamity," Greek <span class="foreign">kedos</span> "care, trouble, sorrow," Welsh <span class="foreign">cas</span> "pain, anger"). Related: <span class="foreign">Hated</span>; <span class="foreign">hating</span>. French <span class="foreign">haine</span> (n.), <span class="foreign">hair</span> (v.) are Germanic. <span class="foreign">Hate crime</span> attested from 1988.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hate">hate (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hete</span> "hatred, spite, envy, malice, hostility," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hatis-</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">hattr</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hat</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">haat</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">has</span>, German <span class="foreign">Hass</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">hatis</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/hate" target="_blank">hate</a> (v.)). Altered in Middle English to conform with the verb. <span class="foreign">Hate mail</span> is first attested 1951.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">

I <b>hate</b> to rush you but I have another appointment later on. </dt> <dd>我真不想催你，但我稍后还有一个约会。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. "A deal!" she said contemptuously, "I <b>hate</b> all deals." </dt> <dd>“交易！”她鄙夷地说，“我厌恶一切交易。”</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. Much as I <b>hate</b> to go, it's now or never. </dt> <dd>虽然我很讨厌去，但也就此一回了。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. They may <b>hate</b> what he does but their survival depends on him. </dt> <dd>他们或许讨厌他的营生，但他们要想活下来必须依靠他。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/hate">hate 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
