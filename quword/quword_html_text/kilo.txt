            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/kilo">kilo</a></dt><dd><b>kilo: </b><span>[19] <i>Khílioi </i>was Greek for a ‘thousand’. It was adopted in French in the 1790s as the prefix for ‘thousand’ in expressions of quantity under the new metric system, and various compound forms (<i>kilogram</i>, <i>kilolitre</i>, <i>kilometre</i>, etc) began to find their way into English from the first decade of the 19th century onwards. The first recorded instance of <i>kilo </i>being used in English for <i>kilogram </i>dates from 1870.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/kilo">kilo (n.)</a></dt><dd>1870, shortening of <a href="/etym/s/kilogram" target="_blank">kilogram</a>. Slang shortening <span class="foreign">key</span> (in drug trafficking) is attested from 1968.</dd></dl></div></div>
