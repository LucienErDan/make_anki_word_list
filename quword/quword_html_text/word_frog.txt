                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/frog">frog</a> 青蛙</span><p>来自PIE*preu, 蹦，跳，词源同frolic, fresh. 用来指青蛙。引申词义法国人，因为法国人爱吃青蛙腿而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frog">frog</a></dt><dd><b>frog: </b><span>[OE] <i>Frog </i>comes from Old English <i>frogga</i>, which probably started life as a playful alternative to the more serious <i>frosc </i>or <i>forsc</i>. This derived from the pre-historic Germanic *<i>fruskaz</i>, which also produced German <i>frosch </i>and Dutch <i>vorsch</i>. Its use as a derogatory synonym for ‘French person’ goes back to the late 18th century, and was presumably inspired by the proverbial French appetite for the animals’ legs (although in fact <i>frog </i>as a general term of abuse can be traced back to the 14th century, and in the 17th century it was used for ‘Dutch person’).<br><br>It is not clear whether <i>frog </i>‘horny wedge-shaped pad in a horse’s hoof’ [17] and <i>frog </i>‘ornamental braiding’ [18] are the same word; the former may have been influenced by French <i>fourchette </i>and Italian <i>forchetta</i>, both literally ‘little fork’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frog">frog (n.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">frogga</span> "frog," a diminutive of <span class="foreign">frosc</span>, <span class="foreign">forsc</span>, <span class="foreign">frox</span> "frog," a common Germanic word but with different formations that are difficult to explain (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">froskr</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vorsc</span>, German <span class="foreign">Frosch</span> "frog"), probably literally "hopper," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*preu-</span> "to hop" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">provate</span> "hops," Russian <span class="foreign">prygat</span> "to hop, jump"). Watkins calls the Old English <span class="foreign">-gga</span> an "obscure expressive suffix."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Latin word for it (<span class="foreign">rana</span>) is imitative of croaking. Also in Middle English as <span class="foreign">frok</span>, <span class="foreign">vrogge</span>, <span class="foreign">frugge</span>, and with sometimes plural form <span class="foreign">froggen</span>. Collateral Middle English forms <span class="foreign">frude</span>, <span class="foreign">froud</span> are from Old Norse <span class="foreign">frauðr</span> "frog," and native alternative form <span class="foreign">frosk</span> "frog" survived in English dialects into the 19c.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; I always eat fricasseed frogs regretfully; they remind one so much of miniature human thighs, and make one feel cannibalistic and horrid .... [H. Ellen Brown, "A Girl's Wanderings in Hungary," 1896] &#13; </blockquote>&#13; As a British derogatory term for "Frenchman," 1778 (short for <span class="foreign">frog-eater</span>), but before that (1650s) it meant "Dutch" (from <span class="foreign">frog-land</span> "marshy land," in reference to their country). To have a <span class="foreign">frog in the throat</span> "be hoarse" is from 1892, from <span class="foreign">frog</span> as a name for a lump or swelling in the mouth (1650s) or throat infections causing a croaking sound.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frog">frog (n.2)</a></dt><dd>type of fastening for clothing, 1719, originally a belt loop for carrying a weapon, of unknown origin; perhaps from Portuguese <span class="foreign">froco</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">floccus</span> "flock of wool."</dd></dl></div></div>
