            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/freeze">freeze</a> 冰冻</span><p>来自PIE*preus, 冷，冰冻，发烧，词源同frost, prurient. 这种看似矛盾的词义比较calm，或感冒发烧，长冻疮等的病症。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/freeze">freeze</a></dt><dd><b>freeze: </b><span>[OE] <i>Freeze </i>is an ancient word, which traces its history back to Indo-European *<i>preus</i>- (source also of Latin <i>pruīna </i>‘hoarfrost’). Its Germanic descendant was *<i>freusan</i>, from which come German <i>frieren</i>, Dutch <i>vriezen</i>, Swedish <i>frysa</i>, and English <i>freeze</i>. The noun <i>frost </i>[OE] was formed in the prehistoric Germanic period from a weakly stressed variant of the base of *<i>freusan </i>plus the suffix -<i>t</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/frost" target="_blank">frost</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/freeze">freeze (v.)</a></dt><dd>alteration of <span class="foreign">freese</span>, <span class="foreign">friese</span>, from Middle English <span class="foreign">fresen</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">freosan</span> (intransitive) "turn to ice" (class II strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">freas</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">froren</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*freusan</span> "to freeze" (cognates: Dutch <span class="foreign">vriezen</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">frjosa</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">friosan</span>, German <span class="foreign">frieren</span> "to freeze," and related to Gothic <span class="foreign">frius</span> "frost"), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*freus-</span>, equivalent to PIE root <span class="foreign">*preus-</span> "to freeze," also "to burn" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">prusva</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">pruina</span> "hoarfrost," Welsh <span class="foreign">rhew</span> "frost," Sanskrit <span class="foreign">prustah</span> "burnt," Albanian <span class="foreign">prus</span> "burning coals," Latin <span class="foreign">pruna</span> "a live coal").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Of weather, "be cold enough to freeze," 13c. Meaning "perish from cold" is c. 1300. Transitive sense "harden into ice, congeal as if by frost" first recorded late 14c.; figurative sense late 14c., "make hard or unfeeling." Intransitive meaning "become rigid or motionless" attested by 1720. Sense of "fix at a certain level" is from 1933; of assets, "make non-transactable," from 1922. <span class="foreign">Freeze frame</span> is from 1960, originally "a briefly Frozen Shot after the Jingle to allow ample time for Change over at the end of a T.V. 'Commercial.' " ["ABC of Film &amp; TV," 1960].</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/freeze">freeze (n.)</a></dt><dd>"freezing conditions," c. 1400, from <a href="/etym/s/freeze" target="_blank">freeze</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
