                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1、archi- + pelag- + -o.<br />
2、直接来源于意大利语的单词多以o结尾，这是由意大利语单词的特性决定的(这就是意大利语单词的特点)。<br />
3、其实，该词的原始含义是指“爱琴海”，该词是意大利语中对爱琴海(Greek: Aigaion pelagos, English: Aegean Sea)名称的误写；由于爱琴海中布满了岛链、岛屿，因此该词的含义后来扩展为任何包含了很多岛屿的海，以及由很多岛屿组成的群岛、列岛。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/archipelago">archipelago</a> 群岛</span><p>前缀arch-, 始，主要。词根pelag, 海，见pelagic, 海面的，词源同plan, 平的。指爱琴海，后指爱琴海众多小岛。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/archipelago">archipelago</a></dt><dd><b>archipelago: </b><span>[16] Originally, <i>archipelago </i>was a quite specific term – it was the name of the Aegean Sea, the sea between Greece and Turkey. Derivationally, it is a compound formed in Greek from <i>arkhi</i>- ‘chief’ and <i>pélagos </i>‘sea’ (source of English <i>pelagic </i>[17] and probably related to <i>plain</i>, <i>placate</i>, and <i>please</i>). The term ‘chief sea’ identified the Aegean, as contrasted with all the smaller lagoons, lakes, and inlets to which the word <i>pélagos </i>was also applied.<br><br>An ‘Englished’ form of the word, <i>Arch-sea</i>, was in use in the 17th century, and in sailors’ jargon it was often abbreviated to <i>Arches</i>: ‘An island called Augusto near Paros, in the Arches’, Sir T Roe, <i>Negotiations </i>1626. A leading characteristic of the Aegean Sea is of course that it contains a large number of islands, and from the 16th century onwards we see a strong and steady move towards what is now the word’s main meaning, ‘large group of islands’.<br><br>The immediate source of the English word was Italian <i>arcipelago</i>, and some etymologists have speculated that rather than coming directly from Greek <i>arkhipélagos</i>, this may have been a sort of folk-etymological resuscitation of it based on a misunderstanding of Greek <i>Aigaion pelagos </i>‘Aegean Sea’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pelagic" target="_blank">pelagic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/archipelago">archipelago (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1500, from Italian <span class="foreign">arcipelago</span> "the Aegean Sea" (13c.), from Greek <span class="foreign">arkhipelagos</span>, from <span class="foreign">arkhi-</span> "chief" (see <a href="/etym/s/archon" target="_blank">archon</a>) + <span class="foreign">pelagos</span> "sea" (see <a href="/etym/s/pelagic" target="_blank">pelagic</a>). The Aegean Sea being full of island chains, the meaning was extended in Italian to "any sea studded with islands." Klein, noting the absence of <span class="foreign">arkhipelagos</span> in ancient or Medieval Greek (the modern word in Greek is borrowed from Italian) believe it is an Italian mistake for <span class="foreign">Aigaion pelagos</span> "Aegean Sea" (Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">Egeopelagus</span>), or influenced by that name.</dd></dl></div></div>
