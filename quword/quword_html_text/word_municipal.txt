                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1、muni- + cip- + -al.<br />
2、字面含义：that which take or hold of duties, the town or city that take or hold of duties.<br />
3、含义：free town, city whose citizens have the privileges of Roman citizens but are governed by their own laws.<br />
3、也就是说：城市、市镇拥有自己的职权。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/municipal">municipal</a> 市政的，市政当局的，地方自治的</span><p>来自拉丁语municeps,市民，自治镇公民，来自munus,共同职责，共同责任，相互给予，礼 物，来自PIE*mei,交换，改变，词源同common,mutual.-cep,承担，词源同capable,accept.该 词原指古罗马时期享有罗马公民的特权但是按照自己的法律进行管理的特别自治镇。后词义 通用化，引申词义市政的，市政当局的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/municipal">municipal</a></dt><dd><b>municipal: </b><span>[16] Latin <i>mūnus </i>meant ‘office, duty, gift’. Combined with -<i>ceps </i>‘taker’ (a derivative of the verb <i>capere </i>‘take’, source of English <i>capture</i>) it formed <i>mūniceps</i>, which denoted a ‘citizen of a Roman city (known as a <i>mūnicipium</i>) whose inhabitants had Roman citizenship but could not be magistrates’. From <i>mūnicipium </i>was derived the adjective <i>mūnicipālis</i>, source of English <i>municipal</i>; this was originally used for ‘of the internal affairs of a state, domestic’, and the modern application to the sphere of local government did not emerge strongly until the 19th century.<br><br>The stem of Latin <i>mūnus </i>also crops up in <i>commūnis </i>(source of English <i>common</i>), and so <i>community </i>and <i>municipality </i>are etymologically related. <i>Mūnus </i>in the later sense ‘gift’ formed the basis of the Latin adjective <i>mūnificus </i>‘giving gifts’, hence ‘generous’, from which ultimately English gets <i>munificent </i>[16].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/capture" target="_blank">capture</a>, <a href="/etym/s/common" target="_blank">common</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/municipal">municipal (adj.)</a></dt><dd>1540s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">municipal</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">municipalis</span> "of a citizen of a free town, of a free town," also "of a petty town, provincial," from <span class="foreign">municipium</span> "free town, city whose citizens have the privileges of Roman citizens but are governed by their own laws," from <span class="foreign">municeps</span> "citizen, inhabitant of a free town." Second element is root of <span class="foreign">capere</span> "assume, take" (see <a href="/etym/s/capable" target="_blank">capable</a>). First element is from <span class="foreign">munus</span> (plural <span class="foreign">munia</span>) "service performed for the community, duty, work," also "public spectacle paid for by the magistrate, (gladiatorial) entertainment, gift," from Old Latin <span class="foreign">moenus</span> "service, duty, burden," from PIE <span class="foreign">*moi-n-es-</span>, generally taken as a suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*mei-</span> (1) "to change, go, move" (Watkins; see <a href="/etym/s/mutable" target="_blank">mutable</a>); but Tucker says "more probably" from the other PIE root <span class="foreign">&#13; *mei-</span> meaning "bind," so that <span class="foreign">munia</span> = "obligations" and <span class="foreign">communis</span> = "bound together."</dd></dl></div></div>
