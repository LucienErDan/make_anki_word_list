                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/police">police</a> 警察</span><p>来自拉丁语politia,城市管理，来自希腊语polis,城市，词源同policy,polity.该词为policy对词，后用于指警察。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/police">police</a></dt><dd><b>police: </b><span>[16] Etymologically, the <i>police </i>are in charge of the administration of a ‘city’. In fact, <i>police </i>is essentially the same word as <i>policy </i>‘plan of action’. Both go back to Latin <i>polītīa </i>‘civil administration’, a descendant of Greek <i>pólis </i>‘city’. In medieval Latin a variant <i>polītia </i>emerged, which became French <i>police</i>.<br><br>English took it over, and at first continued to use it for ‘civil administration’ (Edmund Burke as late as 1791 described the Turks as ‘a barbarous nation, with a barbarous neglect of police, fatal to the human race’). Its specific application to the administration of public order emerged in France in the early 18th century, and the first body of public-order officers to be named <i>police </i>in England was the Marine Police, a force set up around 1798 to protect merchandise in the Port of London.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/politics" target="_blank">politics</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/police">police (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1530, at first essentially the same word as <a href="/etym/s/policy" target="_blank">policy</a> (n.1); from Middle French <span class="foreign">police</span> (late 15c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">politia</span> "civil administration," from Greek <span class="foreign">polis</span> "city" (see <a href="/etym/s/polis" target="_blank">polis</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Until mid-19c. used in England for "civil administration;" application to "administration of public order" (1716) is from French (late 17c.), and originally in English referred to France or other foreign nations. The first force so-named in England was the Marine Police, set up 1798 to protect merchandise at the Port of London. <span class="foreign">Police state</span> "state regulated by means of national police" first recorded 1865, with reference to Austria. <span class="foreign">Police action</span> in the international sense of "military intervention short of war, ostensibly to correct lawlessness" is from 1933. <span class="foreign">Police officer</span> is attested from 1800. <span class="foreign">Police station</span> is from 1817.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/police">police (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to keep order in," 1580s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">policer</span>, from <span class="foreign">police</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/police" target="_blank">police</a> (n.)). Meaning "to keep order by means of police" is from 1837. Related: <span class="foreign">Policed</span>; <span class="foreign">policing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
