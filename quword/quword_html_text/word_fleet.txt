                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fleet">fleet</a> 舰队</span><p>来自PIE*pleu,浮动，流，词源同flow, float, pluvial. 用来指船只，舰队。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fleet">fleet</a></dt><dd><b>fleet: </b><span>[OE] <i>Fleet </i>is one of a vast tangled web of words which traces its history back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>pleu</i>-, denoting ‘flow, float’ (amongst its other English descendants are <i>fly</i>, <i>flood</i>, <i>flow</i>, <i>fledge</i>, <i>fowl</i>, <i>plover</i>, and <i>pluvial</i>). <i>Fleet </i>itself comes from the extended Indo- European base *<i>pleud</i>-, via the Germanic verb *<i>fleutan </i>and Old English <i>flēotan </i>‘float, swim’ (modern English <i>float </i>comes from the related Old English <i>flotian</i>).<br><br>The verb has now virtually died out, but it survives in the form of the present participial adjective <i>fleeting</i>, which developed the sense ‘transient’ in the 16th century, and in the derived noun <i>fleet</i>: Old English seems to have had two distinct nouns <i>flēot </i>based on the verb <i>flēotan</i>, one of which meant ‘ships’, and the other of which signified ‘creek, inlet’ (it survives in the name of London’s Fleet Street, which runs down to the now covered-up Thames tributary, the river Fleet).<br><br>The adjective <i>fleet </i>‘quick’ (as in ‘fleet of foot’) was probably borrowed from Old Norse <i>fljótr</i>, likewise a descendant of Germanic *<i>fleut</i>-.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fledge" target="_blank">fledge</a>, <a href="/etym/s/float" target="_blank">float</a>, <a href="/etym/s/flood" target="_blank">flood</a>, <a href="/etym/s/flow" target="_blank">flow</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fowl" target="_blank">fowl</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plover" target="_blank">plover</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fleet">fleet (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fleot</span> "a ship, raft, floating vessel," also, collectively, "means of sea travel; boats generally," from <span class="foreign">fleotan</span> "to float" (see <a href="/etym/s/fleet" target="_blank">fleet</a> (v.)). Sense of "naval force, group of ships under one command" is in late Old English. The more usual Old English word was <span class="foreign">flota</span> "a ship," also "a fleet; a sailor." <span class="foreign">The fleet</span> for "the navy" is from 1712.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Old English word also meant "estuary, inlet, flow of water," especially the one into the Thames near Ludgate Hill, which lent its name to <span class="foreign">Fleet Street</span> (home of newspaper and magazine houses, standing for "the English press" since 1882), <span class="foreign">Fleet prison</span> (long used for debtors), etc.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fleet">fleet (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"swift," 1520s, but probably older than the record; apparently from or cognate with Old Norse <span class="foreign">fliotr</span> "swift," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fleuta</span>, which is related to the source of <a href="/etym/s/fleet" target="_blank">fleet</a> (v.). Related: <span class="foreign">Fleetness</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fleet">fleet (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fleotan</span> "to float; drift; flow, run (as water); swim; sail (of a ship)," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fleutan</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fliata</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fliotan</span> "to flow," Old High German <span class="foreign">fliozzan</span> "to float, flow," German <span class="foreign">fliessen</span> "to flow, run, trickle" (as water), Old Norse <span class="foreign">fliota</span> "to float, flow"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pleu-</span> "to flow, swim" (see <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "to glide away like a stream, vanish imperceptibly" is from c. 1200; hence "to fade, to vanish" (1570s). Related: <span class="foreign">Fleeted</span>; <a href="/etym/s/fleeting" target="_blank">fleeting</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
