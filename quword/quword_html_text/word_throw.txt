                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/throw">throw</a> 投，掷，抛，扔</span><p>来自中英语 thrawan,转动，旋转，拧，来自 Proto-Germanic*threw,转动，来自 PIE*tere,弯， 转，词源同 turn,torque.词义由旋转引申为扔，来自投掷的姿势和动作。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/throw">throw</a></dt><dd><b>throw: </b><span>[OE] Old English <i>thrāwan </i>meant ‘twist, turn’. It came from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>thrējan</i>, which also produced German <i>drehen </i>‘turn’. This in turn went back to the Indo- European base *<i>ter</i>-, whose other descendants include Greek <i>teírein </i>‘wear out’, Latin <i>terere </i>‘rub’ (source of English <i>attrition </i>[14], <i>contrition </i>[13], and <i>trite </i>[16]), Lithuanian <i>trinù </i>‘rub, file, saw’, Welsh <i>taradr </i>‘auger’, and English <i>thread </i>and <i>turn</i>.<br><br>It is not clear how the original sense ‘twist, turn’ (which survives in ‘throwing a pot’ on a potter’s wheel) evolved in English into ‘project, hurl’ (first recorded in the 13th century), but presumably there must have been some intermediate phase such as ‘throw with a twisting action – as in throwing the discus’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/attrition" target="_blank">attrition</a>, <a href="/etym/s/contrition" target="_blank">contrition</a>, <a href="/etym/s/thread" target="_blank">thread</a>, <a href="/etym/s/trite" target="_blank">trite</a>, <a href="/etym/s/turn" target="_blank">turn</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/throw">throw (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to project, propel," c. 1300, from Old English <span class="foreign">þrawan</span> "to twist, turn, writhe, curl," (past tense <span class="foreign">þreow</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">þrawen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*threw-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">thraian</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">dræyen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">draaien</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">draen</span>, German <span class="foreign">drehen</span> "to turn, twist;" not found in Scandinavian or Gothic), from PIE <span class="foreign">*tere-</span> (1) "to rub, turn, rub by turning, bore" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">turah</span> "wounded, hurt," Greek <span class="foreign">teirein</span> "to rub, rub away," Latin <span class="foreign">terere</span> "to rub, thresh, grind, wear away," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">tiro</span> "to rub," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">trinu</span> "to rub," Old Irish <span class="foreign">tarathar</span> "borer," Welsh <span class="foreign">taraw</span> "to strike").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Not the usual Old English word for "to throw" (<span class="foreign">weorpan</span>, related to <a href="/etym/s/warp" target="_blank">warp</a> (v.) was common in this sense). The sense evolution may be via the notion of whirling a missile before throwing it. The sense of "put by force" (as in <span class="foreign">throw in jail</span>) is first recorded 1550s; that of "confuse, flabbergast" is from 1844; that of "lose deliberately" is from 1868.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To <span class="foreign">throw the book at (someone)</span> is 1932, from notion of judge sentencing a criminal from a law book full of possible punishments. To <span class="foreign">throw (one's) hat in the ring</span> "issue a challenge," especially to announce one's candidacy, first recorded 1917. To <span class="foreign">throw up</span> "vomit" is first recorded 1732. To <span class="foreign">throw (someone) off</span> "confuse by a false scent" is from 1891.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/throw">throw (n.)</a></dt><dd>"act of throwing," 1520s, from <a href="/etym/s/throw" target="_blank">throw</a> (v.). Wrestling sense is first attested 1819.</dd></dl></div></div>
