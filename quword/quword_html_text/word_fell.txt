<li>adj. 凶猛的；毁灭性的</li>
<li>vt. 砍伐；打倒；击倒</li>
<li>n. [林] 一季所伐的木材；折缝；兽皮</li>
<li>v. 掉下；摔倒；下垂；变坏（fall的过去式）</li>
<li>n. (Fell)人名；(英、法、德)费尔</li>

            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fell">fell</a> 感觉</span><p>来自PIE*pal, 触摸，感受，拟声词，词源同palpitate.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fell">fell</a> 击倒</span><p>词源同fall, 倒下。引申词义击倒，砍伐树木。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fell">fell</a></dt><dd><b>fell: </b><span>English has no fewer than four separate words <i>fell</i>, not counting the past tense of <i>fall</i>. <i>Fell </i>‘cut down’ [OE] originated as the ‘causative’ version of <i>fall </i>– that is to say, it means literally ‘cause to fall’. It comes ultimately from prehistoric Germanic *<i>falljan</i>, causative of *<i>fallan </i>‘fall’. <i>Fell </i>‘animal’s skin’ [OE] goes back via Germanic *<i>fellam </i>(source also of English <i>film</i>) to Indo-European *<i>pello</i>- (whence Latin <i>pellis </i>‘skin’, from which English gets <i>pellagra </i>[19], <i>pellicle </i>[16], and <i>pelt </i>‘skin’ [15]). <i>Fell </i>‘hill’ [13] was borrowed from Old Norse <i>fjall </i>‘hill’; this seems to be related to German <i>fels </i>‘rock’, whose ultimate ancestor was Indo-European *<i>pels</i>-.<br><br>And the adjective <i>fell </i>‘fierce, lethal’ [13] was borrowed from Old French <i>fel</i>, ancestor of English <i>felon</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fall" target="_blank">fall</a>; <a href="/etym/s/film" target="_blank">film</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pelt" target="_blank">pelt</a>; <a href="/etym/s/felon" target="_blank">felon</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fell">fell (v.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fællan</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">fyllan</span> (West Saxon) "make fall, cause to fall," also "strike down, demolish, kill," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*falljan</span> "strike down, cause to fall" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">falla</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fellian</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">fellen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fellen</span>, German <span class="foreign">fällen</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fella</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">fælde</span>), causative of <span class="foreign">*fallan</span> (source of Old English <span class="foreign">feallan</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/fall" target="_blank">fall</a> (v.)), showing <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/imutate.php">i-mutation</a>. Related: <span class="foreign">Felled</span>; <span class="foreign">feller</span>; <span class="foreign">felling</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fell">fell (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"cruel," late 13c., possibly late Old English, perhaps from Old French <span class="foreign">fel</span> "cruel, fierce, vicious," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">fello</span> "villain" (see <a href="/etym/s/felon" target="_blank">felon</a>). Phrase <span class="foreign">at one fell swoop</span> is from "Macbeth." Related: <span class="foreign">Fellness</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fell">fell (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"rocky hill," c. 1300, from Old Norse <span class="foreign">fiall</span> "mountain," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*felzam-</span> "rock" (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">felisa</span>, German <span class="foreign">Fels</span> "stone, rock"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pel(i)s-</span> "rock, cliff." Old High German <span class="foreign">felisa</span> "a rock" is the source of French <span class="foreign">falaise</span> (formerly <span class="foreign">falize</span>) "cliff." Now mostly in place-names, such as <span class="foreign">Scafell Pike</span>, highest mountain in England.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fell">fell (v.2)</a></dt><dd>past tense of <a href="/etym/s/fall" target="_blank">fall</a> (v.), Old English <span class="foreign">feoll</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fell">fell (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"skin or hide of an animal," Old English <span class="foreign">fel</span> "skin, hide, garment of skin," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*felnam-</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fel</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fel</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fel</span>, German <span class="foreign">fell</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fiall</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fill</span> "skin, hide"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*pel-no-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*pel-</span> (4) "skin, hide" (see <a href="/etym/s/film" target="_blank">film</a> (n.)). Related: <span class="foreign">Fellmonger</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
