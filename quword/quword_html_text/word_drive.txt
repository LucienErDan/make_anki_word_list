                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/drive">drive</a> 驾驶</span><p>来自PIE*dhreibh, 推，词源同drift. 原义为从后面推，后用于驾驶汽车等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/drive">drive</a></dt><dd><b>drive: </b><span>[OE] As far as is known, <i>drive </i>is an exclusively Germanic word. It and its relatives German <i>treiben</i>, Dutch <i>drijven</i>, Swedish <i>driva</i>, Danish <i>drive</i>, and Gothic <i>dreiban </i>point to a prehistoric Germanic ancestor *<i>drīban</i>. Its base also produced English <i>drift </i>and <i>drove </i>[OE]. The central modern sense of <i>drive</i>, ‘drive a car’, comes from the earlier notion of driving a horse, ox, etc by pushing it, whipping it, etc from behind, forcing it onwards, but in most other modern European languages the verb for ‘driving a vehicle’ denotes basically ‘leading’ or ‘guiding’ (French <i>conduire</i>, for example, or German <i>lenken</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/drift" target="_blank">drift</a>, <a href="/etym/s/drove" target="_blank">drove</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/drive">drive (n.)</a></dt><dd>1690s, "act of driving," from <a href="/etym/s/drive" target="_blank">drive</a> (v.). Meaning "excursion by vehicle" is from 1785. Golfing sense of "forcible blow" is from 1836. Meaning "organized effort to raise money" is 1889, American English. Sense of "dynamism" is from 1908. In the computing sense, first attested 1963.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/drive">drive (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">drifan</span> "to drive, force, hunt, pursue; rush against" (class I strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">draf</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">drifen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*driban</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">driva</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">driban</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">drijven</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">triban</span>, German <span class="foreign">treiben</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">drifa</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">dreiban</span> "to drive"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*dhreibh-</span> "to drive, push." Original sense of "pushing from behind," altered in Modern English by application to automobiles. Related: <span class="foreign">Driving</span>.&#13; <blockquote>MILLER: "The more you drive, the less intelligent you are." ["Repo Man," 1984]</blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
