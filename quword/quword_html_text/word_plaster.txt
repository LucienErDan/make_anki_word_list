                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plaster">plaster</a> 灰泥，石膏</span><p>来自拉丁语plastrum,灰泥，石膏，来自希腊语plastos,形成的，铺平的，来自plassein,形成，铺开，来自PIE*pele,展开，放平，词源同plan,plain.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plaster">plaster</a></dt><dd><b>plaster: </b><span>[OE] Like <i>plastic</i>, <i>plaster </i>comes ultimately from the Greek verb <i>plássein </i>‘mould’. Combination with the prefix <i>en</i>- ‘in’ produced <i>emplássein </i>‘daub on, plaster’. From its past participle <i>emplastós </i>was derived <i>émplastron </i>‘medicinal application to the skin’, which reached Latin as <i>emplastrum</i>. Medieval Latin shortened it to <i>plastrum</i>, which Old English adopted as <i>plaster</i>. Its use for a ‘soft substance spread on walls, etc’ was introduced via Old French <i>plastre </i>in the 14th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/plastic" target="_blank">plastic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plaster">plaster (n.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">plaster</span> "medicinal application," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">plastrum</span>, shortened from Latin <span class="foreign">emplastrum</span> "a plaster" (in the medical as well as the building sense), from Greek <span class="foreign">emplastron</span> "salve, plaster" (used by Galen instead of more usual <span class="foreign">emplaston</span>), noun use of neuter of <span class="foreign">emplastos</span> "daubed on," from <span class="foreign">en-</span> "on" + <span class="foreign">plastos</span> "molded," from <span class="foreign">plassein</span> "to mold" (see <a href="/etym/s/plasma" target="_blank">plasma</a>). The building construction material is first recorded in English c. 1300, via Old French <span class="foreign">plastre</span>, from the same source, and in early use the English word often had the French spelling.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plaster">plaster (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to coat with plaster," early 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/plaster" target="_blank">plaster</a> (n.) and partly Old French <span class="foreign">plastrier</span> "to cover with plaster" (Modern French <span class="foreign">plâtrer</span>), from <span class="foreign">plastre</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/plaster" target="_blank">plaster</a> (n.). Related: <a href="/etym/s/Plastered" target="_blank">Plastered</a>; <span class="foreign">plastering</span>. Figurative use from c. 1600. Meaning "to bomb (a target) heavily" is first recorded 1915. Sports sense of "to defeat decisively" is from 1919.</dd></dl></div></div>
