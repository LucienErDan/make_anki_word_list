                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/meat">meat</a> 肉</span><p>来自古英语mete,食物，来自Proto-Germanic*mati,来自PIE*mad,湿的，多汁的，词源同mate,mastoid.后词义限定为肉，其原义见中古英语词grene-mete,蔬菜，即green meat.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/meat">meat</a></dt><dd><b>meat: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, <i>meat </i>is a ‘portion of food measured out’. The word’s ultimate source is Indo-European *<i>mat</i>-, *<i>met</i>- ‘measure’, which also lies behind English <i>measure</i>. This produced a prehistoric Germanic *<i>matiz</i>, which by the time it passed into Old English as <i>mete </i>had broadened out in meaning from ‘portion of food’ to simply ‘food’.<br><br>That is still the meaning of its Germanic relatives, Swedish <i>mat </i>and Danish <i>mad</i>, and it survives for English <i>meat </i>in certain fixed contexts, such as <i>meat and drink </i>and <i>What’s one man’s meat is another man’s poison</i>, but for the most part the more specific ‘flesh used as food’, which began to emerge in the 14th century, now dominates.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/measure" target="_blank">measure</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/meat">meat (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">mete</span> "food, item of food" (paired with <span class="foreign">drink</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*mati</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mete</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">meti</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">matr</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">maz</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">mats</span> "food," Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">metworst</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mettwurst</span> "type of sausage"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*mad-i-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*mad-</span> "moist, wet," also with reference to food qualities, (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">medas-</span> "fat" (n.), Old Irish <span class="foreign">mat</span> "pig;" see <a href="/etym/s/mast" target="_blank">mast</a> (n.2)).&#13; <br /><br />Narrower sense of "flesh used as food" is first attested c. 1300; similar sense evolution in French <span class="foreign">viande</span> "meat," originally "food." In Middle English, vegetables still could be called <span class="foreign">grene-mete</span> (15c.). Figurative sense of "essential part" is from 1901. <span class="foreign">Dark meat, white meat</span> popularized 19c., supposedly as euphemisms for <span class="foreign">leg</span> and <span class="foreign">breast</span>, but earliest sources use both terms without apparent embarrassment.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The choicest parts of a turkey are the side bones, the breast, and the thigh bones. The breast and wings are called light meat; the thigh-bones and side-bones dark meat. When a person declines expressing a preference, it is polite to help to both kinds. [Lydia Maria Child, "The American Frugal Housewife," Boston, 1835]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; First record of <span class="foreign">meat loaf</span> is from 1876. <span class="foreign">Meat-market</span> "place where one looks for sex partners" is from 1896 (<span class="foreign">meat</span> in various sexual senses of "penis, vagina, body regarded as a sex object, prostitute" are attested from 1590s; Old English for "meat-market" was <span class="foreign">flæsccyping</span> ('flesh-cheaping')); <span class="foreign">meat wagon</span> "ambulance" is from 1920, American English slang, said to date from World War I (in a literal sense by 1857). <span class="foreign">Meat-grinder</span> in the figurative sense attested by 1951. <span class="foreign">Meat-hook</span> in colloquial transferred sense "arm" attested by 1919.</dd></dl></div></div>
