                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/six">six</a> 六</span><p>来自古英语 siex,来自 Proto-Germanic*sehs,来自 PIE*sweks,六，词源同拉丁语 sex,六。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/six">six</a></dt><dd><b>six: </b><span>[OE] The Indo-European ancestor of <i>six </i>was *<i>seks</i>, which also produced Latin <i>sex </i>(source of English <i>sextant</i>, <i>sextuplet</i>, etc), Greek <i>héx</i>, Welsh <i>chwech</i>, Russian <i>shest’</i>, etc. The word’s Germanic relatives include German <i>sechs</i>, Dutch <i>zes</i>, and Swedish and Danish <i>sex. </i></span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/sextant" target="_blank">sextant</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/six">six (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">siex</span>, <span class="foreign">six</span>, <span class="foreign">sex</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sekhs</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Danish <span class="foreign">seks</span>, Old Norse, Swedish, and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">sex</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sesse</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zes</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sehs</span>, German <span class="foreign">sechs</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">saihs</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*s(w)eks</span> (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sas</span>, Avestan <span class="foreign">kshvash</span>, Persian <span class="foreign">shash</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">hex</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">sex</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">sesti</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">sześć</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">shesti</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">szeszi</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">se</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">chwech</span>).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Six-shooter</span>, usually a revolver with six chambers, is first attested 1844; <span class="foreign">six-pack</span> of beverage containers is from 1952, of abdominal muscles by 1995. <span class="foreign">Six of one and half-a-dozen of the other</span> "little difference" is recorded from 1833. <span class="foreign">Six-figure</span> in reference to hundreds of thousands (of dollars, etc.) is from 1840. <span class="foreign">Six feet under</span> "dead" is from 1942.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Phrase <span class="foreign">at sixes and sevens</span> originally was "hazarding all one's chances," first in Chaucer, perhaps from dicing (the original form was <span class="foreign">on six and seven</span>); it could be a corruption of <span class="foreign">on cinque and sice</span>, using the French names (which were common in Middle English) for the highest numbers on the dice. Meaning "at odds, in disagreement or confusion" is from 1785, perhaps via a notion of "left unsettled."</dd></dl></div></div>
