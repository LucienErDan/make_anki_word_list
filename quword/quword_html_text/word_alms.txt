3. eleemosyne: ele-emo-syne or ele-em-osyne; from eleemon "compassionate," from eleos "pity, mercy," of unknown origin, perhaps imitative of cries for alms.<br />

            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/alms">alms</a> 施舍物</span><p>词源不详。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/alms">alms</a></dt><dd><b>alms: </b><span>[OE] The word <i>alms </i>has become much reduced in its passage through time from its ultimate Greek source, <i>eleēmosúnē </i>‘pity, alms’. This was borrowed into post-classical (Christian) Latin as <i>eleēmosyna</i>, which subsequently became simplified in Vulgar Latin to *<i>alimosina </i>(source of the word for ‘alms’ in many Romance languages, such as French <i>aumône </i>and Italian <i>limosina</i>).<br><br>At this stage Germanic borrowed it, and in due course dispersed it (German <i>almosen</i>, Dutch <i>aalmoes</i>). It entered Old English as <i>ælmesse</i>, which became reduced in Middle English to <i>almes </i>and finally by the 17th century to <i>alms </i>(which because of its -<i>s </i>had come to be regarded as a plural noun). The original Greek <i>eleēmosúnē </i>is itself a derivative, of the adjective <i>eleémōn </i>‘compassionate’, which in turn came from the noun <i>éleos </i>‘pity’.<br><br>From medieval Latin <i>eleēmosyna </i>was derived the adjective <i>eleēmosynarius </i>(borrowed into English in the 17th century as the almost unpronounceable <i>eleemosynary </i>‘giving alms’). Used as a noun, this passed into Old French as <i>a(u)lmonier</i>, and eventually, in the 13th century, became English <i>aumoner </i>‘giver of alms’. The modern sense of <i>almoner </i>as a hospital social worker did not develop until the end of the 19th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/almoner" target="_blank">almoner</a>, <a href="/etym/s/eleemosynary" target="_blank">eleemosynary</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/alms">alms (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ælmesse</span> "alms, almsgiving," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*alemosna</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">alamosna</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">alamuosan</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">ölmusa</span>), an early borrowing of Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*alemosyna</span> (source of Old Spanish <span class="foreign">almosna</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">almosne</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">limosina</span>), from Church Latin <span class="foreign">eleemosyna</span> (Tertullian, 3c.), from Greek <span class="foreign">eleemosyne</span> "pity, mercy," in Ecclesiastical Greek "charity, alms," from <span class="foreign">eleemon</span> "compassionate," from <span class="foreign">eleos</span> "pity, mercy," which is of unknown origin, perhaps imitative of cries for alms. Spelling perversion in Vulgar Latin is perhaps by influence of <span class="foreign">alimonia</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
