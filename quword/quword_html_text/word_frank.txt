                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/frank">frank</a> 坦率的，真诚的，优雅的</span><p>来自Frank, 法兰克人，部落名。在公元五世纪，整个西欧仍处在一种野蛮的部落时代，整个社会被分成自由人，俘虏，和奴隶三个等级，而Frank人做为战胜级，享受唯一的自由权。后部落名通用化，赋予诸多美好的词义，如自由的，高贵的，美丽的，优雅的，大方的，真诚的等。后主要用于坦率的，其它词义逐渐废弃。参照电影《庞贝末日》。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/Frank">Frank</a> 法兰克人</span><p>可能来自old Germanic*frankon, 标枪，词源可能同fork. 比较Saxon, 词源同sax, 斧头。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frank">frank</a></dt><dd><b>frank: </b><span>[13] To call someone <i>frank </i>is to link them with the Germanic people who conquered Gaul around 500 AD, the Franks, who gave their name to modern France and the French. After the conquest, full political freedom was granted only to ethnic Franks or to those of the subjugated Celts who were specifically brought under their protection. Hence, <i>franc </i>came to be used as an adjective meaning ‘free’ – a sense it retained when English acquired it from Old French: ‘He was frank and free born in a free city’, John Tiptoft, <i>Julius Caesar’s commentaries </i>1470.<br><br>In both French and English, however, it gradually progressed semantically via ‘liberal, generous’ and ‘open’ to ‘candid’. Of related words in English, <i>frankincense </i>[14] comes from Old French <i>franc encens</i>, literally ‘superior incense’ (‘superior’ being a now obsolete sense of French <i>franc</i>), and <i>franc </i>[14], the French unit of currency, comes from the Latin phrase <i>Francorum rex </i>‘king of the Franks’, which appeared on the coins minted during the reign of Jean le Bon (1350–64).<br><br>The Franks, incidentally, supposedly got their name from their preferred weapon, the throwing spear, in Old English <i>franca</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/french" target="_blank">french</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frank">frank (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "free, liberal, generous;" 1540s, "outspoken," from Old French <span class="foreign">franc</span> "free (not servile); without hindrance, exempt from; sincere, genuine, open, gracious, generous; worthy, noble, illustrious" (12c.), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">francus</span> "free, at liberty, exempt from service," as a noun, "a freeman, a Frank" (see <a href="/etym/s/Frank" target="_blank">Frank</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; A generalization of the tribal name; the connection is that Franks, as the conquering class, alone had the status of freemen in a world that knew only free, captive, or slave. For sense connection of "being one of the nation" and "free," compare Latin <span class="foreign">liber</span> "free," from the same root as German <span class="foreign">Leute</span> "nation, people" (see <a href="/etym/s/liberal" target="_blank">liberal</a> (adj.)) and Slavic "free" words (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">svobodi</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">swobodny</span>, Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">slobodan</span>) which are cognates of the first element in English <a href="/etym/s/sibling" target="_blank">sibling</a> "brother, sister" (in Old English used more generally: "relative, kinsman").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Frank">Frank (n.)</a></dt><dd>one of the Germanic tribal people (Salian Franks) situated on the lower Rhine from 3c. that conquered Romano-Celtic northern Gaul c.500 C.E.; from their territory and partly from their language grew modern France and French. Old English <span class="foreign">franc</span>, <span class="foreign">franca</span> "freeman, noble; Frank, Frenchman," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">francus</span>, a Late Latin borrowing of Frankish <span class="foreign">*Frank</span>, the people's self-designation (cognate with Old High German <span class="foreign">Franko</span>, the Latin word also is the source of Spanish and Italian names <span class="foreign">Franco</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The origin of the ethnic name is uncertain; it traditionally is said to be from the old Germanic word <span class="foreign">*frankon</span> "javelin, lance" (compare Old English <span class="foreign">franca</span> "lance, javelin"), their preferred weapon, but the reverse may be the case. Compare also <a href="/etym/s/Saxon" target="_blank">Saxon</a>, traditionally from root of Old English <span class="foreign">seax</span> "knife." The adjectival sense of "free, at liberty" (see <a href="/etym/s/frank" target="_blank">frank</a> (adj.)) probably developed from the tribal name, not the other way round. It was noted by 1680s that, in the Levant, this was the name given to anyone of Western nationality (compare <a href="/etym/s/Feringhee" target="_blank">Feringhee</a> and <a href="/etym/s/lingua_franca" target="_blank">lingua franca</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frank">frank (n.)</a></dt><dd>short for <a href="/etym/s/frankfurter" target="_blank">frankfurter</a>, by 1916, American English. <span class="foreign">Franks and beans</span> attested by 1953.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/frank">frank (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to free a letter for carriage or an article for publication, to send by public conveyance free of expense," 1708, from shortened form of French <span class="foreign">affranchir</span>, from <span class="foreign">a-</span> "to" + <span class="foreign">franchir</span> "to free" (see <a href="/etym/s/franchise" target="_blank">franchise</a> (v.)). A British parliamentary privilege from 1660-1840; in U.S. Congress, technically abolished 1873. Related: <span class="foreign">Franked</span>; <span class="foreign">franking</span>. As a noun, "signature of one entitled to send letters for free," from 1713.</dd></dl></div></div>
