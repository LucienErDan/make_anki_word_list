                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tongue">tongue</a> 舌头，语言</span><p>来自古英语 tunge,舌头，来自 Proto-Germanic*tungon,舌头，来自 PIE*tnghwa,舌头，词源同 language,lingual.字母 d,l 音变，比较 tear,lachrymose.引申词义语言。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tongue">tongue</a></dt><dd><b>tongue: </b><span>[OE] <i>Tongue </i>is a general Germanic word, with relatives in German <i>zunge</i>, Dutch <i>tong</i>, Swedish <i>tonga</i>, and Danish <i>tonge</i>. These all evolved from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>tunggōn</i>, whose ultimate ancestor was Indo- European *<i>dnghwā</i>-. This also produced Latin <i>lingua </i>‘tongue, language’ (source of English <i>language</i>, <i>linguistic</i>, etc), Welsh <i>tafod </i>‘tongue’, Russian <i>jazyk </i>‘tongue’, etc.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/language" target="_blank">language</a>, <a href="/etym/s/linguistic" target="_blank">linguistic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tongue">tongue (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">tunge</span> "tongue, organ of speech; speech, a people's language," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*tungon</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Old Norse <span class="foreign">tunga</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">tunge</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">tonghe</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">tong</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">zunga</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zunge</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">tuggo</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*dnghwa-</span> (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">lingua</span> "tongue, speech, language," from Old Latin <span class="foreign">dingua</span>; Old Irish <span class="foreign">tenge</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">tafod</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">liezuvis</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">jezyku</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; For substitution of <span class="foreign">-o-</span> for <span class="foreign">-u-</span>, see <a href="/etym/s/come" target="_blank">come</a>. The spelling of the ending of the word apparently is a 14c. attempt to indicate proper pronunciation, but the result is "neither etymological nor phonetic, and is only in a very small degree historical" [OED]. In the "knowledge of a foreign language" sense in the Pentecostal miracle, from 1520s. <span class="foreign">Tongue-tied</span> is first recorded 1520s. To <span class="foreign">hold (one's) tongue</span> "refrain from speaking" was in Old English. Johnson has <span class="foreign">tonguepad</span> "A great talker."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tongue">tongue (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to touch with the tongue, lick," 1680s, from <a href="/etym/s/tongue" target="_blank">tongue</a> (n.). Earlier as a verb it meant "drive out by order or reproach" (late 14c.). Related: <span class="foreign">Tongued</span>; <span class="foreign">tonguing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
