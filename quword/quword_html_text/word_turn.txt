                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/turn">turn</a> 旋转，转动</span><p>来自古英语 turnian,旋转，转动，来自拉丁语 tornare,磨，磨圆，用车床加工，来自 PIE*ter,*tere, 转动，摩擦，旋转，可能缩写自 PIE*stwer,转动，旋转，词源同 storm,stir,turbid.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/turn">turn</a></dt><dd><b>turn: </b><span>[OE] The ultimate source of <i>turn </i>is Greek <i>tórnos </i>‘lathe’, which was probably related to Latin <i>terere </i>‘rub’ (source of English <i>attrition</i>, <i>detritus</i>, <i>trite</i>, etc). Latin took this over as <i>tornus </i> and formed a verb from it, <i>tornāre </i>‘turn on a lathe’, hence ‘round off, make smooth’. Old English borrowed <i>tornāre </i>as <i>turnian</i>, which was later reinforced by Old French <i>turner</i>. To the same word family belong <i>tour </i>and <i>tournament</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/attrition" target="_blank">attrition</a>, <a href="/etym/s/detriment" target="_blank">detriment</a>, <a href="/etym/s/detritus" target="_blank">detritus</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tour" target="_blank">tour</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tournament" target="_blank">tournament</a>, <a href="/etym/s/trite" target="_blank">trite</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/turn">turn (v.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">turnian</span> "to rotate, revolve," in part also from Old French <span class="foreign">torner</span> "to turn away or around; draw aside, cause to turn; change, transform; turn on a lathe" (Modern French <span class="foreign">tourner</span>), both from Latin <span class="foreign">tornare</span> "to polish, round off, fashion, turn on a lathe," from <span class="foreign">tornus</span> "lathe," from Greek <span class="foreign">tornos</span> "lathe, tool for drawing circles," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*tere-</span> (1) "to rub, rub by turning, turn, twist" (see <a href="/etym/s/throw" target="_blank">throw</a> (v.)). Transitive sense in English is from c. 1300. Related: <span class="foreign">Turned</span>; <span class="foreign">turning</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Use in expression <span class="foreign">to turn (something) into (something else)</span> probably retains the classical sense of "to shape on a lathe." To <span class="foreign">turn up</span> "arrive, make an appearance" is recorded from 1755. <span class="foreign">Turn about</span> "by turns, alternately" is recorded from 1640s. To <span class="foreign">turn (something) loose</span> "set free" is recorded from 1590s. <span class="foreign">Turn down</span> (v.) "reject" first recorded 1891, American English. <span class="foreign">Turn in</span> "go to bed" is attested from 1690s, originally nautical. To <span class="foreign">turn the stomach</span> "nauseate" is recorded from 1620s. To <span class="foreign">turn up one's nose</span> as an expression of contempt is attested from 1779.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Turning point</span> is attested by 1640s in a figurative sense "point at which a decisive change takes place;" literal sense "point on which a thing turns; point at which motion in one direction ceases and that in another or contrary direction begins" is from 1660s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/turn">turn (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "action of rotating," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">tourn</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">torn</span>, <span class="foreign">tour</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">tornus</span> "turning lathe;" also partly from <a href="/etym/s/turn" target="_blank">turn</a> (v.). Meaning "an act of turning, a single revolution or part of a revolution" is attested from late 15c. Sense of "place of bending" (in a road, river, etc.) is recorded from early 15c. Meaning "beginning of a period of time" is attested from 1853 (as in <span class="foreign">turn-of-the-century</span>, from 1921 as an adjectival phrase).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "act of good will" is recorded from c. 1300. Meaning "spell of work" is from late 14c.; that of "an individual's time for action, when these go around in succession" is recorded from late 14c. The automatic automobile <span class="foreign">turn-signal</span> is from 1915. <span class="foreign">Turn-sick</span> "dizzy," is attested from early 15c. Phrase <span class="foreign">done to a turn</span> (1780) suggests meat roasted on a spit. The <span class="foreign">turn of the screw</span> (1796) is the additional twist to tighten its hold, sometimes with reference to torture by thumbscrews.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/turn">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. For what do we live, but to make sport for our neighbours, and laugh at them in our <b>turn</b>? </dt> <dd>我们活着是为了什么？不就是给邻居当笑柄，再反过来笑他们。</dd> <p>来自《傲慢与偏见》</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. He said they should <b>turn</b> their fire on the Conservative Party instead. </dt> <dd>他说他们应该掉转枪口，向保守党开火。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. Today I might hesitate to <b>turn</b> in a burglar. </dt> <dd>现在让我把入室窃贼送交警方，我可能会有所顾虑。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. Sometimes things don't <b>turn</b> out the way we think they're going to. </dt> <dd>有时事情并不像我们认为的那样发展。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. The river widens considerably as it begins to <b>turn</b> east. </dt> <dd>河流转向东流时河道大幅度变宽。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/turn">turn 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
