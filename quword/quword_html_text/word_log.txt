                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/log">log</a> 原木，航海日志，日志，计算机登录</span><p>词源不详，可能来自拟声词，大块物体滚动的隆隆声。在早期航海过程中，水手会在一根打结(knot)的长绳末端寄上一块原木，并通过绳结来计算船航行的速度并进行记录，因此，引申词义航海日志，记录，计算机登录等，而节则作为航海的速度单位流传下来。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/log">log</a></dt><dd><b>log: </b><span>[14] <i>Log </i>is a mystery word. It first turns up (in the sense ‘felled timber’) towards the end of the 14th century, but it has no ascertainable relatives in any other language. Nor is it altogether clear how the sense ‘ship’s record’ came about. It was inspired by the use of <i>log </i>for a thin piece of wood floated in the water from a line to determine the speed of a ship, but some etymologists have speculated that this is not the same word as <i>log </i>‘piece of timber’, but was adapted from Arabic <i>lauh </i>‘tablet’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/log">log (n.1)</a></dt><dd>unshaped large piece of tree, early 14c., of unknown origin. Old Norse had <span class="foreign">lag</span> "felled tree" (from stem of <span class="foreign">liggja</span> "to lie"), but on phonological grounds many etymologists deny that this is the root of English <span class="foreign">log</span>. Instead, they suggest an independent formation meant to "express the notion of something massive by a word of appropriate sound." OED compares <a href="/etym/s/clog" target="_blank">clog</a> (n.) in its original Middle English sense "lump of wood." <span class="foreign">Log cabin</span> (1770) in American English has been a figure of the honest pioneer since the 1840 presidential campaign of William Henry Harrison. <span class="foreign">Falling off a log</span> as a type of something easy to do is from 1839.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/log">log (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to enter into a log-book," 1823, from <a href="/etym/s/log" target="_blank">log</a> (n.2). Meaning "to attain (a speed) as noted in a log" is recorded by 1883. Related: <span class="foreign">Logged</span>; <a href="/etym/s/logging" target="_blank">logging</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/log">log (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"record of observations, readings, etc.," 1842, sailor's shortening of <span class="foreign">log-book</span> "daily record of a ship's speed, progress, etc." (1670s), from <a href="/etym/s/log" target="_blank">log</a> (n.1). The book so called because a wooden float at the end of a line was cast out to measure a ship's speed. General sense by 1913.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/log">log (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to fell a tree," 1717; earlier "to strip a tree" (1690s), from <a href="/etym/s/log" target="_blank">log</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Logged</span>; <a href="/etym/s/logging" target="_blank">logging</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
