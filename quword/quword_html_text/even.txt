                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/even">even</a> 平的，甚至，黄昏</span><p>1.平的，甚至，来自PIE*aim, 相似，持平，词源同emulate, imitate. 形容词用修饰副词，参照very, 真实的。</p><p> 2.黄昏，来自古英语aefen, 黄昏。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/even">even</a></dt><dd><b>even: </b><span>[OE] <i>Even </i>can be traced back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>ebnaz</i>, although it is not clear whether it meant originally ‘flat, level’ or ‘equal, alike’ (both strands of meaning are still present in the word, the latter in such expressions as ‘get even with’ and ‘even number’, and also in its various adverbial uses, the former in ‘even keel’, ‘even light’, etc)</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/even">even (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">efen</span> "level," also "equal, like; calm, harmonious; equally; quite, fully; namely," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ebnaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">eban</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">even</span> "level, plain, smooth," Dutch <span class="foreign">even</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">eban</span>, German <span class="foreign">eben</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">jafn</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">jævn</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">ibns</span>). The adverb is Old English <span class="foreign">efne</span> "exactly, just, likewise." Modern adverbial sense (introducing an extreme case of something more generally implied) seems to have arisen 16c. from use of the word to emphasize identity ("Who, me?" "Even you").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Etymologists are uncertain whether the original sense was "level" or "alike." Used extensively in Old English compounds, with a sense of "fellow, co-" (as in <span class="foreign">efeneald</span> "of the same age;" Middle English <span class="foreign">even-sucker</span> "foster-brother"). Of numbers, from 1550s. Sense of "on an equal footing" is from 1630s. Rhyming reduplication phrase <span class="foreign">even steven</span> is attested from 1866; <span class="foreign">even break</span> (n.) first recorded 1907. <span class="foreign">Even-tempered</span> from 1712. To <span class="foreign">get even with</span> "retaliate upon" is attested by 1833.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/even">even (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">efnan</span> "to make even, to make level; liken, compare" (see <a href="/etym/s/even" target="_blank">even</a> (adj.)). Intransitive sense of "become even" is attested from early 13c. Related: <span class="foreign">Evened</span>; <span class="foreign">evening</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/even">even (n.)</a></dt><dd>"end of the day," Old English <span class="foreign">æfen</span>, Mercian <span class="foreign">efen</span>, Northumbrian <span class="foreign">efern</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/eve" target="_blank">eve</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
