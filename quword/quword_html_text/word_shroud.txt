                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/shroud">shroud</a> 覆盖物，遮蔽物</span><p>来自古英语 scrud,一件衣服，遮盖物，来自 West-Germanic*skrud,切，剪，来自 PIE*sker,切， 剪，词源同 shred,shear.引申词义遮盖物，遮蔽物等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shroud">shroud</a></dt><dd><b>shroud: </b><span>[OE] <i>Shroud </i>originally meant simply ‘garment’ – a sense which survived into the early modern English period (‘My princely robes are laid aside, whose glittering pomp Diana’s shrouds supplies’, Christopher Marlowe and Thomas Nashe, <i>Dido Queen of Carthage </i>1594). Not until the late 16th century did the modern meaning ‘winding-sheet’ begin to emerge. The word derives ultimately from the prehistoric West Germanic base *<i>skraud</i>-, *<i>skreud</i>-, *<i>skrud</i>- ‘cut’ (source also of English <i>shred</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/shred" target="_blank">shred</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shroud">shroud (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">scrud</span> "a garment, clothing, dress," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*skruthan</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skrud-</span> "cut" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">skruð</span> "shrouds of a ship, tackle, gear; furniture of a church," Danish, Swedish <span class="foreign">skrud</span> "dress, attire"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*skreu-</span> "to cut" (see <a href="/etym/s/shred" target="_blank">shred</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Specific meaning "winding-sheet, cloth or sheet for burial," to which the word now is restricted, first attested 1560s. Sense of "strong rope supporting the mast of a ship" (mid-15c.) is from the notion of "clothing" a spar or mast; one without rigging was said to be <span class="foreign">naked</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shroud">shroud (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "to clothe, to cover, protect," from Old English <span class="foreign">scrydan</span>, <span class="foreign">scridan</span> "to clothe, dress;" see <a href="/etym/s/shroud" target="_blank">shroud</a> (n.). Meaning "to hide from view, conceal" (transitive) is attested from early 15c. Related: <span class="foreign">Shrouded</span>; <span class="foreign">shrouding</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
