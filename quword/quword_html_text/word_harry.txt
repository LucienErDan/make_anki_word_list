                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. hare "野兔"，做动词讲是“飞跑”之意，因为野兔跑得很快，但是 hare (v.) 的古义并不是“飞跑”，而是“harry, harass, frighten”，这是源于 harry 或者与 harry 同源，因此可以说 hare (v.) 是先受 harry 的影响，后受到 hare (n.) 的影响，在这两个单词的影响下就变成了如今的含义，野兔在受到骚扰、惊吓的情况下肯定会飞跑逃命的。<br />
2. hare (v.) 今义“飞跑”=> 古义：harry, harass, frighten => har-"harry, harass, frighten": harry (双写r加-y), harass.<br />
3. 因此 harry 与 harass 是同源近义词，都表示骚扰、侵扰；使烦恼、苦恼。<br />
4. harry：Harry Porter从小就受到——袭扰，折磨。</div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/harry">harry</a> 烦扰，折磨</span><p>来自古英语hergian,开战，劫掠，抢劫，来自here,军队，来自PIE*koro,军队，队伍，词源同harbinger,harbor.由打战引申词义烦扰，折磨，在古代军队也常常形如土匪。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harry">harry</a></dt><dd><b>harry: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, to <i>harry </i>is to ‘go on a raid as an army does’. The word comes ultimately from prehistoric Germanic *<i>kharjaz</i>, which meant ‘crowd of people’ and also ‘army’ (it also produced English <i>harangue</i>, <i>harbinger</i>, <i>harbour</i>, and <i>harness</i>). From it was formed the verb *<i>kharōjan</i>, which passed into Old English as <i>hergian</i>. This developed into modern English <i>harry</i>, and it also produced the verb <i>harrow </i>‘rob, plunder’, now obsolete except in the expression <i>harrowing of hell </i>(which denotes the rescuing by Christ, after his crucifixion, of the souls of the righteous held in captivity in hell).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/harangue" target="_blank">harangue</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harbinger" target="_blank">harbinger</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harbour" target="_blank">harbour</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harness" target="_blank">harness</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harrow" target="_blank">harrow</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harry">harry (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hergian</span> "make war, lay waste, ravage, plunder," the word used in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle for what the Vikings did to England, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*harjon</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">urheria</span> "lay waste, ravage, plunder," Old Norse <span class="foreign">herja</span> "to make a raid, to plunder," Old Saxon and Old High German <span class="foreign">herion</span>, German <span class="foreign">verheeren</span> "to destroy, lay waste, devastate"). This is literally "to overrun with an army," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*harjaz</span> "an armed force" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">here</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">herr</span> "crowd, great number; army, troop," Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">heri</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">heir</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">har</span>, German <span class="foreign">Heer</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">harjis</span> "a host, army").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Germanic words come from PIE root <span class="foreign">*koro-</span> "war" also "war-band, host, army" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">karas</span> "war, quarrel," <span class="foreign">karias</span> "host, army;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kara</span> "strife;" Middle Irish <span class="foreign">cuire</span> "troop;" Old Persian <span class="foreign">kara</span> "host, people, army;" Greek <span class="foreign">koiranos</span> "ruler, leader, commander"). Weakened sense of "worry, goad, harass" is from c. 1400. Related: <span class="foreign">Harried</span>; <span class="foreign">harrying</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Harry">Harry</a></dt><dd>masc. proper name, a familiar form of <a href="/etym/s/Henry" target="_blank">Henry</a>. Weekley takes the overwhelming number of <span class="foreign">Harris</span> and <span class="foreign">Harrison</span> surnames as evidence that "Harry," not "Henry," was the Middle English pronunciation of <span class="foreign">Henry</span>. Compare <a href="/etym/s/Harriet" target="_blank">Harriet</a>, English equivalent of French <span class="foreign">Henriette</span>, fem. diminutive of <span class="foreign">Henri</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
