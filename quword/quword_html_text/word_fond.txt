                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fond">fond</a> 喜爱，溺爱</span><p>来自古英语fon, 傻，简单，词源同fun. 词义由傻引申为发狂，喜爱，溺爱等。类似词义演变比较infatuation.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fond">fond</a></dt><dd><b>fond: </b><span>[14] <i>Fond </i>originally meant ‘foolish’, and the likeliest explanation of its rather problematic origin is that it was a derivative of the Middle English noun <i>fon </i>‘fool’ (its Middle English spelling <i>fonned </i>suggests that it was formed with the suffix -<i>ed</i>, just as <i>wretched </i>was formed from <i>wretch</i>). However, where <i>fon </i>(probably a relative of modern English <i>fun</i>) comes from is another matter.<br><br>Links with Swedish <i>fåne </i>‘fool’ have been suggested but never established for certain. The adjective’s modern meaning ‘having a great liking’, incidentally, developed in the 16th century via an intermediate ‘foolishly doting’. <i>Fondle </i>[17] is a back-formation from the now obsolete <i>fondling </i>‘foolish person’, a derivative of <i>fond</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fondle" target="_blank">fondle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fun" target="_blank">fun</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fond">fond (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "deranged, insane;" also "foolish, silly, unwise," from <span class="foreign">fonned</span>, past participle adjective from obsolete verb <span class="foreign">fon</span>, <span class="foreign">fonne</span> (Middle English <span class="foreign">fonnen</span>) "be foolish, be simple," from Middle English <span class="foreign">fonne</span> "a fool, stupid person" (early 14c.), which is of uncertain origin but perhaps from Scandinavian. Related: <span class="foreign">Fonder</span>; <span class="foreign">fondest</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning evolved via "foolishly tender" to "having strong affections for" (by 1570s). Another sense of the verb <span class="foreign">fon</span> was "to lose savor" (late 14c. in Middle English past participle <span class="foreign">fonnyd</span>), which may be the original meaning of the word:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Gif þe salt be fonnyd it is not worþi [Wyclif, Matt. v:13, c. 1380]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
