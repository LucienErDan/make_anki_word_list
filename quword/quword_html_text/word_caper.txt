                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/caper">caper</a> 跳跃</span><p>来自拉丁词caper, 山羊。词源同cab, cabriolet.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/caper">caper</a></dt><dd><b>caper: </b><span><i>Caper </i>‘jump about’ [16] and the edible <i>caper </i>[15] are two different words. The former is a shortening of <i>capriole </i>‘leap’, now obsolete except as a technical term in horsemanship, which comes via early French <i>capriole </i>from Italian <i>capriola</i>, a derivative of the verb <i>capriolare </i>‘leap’, which in turn was formed from <i>capriolo </i>‘roebuck’; its ultimate source was Latin <i>capreolus</i>, a diminutive form of <i>caper </i>‘goat’ (whence the English astrological term <i>Capricorn</i>, literally ‘goat’s horn’). (The French by-form <i>cabrioler </i>was the source of English <i>cab</i>.) <i>Caper </i>‘edible bud’ came via French <i>câpres </i>and Latin <i>capparis </i>from Greek <i>kápparis</i>; the earliest English form was <i>capres</i>, but this came to be misinterpreted as a plural, and the -<i>s </i>was dropped from the singular in the 16th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cab" target="_blank">cab</a>, <a href="/etym/s/capricorn" target="_blank">capricorn</a>, <a href="/etym/s/capriole" target="_blank">capriole</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/caper">caper (v.)</a></dt><dd>1580s, apparently short for obsolete <span class="foreign">capriole</span> "to leap, skip," probably from Italian <span class="foreign">capriolare</span> "jump in the air" (see <a href="/etym/s/cab" target="_blank">cab</a>). Related: <span class="foreign">Capered</span>; <span class="foreign">capering</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/caper">caper (n.1)</a></dt><dd>type of prickly Mediterranean bush, also in reference to the plant's edible buds, late 14c., from Latin <span class="foreign">capparis</span> (source of Italian <span class="foreign">cappero</span>, French <span class="foreign">câpre</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kaper</span>), from Greek <span class="foreign">kapparis</span> "the caper plant or its fruit," which is of uncertain origin. Arabic <span class="foreign">kabbar</span>, Persian <span class="foreign">kabar</span> are from Greek. Perhaps reborrowed into English 16c. The final <span class="foreign">-s</span> was mistaken for a plural inflection in English and dropped.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/caper">caper (n.2)</a></dt><dd>by 1590s, "playful leap or jump," from <a href="/etym/s/caper" target="_blank">caper</a> (v.); meaning "prank" is from 1840; that of "crime" is from 1926. To <span class="foreign">cut capers</span> "dance in a frolicsome way" is from c. 1600.</dd></dl></div></div>
