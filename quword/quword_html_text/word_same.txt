            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/same">same</a> 相同的，同样的</span><p>来自古英语 same,相同的，同样的，来自 Proto-Germanic*sama,相同的，来自 PIE*sem,一， 一样，一致，词源同 some,homo-.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/same">same</a></dt><dd><b>same: </b><span>[12] <i>Same </i>comes ultimately from Indo- European *<i>somós </i>‘same’. This also produced Greek <i>homós </i>‘same’ (source of the English prefix <i>homo</i>-, as in <i>homosexual</i>), and was a variant of the base that gave Latin <i>similis </i>‘similar’ (source of English <i>similar </i>and <i>simulate</i>), Latin <i>simul </i>‘at the same time’ (source of English <i>assemble </i>and <i>simultaneous</i>), Latin <i>simplus </i>‘simple’ (source of English <i>simple</i>).<br><br>Latin <i>singulus </i>‘single’ (source of English <i>single </i>and <i>singular</i>), and English <i>seem </i>and <i>some</i>. The Indo-European adjective passed into prehistoric Germanic as *<i>samaz</i>, which in due course evolved into Old Norse <i>same</i>. The Vikings brought the word with them to England, where it gradually replaced the native terms for ‘same’, <i>ilk </i>and <i>self</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/same">same (adj.)</a></dt><dd>perhaps abstracted from Old English <span class="foreign">swa same</span> "the same as," but more likely from Old Norse <span class="foreign">same</span>, <span class="foreign">samr</span> "same," both from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sama-</span> "same" (cognates: Old Saxon, Old High German, Gothic <span class="foreign">sama</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">samant</span>, German <span class="foreign">samt</span> "together, with," Gothic <span class="foreign">samana</span> "together," Dutch <span class="foreign">zamelen</span> "to collect," German <span class="foreign">zusammen</span> "together"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*samos</span> "same," from root <span class="foreign">*sem-</span> (1) "one," also "as one" (adv.), "together with" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">samah</span> "even, level, similar, identical;" Avestan <span class="foreign">hama</span> "similar, the same;" Greek <span class="foreign">hama</span> "together with, at the same time," <span class="foreign">homos</span> "one and the same," <span class="foreign">homios</span> "like, resembling," <span class="foreign">homalos</span> "even;" Latin <span class="foreign">similis</span> "like;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">samail</span> "likeness;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">samu</span> "himself").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Old English had lost the pure form of the word; the modern word replaced synonymous <a href="/etym/s/ilk" target="_blank">ilk</a>. As a pronoun from c. 1300. Colloquial phrase <span class="foreign">same here</span> as an exclamation of agreement is from 1895. <span class="foreign">Same difference</span> curious way to say "equal," is attested from 1945.</dd></dl></div></div>
