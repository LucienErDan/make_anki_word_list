                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/woman">woman</a> 女人</span><p>缩写自古英语wifman,女人，wif-,女人，词源同wife,man,人。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/woman">woman</a></dt><dd><b>woman: </b><span>[OE] A <i>woman </i>is etymologically a ‘wife-man’ – that is to say, a ‘female person’. The word was compounded in the Old English period from <i>wīf </i>‘woman’ (source of modern English <i>wife</i>) and <i>man </i>‘person’ (source of modern English <i>man</i>). Already by the end of the Old English period the <i>f </i>of <i>wifman </i>was disappearing, giving <i>wiman</i>, and the influence of the <i>w </i>sound started to turn this into <i>woman </i>in the 13th century. <i>Woman </i>did not finally oust the two more ancient words for ‘female person’, <i>wife </i>and the now obsolete <i>quean</i>, until the end of the Middle English period.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/man" target="_blank">man</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wife" target="_blank">wife</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/woman">woman (n.)</a></dt><dd>"adult female human," late Old English <span class="foreign">wimman</span>, <span class="foreign">wiman</span> (plural <span class="foreign">wimmen</span>), literally "woman-man," alteration of <span class="foreign">wifman</span> (plural <span class="foreign">wifmen</span>) "woman, female servant" (8c.), a compound of <span class="foreign">wif</span> "woman" (see <a href="/etym/s/wife" target="_blank">wife</a>) + <span class="foreign">man</span> "human being" (in Old English used in reference to both sexes; see <a href="/etym/s/man" target="_blank">man</a> (n.)). Compare Dutch <span class="foreign">vrouwmens</span> "wife," literally "woman-man."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; It is notable that it was thought necessary to join <span class="foreign">wif</span>, a neuter noun, representing a female person, to <span class="foreign">man</span>, a masc. noun representing either a male or female person, to form a word denoting a female person exclusively. [Century Dictionary]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The formation is peculiar to English and Dutch. Replaced older Old English <span class="foreign">wif</span> and <span class="foreign">quean</span> as the word for "female human being." The pronunciation of the singular altered in Middle English by the rounding influence of <span class="foreign">-w-</span>; the plural retains the original vowel. Meaning "wife," now largely restricted to U.S. dialectal use, is attested from mid-15c. <span class="foreign">Woman-hater</span> "misogynist" is from c. 1600. <span class="foreign">Women's work</span> is from 1660s. <span class="foreign">Women's liberation</span> is attested from 1966; <span class="foreign">women's rights</span> is from 1840, with an isolated example in 1630s.</dd></dl></div></div>
