                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gain">gain</a> 赢得</span><p>来自PIE*weie, 努力，追寻，词源同virile, venison, Venus.原指农业收成。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gain">gain</a></dt><dd><b>gain: </b><span>[15] <i>Gain </i>is Germanic in origin, although English acquired it via Old French. Its distant ancestor is the Germanic noun *<i>waithā</i>. The etymological meaning of this was ‘hunting ground’ (it came ultimately from a prehistoric Indo-European base *<i>wei</i>-, which also produced Lithuanian <i>vyti </i>‘pursue, hunt’ and Sanskrit <i>veti</i>, <i>vayati </i>‘seeks, follows’), but gradually this extended via ‘place where food or fodder is sought’ to ‘grazing place’ (its modern German and Dutch descendant <i>weide </i>means ‘pasture’).<br><br>From it was formed a verb *<i>waithanjan </i>‘hunt’ and ‘graze, pasture’, which Vulgar Latin took over as *<i>gwadanjāre</i>. This preserved the semantic dichotomy that had grown up in Germanic: the agricultural sense developed to ‘cultivate land’, and it appears that the ‘hunting’ sense gave rise metaphorically to ‘win, earn’. Both passed into Old French <i>gaaigner</i>, but evidently by the time English acquired the word, the former meaning had all but died out (although it is interesting to note that it was introduced into English as a pseudo-archaism in the 17th and 18th centuries: ‘Of old to gain land was as much as to till and manure it’, <i>Termes de la ley </i>1708).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gain">gain (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 15c., "that which has been acquired" (possessions, resources, wealth), from Middle French <span class="foreign">gain</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">gaaigne</span> "gain, profit, advantage; work, business; booty; arable land" (12c.), from <span class="foreign">gaaignier</span> "to gain, earn; capture, win" (see <a href="/etym/s/gain" target="_blank">gain</a> (v.)). Meaning "any incremental increase" (in weight, etc.) is by 1851. Related: <span class="foreign">Gains</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The original French word enfolded the notions of "profit from agriculture" and "booty, prey." Neither the verb nor the noun <span class="foreign">gain</span> is in Middle English, which however had <span class="foreign">gainage</span> "profit derived from agriculture" (late 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">gaaignage</span>); <span class="foreign">gaineier</span> "farmer" (late 13c. as a surname); <span class="foreign">gainerie</span> "a farm" (mid-15c.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gain">gain (v.)</a></dt><dd>1520s, "obtain as profit," from Middle French <span class="foreign">gagner</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">gaaignier</span> "to earn, gain; trade; capture, win," also "work in the fields, cultivate land," from Frankish <span class="foreign">*waidanjan</span> "hunt, forage," also "graze, pasture," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*waithanjan</span> "to hunt, plunder," from <span class="foreign">*waithjo-</span> "pursuit, hunting" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">waþ</span> "hunting," German <span class="foreign">Weide</span> "pasture, pasturage," Old Norse <span class="foreign">veiðr</span> "hunting, fishing, catch of fish"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*weie-</span> "to strive after, pursue with vigor, desire" (see <a href="/etym/s/venison" target="_blank">venison</a>). Meaning "obtain by effort or striving" is from 1540s; intransitive sense of "profit, make gain" is from 1570s. Meaning "arrive at" is from c. 1600. Of timepieces by 1861. Related: <span class="foreign">Gained</span>; <span class="foreign">gaining</span>. To <span class="foreign">gain on</span> "advance nearer" is from 1719. To <span class="foreign">gain ground</span> (1620s) was originally military.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. This is a cynical manipulation of the situation for short-term political <b>gain</b>. </dt> <dd>这是为了获取短期政治利益而损人利己地对局势进行操纵。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. White wines tend to <b>gain</b> depth of colour with age. </dt> <dd>白葡萄酒年头愈久，颜色愈深。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. I was able to <b>gain</b> invaluable experience over that year. </dt> <dd>在那一年里我有幸获得了非常宝贵的经验。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. Through superior production techniques they were able to <b>gain</b> the competitive edge. </dt> <dd>凭借先进的生产技术，他们得以占据竞争优势。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. I hoped to <b>gain</b> time by keeping him talking. </dt> <dd>我希望让他一直说下去以争取时间。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/gain">gain 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
