                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ban">ban</a></dt><dd><b>ban: </b><span>[OE] <i>Ban </i>is one of a widespread group of words in the European languages. Its ultimate source is the Indo-European base *<i>bha</i>-, which also gave English <i>fame </i>(from a derivative of Latin <i>fārī </i>‘speak’) and <i>phase </i>(from Greek <i>phāsis</i>). The Germanic offshoot of the Indo- European base, and source of the English word, was *<i>bannan</i>, which originally probably meant simply ‘speak, proclaim’.<br><br>This gradually developed through ‘proclaim with threats’ to ‘put a curse on’, but the sense ‘prohibit’ does not seem to have arisen until as late as the 19th century. The Germanic base *<i>bann</i>- was borrowed into Old French as the noun <i>ban </i>‘proclamation’. From there it crossed into English and probably mingled with the cognate English noun, Middle English <i>iban </i>(the descendant of Old English <i>gebann</i>).<br><br>It survives today in the plural form <i>banns </i>‘proclamation of marriage’. The adjective derived from Old French <i>ban </i>was <i>banal</i>, acquired by English in the 18th century. It originally meant ‘of compulsory military service’ (from the word’s basic sense of ‘summoning by proclamation’); this was gradually generalized through ‘open to everyone’ to ‘commonplace’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/banal" target="_blank">banal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bandit" target="_blank">bandit</a>, <a href="/etym/s/banish" target="_blank">banish</a>, <a href="/etym/s/contraband" target="_blank">contraband</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fame" target="_blank">fame</a>, <a href="/etym/s/phase" target="_blank">phase</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ban">ban (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">bannan</span> "to summon, command, proclaim," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bannan</span> "proclaim, command, forbid" (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">bannan</span> "to command or forbid under threat of punishment," German <span class="foreign">bannen</span> "banish, expel, curse"), originally "to speak publicly," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bha-</span> (2) "to speak" (cognates: Old Irish <span class="foreign">bann</span> "law," Armenian <span class="foreign">ban</span> "word;" see <a href="/etym/s/fame" target="_blank">fame</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Main modern sense of "to prohibit" (late 14c.) is from Old Norse cognate <span class="foreign">banna</span> "to curse, prohibit," and probably in part from Old French <span class="foreign">ban</span>, which meant "outlawry, banishment," among other things (see <a href="/etym/s/banal" target="_blank">banal</a>) and was a borrowing from Germanic. The sense evolution in Germanic was from "speak" to "proclaim a threat" to (in Norse, German, etc.) "curse."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Germanic root, borrowed in Latin and French, has been productive: <a href="/etym/s/banish" target="_blank">banish</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bandit" target="_blank">bandit</a>, <a href="/etym/s/contraband" target="_blank">contraband</a>, etc. Related: <span class="foreign">Banned</span>; <span class="foreign">banning</span>. <span class="foreign">Banned in Boston</span> dates from 1920s, in allusion to the excessive zeal and power of that city's Watch and Ward Society.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ban">ban (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"governor of Croatia," from Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">ban</span> "lord, master, ruler," from Persian <span class="foreign">ban</span> "prince, lord, chief, governor," related to Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pati</span> "guards, protects." Hence <span class="foreign">banat</span> "district governed by a <span class="foreign">ban</span>," with Latinate suffix <span class="foreign">-atus</span>. The Persian word got into Slavic perhaps via the Avars.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ban">ban (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"edict of prohibition," c. 1300, "proclamation or edict of an overlord," from Old English <span class="foreign">(ge)bann</span> "proclamation, summons, command" and Old French <span class="foreign">ban</span>, both from Germanic; see <a href="/etym/s/ban" target="_blank">ban</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
