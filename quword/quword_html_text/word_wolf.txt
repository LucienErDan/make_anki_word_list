                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wolf">wolf</a></dt><dd><b>wolf: </b><span>[OE] <i>Wolf </i>is an ancient word, which has been traced back to Indo-European *<i>wlqwos</i>. This, or its variant *<i>lukwos</i>, also produced Greek <i>lúkos </i>(source of English <i>lycanthropy </i>‘(delusion of) turning into a wolf’ [16]), Latin <i>lupus </i>(source of French <i>loup</i>, Italian <i>lupo</i>, and Spanish <i>lobo</i>, and probably also of English <i>lupin </i>[14]), Sanskrit <i>vrkas</i>, Russian <i>volk</i>, Polish <i>wilk</i>, Czech <i>vlk</i>, Serbo-Croat <i>vuk</i>, Lithuanian <i>vilkas</i>, Latvian <i>vilks</i>, Albanian <i>u’lk</i>, and Armenian <i>gail</i>. In prehistoric Germanic it gave *<i>wulfaz</i>, which has evolved into German, Dutch, and English <i>wolf </i>and Swedish and Danish <i>ulv</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/lupin" target="_blank">lupin</a>, <a href="/etym/s/lycanthropy" target="_blank">lycanthropy</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wolf">wolf (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">wulf</span> "wolf, wolfish person, devil," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wulfaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">wulf</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">ulfr</span>, Old Frisian, Dutch, Old High German, German <span class="foreign">wolf</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">wulfs</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*wlkwo-</span> "wolf" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vrkas</span>, Avestan <span class="foreign">vehrka-</span>; Albanian <span class="foreign">ul'k</span>; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">vluku</span>; Russian <span class="foreign">volcica</span>; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vilkas</span> "wolf;" Old Persian <span class="foreign">Varkana-</span> "Hyrcania," district southeast of the Caspian Sea, literally "wolf-land;" probably also Greek <span class="foreign">lykos</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">lupus</span>).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; This manne can litle skyl ... to saue himself harmlesse from the perilous accidentes of this world, keping ye wulf from the doore (as they cal it). ["The Institution of a Gentleman," 1555]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Probably extinct in England from the end of the 15th century; in Scotland from the early 18th. Wolves as a symbol of lust are ancient, such as Roman slang <span class="foreign">lupa</span> "whore," literally "she-wolf" (preserved in Spanish <span class="foreign">loba</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">lupa</span>, French <span class="foreign">louve</span>). The equation of "wolf" and "prostitute, sexually voracious female" persisted into 12c., but by Elizabethan times wolves had become primarily symbolic of male lust. The specific use of <span class="foreign">wolf</span> for "sexually aggressive male" first recorded 1847; <span class="foreign">wolf-whistle</span> attested by 1945, American English, at first associated with sailors. The image of a wolf in sheep's skin is attested from c. 1400. See <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/wolf.php">here</a> for a discussion of "wolf" in Indo-European history. The <span class="foreign">wolf-spider</span> so called for prowling and leaping on its prey rather than waiting in a web.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wolf">wolf (v.)</a></dt><dd>"eat like a wolf," 1862, from <a href="/etym/s/wolf" target="_blank">wolf</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Wolfed</span>; <span class="foreign">wolfing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
