            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/caterpillar">caterpillar</a> 毛虫</span><p>cat, 猫。-pil, 毛，词源同peel, depilatory(脱毛)。因其毛如猫毛而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/caterpillar">caterpillar</a></dt><dd><b>caterpillar: </b><span>[15] Etymologically, a <i>caterpillar </i>is a ‘hairy cat’. The word comes ultimately from late Latin *<i>catta pilōsa</i>: <i>catta </i>is the source of English <i>cat</i>, while <i>pilōsus </i>‘hairy’ is a derivative of Latin <i>pilus </i>‘hair’, from which English gets <i>pile </i>of a carpet. In Old French *<i>catta pilōsa </i>became <i>chatepelose</i>, which passed into English as <i>catyrpel</i>.<br><br>The present-day form arose in the 16th century, probably from association with the now obsolete <i>piller </i>‘plunderer’ (related to English <i>pillage</i>) – caterpillars being regarded, of course, as plunderers of leaves. The notion that caterpillars resemble small furry mammals is also reflected in such names as <i>pussmoth </i>and <i>woolly bear</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cat" target="_blank">cat</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pile" target="_blank">pile</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/caterpillar">caterpillar (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-15c., <span class="foreign">catyrpel</span>, probably altered (by association with Middle English <span class="foreign">piller</span> "plunderer;" see <a href="/etym/s/pillage" target="_blank">pillage</a>) from Old North French <span class="foreign">caterpilose</span> "caterpillar" (Old French <span class="foreign">chatepelose</span>), literally "shaggy cat" (probably in reference to the "wooly-bear" variety), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">catta pilosa</span>, from <span class="foreign">catta</span> "cat" (see <a href="/etym/s/cat" target="_blank">cat</a> (n.)) + <span class="foreign">pilosus</span> "hairy, shaggy, covered with hair," from <span class="foreign">pilus</span> "hair" (see <a href="/etym/s/pile" target="_blank">pile</a> (n.3)). Compare also French <span class="foreign">chenille</span> "caterpillar," literally "little dog." A Swiss German name for it is <span class="foreign">teufelskatz</span> "devil's cat." "The caterpillar has in many idioms received the name of other animals" [Kitchin, who cites also Milanese <span class="foreign">cagnon</span> "little dog," Italian dialectal <span class="foreign">gattola</span> "little cat," Kentish <span class="foreign">hop-dog</span>, <span class="foreign">hop-cat</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">lagarta</span> "lizard." Compare also American English <span class="foreign">wooly-bear</span> for the hairy variety. An Old English name for it was <span class="foreign">cawelworm</span> "cole-worm." <span class="foreign">Caterpillar tractor</span> is from 1908.</dd></dl></div></div>
