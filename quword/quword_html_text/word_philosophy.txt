                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/philosophy">philosophy</a></dt><dd><b>philosophy: </b><span>[14] Greek <i>phílos </i>(a word of uncertain origin) meant ‘loving’. It has entered into an enormous range of English compounds, including <i>philander </i>[17] (adopted from a Greek word meaning ‘loving men’), <i>philanthropy </i>[17], <i>philately</i>, and <i>philology </i>[17], not to mention all the terms suffixed with <i>-phil </i>or <i>-phile</i>, such as <i>Anglophile </i>[19] and <i>paedophile </i>[20]. <i>Philosophy </i>itself means etymologically ‘loving wisdom’. It comes via Old French <i>filosofie </i>and Latin <i>philosophia </i>from Greek <i>philosophíā</i>, whose second element was a derivative of <i>sophós </i>‘wise’ (source of English <i>sophisticate</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/sophisticate" target="_blank">sophisticate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/philosophy">philosophy (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "knowledge, body of knowledge," from Old French <span class="foreign">filosofie</span> "philosophy, knowledge" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">philosophie</span>) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">philosophia</span> and from Greek <span class="foreign">philosophia</span> "love of knowledge, pursuit of wisdom; systematic investigation," from <span class="foreign">philo-</span> "loving" (see <a href="/etym/s/philo-" target="_blank">philo-</a>) + <span class="foreign">sophia</span> "knowledge, wisdom," from <span class="foreign">sophis</span> "wise, learned;" of unknown origin.<blockquote><span class="foreign">Nec quicquam aliud est philosophia, si interpretari velis, praeter studium sapientiae; sapientia autem est rerum divinarum et humanarum causarumque quibus eae res continentur scientia.</span> [Cicero, <span class="foreign">"De Officiis"</span>]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; [Philosophical problems] are, of course, not empirical problems; but they are solved through an insight into the workings of our language, and that in such a way that these workings are recognized -- <span class="foreign">despite</span> an urge to misunderstand them. The problems are solved, not through the contribution of new knowledge, rather through the arrangement of things long familiar. Philosophy is a struggle against the bewitchment (<span class="foreign">Verhexung</span>) of our understanding by the resources of our language. [Ludwig Wittgenstein, "Philosophical Investigations," 1953]&#13; </blockquote>Meaning "system a person forms for conduct of life" is attested from 1771.</dd></dl></div></div>
