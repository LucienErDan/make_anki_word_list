                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/suburb">suburb</a> 郊区</span><p>sub-,在下，-urb,城市，词源同 urban,urbane.引申词义郊区，城乡结合部。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/suburb">suburb</a></dt><dd><b>suburb: </b><span>see <a href="/etym/s/urban" target="_blank">urban</a></span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/suburb">suburb (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "area outside a town or city," whether agricultural or residential but most frequently residential, from Old French <span class="foreign">suburbe</span> "suburb of a town," from Latin <span class="foreign">suburbium</span> "an outlying part of a city" (especially Rome), from <span class="foreign">sub</span> "below, near" (see <a href="/etym/s/sub-" target="_blank">sub-</a>) + <span class="foreign">urbs</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">urbis</span>) "city" (see <a href="/etym/s/urban" target="_blank">urban</a>). Glossed in Old English as <span class="foreign">underburg</span>. Just beyond the reach of municipal jurisdiction, suburbs had a bad reputation in 17c. England, especially those of London, and <span class="foreign">suburban</span> had a sense of "inferior, debased, licentious" (as in <span class="foreign">suburban sinner</span>, slang for "loose woman, prostitute"). By 1817, the tinge had shifted to "of inferior manners and narrow views." Compare also French equivalent <a href="/etym/s/faubourg" target="_blank">faubourg</a>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; [T]he growth of the metropolis throws vast numbers of people into distant dormitories where ... life is carried on without the discipline of rural occupations and without the cultural resources that the Central District of the city still retains. [Lewis Mumford, 1922]</blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
