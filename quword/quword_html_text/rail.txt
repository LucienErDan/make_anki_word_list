                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/rail">rail</a> 栏杆，横竿，铁轨</span><p>来自古法语 reille,柱子，竿，杆，来自拉丁语 regula,直杆，小杆，-ula,小词后缀，来自 regere, 拉直，伸直，词源同 regulate,right.引申词义铁轨。拼写比较 rule,regulate.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rail">rail</a></dt><dd><b>rail: </b><span>English has three words <i>rail</i>. The oldest, ‘rod, bar’ [13], comes via Old French <i>reille </i>‘iron bar’ from Latin <i>rēgula </i>‘straight stick, rod’, source of English <i>regular </i>and <i>rule</i>. The bird-name <i>rail </i>[15] goes back via Old Northern French <i>raille </i>to Vulgar Latin *<i>rascula</i>, which probably originated in imitation of the bird’s hoarse cry.<br><br>And <i>rail </i>‘complain, be abusive’ [15] comes via Old French <i>railler </i>‘mock’ and Provençal <i>ralhar </i>‘scoff’ from Vulgar Latin *<i>ragulāre </i>‘bray’, an alteration of <i>ragere </i>‘neigh, roar’. This in turn was a blend of Latin <i>rugīre </i>‘bellow’ and Vulgar Latin *<i>bragere </i>‘bray’ (source of English <i>bray </i>[13]). <i>Raillery </i>[17] and <i>rally </i>‘tease’ [17] come from the same source.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/regular" target="_blank">regular</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rule" target="_blank">rule</a>; <a href="/etym/s/bray" target="_blank">bray</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rally" target="_blank">rally</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rail">rail (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"horizontal bar passing from one post or support to another," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">reille</span> "bolt, bar," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*regla</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">regula</span> "straight stick," diminutive form related to <span class="foreign">regere</span> "to straighten, guide" (see <a href="/etym/s/regal" target="_blank">regal</a>). Used figuratively for thinness from 1872. To be <span class="foreign">off the rails</span> in a figurative sense is from 1848, an image from the railroads. In U.S. use, "A piece of timber, cleft, hewed, or sawed, inserted in upright posts for fencing" [Webster, 1830].</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rail">rail (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"small wading bird," mid-15c., from Old French <span class="foreign">raale</span> (13c.), related to <span class="foreign">râler</span> "to rattle," of unknown origin, perhaps imitative of its cry.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rail">rail (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"complain," mid-15c., from Middle French <span class="foreign">railler</span> "to tease or joke" (15c.), perhaps from Old Provençal <span class="foreign">ralhar</span> "scoff, to chat, to joke," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*ragulare</span> "to bray" (source also of Italian <span class="foreign">ragghiare</span> "to bray"), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">ragere</span> "to roar," probably of imitative origin. See <a href="/etym/s/rally" target="_blank">rally</a> (v.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Railed</span>; <span class="foreign">railing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rail">rail (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"fence in with rails," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/rail" target="_blank">rail</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Railed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/railing" target="_blank">railing</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
