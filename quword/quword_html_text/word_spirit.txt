                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spirit">spirit</a> 精神，心灵，勇气，意志，烈酒，酒精，灵魂，小精灵，小妖精</span><p>来自古法语 espirit,精神，灵魂，精气，来自拉丁语 spiritus,呼吸，吐气，神灵的呼吸，来自 spirare,呼吸，来自 PIE*speis,吹，词源同 conspire,perspire,respire.引申诸相关比喻义。比较另 一个拉丁词 anima,呼吸，生命，衍生词 animal,animation.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spirit">spirit</a></dt><dd><b>spirit: </b><span>[13] Latin <i>spīritus </i>originally meant ‘breath’: it was derived from the verb <i>spīrāre </i>‘breathe’ (source of English <i>aspire </i>[15], <i>conspire </i>[14], <i>expire </i>[15], <i>inspire </i>[14], <i>perspire </i>[17], <i>respire </i>[14], <i>transpire </i>[16], etc), which probably came ultimately from the prehistoric Indo-European base *<i>speis</i>- or *<i>peis</i>-, imitative of the sound of blowing or breathing out (source also of Old Church Slavonic <i>piskati </i>‘whistle’, Serbo-Croat <i>pistati </i>‘hiss’, and Old Norse <i>físa </i> ‘fart’).<br><br>But in the Augustan period it gradually began to take over as the word for ‘soul’ from <i>anima </i>(source of English <i>animal</i>, <i>animate</i>, etc), which itself originally denoted ‘breath’, and in Christian Latin writings it was the standard term used.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/aspire" target="_blank">aspire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/conspire" target="_blank">conspire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/expire" target="_blank">expire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/inspire" target="_blank">inspire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/perspire" target="_blank">perspire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/respire" target="_blank">respire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/transpire" target="_blank">transpire</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spirit">spirit (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-13c., "animating or vital principle in man and animals," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">spirit</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">espirit</span> "spirit, soul" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">esprit</span>) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">spiritus</span> "a breathing (respiration, and of the wind), breath; breath of a god," hence "inspiration; breath of life," hence "life;" also "disposition, character; high spirit, vigor, courage; pride, arrogance," related to <span class="foreign">spirare</span> "to breathe," from PIE <span class="foreign">*(s)peis-</span> "to blow" (cognates: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pisto</span> "to play on the flute").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "supernatural immaterial creature; angel, demon; an apparition, invisible corporeal being of an airy nature" is attested from mid-14c.; from late 14c. as "a ghost" (see <a href="/etym/s/ghost" target="_blank">ghost</a> (n.)). From c. 1500 as "a nature, character"; sense of "essential principle of something" (in a non-theological context, as in <span class="foreign">Spirit of St. Louis</span>) is attested from 1680s, common after 1800; <span class="foreign">Spirit of '76</span> in reference to the qualities that sparked and sustained the American Revolution is attested by 1797 in William Cobbett's "Porcupine's Gazette and Daily Advertiser."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From late 14c. in alchemy as "volatile substance; distillate;" from c. 1500 as "substance capable of uniting the fixed and the volatile elements of the philosopher's stone." Hence <span class="foreign">spirits</span> "volatile substance;" sense narrowed to "strong alcoholic liquor" by 1670s. This also is the sense in <span class="foreign">spirit level</span> (1768). Also from mid-14c. as "character, disposition; way of thinking and feeling, state of mind; source of a human desire;" in Middle English <span class="foreign">freedom of spirit</span> meant "freedom of choice." From late 14c. as "divine substance, divine mind, God;" also "Christ" or His divine nature; "the Holy Ghost; divine power;" also, "extension of divine power to man; inspiration, a charismatic state; charismatic power, especially of prophecy." Also "essential nature, essential quality." From 1580s in metaphoric sense "animation, vitality."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; According to Barnhart and OED, originally in English mainly from passages in Vulgate, where the Latin word translates Greek <span class="foreign">pneuma</span> and Hebrew <span class="foreign">ruah</span>. Distinction between "soul" and "spirit" (as "seat of emotions") became current in Christian terminology (such as Greek <span class="foreign">psykhe</span> vs. <span class="foreign">pneuma</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">anima</span> vs. <span class="foreign">spiritus</span>) but "is without significance for earlier periods" [Buck]. Latin <span class="foreign">spiritus</span>, usually in classical Latin "breath," replaces <span class="foreign">animus</span> in the sense "spirit" in the imperial period and appears in Christian writings as the usual equivalent of Greek <span class="foreign">pneuma</span>. <span class="foreign">Spirit-rapping</span> is from 1852.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spirit">spirit (v.)</a></dt><dd>1590s, "to make more active or energetic" (of blood, alcohol, etc.), from <a href="/etym/s/spirit" target="_blank">spirit</a> (n.). The meaning "carry off or away secretly" (as though by supernatural agency) is first recorded 1660s. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Spirited" target="_blank">Spirited</a>; <span class="foreign">spiriting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/spirit">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. The requirement for work permits violates the <b>spirit</b> of the 1950 treaty. </dt> <dd>对工作许可证的要求违反了1950年条约的精神。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2.
