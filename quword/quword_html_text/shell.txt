                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/shell">shell</a> 壳，壳体，外壳，炮弹</span><p>来自古英语 scell,贝壳，蛋壳，来自 Proto-Germanic*skaljo,分开，切开，来自 PIE*skel,切， 分，词源同 scale,shelf.引申诸相关比喻义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shell">shell</a></dt><dd><b>shell: </b><span>[OE] <i>Shell </i>goes back ultimately to the Germanic base *<i>skal</i>- ‘divide, separate’, which also produced English <i>scale</i>, <i>scalp</i>, <i>school </i>(of fish), <i>shale</i>, <i>shelter</i>, <i>shield</i>, <i>shoal </i>(of fish), <i>skill</i>, and <i>skol</i>. Its underlying meaning is hence a ‘covering that splits off or is peeled off’. Its immediate Germanic ancestor was *<i>skaljō</i>, which also produced Dutch <i>schel </i>and Norwegian <i>skjæl</i>. <i>Shellac </i>[18] is a compound of <i>shell </i>and <i>lac </i>‘lacquer, varnish’ (a word of Sanskrit origin, of which <i>lacquer </i>is a derivative), and is a direct translation of French <i>laque en écailles </i>‘lac (melted) in thin plates’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a>, <a href="/etym/s/scalp" target="_blank">scalp</a>, <a href="/etym/s/school" target="_blank">school</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shale" target="_blank">shale</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shelter" target="_blank">shelter</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shield" target="_blank">shield</a>, <a href="/etym/s/shoal" target="_blank">shoal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skill" target="_blank">skill</a>, <a href="/etym/s/skol" target="_blank">skol</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shell">shell (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">sciell</span>, <span class="foreign">scill</span>, Anglian <span class="foreign">scell</span> "seashell, eggshell," related to Old English <span class="foreign">scealu</span> "shell, husk," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skaljo</span> "piece cut off; shell; scale" (cognates: West Frisian <span class="foreign">skyl</span> "peel, rind," Middle Low German <span class="foreign">schelle</span> "pod, rind, egg shell," Gothic <span class="foreign">skalja</span> "tile"), with the shared notion of "covering that splits off," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)kel-</span> (1) "to cut, cleave" (cognates: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">skolika</span> "shell," Russian <span class="foreign">skala</span> "bark, rind;" see <a href="/etym/s/scale" target="_blank">scale</a> (n.1)). Italian <span class="foreign">scaglia</span> "chip" is from Germanic.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "mere exterior" is from 1650s; that of "hollow framework" is from 1791. Meaning "structure for a band or orchestra" is attested from 1938. Military use (1640s) was first of hand grenades, in reference to the metal case in which the gunpowder and shot were mixed; the notion is of a "hollow object" filled with explosives. Hence <span class="foreign">shell shock</span>, first recorded 1915. <span class="foreign">Shell game</span> "a swindle" is from 1890, from a version of three-card monte played with a pea and walnut shells.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shell">shell (v.)</a></dt><dd>1560s, "to remove (a nut, etc.) from a shell," from <a href="/etym/s/shell" target="_blank">shell</a> (n.). The meaning "to bombard with shells" is first attested 1856. To <span class="foreign">shell out</span> "disburse" (1801) is a figurative use from the image of extracting nuts. Related: <span class="foreign">Shelled</span>; <span class="foreign">shelling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
