                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/approve">approve</a> 同意</span><p>前缀ap-同ad-. prove, 证明。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/approve">approve</a></dt><dd><b>approve: </b><span>[14] The Latin source of <i>approve</i>, <i>approbāre</i>, was a derivative of <i>probāre</i>, source of English <i>prove</i>. <i>Probāre </i>originally meant ‘test something to find if it is good’ (it was based on Latin <i>probus </i>‘good’) and this became extended to ‘show something to be good or valid’. It was this sense that was taken up by <i>approbāre </i>and carried further to ‘assent to as good’. When English acquired the word, via Old French <i>aprover</i>, it still carried the notion of ‘demonstrating’, but this was gradually taken over exclusively by <i>prove</i>, and the senses ‘sanction’ and ‘commend’, present since the beginning, established their primacy.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/probity" target="_blank">probity</a>, <a href="/etym/s/prove" target="_blank">prove</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/approve">approve (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "to demonstrate, prove;" mid-14c., "to attest (something) with authority," from Old French <span class="foreign">aprover</span> (Modern French <span class="foreign">approuver</span>) "approve, agree to," from Latin <span class="foreign">approbare</span> "to assent to as good, regard as good," from <span class="foreign">ad-</span> "to" (see <a href="/etym/s/ad-" target="_blank">ad-</a>) + <span class="foreign">probare</span> "to try, test something (to find if it is good)," from <span class="foreign">probus</span> "honest, genuine" (see <a href="/etym/s/prove" target="_blank">prove</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The meaning extended late 14c. to "to sanction, endorse, confirm formally" then to "assent to (something) as good" (early 15c.), especially in reference to the actions of authorities, parliaments, etc. Related: <span class="foreign">Approved</span>; <span class="foreign">approving</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
