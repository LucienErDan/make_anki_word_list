                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hawk">hawk</a> 鹰，隼</span><p>来自中古英语havek,来自PIE*kap,抓住，词源同have,heave,capable.后用于指猛禽鹰或隼。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hawk">hawk</a></dt><dd><b>hawk: </b><span>English has three current words <i>hawk</i>. The oldest, denoting the bird of prey [OE], comes from a prehistoric West and North Germanic *<i>khabukaz</i>, which also produced German <i>habicht</i>, Dutch <i>havik</i>, Swedish <i>hök</i>, and Danish <i>hög</i>. <i>Hawk </i>‘peddle’ [16] is a back-formation from <i>hawker</i>. This was probably borrowed from Low German <i>höker</i>, a derivative ultimately of Middle Low German <i>hōken </i>‘peddle’, which may well have been formed from the same base as produced English <i>huckster</i>. <i>Hawk </i>‘clear the throat’ [16] probably originated as an imitation of the noise it denotes.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/huckster" target="_blank">huckster</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hawk">hawk (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">hauk</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">havek</span> (c. 1200), from Old English <span class="foreign">hafoc</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">heafuc</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">heafoc</span>, "hawk," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*habukaz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">haukr</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">habuc</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">havik</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">habuh</span>, German <span class="foreign">Habicht</span> "hawk"), from a root meaning "to seize," from PIE <span class="foreign">*kap-</span> "to grasp" (cognates: Russian <span class="foreign">kobec</span> "a kind of falcon;" see <a href="/etym/s/capable" target="_blank">capable</a>). Transferred sense of "militarist" attested from 1956, probably based on its opposite, <a href="/etym/s/dove" target="_blank">dove</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hawk">hawk (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to sell in the open, peddle," late 15c., back-formation from <span class="foreign">hawker</span> "itinerant vendor" (c. 1400), agent noun from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">höken</span> "to peddle, carry on the back, squat," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*huk-</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Hawked</span>; <span class="foreign">hawking</span>. Despite the etymological connection with stooping under a burden on one's back, a <span class="foreign">hawker</span> is technically distinguished from a <span class="foreign">peddler</span> by use of a horse and cart or a van.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hawk">hawk (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to hunt with a hawk," mid-14c., from <a href="/etym/s/hawk" target="_blank">hawk</a> (n.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hawk">hawk (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"to clear one's throat," 1580s, imitative.</dd></dl></div></div>
