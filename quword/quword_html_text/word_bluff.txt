            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bluff">bluff</a> 吹牛</span><p>词源同blow. 或直接来自拟声词。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bluff">bluff</a></dt><dd><b>bluff: </b><span>English has two words <i>bluff</i>, one or perhaps both of them of Dutch origin. The older, ‘hearty’ [17], originally referred to ships, and meant ‘having a flat vertical bow’. This nautical association suggests a Dutch provenance, though no thoroughly convincing source has been found. The sense ‘flat, vertical, (and broad)’ came to be applied to land features, such as cliffs (hence the noun <i>bluff </i>‘high steep bank’, which emerged in America in the 18th century).<br><br>The word’s metaphorical extension to people was at first derogatory – ‘rough, blunt’ – but the more favourable ‘hearty’ had developed by the early 19th century. <i>Bluff </i>‘deceive’ [19] was originally a US poker term. It comes from Dutch <i>bluffen </i>‘boast’, the descendant of Middle Dutch <i>bluffen </i>‘swell up’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bluff">bluff (v.)</a></dt><dd>1839, American English, poker term, perhaps from Dutch <span class="foreign">bluffen</span> "to brag, boast," or <span class="foreign">verbluffen</span> "to baffle, mislead." An identical word meant "blindfold, hoodwink" in 1670s, but the sense evolution and connection are unclear; OED calls it "one of the numerous cant terms ... which arose between the Restoration and the reign of Queen Anne." Extended or figurative sense by 1854. Related: <span class="foreign">Bluffed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/bluffing" target="_blank">bluffing</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bluff">bluff (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"broad, vertical cliff," 1680s, from <span class="foreign">bluff</span> (adj.) "with a broad, flat front" (1620s), a sailors' word, probably from Dutch <span class="foreign">blaf</span> "flat, broad." Apparently a North Sea nautical term for ships with flat vertical bows, later extended to landscape features.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bluff">bluff (n.2)</a></dt><dd>1844 as an alternative name for <span class="foreign">poker</span>; from <a href="/etym/s/bluff" target="_blank">bluff</a> (v.). As "an act of bluffing" by 1864.</dd></dl></div></div>
