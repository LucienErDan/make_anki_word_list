                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mortar">mortar</a> 灰泥，捣灰池，砂浆，研钵</span><p>来自拉丁语mortarium,研钵，捣药罐，搅拌碗，词源不详。可能来自PIE*mer,捣碎，伤害，来自PIE*smi,砍，劈，词源同smith.后引申词义捣灰池，灰泥，砂浆等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mortar">mortar</a></dt><dd><b>mortar: </b><span>[13] Latin <i>mortārium</i>, a word of unknown origin, denoted both a ‘bowl for grinding’ and, by extension, the ‘substance made in such a bowl’. These twin meanings survived through Anglo-Norman <i>morter </i>into modern English <i>mortar </i>as the ‘bowl used with a pestle’ and a ‘building mixture of cement, sand, and water’. The shape of the former led in the 17th century to the word’s application to a ‘short cannon’. The use of <i>mortarboard </i>for a ‘square flat academic cap’ dates from the mid-19th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mortar">mortar (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"mixture of cement," late 13c., from Old French <span class="foreign">mortier</span> "builder's mortar, plaster; bowl for mixing" (13c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">mortarium</span> "mortar," also "crushed drugs," probably the same word as <span class="foreign">mortarium</span> "bowl for mixing or pounding" (see <a href="/etym/s/mortar" target="_blank">mortar</a> (n.2)). Dutch <span class="foreign">mortel</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mörtel</span> are from Latin or French.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mortar">mortar (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"bowl for pounding," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">mortier</span> "bowl; builder's mortar," from Latin <span class="foreign">mortarium</span> "bowl for mixing or pounding," also "material prepared in it," of unknown origin and impossible now to determine which sense was original (Watkins says probably from PIE root <span class="foreign">*mer-</span> "to rub away, harm;" see <a href="/etym/s/morbid" target="_blank">morbid</a>). Late Old English had <span class="foreign">mortere</span>, from the same Latin source, which might also be a source of the modern word. German <span class="foreign">Mörser</span> also is from Latin.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mortar">mortar (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"short cannon" fired at a high angle and meant to secure a vertical fall of the projectile, 1550s, originally <span class="foreign">mortar-piece</span>, from Middle French <span class="foreign">mortier</span> "short cannon," in Old French, "bowl for mixing or pounding" (see <a href="/etym/s/mortar" target="_blank">mortar</a> (n.2)). So called for its shape.</dd></dl></div></div>
