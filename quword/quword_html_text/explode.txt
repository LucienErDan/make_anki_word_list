                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/explode">explode</a> 爆炸</span><p>ex-, 向外。-plod, 拍掌声，爆炸声，词源同implode.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/explode">explode</a></dt><dd><b>explode: </b><span>[16] The use of <i>explode </i>to mean ‘burst with destructive force’ is a comparatively recent, late 19th-century development. The Latin verb <i>explōdere</i>, from which it comes, signified something quite different – ‘drive off the stage with hisses and boos’ (it was a compound formed from the prefix <i>ex</i>- ‘out’ and <i>plaudere </i>‘clap’, source of English <i>applaud </i>and <i>plaudits</i>).<br><br>From this developed the figurative sense ‘reject, disapprove’, which was how the word was used when it was first taken over into English: ‘Not that I wholly explode Astrology; I believe there is something in it’, Thomas Tryon, <i>Miscellanea </i>1696 (the modern notion of ‘exploding a theory’ is descended from this usage). In the 17th century, however, the Latin verb’s original sense was reintroduced, and it survived into the 19th century: ‘In the playhouse when he doth wrong, no critic is so apt to hiss and explode him’, Henry Fielding, <i>Tom Jones </i>1749.<br><br>Towards the end of the 17th century we find the first traces of a metaphorical use that combines the notion of ‘driving out, expelling’ with ‘loud noise’ (‘the effects of Lightning, exploded from the Clouds’, Robert Plot, <i>Natural History of Staffordshire </i>1679), but it was not to be for more than a century that the meaning element ‘drive out’ was replaced by the ‘burst, shatter’ of present-day English <i>explode </i>(Dr Johnson makes no mention of it in his <i>Dictionary </i>1755, for example) Today the notion of ‘bursting violently’ is primary, that of ‘loud noise’ probably secondary, although still present.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/applause" target="_blank">applause</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plaudits" target="_blank">plaudits</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/explode">explode (v.)</a></dt><dd>1530s (transitive), "to reject with scorn," from Latin <span class="foreign">explodere</span> "drive out or off by clapping, hiss off, hoot off," originally theatrical, "to drive an actor off the stage by making noise," hence "drive out, reject, destroy the repute of" (a sense surviving in <span class="foreign">an exploded theory</span>), from <span class="foreign">ex-</span> "out" (see <a href="/etym/s/ex-" target="_blank">ex-</a>) + <span class="foreign">plaudere</span> "to clap the hands, applaud," which is of uncertain origin. Athenian audiences were highly demonstrative. clapping and shouting approval, stamping, hissing, and hooting for disapproval. The Romans seem to have done likewise.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; At the close of the performance of a comedy in the Roman theatre one of the actors dismissed the audience, with a request for their approbation, the expression being usually <span class="foreign">plaudite</span>, <span class="foreign">vos plaudite</span>, or <span class="foreign">vos valete et plaudite</span>. [William Smith, "A First Latin Reading Book," 1890]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; English used it to mean "drive out with violence and sudden noise" (1650s), later "cause to burst suddenly and noisily" (1794). Intransitive sense of "go off with a loud noise" is from 1790, American English; figurative sense of "to burst with destructive force" is by 1882; that of "burst into sudden activity" is from 1817; of population by 1959. Related: <span class="foreign">Exploded</span>; <span class="foreign">exploding</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/explode">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. Catherine was ready to <b>explode</b>. "I think you're contemptible!" </dt> <dd>凯瑟琳气得快要炸了。“我觉得你真是可鄙！”</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. The car was juddering and vibrating as if it would <b>explode</b>. </dt> <dd>汽车剧烈地摇晃着、颤动着，就像要爆炸似的。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. The bomb must <b>explode</b> within less than a millionth of a second. </dt> <dd>炸弹必须在不到百万分之一秒的时间内爆炸。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. These are the facts that <b>explode</b> their so-called economic miracle. </dt> <dd>这些就是用来粉碎他们所谓的经济奇迹的事实。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. She heard laughter <b>explode</b>, then die. </dt> <dd>她听到有人爆笑了一阵，后来就没声音了。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/explode">explode 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
