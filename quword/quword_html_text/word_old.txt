                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/old">old</a> 老的</span><p>来自古英语ald,来自PIE*al,生长，成长，词源同adult,adolescent,elder.-d,过去分词后缀。即长大了的，变老的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/old">old</a></dt><dd><b>old: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, <i>old </i>means ‘grown-up’. It comes from a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>altha </i>(source also of German <i>alt </i>and Dutch <i>oud</i>) which was a past-participial adjective formed from the base of a verb meaning ‘grow, nourish’. (A precisely similar formation from the related Latin verb <i>adolēscere </i>‘grow’ has given English <i>adult</i>, and Latin <i>altus </i>‘high’ – source of English <i>altitude </i>[14] – was originally a past-participial adjective too, derived from <i>alēre </i>‘nourish’, although it has metaphoricized ‘growing’ to ‘height’ rather than ‘age’.) <i>Elderly </i>and the comparative and superlative <i>elder</i>, <i>eldest </i>come ultimately from the same source. <i>World </i>began life as a compound noun of which the noun *<i>ald</i>- ‘age’, a relative of <i>old</i>, formed the second element.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/adult" target="_blank">adult</a>, <a href="/etym/s/altitude" target="_blank">altitude</a>, <a href="/etym/s/elder" target="_blank">elder</a>, <a href="/etym/s/world" target="_blank">world</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/old">old (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ald</span> (Anglian), <span class="foreign">eald</span> (West Saxon) "aged, antique, primeval; elder, experienced," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*althaz</span> "grown up, adult" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ald</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">alþeis</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">oud</span>, German <span class="foreign">alt</span>), originally a past participle stem of a verb meaning "grow, nourish" (compare Gothic <span class="foreign">alan</span> "to grow up," Old Norse <span class="foreign">ala</span> "to nourish"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*al-</span> (3) "to grow, nourish" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">aldaino</span> "make grow, strengthen," <span class="foreign">althein</span>, <span class="foreign"> althainein</span> "to get well;" Latin <span class="foreign">alere</span> "to feed, nourish, bring up, increase," <span class="foreign">altus</span> "high," literally "grown tall," <span class="foreign">almus</span> "nurturing, nourishing," <span class="foreign">alumnus</span> "fosterling, step-child;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">alim</span> "I nourish").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The usual PIE root is <span class="foreign">*sen-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/senior" target="_blank">senior</a> (adj.)). A few Indo-European languages distinguish words for "old" (vs. young) from words for "old" (vs. new), and some have separate words for aged persons as opposed to old things. Latin <span class="foreign">senex</span> was used of aged living things, mostly persons, while <span class="foreign">vetus</span> (literally "having many years") was used of inanimate things. Greek <span class="foreign">geraios</span> was used mostly of humans; Greek <span class="foreign">palaios</span> was used mostly of things, of persons only in a derogatory sense. Greek also had <span class="foreign">arkhaios</span>, literally "belonging to the beginning," which parallels French <span class="foreign">ancien</span>, used mostly with reference to things "of former times."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Old English also had <span class="foreign">fyrn</span> "ancient," related to Old English <span class="foreign">feor</span> "far, distant" (see <a href="/etym/s/far" target="_blank">far</a>, and compare Gothic <span class="foreign">fairneis</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">forn</span> "old, of old, of former times," Old High German <span class="foreign">firni</span> "old, experienced"). The original Old English vowel is preserved in Scots <span class="foreign">auld</span>, also in <a href="/etym/s/alderman" target="_blank">alderman</a>. The original comparative and superlative (<span class="foreign">elder</span>, <span class="foreign">eldest</span>) are retained in particular uses.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; First record of <span class="foreign">old-timer</span> is from 1860. Expression <span class="foreign">old as the hills</span> first recorded 1819. The <span class="foreign">good old days</span> dates from 1828. <span class="foreign">Of old</span> "of old times" is from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Old maid</span> "woman who remains single well beyond the usual marrying age" is from 1520s; the card game is attested by that name from 1844. <span class="foreign">Old man</span> "man who has lived long" is from c. 1200; sense of "husband, father, boss" is from 1854, earlier (1830) it was military slang for "commanding officer;" <span class="foreign">old lady</span> "wife, mother" is attested from c. 1775. <span class="foreign">Old English</span> is attested from 1701, originally as a type of font. <span class="foreign">Old boy</span> originally was a former pupil of one of the English public schools. <span class="foreign">Old Testament</span> attested from mid-14c.</dd></dl></div></div>
