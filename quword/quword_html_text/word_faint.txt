                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. fingere (form root fig-) => Old French variant form feindre (pp. feint) "pretend, imitate, feign".<br />
2. => feint: pretend, imitate, feign.<br />
3. feint => faint (元音音变) "show weakness to avoid one's duty by pretending".<br />
4. 该词的本意是假装虚弱、脆弱来逃避责任、逃避各种事情，后来的词义演变却因假变真，变成了真正的“虚弱、脆弱，微弱，懦弱”，甚至是“昏厥，昏倒”。<br />
5. 谐音“昏(方言化拼音: fen)特、昏倒”。<br />
6. 区分：faint, feint: pain => faint => 因为太痛了，以至于痛昏过去了。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/faint">faint</a> 微弱的，虚弱的</span><p>来自古法语feint, 来自feign, 假装，虚构，欺骗。引申词义底气不足的，微弱的，虚弱的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/faint">faint</a></dt><dd><b>faint: </b><span>[13] <i>Faint </i>comes from Old French <i>faint</i>, which was originally the past participle of the verb <i>faindre</i>, <i>feindre </i>‘pretend, shirk’ (whence English <i>feign</i>). This meant ‘pretended, simulated’, ‘lazy, shirking’, and ‘cowardly’, and all these senses were originally taken over by English. None now survives except the last, in the phrase <i>faint heart</i>, but in their place the underlying notion of ‘feebleness’ has produced ‘not bright, dim’ and ‘weak and dizzy’. The verb, based on the second of these, developed in the late 14th century. The variant spelling <i>feint</i>, used of printed lines, was introduced in the mid 19th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/feign" target="_blank">feign</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/faint">faint (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "enfeebled; wearied, exhausted," from Old French <span class="foreign">faint</span>, <span class="foreign">feint</span> "false, deceitful; sham, artificial; weak, faint, lazy, indolent, cowardly," past participle of <span class="foreign">feindre</span> "hesitate, falter, be indolent, show weakness, avoid one's duty by pretending" (see <a href="/etym/s/feign" target="_blank">feign</a>). Also from c. 1300 as "deceitful; unreliable; false." Meaning "wanting in spirit or courage, cowardly" (a sense now mostly encountered in <a href="/etym/s/faint-hearted" target="_blank">faint-hearted</a>) is from early 14c. From early 15c. of actions, functions, colors, etc., "weak, feeble, poor." Meaning "producing a feeble impression upon the senses" is from 1650s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/faint">faint (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "grow weak, become enfeebled," also "lack courage or spirit, be faint-hearted," and "to pretend, feign;" from <a href="/etym/s/faint" target="_blank">faint</a> (adj.). Sense of "swoon, lose consciousness" is from c. 1400. Also used in Middle English of the fading of colors, flowers, etc. Related: <span class="foreign">Fainted</span>; <span class="foreign">fainting</span>. For Chaucer and Shakespeare, also a transitive verb ("It faints me").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/faint">faint (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "faintness, faint-heartedness," from <a href="/etym/s/faint" target="_blank">faint</a> (adj.). From 1808 as "a swoon."</dd></dl></div></div>
