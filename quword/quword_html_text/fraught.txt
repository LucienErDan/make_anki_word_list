                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. freight => fraught.<br />
2. 该词和freight（货运；货物）同源；满则沉，“充满”和“货物”统一于沉甸甸的感觉，试着想象一辆满载货物的货车的画面。联想：fraught 音似“富饶的”，富饶则物资充裕，从而引申出“充满”之义。进而又引伸出“忧虑的”含义，表示“心里充满了不安和忧虑”等。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fraught">fraught</a> 充满的，忧虑的</span><p>来自freight的拼写变体，原指船只装满货物的，满载的。后来该词用于心理学术语，指心里面装满担子的，忧虑的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fraught">fraught</a></dt><dd><b>fraught: </b><span>[14] <i>Fraught </i>and <i>freight </i>[15] are related, and share the underlying meaning ‘load’. But whereas <i>freight </i>has stayed close to its semantic roots, <i>fraught</i>, which started out as ‘laden’, has moved on via ‘supplied or filled with something’ to specifically ‘filled with anxiety or tension’. It was originally the past participle of a now obsolete verb <i>fraught </i>‘load a ship’, which was borrowed from Middle Dutch <i>vrachten</i>.<br><br>This in turn was a derivative of the noun <i>vracht </i>‘load, cargo’, a variant of <i>vrecht </i>(from which English gets <i>freight</i>). Both <i>vracht </i>and <i>vrecht </i>probably go back to a prehistoric Germanic noun *<i>fraaikhtiz</i>, whose second element *-<i>aikhtiz </i>is related to English <i>owe </i>and <i>own</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/freight" target="_blank">freight</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fraught">fraught (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "freighted, laden, loaded, stored with supplies" (of vessels); figurative use from early 15c.; past participle adjective from obsolete verb <span class="foreign">fraught</span> "to load (a ship) with cargo," Middle English <span class="foreign">fraughten</span> (c. 1400), which always was rarer than the past participle, from noun <span class="foreign">fraught</span> "a load, cargo, lading of a ship" (early 13c.), which is the older form of <a href="/etym/s/freight" target="_blank">freight</a> (n.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; This apparently is from a North Sea Germanic source, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vrecht</span>, <span class="foreign">vracht</span> "hire for a ship, freight," or similar words in Middle Low German or Frisian, apparently originally "earnings," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fra-aihtiz</span> "property, absolute possession," from <span class="foreign">*fra-</span>, here probably intensive + <span class="foreign">*aigan</span> "be master of, possess" (see <a href="/etym/s/owe" target="_blank">owe</a> (v.)). Related: <span class="foreign">Fraughtage</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
