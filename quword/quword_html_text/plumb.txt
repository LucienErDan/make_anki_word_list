                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plumb">plumb</a> 铅，铅垂，使垂直，探索，钻研，恰好</span><p>来自拉丁语plumbum,铅，铅球，可能借自希腊语molybdos,铅，词源同molybdenum.后用于 指测量垂直度的铅球，铅锤，引申词义探索，钻研，以及副词词义恰好，正好等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plumb">plumb</a></dt><dd><b>plumb: </b><span>[13] <i>Plumb </i>comes via Old French *<i>plombe </i>from Latin <i>plumbum </i>‘lead’, a word of uncertain origin. Of its modern English uses, the verbal ‘sound the depths’ comes from the use of a line weighted with lead (a <i>plumb line</i>) to measure the depth of water and the adverbial ‘exactly’ from the use of a similar line to determine verticality. Related words in English include <i>aplomb; plumber </i>[14] (originally simply a ‘worker in lead’, but eventually, since water pipes were once made of lead, a ‘pipe-layer’); <i>plummet </i>[14] (a diminutive form coined in Old French); and <i>plunge </i>[14] (from the Vulgar Latin derivative *<i>plumbicāre </i>‘sound with a plumb’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/aplomb" target="_blank">aplomb</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plumber" target="_blank">plumber</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plummet" target="_blank">plummet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plunge" target="_blank">plunge</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plumb">plumb (n.)</a></dt><dd>"lead hung on a string to show the vertical line," early 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">*plombe</span>, <span class="foreign">plomee</span> "sounding lead," and directly from Late Latin <span class="foreign">*plumba</span>, originally plural of Latin <span class="foreign">plumbum</span> "lead (the metal), lead ball; pipe; pencil," a word of unknown origin, related to Greek <span class="foreign">molybdos</span> "lead" (dialectal <span class="foreign">bolimos</span>) and perhaps from an extinct Mediterranean language, perhaps Iberian.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plumb">plumb (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 15c., "to sink" (like lead), from <a href="/etym/s/plumb" target="_blank">plumb</a> (n.). Meaning "take soundings with a plumb" is first recorded 1560s; figurative sense of "to get to the bottom of" is from 1590s. Related: <span class="foreign">Plumbed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/plumbing" target="_blank">plumbing</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plumb">plumb (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"perpendicular, vertical," mid-15c., from <a href="/etym/s/plumb" target="_blank">plumb</a> (n.). The notion of "exact measurement" led to extended sense of "completely, downright" (1748), sometimes spelled <span class="foreign">plump</span>, <span class="foreign">plum</span>, or <span class="foreign">plunk</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
