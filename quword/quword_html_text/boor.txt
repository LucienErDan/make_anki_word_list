                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/boor">boor</a> 粗鲁的人</span><p>来自PIE *bheue, 存在，生长，居住，同be. 原指农夫，词源同husband.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/boor">boor</a></dt><dd><b>boor: </b><span>[15] <i>Boor </i>was borrowed into English either from Low German <i>hūr </i>or from Dutch <i>boer </i>(<i>Boer </i>‘Dutch colonist in South Africa’ is a later, 19thcentury borrowing). When first acquired it meant ‘peasant farmer’, and did not develop its modern explicit connotations of coarseness and rudeness until the 16th century. Its ultimate source was the Germanic base *<i>bū</i>- ‘dwell’, so its original meaning was something like ‘person who lives in a particular place’ (the related <i>neighbour </i>was literally ‘someone who lives nearby’).<br><br>Other English words from the same source include <i>be</i>, <i>booth</i>, <i>bound </i>‘intending to go’, <i>bower</i>, <i>build</i>, <i>burly</i>, <i>byelaw</i>, <i>byre</i>, and the -<i>band </i>of <i>husband</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/be" target="_blank">be</a>, <a href="/etym/s/boer" target="_blank">boer</a>, <a href="/etym/s/booth" target="_blank">booth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bower" target="_blank">bower</a>, <a href="/etym/s/build" target="_blank">build</a>, <a href="/etym/s/burly" target="_blank">burly</a>, <a href="/etym/s/byelaw" target="_blank">byelaw</a>, <a href="/etym/s/byre" target="_blank">byre</a>, <a href="/etym/s/husband" target="_blank">husband</a>, <a href="/etym/s/neighbour" target="_blank">neighbour</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/boor">boor (n.)</a></dt><dd>13c., from Old French <span class="foreign">bovier</span> "herdsman," from Latin <span class="foreign">bovis</span>, genitive of <span class="foreign">bos</span> "cow, ox." Re-introduced 16c. from Dutch <span class="foreign">boer</span>, from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gheboer</span> "fellow dweller," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*buram</span> "dweller," especially "farmer," from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhu-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*bheue-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/be" target="_blank">be</a>). Original meaning was "peasant farmer" (compare German <span class="foreign">Bauer</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">boer</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">bonde</span>), and in English it was at first applied to agricultural laborers in or from other lands, as opposed to the native <a href="/etym/s/yeoman" target="_blank">yeoman</a>; negative connotation attested by 1560s (in <span class="foreign">boorish</span>), from notion of clownish rustics. Related: <span class="foreign">Boorishness</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
