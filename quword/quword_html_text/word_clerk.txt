                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/clerk">clerk</a> 职员，书记员</span><p>来自clergy,神职人员。因为旧时大多数人是不识字的，神职人员在履行神职的同时，也从事 一些记帐记录之事，因此引申此词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/clerk">clerk</a></dt><dd><b>clerk: </b><span>[11] <i>Clerk </i>and its relatives <i>cleric </i>and <i>clergy </i>owe their existence ultimately to a Biblical reference, in Deuteronomy xviii 2, to the Levites, members of an Israelite tribe whose men were assistants to the Temple priests: ‘Therefore shall they have no inheritance among their brethren: the Lord is their inheritance’. Greek for ‘inheritance’ is <i>klēros</i>, and so it came about that matters relating to the Christian ministry were denoted in late Greek by the derived adjective <i>klērikós</i>.<br><br>This passed into ecclesiastical Latin as <i>clēricus</i>, which was originally borrowed into late Old English as <i>cleric </i>or <i>clerc</i>, later reinforced by Old French <i>clerc </i>to give modern English <i>clerk</i>. Its presentday bureaucratic connotations, which emerged in the 16th century, go back to an earlier time when members of the clergy were virtually the only people who could read or write.<br><br>However, religious associations have of course been preserved in <i>cleric </i>[17], from ecclesiastical Latin <i>clēricus</i>, and <i>clergy </i>[13], a blend of Old French <i>clergie </i>(a derivative of <i>clerc</i>) and <i>clerge </i>(from the ecclestiastical Latin derivative <i>clēricātus</i>). The compound <i>clergyman </i>is 16th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cleric" target="_blank">cleric</a>, <a href="/etym/s/clergy" target="_blank">clergy</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/clerk">clerk (n.)</a></dt><dd>"man ordained in the ministry," c. 1200, from Old English <span class="foreign">cleric</span> and Old French <span class="foreign">clerc</span> "clergyman, priest; scholar, student," both from Church Latin <span class="foreign">clericus</span> "a priest," noun use of adjective meaning "priestly, belonging to the <span class="foreign">clerus</span>" (see <a href="/etym/s/cleric" target="_blank">cleric</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Modern bureaucratic usage is a reminder of the dark ages when clergy alone could read and write and were employed for that skill by secular authorities. In late Old English the word can mean "king's scribe; keeper of accounts;" by c. 1200 <span class="foreign">clerk</span> took on a secondary sense in Middle English (as the cognate word did in Old French) of "anyone who can read or write." This led to the sense "assistant in a business" (c. 1500), originally a keeper of accounts, later, especially in American English, "a retail salesman" (1790). Related: <span class="foreign">Clerkship</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/clerk">clerk (v.)</a></dt><dd>"act as a clerk," 1550s, from <a href="/etym/s/clerk" target="_blank">clerk</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Clerked</span>, <span class="foreign">clerking</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
