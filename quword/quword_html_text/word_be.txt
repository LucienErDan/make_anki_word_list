                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/be">be</a> 是，存在</span><p>来自PIE *bheue , 存在，生产，将要，同源词包括future, physics。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/be">be</a></dt><dd><b>be: </b><span>[OE] There are four distinct components that go to make up the modern English verb <i>be</i>. The infinitive form <i>be </i>comes ultimately from an Indo-European base *<i>bheu</i>-, *<i>bhu</i>-, which also produced, by other routes, <i>future </i>and <i>physical</i>. Its Germanic descendant was *<i>bu</i>-, which signified on the one hand ‘dwell’ (from which we get <i>booth</i>, <i>bower</i>, <i>byre</i>, <i>build</i>, <i>burly</i>, <i>byelaw</i>, and the final element of <i>neighbour</i>), and on the other hand ‘grow, become’, which led to its adoption as part of the verb expressing ‘being’ (in Old English particularly with the future sense of ‘coming to be’). <i>Am </i>and <i>is </i>go back to the ancient Indo- European verb ‘be’, *<i>es</i>- or *<i>s</i>-, which has contributed massively to ‘be’ verbs throughout all Indo-European languages (third person present singulars Greek <i>esti</i>, Latin <i>est</i>, French <i>est</i>, German <i>ist</i>, Sanskrit <i>ásti</i>, Welsh <i>ys</i>, for example) The Indo-European first and third person singular forms were, respectively, <i>ésmi </i>and <i>ésti</i>.<br><br>For the present plural Old English used the related <i>sind(on) </i>(as found in Latin <i>sunt</i>, French <i>sont</i>, and German <i>sind</i>), but this died out in the 12th century, to be replaced by <i>are</i>, which comes from a Germanic base *<i>ar</i>- of unknown origin. From the same source is the now archaic second person singular <i>art</i>. The past tense forms <i>was, were </i>come ultimately from an Indo-European base *<i>wes</i>which meant ‘dwell, remain’.<br><br>Related words in other Indo-European languages include Sanskrit <i>vásati </i>‘dwell, remain’ and Gothic <i>wisan </i>‘remain, continue’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/booth" target="_blank">booth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bower" target="_blank">bower</a>, <a href="/etym/s/build" target="_blank">build</a>, <a href="/etym/s/burly" target="_blank">burly</a>, <a href="/etym/s/byelaw" target="_blank">byelaw</a>, <a href="/etym/s/byre" target="_blank">byre</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/be">be (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">beon</span>, <span class="foreign">beom</span>, <span class="foreign">bion</span> "be, exist, come to be, become, happen," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*biju-</span> "I am, I will be." This "b-root" is from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bheue-</span> "to be, exist, grow, come into being," and in addition to the words in English it yielded German present first and second person singular (<span class="foreign">bin</span>, <span class="foreign">bist</span>, from Old High German <span class="foreign">bim</span> "I am," <span class="foreign">bist</span> "thou art"), Latin perfective tenses of <span class="foreign">esse</span> (<span class="foreign">fui</span> "I was," etc.), Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">byti</span> "be," Greek <span class="foreign">phu-</span> "become," Old Irish <span class="foreign">bi'u</span> "I am," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">bu'ti</span> "to be," Russian <span class="foreign">byt'</span> "to be," etc. It also is behind Sanskrit <span class="foreign">bhavah</span> "becoming," <span class="foreign">bhavati</span> "becomes, happens," <span class="foreign">bhumih</span> "earth, world." &#13; <br /><br />&#13; The modern verb <span class="foreign">to be</span> in its entirety represents the merger of two once-distinct verbs, the "b-root" represented by <span class="foreign">be</span> and the <span class="foreign">am/was</span> verb, which was itself a conglomerate. Roger Lass ("Old English") describes the verb as "a collection of semantically related paradigm fragments," while Weekley calls it "an accidental conglomeration from the different Old English dial[ect]s." It is the most irregular verb in Modern English and the most common. Collective in all Germanic languages, it has eight different forms in Modern English:&#13; <br /><br />BE (infinitive, subjunctive, imperative)&#13; <br />AM (present 1st person singular)&#13; <br />ARE (present 2nd person singular and all plural)&#13; <br />IS (present 3rd person singular)&#13; <br />WAS (past 1st and 3rd persons singular)&#13; <br />WERE (past 2nd person singular, all plural; subjunctive)&#13; <br />BEING (progressive &amp; present participle; gerund)&#13; <br />BEEN (perfect participle).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The paradigm in Old English was:<br /><br /><table width="400" bgcolor="#CCFFFF" cellspacing="1" cellpadding="4" border="1"><tr><td> </td><td>SING.</td><td>PL.</td></tr><tr><td>1st pres.</td><td>ic eom<br />ic beo</td><td>we sind(on)<br />we beoð</td></tr><tr><td>2nd pres.</td><td>þu eart<br />þu bist</td><td>ge sind(on)<br />ge beoð</td></tr><tr><td>3rd pres.</td><td>he is<br />he bið</td><td>hie sind(on)<br />hie beoð</td></tr><tr><td>1st pret.</td><td>ic wæs</td><td>we wæron</td></tr><tr><td>2nd pret.</td><td>þu wære</td><td>ge waeron</td></tr><tr><td>3rd pret.</td><td>heo wæs</td><td>hie wæron</td></tr><tr><td>1st pret. subj.</td><td>ic wære</td><td>we wæren</td></tr><tr><td>2nd pret. subj.</td><td>þu wære</td><td>ge wæren</td></tr><tr><td>3rd pret. subj.</td><td>Egcferð wære</td><td>hie wæren</td></tr></table><br /><br />The "b-root" had no past tense in Old English, but often served as future tense of <span class="foreign">am/was</span>. In 13c. it took the place of the infinitive, participle and imperative forms of <span class="foreign">am/was</span>. Later its plural forms (<span class="foreign">we beth, ye ben, they be</span>) became standard in Middle English and it made inroads into the singular (<span class="foreign">I be, thou beest, he beth</span>), but forms of <span class="foreign">are</span> claimed this turf in the 1500s and replaced <span class="foreign">be</span> in the plural. For the origin and evolution of the <span class="foreign">am/was</span> branches of this tangle, see <a href="/etym/s/am" target="_blank">am</a> and <a href="/etym/s/was" target="_blank">was</a>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; That but this blow Might be the be all, and the end all. ["Macbeth" I.vii.5]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
