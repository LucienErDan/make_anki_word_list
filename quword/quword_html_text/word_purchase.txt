                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/purchase">purchase</a> 采购，购买，握紧，抓牢</span><p>pur-,向前，在前，chase,追逐。引申词义得到，获得，握紧，抓牢，后用于指采购，购买，且成为其主要词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/purchase">purchase</a></dt><dd><b>purchase: </b><span>[13] To <i>purchase </i>something is etymologically to ‘hunt it down’. It comes from Old French <i>pourchacier </i>‘pursue’, hence ‘try to obtain’, a compound verb formed from the intensive prefix <i>pour</i>- and <i>chacier </i>‘pursue’ (source of English <i>chase</i>). It arrived in English meaning ‘obtain’. This sense had virtually died out by the end of the 17th century, but not before it had evolved in the 14th century to ‘buy’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/chase" target="_blank">chase</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/purchase">purchase (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "acquire, obtain; get, receive; procure, provide," also "accomplish or bring about; instigate; cause, contrive, plot; recruit, hire," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">purchaser</span> "go after," Old French <span class="foreign">porchacier</span> "search for, procure; purchase; aim at, strive for, pursue eagerly" (11c., Modern French <span class="foreign">pourchasser</span>), from <span class="foreign">pur-</span> "forth" (possibly used here as an intensive prefix; see <a href="/etym/s/pur-" target="_blank">pur-</a>) + Old French <span class="foreign">chacier</span> "run after, to hunt, chase" (see <a href="/etym/s/chase" target="_blank">chase</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Originally to obtain or receive as due in any way, including through merit or suffering; specific sense of "acquire for money, pay money for, buy" is from mid-14c., though the word continued to be used for "to get by conquest in war, obtain as booty" up to 17c. Related: <span class="foreign">Purchased</span>; <span class="foreign">purchasing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/purchase">purchase (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">purchas</span>, "acquisition, gain;" also, "something acquired or received, a possession; property, goods;" especially "booty, spoil; goods gained by pillage or robbery" (to <span class="foreign">make purchase</span> was "to seize by robbery"). Also "mercenary soldier, one who fights for booty." From Anglo-French <span class="foreign">purchace</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">porchaz</span> "acquisition, gain, profit; seizing, plunder; search pursuit, effort," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">purchaser</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">porchacier</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/purchase" target="_blank">purchase</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From early 14c. as "endeavor, effort, exertion; instigation, contrivance;" late 14c. as "act of acquiring, procurement." Meaning "that which is bought" is from 1580s. The sense of "hold or position for advantageously applying power" (1711) is extended from the nautical verb meaning "to haul or draw (especially by mechanical power)," often used in reference to hauling up anchors, attested from 1560s. <span class="foreign">Wif of purchase</span> (early 14c.) was a term for "concubine."</dd></dl></div></div>
