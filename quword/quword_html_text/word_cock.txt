                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cock">cock</a> 公鸡</span><p>来自PIE*keuk, 鸡叫声，词源同chicken.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cock">cock</a></dt><dd><b>cock: </b><span>[OE] The word <i>cock </i>is probably ultimately of onomatopoeic origin, imitative of the male fowl’s call (like the lengthier English <i>cock-adoodle- doo </i>[16], French <i>coquerico</i>, and German <i>kikeriki</i>). Beyond that it is difficult to go with any certainty; it reflects similar words in other languages, such as medieval Latin <i>coccus </i>and Old Norse <i>kokkr</i>, but which if any the English word was borrowed from is not clear.<br><br>It has been suggested that it goes back to a Germanic base *<i>kuk</i>-, of which a variant was the source of <i>chicken</i>, but typical Old English spellings, such as <i>kok </i>and <i>kokke</i>, suggest that it may have been a foreign borrowing rather than a native Germanic word – perhaps pointing to Germanic <i>coccus</i>. The origin of the interconnected set of senses ‘spout, tap’, ‘hammer of a firearm’, and ‘penis’ is not known; it is possible that it represents an entirely different word, but the fact that German <i>hahn </i>‘hen’ has the same meanings suggests otherwise.<br><br>Of derived words, <i>cocker </i>[19], as in ‘cocker spaniel’, comes from <i>cocking</i>, the sport of shooting woodcock, and <i>cocky </i>[18] is probably based on the notion of the cock as a spirited or swaggering bird, lording it over his hens (there may well be some connection with <i>cock </i>‘penis’, too, for there is an isolated record of <i>cocky </i>meaning ‘lecherous’ in the 16th century). <i>Cockerel </i>[15] was originally a diminutive form.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/chicken" target="_blank">chicken</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cocky" target="_blank">cocky</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cock">cock (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"male chicken," Old English <span class="foreign">cocc</span> "male bird," Old French <span class="foreign">coc</span> (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">coq</span>), Old Norse <span class="foreign">kokkr</span>, all of echoic origin. Old English <span class="foreign">cocc</span> was a nickname for "one who strutted like a cock," thus a common term in the Middle Ages for a pert boy, used of scullions, apprentices, servants, etc.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; A common personal name till c. 1500, it was affixed to Christian names as a pet diminutive, as in <span class="foreign">Wilcox</span>, <span class="foreign">Hitchcock</span>, etc. Slang sense of "penis" is attested since 1610s (but compare <span class="foreign">pillicock</span> "penis," from c. 1300); <span class="foreign">cock-teaser</span> is from 1891. A <span class="foreign">cocker spaniel</span> (1823) was trained to start woodcocks. <span class="foreign">Cock-and-bull</span> is first recorded 1620s, perhaps an allusion to Aesop's fables, with their incredible talking animals, or to a particular story, now forgotten. French has parallel expression <span class="foreign">coq-à-l'âne</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cock">cock (n.2)</a></dt><dd>in various mechanical senses, such as <span class="foreign">cock</span> of a faucet (early 15c.) is of uncertain connection with <a href="/etym/s/cock" target="_blank">cock</a> (n.1), but German has <span class="foreign">hahn</span> "cock" in many of the same senses. The <span class="foreign">cock</span> of an old matchlock firearm is 1560s, hence <span class="foreign">half-cocked</span> (for which see <a href="/etym/s/half" target="_blank">half</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cock">cock (v.)</a></dt><dd>mid-12c., <span class="foreign">cocken</span>, "to fight;" 1570s, "to swagger;" seeming contradictory modern senses of "to stand up" (as in <span class="foreign">cock one's ear</span>), c. 1600, and "to bend" (1898) are from the two <span class="foreign">cock</span> nouns. The first is probably in reference to the posture of the bird's head or tail, the second to the firearm position. To <span class="foreign">cock</span> ones hat carries the notion of "defiant boastfulness."</dd></dl></div></div>
