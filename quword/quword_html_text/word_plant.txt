                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plant">plant</a> 种植，植物，工厂，电厂</span><p>来自拉丁语planta,草木，植物，嫩枝，脚底板，来自PIE*plat,展开，放平，来自PIE*pele,展开，放平，词源同plan,place.引申动词词义种植，栽培，并由此引申工厂，工厂设备，电厂等诸多词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plant">plant</a></dt><dd><b>plant: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, a <i>plant </i>is probably something you press into the ground with the ‘sole’ of your foot. The word was borrowed from Latin <i>planta </i>‘shoot, sprout, cutting’, a derivative of the verb <i>plantāre </i>‘plant, transplant’, and it has been speculated that this was based on Latin <i>planta </i>‘sole of the foot’ (source of English <i>plantain </i>and <i>plantigrade </i>‘walking on the soles of the feet’ [19]).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/plan" target="_blank">plan</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plantain" target="_blank">plantain</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plant">plant (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">plante</span> "young tree or shrub, herb newly planted," from Latin <span class="foreign">planta</span> "sprout, shoot, cutting" (source of Spanish <span class="foreign">planta</span>, French <span class="foreign">plante</span>), perhaps from <span class="foreign">*plantare</span> "to drive in with the feet, push into the ground with the feet," from <span class="foreign">planta</span> "sole of the foot," from nasalized form of PIE <span class="foreign">*plat-</span> "to spread, flat" (see <a href="/etym/s/place" target="_blank">place</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Broader sense of "any vegetable life, vegetation generally" is first recorded 1550s. Most extended usages are from the verb, on the notion of "something planted;" such as "construction for an industrial process," 1789, at first with reference to the set-up of machinery, later also the building; also slang meaning "a spy" (1812). Many of these follow similar developments in the French form of the word. German <span class="foreign">Pflanz</span>, Irish <span class="foreign">cland</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">plant</span> are from Latin.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plant">plant (v.)</a></dt><dd>"put in the ground to grow," Old English <span class="foreign">plantian</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">plantare</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/plant" target="_blank">plant</a> (n.)). Reinforced by cognate Old French <span class="foreign">planter</span>. Without reference to growing, "to insert firmly," late 14c. Of colonies from c. 1300. Figuratively, of ideas, etc., from early 15c. Meaning "to bury" is U.S. slang from U.S., 1855. Related: <span class="foreign">Planted</span>; <span class="foreign">planting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
