                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/romance">romance</a> 浪漫，风流韵事</span><p>来自 Romance,罗曼语。用以指兴起于中世纪的用罗曼语写作的骑士冒险故事小说，常常含 有大量的英雄救美的情节，后由该词引申词义浪漫，风流韵事，且成为主要词义。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/Romance">Romance</a> 罗曼语</span><p>由拉丁语演变而成，包含法语、意大利语、西班牙语等。来自古法语 Romanz,法语，罗马方 言，来自拉丁语 Romanicus,罗马体，来自 Romanus,罗马人，罗马字体，词源同 Roman.相对 于传统拉丁语的严谨或复杂的语法而言，该语言比较通俗，从而更易为普通人所掌握，类似 于古汉语明清时期的语言与唐宋时期的语言差别。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/romance">romance</a></dt><dd><b>romance: </b><span>[13] A <i>romance </i>is etymologically a story written in the language ‘of Rome’. The word comes from Old French <i>romanz</i>, which denoted ‘something written in French (as opposed to classical Latin)’. This went back to the Vulgar Latin adverb *<i>rōmānicē </i>‘in the local vernacular descended from Latin’ (contrasted with <i>latinē </i>‘in Latin’). This in turn came from Latin <i>rōmānicus </i>‘Roman’, a derivative ultimately of <i>Rōma </i>‘Rome’.<br><br>In practice, these medieval vernacular tales were usually about chivalric adventure, and that was the starting point from which the modern meaning of <i>romance</i>, and its derivative <i>romantic </i>[17], developed. The original sense survives in the linguistic term <i>Romance</i>, denoting languages such as French, Italian, Spanish, Portuguese, Romanian, etc that have evolved from Latin.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/romance">romance (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "a story, written or recited, of the adventures of a knight, hero, etc.," often one designed principally for entertainment," from Old French <span class="foreign">romanz</span> "verse narrative" (Modern French <span class="foreign">roman</span>), originally an adverb, "in the vernacular language," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*romanice scribere</span> "to write in a Romance language" (one developed from Latin instead of Frankish), from Latin <span class="foreign">Romanicus</span> "of or in the Roman style," from <span class="foreign">Romanus</span> "Roman" (see <a href="/etym/s/Roman" target="_blank">Roman</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sense evolution is because medieval vernacular tales usually told chivalric adventures full of marvelous incidents and heroic deeds. In reference to literary works, often in Middle English meaning ones written in French but also applied to native compositions. Literary sense extended by 1660s to "a love story." Meaning "adventurous quality" first recorded 1801; that of "love affair" is from 1916. <span class="foreign">Romance novel</span> attested from 1964. Compare <a href="/etym/s/Romance" target="_blank">Romance</a> (adj.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/romance">romance (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "recite a narrative," from Old French <span class="foreign">romancier</span> "narrate in French; translate into French," from <span class="foreign">romanz</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/romance" target="_blank">romance</a> (n.)). Later "invent fictitious stories" (1670s), then "be romantically enthusiastic" (1849); meaning "court as a lover" is from 1938, probably from <a href="/etym/s/romance" target="_blank">romance</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Romanced</span>; <span class="foreign">romancing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Romance">Romance (adj.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "French; in the vernacular language of France" (contrasted to <span class="foreign">Latin</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">romanz</span> "French; vernacular," from Late Latin <span class="foreign">Romanice</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">Romanicus</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/Roman" target="_blank">Roman</a>). Extended 1610s to other modern tongues derived from Latin (Spanish, Italian, etc.); thus "pertaining to the languages which arose out of the Latin language of the provinces of Rome." Compare <a href="/etym/s/romance" target="_blank">romance</a> (n.).</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
