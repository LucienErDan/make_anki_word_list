                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bear">bear</a> 承受，熊</span><p>1.承受，来自PIE *bher(1), 承受，带来，生育，同bring.</p><p> 2.熊，来自PIE *bher(2), 明亮的，棕色的。指棕熊。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bear">bear</a> 熊</span><p>起源于黑海和里海北岸之间的原始印欧人看不到北极熊和黑熊，他们所能看见的是“棕”熊。这个单词与brown n.棕色，以及beaver ['biːvə] n.海狸；海狸皮毛有关。拉丁语借用了这些概念后，将棕色头发或棕色皮肤（较白皮肤颜色深）的女人称为brunette [bruː'net] n.浅黑肤色的女人。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bear">bear</a></dt><dd><b>bear: </b><span>[OE] The two English words <i>bear </i>‘carry’ and <i>bear </i>the animal come from completely different sources. The verb, Old English <i>beran</i>, goes back via Germanic *<i>ber</i>- to Indo-European *<i>bher</i>-, which already contained the two central meaning elements that have remained with its offspring ever since, ‘carry’ and ‘give birth’. It is the source of a very large number of words in the Indo-European languages, including both Germanic (German <i>gebären </i>‘give birth’, Swedish <i>börd </i>‘birth’) and non-Germanic (Latin <i>ferre </i>and Greek <i>phérein </i>‘bear’, source of English <i>fertile </i>and <i>amphora </i>[17], and Russian <i>brat </i>‘seize’).<br><br>And a very large number of other English words are related to it: on the ‘carrying’ side, <i>barrow</i>, <i>berth</i>, <i>bier</i>, <i>burden</i>, and possibly <i>brim</i>; and on the ‘giving birth’ side, <i>birth </i>itself and <i>bairn </i>‘child’ [16]. <i>Borne </i>and <i>born </i>come from <i>boren</i>, the Old English past participle of <i>bear</i>; the distinction in usage between the two (<i>borne </i>for ‘carried’, <i>born </i>for ‘given birth’) arose in the early 17th century.<br><br>Etymologically, the bear is a ‘brown animal’. Old English <i>bera </i>came from West Germanic *<i>bero </i>(whence also German <i>bär </i>and Dutch <i>beer</i>), which may in turn go back to Indo- European *<i>bheros</i>, related to English <i>brown</i>. The poetic name for the bear, <i>bruin </i>[17], follows the same semantic pattern (it comes from Dutch <i>bruin </i>‘brown’), and <i>beaver </i>means etymologically ‘brown animal’ too.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/amphora" target="_blank">amphora</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bairn" target="_blank">bairn</a>, <a href="/etym/s/barrow" target="_blank">barrow</a>, <a href="/etym/s/berth" target="_blank">berth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bier" target="_blank">bier</a>, <a href="/etym/s/born" target="_blank">born</a>, <a href="/etym/s/burden" target="_blank">burden</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fertile" target="_blank">fertile</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fortune" target="_blank">fortune</a>, <a href="/etym/s/paraphernalia" target="_blank">paraphernalia</a>, <a href="/etym/s/suffer" target="_blank">suffer</a>; <a href="/etym/s/brown" target="_blank">brown</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bear">bear (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">beran</span> "to bear, bring; bring forth, produce; to endure, sustain; to wear" (class IV strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">bær</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">boren</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*beran</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">beran</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">bera</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">beran</span>, German <span class="foreign">gebären</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">bera</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">bairan</span> "to carry, bear, give birth to"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bher-</span> (1) meaning both "give birth" (though only English and German strongly retain this sense, and Russian has <span class="foreign">beremennaya</span> "pregnant") and "carry a burden, bring" (see <a href="/etym/s/infer" target="_blank">infer</a>).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Ball bearings</span> "bear" the friction. Many senses are from notion of "move onward by pressure." Old English past tense <span class="foreign">bær</span> became Middle English <span class="foreign">bare</span>; alternative <span class="foreign">bore</span> began to appear c. 1400, but <span class="foreign">bare</span> remained the literary form till after 1600. Past participle distinction of <span class="foreign">borne</span> for "carried" and <span class="foreign">born</span> for "given birth" is from late 18c. To <span class="foreign">bear (something) in mind</span> is from 1530s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bear">bear (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">bera</span> "bear," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*beron</span>, literally "the brown (one)" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">björn</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bere</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">beer</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">bero</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bär</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*bher-</span> (3) "bright, brown" (see <a href="/etym/s/brown" target="_blank">brown</a> (adj.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Greek <span class="foreign">arktos</span> and Latin <span class="foreign">ursus</span> retain the PIE root word for "bear" (<span class="foreign">*rtko</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/Arctic" target="_blank">Arctic</a>), but it is believed to have been ritually replaced in the northern branches because of hunters' taboo on names of wild animals (compare the Irish equivalent "the good calf," Welsh "honey-pig," Lithuanian "the licker," Russian <span class="foreign">medved</span> "honey-eater"). Others connect the Germanic word with Latin <span class="foreign">ferus</span> "wild," as if it meant "the wild animal (<span class="foreign">par excellence</span>) of the northern woods."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Symbolic of Russia since 1794. Used of uncouth persons since 1570s. Stock market meaning "speculator for a fall" is 1709 shortening of <span class="foreign">bearskin jobber</span> (from the proverb <span class="foreign">sell the bearskin before one has caught the bear</span>); i.e. "one who sells stock for future delivery, expecting that meanwhile prices will fall." Paired with <span class="foreign">bull</span> from c. 1720. <span class="foreign">Bear claw</span> as a type of large pastry is from 1942, originally chiefly western U.S.</dd></dl></div></div>
