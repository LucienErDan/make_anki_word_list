                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/help">help</a> 帮助</span><p>来自古英语helpan,帮助，支持，来自PIE*kelp,支持，抓，握，词源同helm,helve.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/help">help</a></dt><dd><b>help: </b><span>[OE] Today, <i>help </i>is essentially a Germanic word. Related forms such as German <i>helfen</i>, Dutch <i>helpen</i>, Swedish <i>hjälpa</i>, and Danish <i>hjælpe </i>point to a Germanic ancestor *<i>khelp</i>-. But there is one clue – Lithuanian <i>shélpti </i>‘help, support’ – that suggests that formerly it may have been much more widespread throughout the Indo-European languages, and came from an Indo-European source *<i>kelp</i>-.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/help">help (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">helpan</span> (class III strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">healp</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">holpen</span>) "help, support, succor; benefit, do good to; cure, amend," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*helpan</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">hjalpa</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">helpa</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">helpen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">helfan</span>, German <span class="foreign">helfen</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kelb-</span> "to help" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">selpiu</span> "to support, help").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Recorded as a cry of distress from late 14c. Sense of "serve someone with food at table" (1680s) is translated from French <span class="foreign">servir</span> "to help, stead, avail," and led to <a href="/etym/s/helping" target="_blank">helping</a> "portion of food." Related: <span class="foreign">Helped</span> (c. 1300). The Middle English past participle <span class="foreign">holpen</span> survives in biblical and U.S. dialectal use.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/help">help (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">help</span> (m.), <span class="foreign">helpe</span> (f.) "assistance, succor;" see <a href="/etym/s/help" target="_blank">help</a> (v.). Most Germanic languages also have the noun form, such as Old Norse <span class="foreign">hjalp</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">hjälp</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">helpe</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">hulp</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">helfa</span>, German <span class="foreign">Hilfe</span>. Use of <span class="foreign">help</span> as euphemism for "servant" is American English, 1640s, tied up in notions of class and race.<blockquote>A domestic servant of American birth, and without negro blood in his or her veins ... is not a servant, but a 'help.' 'Help wanted,' is the common heading of advertisements in the North, when servants are required. [Chas. Mackay, "Life and Liberty in America," 1859].</blockquote>&#13; Though <span class="foreign">help</span> also meant "assistant, helper, supporter" in Middle English (c. 1200).&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
