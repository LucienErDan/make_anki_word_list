                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. anti- "before" + *okw- "appearance" + -e.<br />
2. anti- 词根的本义是 before 这里引申为 before times, former times, ancient times. 也就是以前的时光、古代。<br />
3. *okw- 本义是 see 这里引申为 appearance，即样子、外观、外貌、面貌。*okw- 的同源词：ocular, ogle, optic, myopia.<br />
4. 由此该词的含义表示古代的样子、古代的面貌。<br />
5. => aged, venerable.</div>

                                                                        <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/antique">antique</a></dt><dd><b>antique: </b><span>[16] Originally, in Latin, <i>antique </i>was an adjectivized version of the adverb and preposition ‘before’: to <i>ante </i>‘before’ was added the adjective suffix -<i>īcus</i>, to produce the adjective <i>antīquus </i>(somewhat later an exactly parallel formation, using the suffix -<i>ānus </i>rather than -<i>īcus</i>, produced the adjective which became English <i>ancient</i>).<br><br>English acquired the word either via French <i>antique </i>or directly from Latin. To begin with, and until relatively recently, it meant simply ‘ancient’, or specifically ‘of the ancient world’; it was only towards the end of the 18th century that the modern sense ‘made long ago and therefore collectable’ began to become established. In Italian, <i>antico </i>(from Latin <i>antīquus</i>) was often applied to grotesque carvings found in ancient remains.<br><br>It was borrowed into English in the 16th century as an adjective, <i>antic</i>, meaning ‘bizarre’, but also as a noun, usually used in the plural, in the sense ‘absurd behaviour’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/ancient" target="_blank">ancient</a>, <a href="/etym/s/antic" target="_blank">antic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/antique">antique (adj.)</a></dt><dd>1530s, "aged, venerable," from Middle French <span class="foreign">antique</span> "old" (14c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">antiquus</span> (later <span class="foreign">anticus</span>) "ancient, former, of olden times; old, long in existence, aged; venerable; old-fashioned," from PIE <span class="foreign">*anti</span> in sense of "before" (see <a href="/etym/s/ante" target="_blank">ante</a>) + <span class="foreign">*okw-</span> "appearance" (see <a href="/etym/s/eye" target="_blank">eye</a> (n.)). Originally pronounced in English like its parallel <a href="/etym/s/antic" target="_blank">antic</a>, but French pronunciation and spelling were adopted from c. 1700.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/antique">antique (n.)</a></dt><dd>"an old and collectible thing," 1771, from <a href="/etym/s/antique" target="_blank">antique</a> (adj.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/antique">antique (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to give an antique appearance to," 1896, from <a href="/etym/s/antique" target="_blank">antique</a> (adj.). Related: <span class="foreign">Antiqued</span>; <span class="foreign">antiquing</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
