                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bad">bad</a> 坏的</span><p>词源同boar, 野猪。古义指狂野的，邪恶的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bad">bad</a></dt><dd><b>bad: </b><span>[13] For such a common word, <i>bad </i>has a remarkably clouded history. It does not begin to appear in English until the end of the 13th century, and has no apparent relatives in other languages (the uncanny resemblance to Persian <i>bad </i>is purely coincidental). The few clues we have suggest a regrettably homophobic origin. Old English had a pair of words, <i>bǣddel </i>and <i>bǣdling</i>, which appear to have been derogatory terms for homosexuals, with overtones of sodomy.<br><br>The fact that the first examples we have of <i>bad</i>, from the late 13th and early 14th centuries, are in the sense ‘contemptible, worthless’ as applied to people indicates that the connotations of moral depravity may have become generalized from an earlier, specifically anti-homosexual sense.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bad">bad (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "inferior in quality;" early 13c., "wicked, evil, vicious," a mystery word with no apparent relatives in other languages.* Possibly from Old English derogatory term <span class="foreign">bæddel</span> and its diminutive <span class="foreign">bædling</span> "effeminate man, hermaphrodite, pederast," probably related to <span class="foreign">bædan</span> "to defile." A rare word before 1400, and <a href="/etym/s/evil" target="_blank">evil</a> was more common in this sense until c. 1700. Meaning "uncomfortable, sorry" is 1839, American English colloquial.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Comparable words in the other Indo-European languages tend to have grown from descriptions of specific qualities, such as "ugly," "defective," "weak," "faithless," "impudent," "crooked," "filthy" (such as Greek <span class="foreign">kakos</span>, probably from the word for "excrement;" Russian <span class="foreign">plochoj</span>, related to Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">plachu</span> "wavering, timid;" Persian <span class="foreign">gast</span>, Old Persian <span class="foreign">gasta-</span>, related to <span class="foreign">gand</span> "stench;" German <span class="foreign">schlecht</span>, originally "level, straight, smooth," whence "simple, ordinary," then "bad").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Comparative and superlative forms <span class="foreign">badder, baddest</span> were common 14c.-18c. and used as recently as Defoe (but not by Shakespeare), but yielded to comparative <a href="/etym/s/worse" target="_blank">worse</a> and superlative <a href="/etym/s/worst" target="_blank">worst</a> (which had belonged to <span class="foreign">evil</span> and <span class="foreign">ill</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As a noun, late 14c., "evil, wickedness." In U.S. place names, sometimes translating native terms meaning "supernaturally dangerous." Ironic use as a word of approval is said to be at least since 1890s orally, originally in Black English, emerging in print 1928 in a jazz context. It might have emerged from the ambivalence of expressions like <span class="foreign">bad nigger</span>, used as a term of reproach by whites, but among blacks sometimes representing one who stood up to injustice, but in the U.S. West <span class="foreign">bad man</span> also had a certain ambivalence:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; These are the men who do most of the killing in frontier communities, yet it is a noteworthy fact that the men who are killed generally deserve their fate. [Farmer &amp; Henley]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; *Farsi has <span class="foreign">bad</span> in more or less the same sense as the English word, but this is regarded by linguists as a coincidence. The forms of the words diverge as they are traced back in time (Farsi <span class="foreign">bad</span> comes from Middle Persian <span class="foreign">vat</span>), and such accidental convergences exist across many languages, given the vast number of words in each and the limited range of sounds humans can make to signify them. Among other coincidental matches with English are Korean <span class="foreign">mani</span> "many," Chinese <span class="foreign">pei</span> "pay," Nahuatl (Aztecan) <span class="foreign">huel</span> "well," Maya <span class="foreign">hol</span> "hole."</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
