                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plate">plate</a> 盘子，盆子，板，金属板，覆盖，电镀</span><p>来自古法语plate,薄板，薄金属板，来自拉丁语plata,盘子，金属板，来自希腊语platys,平的，宽的，来自PIE*pele,展开，放平，词源同plan,plain.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plate">plate</a></dt><dd><b>plate: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, a <i>plate </i>is something ‘flat’. It comes from Vulgar Latin *<i>plattus </i>‘flat’, which may go back to Greek <i>platús </i>‘broad’ (source of English <i>place</i>, <i>plane </i>the tree, and <i>platypus</i>). It reached English via two separate Old French words, which have since coalesced: first <i>plate</i>, which gives the sense ‘flat sheet’, as in <i>silver plate </i>and <i>plate glass</i>; and then, in the 15th century, <i>plat</i>, ‘dish for food’.<br><br>Related forms in English include <i>plateau </i>[18], <i>platform </i>[16] (etymologically a ‘flat form’), <i>platinum </i>[19], <i>platitude </i>[19] (a ‘flat’ or dull remark), and <i>platter </i>[14].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/flat" target="_blank">flat</a>, <a href="/etym/s/place" target="_blank">place</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plateau" target="_blank">plateau</a>, <a href="/etym/s/platform" target="_blank">platform</a>, <a href="/etym/s/platinum" target="_blank">platinum</a>, <a href="/etym/s/platitude" target="_blank">platitude</a>, <a href="/etym/s/platter" target="_blank">platter</a>, <a href="/etym/s/platypus" target="_blank">platypus</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plate">plate (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-13c., "flat sheet of gold or silver," also "flat, round coin," from Old French <span class="foreign">plate</span> "thin piece of metal" (late 12c.), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">plata</span> "plate, piece of metal," perhaps via Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*plattus</span>, formed on model of Greek <span class="foreign">platys</span> "flat, broad" (see <a href="/etym/s/plaice" target="_blank">plaice</a> (n.)). The cognate in Spanish (<span class="foreign">plata</span>) and Portuguese (<span class="foreign">prata</span>) has become the usual word for "silver," superseding <span class="foreign">argento</span> via shortening of <span class="foreign">*plata d'argento</span> "plate of silver, coin." Meaning "table utensils" (originally of silver or gold only) is from Middle English. Meaning "shallow dish for food," now usually of china or earthenware, originally of metal or wood, is from mid-15c. Baseball sense is from 1857. Geological sense is first attested 1904; <span class="foreign">plate tectonics</span> first recorded 1969. <span class="foreign">Plate-glass</span> first recorded 1727.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plate">plate (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/plate" target="_blank">plate</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Plated</span>; <span class="foreign">plating</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
