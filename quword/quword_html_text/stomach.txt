                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stomach">stomach</a> 胃，腹部</span><p>来自古法语 estomac,胃，来自拉丁语 stomachus,喉咙，食道，胃，来自希腊语 stomachos,喉 咙，食道，嘴，口，来自 stoma,嘴，口，来自 PIE*stomen,嘴，口。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stomach">stomach</a></dt><dd><b>stomach: </b><span>[14] Greek <i>stómakhos </i>was derived from <i>stóma </i>‘mouth’, and originally denoted the ‘throat’ or ‘oesophagus’. It was also applied to the opening or ‘mouth’ of various internal organs, particularly the stomach, and eventually came to be used for the stomach itself. English acquired the word via Latin <i>stomachus </i>and Old French <i>stomaque</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stomach">stomach (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., earlier <span class="foreign">stomak</span> (early 14c.), "internal pouch into which food is digested," from Old French <span class="foreign">stomaque</span>, <span class="foreign">estomac</span> "stomach," from Latin <span class="foreign">stomachus</span> "throat, gullet; stomach," also "taste, inclination, liking; distaste, dislike;" also "pride, indignation," which were thought to have their origin in that organ (source also of Spanish <span class="foreign">estómago</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">stomaco</span>), from Greek <span class="foreign">stomachos</span> "throat, gullet, esophagus," literally "mouth, opening," from <span class="foreign">stoma</span> "mouth" (see <a href="/etym/s/stoma" target="_blank">stoma</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Applied anciently to the openings of various internal organs, especially that of the stomach, then by the later Greek physicians to the stomach itself. The native word is <a href="/etym/s/maw" target="_blank">maw</a>. Some 16c. anatomists tried to correct the sense back to "esophagus" and introduce <span class="foreign">ventricle</span> for what we call the stomach. Meaning "belly, midriff, part of the body that contains the stomach" is from late 14c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The spelling of the ending of the word was conformed to Latin, but the pronunciation remains as in Middle English. Related: <span class="foreign">stomachial</span> (1580s); <span class="foreign">stomachical</span> (c. 1600); <span class="foreign">stomachic</span> (1650s). Pugilistic <span class="foreign">stomacher</span> "punch in the stomach" is from 1814; from mid-15c. as "vest or other garment which covers the belly." The Latin figurative senses also were in Middle English (such as "relish, inclination, desire," mid-15c.) or early Modern English. Also sometimes regarded in Middle Ages as the seat of sexual desire.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stomach">stomach (v.)</a></dt><dd>"tolerate, put up with," 1570s, from <a href="/etym/s/stomach" target="_blank">stomach</a> (n.), probably in reference to digestion; earlier sense was opposite: "to be offended at, resent" (1520s), echoing Latin <span class="foreign">stomachari</span> "to be resentful, be irritated, be angry," from <span class="foreign">stomachus</span> (n.) in its secondary sense of "pride, indignation." Related: <span class="foreign">Stomached</span>; <span class="foreign">stomaching</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
