            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/station">station</a> 火车站，汽车站，局，所，岗位</span><p>来自拉丁语 stare,站立，来自 PIE*sta,站立，使固定，词源同 stand,state.-ation,名词后缀。引 申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/station">station</a></dt><dd><b>station: </b><span>[14] A <i>station </i>is etymologically a ‘standing’, hence a ‘place for standing’ – a guard who takes up his ‘station’ outside a building goes and ‘stands’ there. The word comes via Old French <i>station </i>from Latin <i>statiō </i>‘standing’, a descendant of the base *<i>stā</i>- ‘stand’ (to which English <i>stand </i>is related). Various metaphorical senses emerged in Latin, such as ‘post, job’ and ‘abode, residence’, but ‘stopping place for vehicles’ is a post-Latin development.<br><br>It came out of an earlier ‘stopping place on a journey’, and is first recorded in English at the end of the 18th century, in the USA, with reference to coach routes. The application to ‘railway stations’ dates from the 1830s. The notion of ‘standing still’ is preserved in the derived adjective <i>stationary </i>[15].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/constant" target="_blank">constant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/instant" target="_blank">instant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stand" target="_blank">stand</a>, <a href="/etym/s/state" target="_blank">state</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stationary" target="_blank">stationary</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stationery" target="_blank">stationery</a>, <a href="/etym/s/statue" target="_blank">statue</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/station">station (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to assign a post or position to," 1748, from <a href="/etym/s/station" target="_blank">station</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Stationed</span>; <span class="foreign">stationing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/station">station (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 13c., "place which one normally occupies," from Old French <span class="foreign">stacion</span>, <span class="foreign">estacion</span> "site, location; station of the Cross; stop, standstill," from Latin <span class="foreign">stationem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">statio</span>) "a standing, standing firm; a post, job, position; military post; a watch, guard, sentinel; anchorage, port" (related to <span class="foreign">stare</span> "to stand"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ste-ti-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*sta-</span> "to stand" (see <a href="/etym/s/stet" target="_blank">stet</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "each of a number of holy places visited in succession by pilgrims" is from late 14c., as in <span class="foreign">Station of the Cross</span> (1550s). Meaning "fixed uniform distance in surveying" is from 1570s. Sense of "status, rank" is from c. 1600. Meaning "military post" in English is from c. 1600. The meaning "place where people are stationed for some special purpose" (as in <span class="foreign">polling station</span>) is first recorded 1823. <span class="foreign">Radio station</span> is from 1912; <span class="foreign">station break</span>, pause in broadcasting to give the local station a chance to identify itself, is from 1942.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The meaning "regular stopping place" is first recorded 1797, in reference to coach routes; applied to railroads 1830. <span class="foreign">Station-master</span> is from 1836. <span class="foreign">Station wagon</span> in the automobile sense is first recorded 1929, from earlier use for a horse-drawn conveyance that took passengers to and from railroad stations (1894). <span class="foreign">Station house</span> "police station" is attested from 1836.</dd></dl></div></div>
