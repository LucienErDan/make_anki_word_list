                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/plain">plain</a> 平原，平地，明显的，直白的，简单的，素的</span><p>来自拉丁语planus,平的，来自PIE*pele,展开，放平，词源同plan,place.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plain">plain</a></dt><dd><b>plain: </b><span>[13] <i>Plain </i>is etymologically the same word as <i>plane </i>in all its uses except the tree-name, and even that comes from the same ultimate source. This was Indo-European *<i>plā</i>- ‘flat’, which produced Greek <i>platús </i>‘broad’ (source of English <i>place</i>, <i>plaice</i>, and <i>platypus</i>), Latin <i>plānus </i>‘flat, clear’, and possibly English <i>flat</i>. The Latin word passed into English via Old French <i>plain</i>, but its original ‘flat’ senses have been hived off into the separately acquired <i>plane</i>, leaving only the metaphorically derived ‘clear’ senses. The Italian descendant of <i>plānus </i>has given English <i>piano</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plain">plain (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "flat, smooth," from Old French <span class="foreign">plain</span> "flat, smooth, even" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">planus</span> "flat, even, level" (see <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a> (n.1)). Sense of "evident" is from, c. 1300; that of "free from obstruction" is early 14c.; meaning "simple, sincere, ordinary" is recorded from late 14c., especially of dress, "unembellished, without decoration."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In reference to the dress and speech of Quakers, it is recorded from 1824; of Amish and Mennonites, from 1894 (in the Dutch regions of Pennsylvania <span class="foreign">Plain</span> with the capital is shorthand adjective for "Amish and Old Order Mennonite"). Of appearance, as a euphemism for "ill-favored, ugly" it dates from 1749. Of envelopes from 1913. As an adverb from early 14c. <span class="foreign">Plain English</span> is from c. 1500. <span class="foreign">Plain dealer</span> "one who deals plainly or speaks candidly" is from 1570s, marked "Now <span class="foreign">rare</span>" in OED 2nd edition. To be <span class="foreign">as plain as the nose on (one's) face</span> is from 1690s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/plain">plain (n.)</a></dt><dd>"level country," c. 1300 (in reference to Salisbury Plain), from Old French <span class="foreign">plain</span> "open countryside," from Latin <span class="foreign">planum</span> "level ground, plain," noun use of neuter of <span class="foreign">planus</span> (adj.) "flat, even, level" (see <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a> (n.1)). Latin <span class="foreign">planum</span> was used for "level ground" but much more common was <span class="foreign">campus</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
