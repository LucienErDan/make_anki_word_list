                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stone">stone</a> 石头，石块</span><p>来自古英语 stan,石头，来自 Proto-Germanic*stainaz,石头，来自 PIE*stai,硬化，石头，词源 同 stearin,硬脂，硬脂酸甘油油酯。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stone">stone</a></dt><dd><b>stone: </b><span>[OE] <i>Stone </i>is a general Germanic word, with relatives in German <i>stein</i>, Dutch <i>steen</i>, and Swedish and Danish <i>sten</i>. These all go back to a prehistoric *<i>stainaz</i>, which was derived from a base denoting ‘stiffness’ or ‘solidity’ (source also of Greek <i>stía </i>‘pebble’ and <i>stéar </i>‘stiff, fat’, Sanskrit <i>styā</i>- ‘stiffen’, and Serbo-Croat <i>stijena </i>‘rock’). The use of the English term for a measure of weight, equal to fourteen pounds, dates from the 14th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stone">stone (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">stan</span>, used of common rocks, precious gems, concretions in the body, memorial stones, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stainaz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">steinn</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">steen</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sten</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">sten</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">steen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">stein</span>, German <span class="foreign">Stein</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">stains</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*stoi-no-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*stai-</span> "stone," also "to thicken, stiffen" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">styayate</span> "curdles, becomes hard;" Avestan <span class="foreign">stay-</span> "heap;" Greek <span class="foreign">stear</span> "fat, tallow," <span class="foreign">stia, stion</span> "pebble;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">stena</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">stiena</span> "wall").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "testicle" is from late Old English. The British measure of weight (usually equal to 14 pounds) is from late 14c., originally a specific stone. <span class="foreign">Stone-fruit</span>, one with a pit, is from 1520s. <span class="foreign">Stone's throw</span> for "a short distance" is attested from 1580s. <span class="foreign">Stone Age</span> is from 1864. To <span class="foreign">kill two birds with one stone</span> is first attested 1650s. To <span class="foreign">leave no stone unturned</span> is from 1540s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stone">stone (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"made of stone," Old English (which also had <span class="foreign">stænan</span> "stonen"); see <a href="/etym/s/stone" target="_blank">stone</a> (n.). As an intensifying adjective recorded from 1935, first recorded in black slang, probably from earlier use in phrases like <span class="foreign">stone blind</span> (late 14c., literally "blind as a stone"), <span class="foreign">stone deaf</span>, <span class="foreign">stone-cold</span> (1590s), etc. <span class="foreign">Stone cold sober</span> dates from 1937.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stone">stone (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "to pelt with stones," from <a href="/etym/s/stone" target="_blank">stone</a> (n.). From c. 1600 as "to fit with stones;" 1630s as "to free from stones" (of fruit, etc.). Related: <a href="/etym/s/Stoned" target="_blank">Stoned</a>; <span class="foreign">stoning</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
