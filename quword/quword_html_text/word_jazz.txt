                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/jazz">jazz</a> 爵士乐，爵士舞</span><p>一种发源于美国路易斯安那克里奥尔区的流行音乐或舞蹈，词源不确定，可能来自非洲某语言jasm,精力，活动，衍生俚语词jism,种子，精液，后衍生俚语词jazz,性交。后黑人音乐家用于命名他们创作的一种音乐形式，即爵士。但始料未及的是，该音乐形式在创作出来后，迅速流行于全世界，并赋予了多种褒义词义。因此，也有人评论说：“If the truth were known about the origin of the word”Jazz”,it would never be mentioned in polite society.”比较funk.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/jazz">jazz</a></dt><dd><b>jazz: </b><span>[20] Words of unknown origin always attract speculation, and it is hardly surprising that such an unusual and high-profile one as <i>jazz </i>(first recorded in 1913) should have had more than its fair share (one of the more ingenious and colourful theories is that it comes from the nickname of one Jasbo Brown, an itinerant black musician along the Mississippi – <i>Jasbo </i>perhaps being an alteration of <i>Jasper</i>).<br><br>Given that the word emerged in Black English (probably originally in the sense ‘copulation’), it is not surprising that attempts have been made to link it with some West African language, and picture it crossing the Atlantic with the slave ships, but there is no convincing evidence for that (the scenario seems to have got started with a 1917 article in the New York <i>Sun</i>, which was purely the invention of a press agent).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/jazz">jazz (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to speed or liven up," 1917, from <a href="/etym/s/jazz" target="_blank">jazz</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">jazzed</span>; <span class="foreign">jazzing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/jazz">jazz (n.)</a></dt><dd>by 1912, American English, first attested in baseball slang; as a type of music, attested from 1913. Probably ultimately from Creole patois <span class="foreign">jass</span> "strenuous activity," especially "sexual intercourse" but also used of Congo dances, from <span class="foreign">jasm</span> (1860) "energy, drive," of African origin (compare Mandingo <span class="foreign">jasi</span>, Temne <span class="foreign">yas</span>), also the source of slang <span class="foreign">jism</span>.<blockquote>If the truth were known about the origin of the word 'Jazz' it would never be mentioned in polite society. [<span class="foreign">"Étude,"</span> Sept. 1924]</blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">All that jazz</span> "et cetera" first recorded 1939.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
