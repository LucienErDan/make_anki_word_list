2. swine => sow.<br />
3. 谐音“撒”。<br />
4. 谐音“收”-----收获------要收获，先播种。<br />
5. 音：骚，指母猪发情，母猪要播种.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sow">sow</a> 播种，散布</span><p>来自古英语 sawan,播种，播撒，授精，来自 Proto-Germanic*sean,播种，来自 PIE*se,播种， 词源同 seed,semen,season.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sow">sow</a> 母猪</span><p>来自中古英语 sowe,来自古英语 sugu,母猪，来自 Proto-Germanic*sugo,猪，来自 PIE*su,猪， 可能为拟声词，模仿猪的哼哼声，词源同 swine.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sow">sow</a></dt><dd><b>sow: </b><span>English has two words <i>sow</i>, both of which go back to the Old English period. The verb, ‘put seeds in the ground’, comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>sǣjan</i>, which also produced German <i>säen</i>, Dutch <i>zaaien</i>, Swedish <i>så</i>, and Danish <i>saa</i>. It was formed from the base *<i>sǣ</i>- (source of English <i>seed</i>), which goes back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>sē</i>- (source of English <i>season</i>, <i>semen</i>, etc). <i>Sow </i>‘female pig’ is descended from an Indo-European base *<i>su</i>- (possibly imitative of the noise made by a pig), which also produced Greek <i>hus </i>‘pig’ (whose feminine form <i>húaina </i>is the source of English <i>hyena </i>[16]), Latin <i>sūs </i>‘pig’, German <i>sau </i>‘sow’, and English <i>swine</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/season" target="_blank">season</a>, <a href="/etym/s/seed" target="_blank">seed</a>, <a href="/etym/s/semen" target="_blank">semen</a>; <a href="/etym/s/hyena" target="_blank">hyena</a>, <a href="/etym/s/swine" target="_blank">swine</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sow">sow (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">sawan</span> "to scatter seed upon the ground or plant it in the earth, disseminate" (class VII strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">seow</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">sawen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sean</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">sa</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">saian</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sayen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zaaien</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sawen</span>, German <span class="foreign">säen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">saian</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*se-</span> (1) "to sow" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">sero</span>, past tense <span class="foreign">sevi</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">satum</span> "to sow;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">sejo</span>, <span class="foreign">sejati</span>; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">seju</span>, <span class="foreign">seti</span> "to sow"), source of <a href="/etym/s/semen" target="_blank">semen</a>, <a href="/etym/s/season" target="_blank">season</a> (n.), <a href="/etym/s/seed" target="_blank">seed</a> (n.), etc. Figurative sense was in Old English.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sow">sow (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">sugu</span>, <span class="foreign">su</span> "female of the swine," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*su-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Old High German <span class="foreign">su</span>, German <span class="foreign">Sau</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zeug</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">syr</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*su-</span> (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sukarah</span> "wild boar, swine;" Avestan <span class="foreign">hu</span> "wild boar;" Greek <span class="foreign">hys</span> "swine;" Latin <span class="foreign">sus</span> "swine," <span class="foreign">swinus</span> "pertaining to swine;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">svinija</span> "swine;" Lettish <span class="foreign">sivens</span> "young pig;" Welsh <span class="foreign">hucc</span>, Irish <span class="foreign">suig</span> "swine; Old Irish <span class="foreign">socc</span> "snout, plowshare"), possibly imitative of pig noise, a notion reinforced by the fact that Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sukharah</span> means "maker of (the sound) 'su.' " Related to <a href="/etym/s/swine" target="_blank">swine</a>. As a term of abuse for a woman, attested from c. 1500. <span class="foreign">Sow-bug</span> "hog louse" is from 1750.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
