            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/quiz">quiz</a> 测试，查问，盘问</span><p>可能来自拉丁语qui es,你是谁，来自qui,谁，词源同who,es,是，词源同is.原为过去拉丁语 语法考试时问考生的第一个问题。后用于指小测试，小考试。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quiz">quiz</a></dt><dd><b>quiz: </b><span>[19] No one has ever been able satisfactorily to explain the origins of <i>quiz</i>. A word of that form first appeared at the end of the 18th century, meaning ‘odd person’ or, as a verb, ‘make fun of’ (in the early 19th century it was claimed to have been coined by a Dublin theatre proprietor by the name of Daly, but no proof has ever been found for this). The verb later came to be used for ‘look at mockingly or questioningly through a monocle’, and it may be that this led on (perhaps helped by associations with <i>inquisitive </i>or Latin <i>quis? </i>‘who?, what?’) to the sense ‘interrogate’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quiz">quiz (n.)</a></dt><dd>"brief examination of a student on some subject," 1852, perhaps from <a href="/etym/s/quiz" target="_blank">quiz</a> (v.), or from slang <span class="foreign">quiz</span> "odd person" (1782, perhaps originally university slang), via the notion of "schoolboy prank or joke played at the expense of a person deemed a quiz" (a noun sense attested frequently 1840s).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; A Quiz, in the common acceptation of the word, signifies one who thinks, speaks, or acts differently from the rest of the world in general. But, as manners and opinions are as various as mankind, it will be difficult to say who shall be termed a Quiz, and who shall not: each person indiscriminately applying the name of Quiz to every one who differs from himself .... ["The London Magazine," November, 1783]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; According to OED, the anecdote that credits this word to a bet by the Dublin theater-manager Daly or Daley that he could coin a word is regarded by authorities as "doubtful" and the first record of it appears to be in 1836 (in Smart's "Walker Remodelled"; the story is omitted in the edition of 1840).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The word Quiz is a sort of a kind of a word&#13; <br />That people apply to some being absurd;&#13; <br />One who seems, as t'were oddly your fancy to strike&#13; <br />In a sort of a fashion you somehow don't like&#13; <br />A mixture of odd, and of queer, and all that&#13; <br />Which one hates, just, you know, as some folks hate a cat;&#13; <br />A comical, whimsical, strange, droll -- that is,&#13; <br />You know what I mean; 'tis -- in short, -- 'tis a quiz!&#13; <br /><br />&#13; [from "Etymology of Quiz," Charles Dibdin, 1842]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quiz">quiz (v.)</a></dt><dd>1847, "to question," <span class="foreign">quies</span>, perhaps from Latin <span class="foreign">qui es?</span> "who are you?," first question in oral exams in Latin in old-time grammar schools. Spelling <span class="foreign">quiz</span> first recorded 1886, though it was in use as a noun spelling from 1854, perhaps in this case from apparently unrelated slang word <span class="foreign">quiz</span> "odd person" (1782, source of <a href="/etym/s/quizzical" target="_blank">quizzical</a>). Compare <span class="foreign">quisby</span> "queer, not quite right; bankrupt" (slang from 1807). From the era of radio quiz shows comes <span class="foreign">quizzee</span> (n.), 1940.</dd></dl></div></div>
