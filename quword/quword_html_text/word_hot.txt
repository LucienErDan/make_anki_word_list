            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hot">hot</a> 热的，辛辣的</span><p>来自古英语hat,热的，来自PIE*kai,热，进一步来自PIE*skai,发光，照耀，词源同shine,candle.由热引申多种词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hot">hot</a></dt><dd><b>hot: </b><span>[OE] <i>Hot </i>is the English member of a family of adjectives widespread in Germanic, but with very few outside relatives. Its first cousins are German <i>heiss</i>, Dutch <i>heet</i>, Swedish <i>het</i>, and Danish <i>hed</i>, which point back to a prehistoric Germanic ancestor *<i>khaitaz </i>(the English noun and verb <i>heat </i>come from the same source). Lithuanian <i>kaisti </i>and Latvian <i>kaist </i>‘become hot’ are allied forms.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/heat" target="_blank">heat</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hot">hot (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hat</span> "hot, flaming, opposite of cold," also "fervent, fierce, intense, excited," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haita-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">het</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">heitr</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">heet</span>, German <span class="foreign">heiß</span> "hot," Gothic <span class="foreign">heito</span> "heat of a fever"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kai-</span> "heat" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kaistu</span> "to grow hot").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The association of <span class="foreign">hot</span> with sexuality dates back to c. 1500. Taste sense of "pungent, acrid, biting" is from 1540s. Sense of "exciting, remarkable, very good" is 1895; that of "stolen" is first recorded 1925 (originally with overtones of "easily identified and difficult to dispose of"); that of "radioactive" is from 1942.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Hot flashes</span> in the menopausal sense attested from 1887. <span class="foreign">Hot air</span> "unsubstantiated statements, boastful talk" is from 1900. <span class="foreign">Hot stuff</span> for anything good or excellent is by 1889. <span class="foreign">Hot potato</span> in figurative sense is from 1846. The <span class="foreign">hot</span> and <span class="foreign">cold</span> in hide-and-seek or guessing games are from hunting (1640s), with notion of tracking a scent.</dd></dl></div></div>
