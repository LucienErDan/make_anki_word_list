                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cow">cow</a> 奶牛</span><p>来自PIE*gwous, 低声嚎叫，拟声词，词源同bovine.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cow">cow</a></dt><dd><b>cow: </b><span>English has two completely distinct words <i>cow</i>. The commoner, ‘female of cattle’ [OE], is a word of very ancient ancestry. It goes back via West and North Germanic *<i>kōuz </i>to a hypothetical Indo-European *<i>gwōus</i>, which was also the source of Latin <i>bōs </i>(from which English gets <i>bovine</i>, <i>beef</i>, and <i>bugle</i>, not to mention <i>Bovril</i>). In modern English its plural is <i>cows</i>, but Old English <i>cū </i>had an anomalous plural, <i>cy</i>, which in the remodelled form <i>kine </i>survived dialectally into the 20th century. The other <i>cow</i>, ‘intimidate, daunt’ [17], probably comes from Old Norse <i>kúga </i>‘oppress’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/beef" target="_blank">beef</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bovine" target="_blank">bovine</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bugle" target="_blank">bugle</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cow">cow (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">cu</span> "cow," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kwon</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ku</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">coe</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">koe</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kuo</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kuh</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">kyr</span>, Danish, Swedish <span class="foreign">ko</span>), earlier <span class="foreign">*kwom</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*gwou-</span> "cow, ox, bull" (cognates Sanskrit <span class="foreign">gaus</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">bous</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">bov-</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">bo</span>, Latvian <span class="foreign">guovs</span>, Armenian <span class="foreign">gaus</span> "cow," Slovak <span class="foreign">hovado</span> "ox"), perhaps ultimately imitative of lowing (compare Sumerian <span class="foreign">gu</span>, Chinese <span class="foreign">ngu</span>, <span class="foreign">ngo</span> "ox"). In Germanic and Celtic, of females only; in most other languages, of either gender. Other "cow" words sometimes are from roots meaning "horn, horned," such as Lithuanian <span class="foreign">karve</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">krava</span>. Compare <a href="/etym/s/kine" target="_blank">kine</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cow">cow (v.)</a></dt><dd>"intimidate," c. 1600, probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">kuga</span> "oppress," which is of unknown origin, but perhaps having something to do with <a href="/etym/s/cow" target="_blank">cow</a> (n.) on the notion of easily herded. Related: <span class="foreign">Cowed</span>; <span class="foreign">cowing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
