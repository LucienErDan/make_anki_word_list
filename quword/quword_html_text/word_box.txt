                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/box">box</a> 箱子，黄杨木，拳击</span><p>1.箱子，来自拉丁词buxis, 黄杨木，后指黄杨木做的箱子。可能同bush. </p><p> 2.拳击，拟声词。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/box">box</a></dt><dd><b>box: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>box</i>. The ‘receptacle’ [OE] probably comes from late Latin <i>buxis</i>, a variant of Latin <i>pyxis </i>(whence English <i>pyx </i>‘container for Communion bread’ [14]). This was borrowed from Greek <i>puxís</i>, which originally meant not simply ‘box’, but specifically ‘box made of wood’; for it was a derivative of Greek <i>púxos</i>, which via Latin <i>buxus </i>has given English <i>box </i>the tree [OE]. <i>Box </i>‘fight with the fists’ first appeared in English as a noun, meaning ‘blow’ [14], now preserved mainly in ‘a box round the ears’.<br><br>Its ancestry is uncertain: it may be related to Middle Dutch <i>bōke </i>and Danish <i>bask </i>‘blow’, or it could simply be an obscure metaphorical extension of <i>box </i>‘receptacle’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pyx" target="_blank">pyx</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/box">box (n.1.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">box</span> "a wooden container," also the name of a type of shrub, from Late Latin <span class="foreign">buxis</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">pyxis</span> "boxwood box," from <span class="foreign">pyxos</span> "box tree," which is of uncertain origin. See OED entry for discussion. German <span class="foreign">Büchse</span> also is a Latin loan word.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "compartment at a theater" is from c. 1600. Meaning "pigeon-hole at a post office" is from 1832. Meaning "television" is from 1950. Slang meaning "vulva" is attested 17c., according to "Dictionary of American Slang;" modern use seems to date from c.World War II, perhaps originally Australian, on notion of "box of tricks." <span class="foreign">Box office</span> is 1786; in the figurative sense of "financial element of a performance" it is first recorded 1904. <span class="foreign">Box lunch</span> (n.) attested from 1899. The <span class="foreign">box set</span>, "multiple-album, CD or cassette issue of the work of an artist" is attested by 1955.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/box">box (n.2.)</a></dt><dd>"a blow," c. 1300, of uncertain origin, possibly related to Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">boke</span>, Middle High German <span class="foreign">buc</span>, and Danish <span class="foreign">bask</span>, all meaning "a blow," perhaps imitative.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/box">box (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to put into storage, put into a box," mid-15c., from <a href="/etym/s/box" target="_blank">box</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Boxed</span>; <span class="foreign">boxing</span>.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/box">box (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to beat or whip," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/box" target="_blank">box</a> (n.2). Meaning "to fight with the fists" is from 1560s. Related: <span class="foreign">Boxed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/boxing" target="_blank">boxing</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
