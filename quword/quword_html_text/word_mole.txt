            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. 音译“摩尔”，物质的量的单位。<br />
2. molecule (指小词, 指小词缀：-cul-) <====> mole(摩尔)：也许最初好像是认为分子是最小的微粒，据此创造了此单词吧。<br />
3. 一摩尔的微粒是很微小的量 =》 微量，据此表示“微小的污迹，斑点”，进而引申为：痣、胎块。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mole">mole</a> 鼹鼠，内奸</span><p>缩写自molewarp,欧洲鼹鼠。后用于鼹鼠通用名。引申俚语义内奸。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mole">mole</a> 防洪堤</span><p>来自拉丁语moles,大块，防洪堤，大工程，大建筑物，来自PIE*mo,努力，劳苦，词源同demolish,molecule,molest.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mole">mole</a> 痣</span><p>来自古英语mal,斑点，痣，来自Proto-Germanic*mailan,斑点，记号，来自PIE*mai,涂沫，弄脏，玷污，词源同miasma.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mole">mole</a> 摩尔</span><p>分子单位，缩写自molecule.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mole">mole</a> 沙司</span><p>来自西班牙语，来自南美土著语molli,沙司，调料，炖料，词源同guacamole.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mole">mole</a></dt><dd><b>mole: </b><span>English has four distinct words <i>mole</i>. The oldest is ‘brown spot’ [OE]. It is the descendant of Old English <i>māl</i>, which meant broadly ‘discoloured mark’. This developed in Middle English to ‘spot on the skin’, but the specific sense ‘brown mark’ did not emerge until fairly recently. The word goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>mailam</i>, a derivative of a base meaning ‘spot, mark’ which also produced German <i>malen </i>‘paint’ and Dutch <i>maalen </i>‘paint’ (source of English <i>maulstick </i>‘stick used as a rest by painters’ [17]). <i>Mole </i>the animal [14] was borrowed from Middle Dutch <i>mol</i>.<br><br>No one knows for sure where this came from, but its similarity to the now obsolete <i>mouldwarp </i>‘mole’ [14] (a compound noun whose etymological meaning is ‘earththrower’) suggests that it could represent a truncated version of <i>mouldwarp</i>’s prehistoric Germanic ancestor. The metaphorical application of the word to a ‘traitor working secretly’ has been traced back as far as the 17th century, but its modern currency is due to its use by the British espionage writer John le Carré. <i>Mole </i>‘harbour wall’ [16] comes via French <i>môle </i>and medieval Greek <i>mólos </i>from Latin <i>mōlēs </i>‘mass, massive structure’.<br><br>The diminutive form of this, coined in modern times, is <i>mōlēcula</i>, from which, via French <i>molécule</i>, English gets <i>molecule </i>[18]. Other relatives are <i>demolish </i>and, possibly, <i>molest </i>[14], which comes ultimately from Latin <i>molestus </i>‘troublesome’, connected by some scholars with <i>mōlēs</i>. And German <i>mol</i>, a convenient shortening of <i>molekulargewicht </i>‘molecular weight’, has given English its fourth <i>mole </i>[20], used as the basic unit of measurement for the amount of a substance.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/maulstick" target="_blank">maulstick</a>; <a href="/etym/s/molecule" target="_blank">molecule</a>, <a href="/etym/s/molest" target="_blank">molest</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mole">mole (n.1)</a></dt><dd>spot on skin, Old English <span class="foreign">mal</span> "spot, mark, blemish," especially on cloth or linen, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*mailan</span> "spot, mark" (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">meil</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mal</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">mail</span> "wrinkle"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*mai-</span> "to stain, defile" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">miainein</span> "to stain, defile," see <a href="/etym/s/miasma" target="_blank">miasma</a>). Specifically of dark marks on human skin from late 14c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mole">mole (n.2)</a></dt><dd>type of small burrowing mammal (<span class="foreign">Talpa europea</span>), mid-14c., probably from obsolete <a href="/etym/s/moldwarp" target="_blank">moldwarp</a>, literally "earth-thrower." Spy sense first recorded 1974 in John le Carré (but suggested from early 20c.), from notion of "burrowing." Metaphoric use for "one who works in darkness" is from c. 1600.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mole">mole (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"breakwater," 1540s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">môle</span> "breakwater" (16c.), ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">moles</span> "mass, massive structure, barrier," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*mo-</span> "to exert oneself" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">molos</span> "effort," <span class="foreign">molis</span> "hardly, scarcely;" German <span class="foreign">mühen</span> "to tire," <span class="foreign">müde</span> "weary, tired;" Russian <span class="foreign">majat'</span> "to fatigue, exhaust," <span class="foreign">maja</span> "hard work").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mole">mole (n.4)</a></dt><dd>unit of molecular quantity, 1902, from German <span class="foreign">Mol</span> coined 1900 by German chemist Wilhelm Ostwald (1853-1912), short for <span class="foreign">Molekül</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/molecule" target="_blank">molecule</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
