                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/foul">foul</a> 恶臭的</span><p>来自PIE*pu, 腐烂，发臭，词源同pus, putrid. 可能来自人们闻到臭味时发出的声音。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/foul">foul</a></dt><dd><b>foul: </b><span>[OE] The underlying meaning of <i>foul </i>is probably ‘rotten, putrid’, with overtones of ‘evilsmelling’. It goes back to an Indo-European *<i>pu</i>-, which may originally have been inspired by the same reaction as produced the English exclamation of disgust at a bad smell, <i>pooh</i>. Amongst its other off-spring were Latin <i>pūs</i>, source of English <i>pus</i>, <i>purulent</i>, and <i>supurate</i>, and Latin <i>putridus</i>, source of English <i>putrid</i>.<br><br>Its Germanic descendant was *<i>fu</i>-, on which the adjective *<i>fūlaz </i>was based. This produced German <i>faul </i>‘rotten, lazy’, Dutch <i>vuil </i>‘dirty’, and English <i>foul</i>, and also the derived noun <i>filth </i>[OE]. <i>Defile </i>‘make dirty’ is not directly related, but its form was influenced by the now obsolete verb <i>befile</i>, which was connected with <i>foul</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/filth" target="_blank">filth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pus" target="_blank">pus</a>, <a href="/etym/s/putrid" target="_blank">putrid</a>, <a href="/etym/s/suppurate" target="_blank">suppurate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/foul">foul (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ful</span> "rotten, unclean, vile, corrupt, offensive to the senses," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fulaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ful</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">voul</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vuil</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fül</span>, German <span class="foreign">faul</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">füls</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*pu-</span> (2) "to rot, decay," perhaps from the sound made in reaction to smelling something bad (see <a href="/etym/s/pus" target="_blank">pus</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Old English <span class="foreign">ful</span> occasionally meant "ugly" (as contrasted with <span class="foreign">fæger</span> (adj.), modern <a href="/etym/s/fair" target="_blank">fair</a> (adj.)), and this sense became frequent in Middle English. The cognate in Swedish is the usual word for "ugly." Of weather from mid-14c. In the sporting sense of "irregular, unfair, contrary to established rule or practice" it is first attested 1797, though <span class="foreign">foul play</span> is recorded from mid-15c. Baseball sense of "out of play" attested by 1860.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/foul">foul (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fulian</span> "to become foul, rot, decay," from <span class="foreign">ful</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/foul" target="_blank">foul</a> (adj.)). Transitive meaning "make foul, pollute" is from c. 1200. Meaning "become entangled" (chiefly nautical) is from 1832, probably from <span class="foreign">foul</span> (adj.) in the sense "obstructed by anything fixed or attached" (late 15c.). "A term generally used in contrast to <span class="foreign">clear</span>, and implies entangled, embarrassed or contrary to: e.g. <span class="foreign">to foul the helm</span>, to find steerage impracticable owing to the rudder becoming entangled with rope or other gear" [Sir Geoffrey Callender, "Sea Passages," 1943]. Related: <span class="foreign">Fouled</span>; <span class="foreign">fouling</span>. Hence also <span class="foreign">foul anchor</span> (1769), one with the slack of the cable twisted round the stock or a fluke; noted by 1832 as naval insignia.</dd></dl></div></div>
