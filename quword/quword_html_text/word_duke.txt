                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/duke">duke</a> 公爵</span><p>来自PIE*deuk, 引导，领导，词源同duct, conduct. 用于指爵位。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/duke">duke</a></dt><dd><b>duke: </b><span>[12] <i>Duke </i>is one of a wide range of English words which come ultimately from the Latin verb <i>dūcere </i>‘lead’ (see DUCT). In this case its source was the Latin derivative <i>dux </i>‘leader’ (ancestor also of Italian <i>duce</i>, the title adopted by the 20th-century dictator Benito Mussolini), which passed into English via Old French <i>duc</i>. In Latin the word signified ‘military commander of a province’, and in the so-called Dark Ages it was taken up in various European languages as the term for a ‘prince ruling a small state’.<br><br>Old English never adopted it though, preferring its own word <i>earl</i>, and it was not until the 14th century that it was formally introduced, by Edward III, as a rank of the English peerage. Before that the word had been used in English only in the titles of foreign dukes, or (echoing the word’s etymological meaning) as a general term for ‘leader’ or ‘military commander’. The feminine form <i>duchess </i>[14] comes from Old French, while English has two terms for a duke’s rank or territory: the native <i>dukedom </i>[15], and <i>duchy </i>[14], borrowed from Old French <i>duche </i>(this came partly from medieval Latin <i>ducātus</i>, ultimate source of English <i>ducat </i>[14], a former Italian coin).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/conduct" target="_blank">conduct</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ducat" target="_blank">ducat</a>, <a href="/etym/s/duchess" target="_blank">duchess</a>, <a href="/etym/s/duchy" target="_blank">duchy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/duct" target="_blank">duct</a>, <a href="/etym/s/produce" target="_blank">produce</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/duke">duke (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 12c., "sovereign prince," from Old French <span class="foreign">duc</span> (12c.) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">dux</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">ducis</span>) "leader, commander," in Late Latin "governor of a province," from <span class="foreign">ducere</span> "to lead," from PIE <span class="foreign">*deuk-</span> "to lead" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">togian</span> "to pull, drag," Old High German <span class="foreign">ziohan</span> "to pull," Old English <span class="foreign">togian</span> "to draw, drag," Middle Welsh <span class="foreign">dygaf</span> "I draw").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Applied in English to "nobleman of the highest rank" probably first mid-14c., ousting native <a href="/etym/s/earl" target="_blank">earl</a>. Also used to translate various European titles (such as Russian <span class="foreign">knyaz</span>).</dd></dl></div></div>
