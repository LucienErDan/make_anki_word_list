                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/front">front</a> 前部</span><p>来自PIE*bhren, 伸出，突出，词源同brink.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/front">front</a></dt><dd><b>front: </b><span>[13] As its close French relative <i>front </i>still does, <i>front </i>used to mean ‘forehead’. Both come from Latin <i>frōns</i>, a word of dubious origins whose primary meaning was ‘forehead’, but which already in the classical period was extending figuratively to the ‘most forwardly prominent part’ of anything. In present-day English, only distant memories remain of the original sense, in such contexts as ‘put up a brave front’ (a now virtually dead metaphor in which the forehead, and hence the countenance in general, once stood for the ‘demeanour’).<br><br>The related <i>frontier </i>[14], borrowed from Old French <i>frontiere</i>, originally meant ‘front part’; its modern sense is a secondary development.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/frontier" target="_blank">frontier</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/front">front (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 13c., "forehead," from Old French <span class="foreign">front</span> "forehead, brow" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">frontem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">frons</span>) "forehead, brow, front; countenance, expression (especially as an indicator of truthfulness or shame); facade of a building, forepart; external appearance; vanguard, front rank," a word of "no plausible etymology" (de Vaan). Perhaps literally "that which projects," from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhront-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*bhren-</span> "to project, stand out" (see <a href="/etym/s/brink" target="_blank">brink</a>). Or from PIE <span class="foreign">*ser-</span> (4), "base of prepositions and preverbs with the basic meaning 'above, over, up, upper'" [Watkins, not in Pokorny].&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense "foremost part of anything" emerged in the English word mid-14c.; sense of "the face as expressive of temper or character" is from late 14c. (hence <span class="foreign">frontless</span> "shameless," c. 1600). The military sense of "foremost part of an army" (mid-14c.) led to the meaning "field of operations in contact with the enemy" (1660s); <span class="foreign">home front</span> is from 1919. Meaning "organized body of political forces" is from 1926.&#13; &#13; &#13; Sense of "public facade" is from 1891; that of "something serving as a cover for illegal activities" is from 1905. Adverbial phrase <span class="foreign">in front</span> is from 1610s. Meteorological sense first recorded 1921.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/front">front (v.)</a></dt><dd>1520s, "have the face toward," from Middle French <span class="foreign">fronter</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">front</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/front" target="_blank">front</a> (n.)). Meaning "meet face-to-face" is from 1580s. Meaning "serve as a public facade for" is from 1932. Related: <span class="foreign">Fronted</span>; <span class="foreign">fronting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/front">front (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"relating to the front," 1610s, from <a href="/etym/s/front" target="_blank">front</a> (n.). <span class="foreign">Front yard</span> first attested 1767; <span class="foreign">front door</span> is from 1807. The newspaper <span class="foreign">front page</span> is attested from 1892; as an adjective in reference to sensational news, 1907.</dd></dl></div></div>
