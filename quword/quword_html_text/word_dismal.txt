                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/dismal">dismal</a> 凄凉的，阴沉的</span><p>来自拉丁语dies mali, 即malicious day. 后用做形容词，凄凉的，阴沉的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dismal">dismal</a></dt><dd><b>dismal: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, <i>dismal </i>means ‘bad day’. It comes, via Anglo-Norman <i>dis mal</i>, from Latin <i>diēs malī</i>, literally ‘evil days’, a term used to denote the two days in each month which according to ancient superstition were supposed to be unlucky (these days, of set date, were said originally to have been computed by Egyptian astrologers, and were hence also called <i>Egyptian days</i>). The term <i>dismal </i>thus acquired connotations of ‘gloom’ and ‘calamity’. Its earliest adjectival use, somewhat tautologically, was in the phrase <i>dismal day</i>, but in the late 16th century it broadened out considerably in application.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dismal">dismal (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">dismal</span> (mid-13c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">(li) dis mals</span> "(the) bad days," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">dies mali</span> "evil or unlucky days" (also called <span class="foreign">dies Ægyptiaci</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">dies</span> "days" (see <a href="/etym/s/diurnal" target="_blank">diurnal</a>) + <span class="foreign">mali</span>, plural of <span class="foreign">malus</span> "bad" (see <a href="/etym/s/mal-" target="_blank">mal-</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Through the Middle Ages, calendars marked two days of each month as unlucky, supposedly based on the ancient calculations of Egyptian astrologers (Jan. 1, 25; Feb. 4, 26; March 1, 28; April 10, 20; May 3, 25; June 10, 16; July 13, 22; Aug. 1, 30; Sept. 3, 21; Oct. 3, 22; Nov. 5, 28; Dec. 7, 22). Modern sense of "gloomy, dreary" first recorded in English 1590s, in reference to sounds. Related: <span class="foreign">Dismally</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>

The singer gave a <b>dismal</b> performance of some old songs. </dt> <dd>那歌手唱了几首老歌，唱得不怎么样。</dd> <p>来自《权威词典》</p> </dl><dl><dt>5.
