                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fox">fox</a> 狐狸，欺骗，迷糊</span><p>来自PIE*puk, 尾巴。因狐狸毛茸茸的大尾巴而得名。引申词义欺骗，迷糊，耍花招等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fox">fox</a></dt><dd><b>fox: </b><span>[OE] <i>Fox </i>probably means literally ‘tailed animal’ – the fox’s brush being perhaps its most distinctive feature. It has been traced back to a prehistoric Indo-European *<i>puk</i>-, which also produced Sanskrit <i>púcchas </i>‘tail’. In West Germanic this gave *<i>fukhs</i>, from which come German <i>fuchs</i>, Dutch <i>vos</i>, and English <i>fox</i>. The fox is also named after its tail in Spanish (<i>raposa </i>‘fox’ is a derivative of <i>rabo </i>‘tail’) and in Welsh (<i>llwynog </i>‘fox’ comes from <i>llwyn </i>‘bush’ – that is, ‘bushy tail’).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fox">fox (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fox</span> "a fox," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fuhsaz</span> "fox" (cognates Old Saxon <span class="foreign">vohs</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">vos</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fuhs</span>, German <span class="foreign">Fuchs</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">foa</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fauho</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fuh-</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*puk-</span> "tail" (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">puccha-</span> "tail").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The bushy tail also inspired words for "fox" in Welsh (<span class="foreign">llwynog</span>, from <span class="foreign">llwyn</span> "bush"); Spanish (<span class="foreign">raposa</span>, from <span class="foreign">rabo</span> "tail"); and Lithuanian (<span class="foreign">uodegis</span>, from <span class="foreign">uodega</span> "tail"). Metaphoric extension to "clever person" was in late Old English. Meaning "sexually attractive woman" is from 1940s; but <a href="/etym/s/foxy" target="_blank">foxy</a> in this sense is recorded from 1895. A <span class="foreign">fox-tail</span> was anciently one of the badges of a fool (late 14c.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; A late Old English translation of the <span class="foreign">Medicina de Quadrupedibus</span> of Sextus Placitus advises, for women "who suffer troubles in their inward places, work for them into a salve a foxes limbs and his grease, with old oil and with tar; apply to the womens places; quickly it healeth the troubles." It also recommends, for sexual intercourse without irritation, "the extremest end of a foxes tail hung upon the arm." Rubbing a fox's testicles on warts was supposed a means to get rid of them.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Fox">Fox</a></dt><dd>name of an Algonquian people (confederated with the <span class="foreign">Sac</span> after 1760), translating French <span class="foreign">renards</span>, which itself may be a translation of an Iroquoian term meaning "red fox people." Their name for themselves is <span class="foreign">/meškwahki:-haki/</span> "red earths." French <span class="foreign">renard</span> "fox" is from <span class="foreign">Reginhard</span>, the name of the fox in old Northern European fables (as in Low German <span class="foreign">Reinke de Vos</span>, but Chaucer in The Nun's Priest's Tale calls him <span class="foreign">Daun Russell</span>); it is Germanic and means literally "strong in council, wily."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fox">fox (v.)</a></dt><dd>1660s, "to delude" (perhaps implied in Old English <span class="foreign">foxung</span> "fox-like wile, craftiness"), from <a href="/etym/s/fox" target="_blank">fox</a> (n.). The same notion is implied in Old English verbal noun <span class="foreign">foxung</span> "fox-like wile, craftiness;" and Middle English had <span class="foreign">foxerie</span> "wiliness, trickery, deceit." <span class="foreign">Foxed</span> in booksellers' catalogues (1847) means "stained with fox-colored marks" (rusty red-brown). In other contexts the past-participle adjective typically meant "drunk" (1610s).</dd></dl></div></div>
