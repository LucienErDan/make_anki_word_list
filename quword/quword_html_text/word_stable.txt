3. 站着岿然不动，确实很稳定。<br />

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stable">stable</a> 稳定的</span><p>来自拉丁语 stare,站立，词源同 stand.-able,形容词后缀。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stable">stable</a> 马厩，马房，养马场</span><p>来自拉丁语 stabulum,马厩，棚屋，妓院，来自 stare,站立，词源同 stand,-bulum,工具格后缀， 词源同 table,fable.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stable">stable</a></dt><dd><b>stable: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>stable</i>, but both come ultimately from the same source: the Indo-European base *<i>stā</i>- ‘stand’, ancestor also of English <i>stand</i>. The adjective <i>stable </i>[13] comes via Old French <i>estable </i>from Latin <i>stabilis </i>‘standing firm’, which has also given English <i>establish</i>, <i>stability </i>[15], and <i>stabilize </i>[19]. It was formed from the base *<i>stā</i>-, as was Latin <i>stabulum </i>‘standing-place’, hence ‘enclosure for animals’, which English acquired via Old French <i>estable </i>as <i>stable </i>[13]. The corresponding Germanic formation, also based on *<i>stā</i>-, is <i>stall</i>. A <i>constable </i>is etymologically an ‘officer in charge of stables’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stable">stable (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., "building or enclosure where horses or cows are kept, building for domestic animals," from Old French <span class="foreign">stable</span>, <span class="foreign">estable</span> "a stable, stall" (Modern French <span class="foreign">étable</span>), also applied to cowsheds and pigsties, from Latin <span class="foreign">stabulum</span> "a stall, fold, aviary, beehive, lowly cottage, brothel, etc.," literally "a standing place," from PIE <span class="foreign">*ste-dhlo-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*sta-</span> "to stand" (see <a href="/etym/s/stet" target="_blank">stet</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "collection of horses belonging to one stable" is attested from 1570s; transferred sense of "group of fighters under same management" is from 1897; that of "group of prostitutes working for the same employer" is from 1937.&#13; <blockquote>For what the grete Stiede&#13; <br />Is stole, thanne he taketh hiede,&#13; <br />And makth the stable dore fast.&#13; <br />[John Gower, <span class="foreign">"Confessio Amantis,"</span> 1390]</blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stable">stable (adj.)</a></dt><dd>mid-12c., "trustworthy, reliable;" mid-13c., "constant, steadfast; virtuous;" from Old French <span class="foreign">stable</span>, <span class="foreign">estable</span> "constant, steadfast, unchanging," from Latin <span class="foreign">stabilis</span> "firm, steadfast, stable, fixed," figuratively "durable, unwavering," literally "able to stand," from PIE <span class="foreign">*ste-dhli-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*sta-</span> "to stand" (see <a href="/etym/s/stet" target="_blank">stet</a>). From c. 1300 as "well-founded, well-established, secure" (of governments, etc.). Physical sense of "secure against falling" is recorded from late 14c.; also "of even temperament." Of nuclear isotopes, from 1904.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stable">stable (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to put in a certain place or position," c. 1300; "to put (a horse) in a stable," early 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/stable" target="_blank">stable</a> (n.) or from Old French <span class="foreign">establer</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Stabled</span>; <span class="foreign">stabling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
