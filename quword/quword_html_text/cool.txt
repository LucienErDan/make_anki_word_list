            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cool">cool</a> 冷的</span><p>来自*gel, 冷，词源同cold, jelly.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cool">cool</a></dt><dd><b>cool: </b><span>[OE] <i>Cool </i>comes from the same source as <i>cold</i>, namely Indo-European *<i>gel</i>-, *<i>gol</i>- (from which English also gets <i>congeal</i>, <i>gel</i>, and <i>jelly</i>). The Germanic descendants of this Indo- European base were *<i>kal</i>-, *<i>kōl</i>-. From these were derived the Germanic adjective *<i>kōluz</i>, which passed into Old English as <i>cōl</i>. Its use for ‘fashionable, hip’ is mid-20th-century, but its nonchalant application to large sums of money is of surprisingly long standing: ‘I just made a couple of bets with him, took up a cool hundred, and so went to the King’s Arms’, John Vanbrugh and Colly Cibber, <i>The Provok’d Husband </i>1728.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cold" target="_blank">cold</a>, <a href="/etym/s/congeal" target="_blank">congeal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gel" target="_blank">gel</a>, <a href="/etym/s/jelly" target="_blank">jelly</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cool">cool (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">col</span> "not warm" (but usually not as severe as <span class="foreign">cold</span>), also, of persons, "unperturbed, undemonstrative," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*koluz</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">coel</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">koel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kuoli</span>, German <span class="foreign">kühl</span> "cool," Old Norse <span class="foreign">kala</span> "be cold"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*gel-</span> "cold, to freeze" (see <a href="/etym/s/cold" target="_blank">cold</a> (adj.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Applied since 1728 to large sums of money to give emphasis to amount. Meaning "calmly audacious" is from 1825. Slang use for "fashionable" is 1933, originally Black English; modern use as a general term of approval is from late 1940s, probably from bop talk and originally in reference to a style of jazz; said to have been popularized in jazz circles by tenor saxophonist Lester Young. Related: <span class="foreign">Coolly</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cool">cool (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "coldness, coolness," from <a href="/etym/s/cool" target="_blank">cool</a> (adj.). Meaning "one's self-control, composure" (the thing you either <span class="foreign">keep</span> or <span class="foreign">lose</span>) is from 1966.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cool">cool (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">colian</span>, "to lose warmth," also figuratively, "to lose ardor," from the root of <a href="/etym/s/cool" target="_blank">cool</a> (adj.). Meaning "to cause to lose warmth" is from late 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Cooled</span>; <span class="foreign">cooling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
