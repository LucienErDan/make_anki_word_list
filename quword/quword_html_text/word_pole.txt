                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pole">pole</a> 杆，棍</span><p>来自古英语pal,杆，桩，来自拉丁语palus,杆，桩，来自PIE*pag,固定，词源同pale,pile,pillar.拼写比较rule,regulate.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pole">pole</a> 地级，地轴</span><p>来自拉丁语polus,地级，天空，来自希腊语polos,轴心，地轴，来自PIE*kwel,转，转动，词 源同cycle,wheel.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/Pole">Pole</a> 波兰人</span><p>来自波兰语pole,土地，平原，来自PIE*pele,展开，词源同plan,plain.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pole">pole</a></dt><dd><b>pole: </b><span>There are two separate words <i>pole </i>in English. <i>Pole </i>‘long thin piece of wood’ [OE] comes from a pre- historic Germanic *<i>pāl</i>- (source also of German <i>pfahl</i>, Dutch <i>paal</i>, and Swedish <i>påla</i>). This was borrowed from Latin <i>pālus </i>‘stake’, from which English gets <i>pale </i>‘stake’. <i>Pole </i>‘extremity’ [14] was acquired from Latin <i>polus</i>, which in turn went back to Greek <i>pólos </i>‘axis of a sphere’. This was a descendant of Indo-European *<i>qwolo</i>- ‘turn round’ (source of English <i>wheel</i>), and has also given English <i>pulley</i>. The derivative <i>polar </i>[16] is an anglicization of the modern Latin coinage <i>polāris</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pale" target="_blank">pale</a>; <a href="/etym/s/polar" target="_blank">polar</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pulley" target="_blank">pulley</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wheel" target="_blank">wheel</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pole">pole (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"stake," late Old English <span class="foreign">pal</span> "stake, pole, post," a general Germanic borrowing (Old Frisian and Old Saxon <span class="foreign">pal</span> "stake," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">pael</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">paal</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">pfal</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">pall</span>) from Latin <span class="foreign">palus</span> "stake" (see <a href="/etym/s/pale" target="_blank">pale</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Racing sense of "inside fence surrounding a course" is from 1851; <span class="foreign">pole position</span> in auto racing attested from 1904.&#13; A <span class="foreign">ten-foot pole</span> as a metaphoric measure of something one would not touch something (or someone) else with is by 1839, American English. The ten-foot pole was a common tool used to set stakes for fences, etc., and the phrase "Can't touch de bottom with a ten foot pole" is in the popular old minstrel show song "Camptown Races."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; "I saw her eat."&#13; <br />"No very unnatural occurrence I should think."&#13; <br />"But she ate an <span class="foreign">onion!</span>"&#13; <br />"Right my boy, right, never marry a woman who would touch an onion with a ten foot pole."&#13; <br />["The Collegian," University of Virginia, 1839]&#13; </blockquote>&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pole">pole (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"ends of Earth's axis," late 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">pole</span> or directly from Latin <span class="foreign">polus</span> "end of an axis;" also "the sky, the heavens" (a sense sometimes used in English from 16c.), from Greek <span class="foreign">polos</span> "pivot, axis of a sphere, the sky," from PIE <span class="foreign">*kwol-</span> "turn round," from root <span class="foreign">*kwel-</span> (1) "wheel; turn, roll around" (see <a href="/etym/s/cycle" target="_blank">cycle</a> (n.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Pole">Pole (n.)</a></dt><dd>"inhabitant or native of Poland," 1650s, from German <span class="foreign">Pole</span>, singular of <span class="foreign">Polen</span>, from Polish <span class="foreign">Polanie</span> "Poles," literally "field-dwellers," from <span class="foreign">pole</span> "field," related to Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">polje</span> "field," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pele-</span> (2) "flat, to spread" (see <a href="/etym/s/plane" target="_blank">plane</a> (n.1)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pole">pole (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to furnish with poles," 1570s, from <a href="/etym/s/pole" target="_blank">pole</a> (n.1). Meaning "to push with a pole" is from 1753. Related: <span class="foreign">Poled</span>; <span class="foreign">poling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
