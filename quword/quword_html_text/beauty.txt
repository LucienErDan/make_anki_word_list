            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/beauty">beauty</a> 美丽</span><p>词源同beau, 美丽的，漂亮的。-ty, 抽象名词后缀。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/beauty">beauty</a></dt><dd><b>beauty: </b><span>[13] <i>Beauty </i>came via Anglo-Norman <i>beute </i>and Old French <i>bealte </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>bellitas</i>, a derivative of Latin <i>bellus </i>‘beautiful’ (this developed from an earlier, unrecorded *<i>dwenolos</i>, a diminutive form of Old Latin *<i>duenos</i>, *<i>duonos</i>, which is related to Latin <i>bonus </i>‘good’ – source of English <i>bonus </i>[18], <i>bounty </i>[13], and <i>bounteous </i>[14]).<br><br>Other English words from the same ultimate source are <i>beau </i>[17] and its feminine form <i>belle </i>[17]; <i>beatific </i>[17], which comes from Latin <i>beātus </i>‘blessed, happy’, the past participle of the verb <i>beāre</i>, a relative of <i>bellus</i>; <i>embellish</i>; and <i>bibelot </i>‘small ornament’ [19], originally a French word based ultimately on *<i>belbel</i>, a reduplication of Old French <i>bel </i>‘beautiful’.<br><br>English <i>beautiful </i>is 15th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/beau" target="_blank">beau</a>, <a href="/etym/s/belle" target="_blank">belle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/beatific" target="_blank">beatific</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bibelot" target="_blank">bibelot</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bonus" target="_blank">bonus</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bounty" target="_blank">bounty</a>, <a href="/etym/s/embellish" target="_blank">embellish</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/beauty">beauty (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "physical attractiveness," also "goodness, courtesy," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">beute</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">biauté</span> "beauty, seductiveness, beautiful person" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">beauté</span>), earlier <span class="foreign">beltet</span>, from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">bellitatem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">bellitas</span>) "state of being handsome," from Latin <span class="foreign">bellus</span> "pretty, handsome, charming," in classical Latin used especially of women and children, or ironically or insultingly of men, perhaps from PIE <span class="foreign">*dw-en-elo-</span>, diminutive of root <span class="foreign">*deu-</span> (2) "to do, perform; show favor, revere" (see <a href="/etym/s/bene-" target="_blank">bene-</a>). Famously defined by Stendhal as <span class="foreign">la promesse de bonheur</span> "the promise of happiness."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; [I]t takes the one hundred men in ten million who understand beauty, which isn't imitation or an improvement on the beautiful as already understood by the common herd, twenty or thirty years to convince the twenty thousand next most sensitive souls after their own that this new beauty is truly beautiful. [Stendhal, "Life of Henry Brulard"]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">wlite</span>. Concrete meaning "a beautiful woman" is first recorded late 14c. <span class="foreign">Beauty sleep</span> "sleep before midnight" is attested by 1850. <span class="foreign">Beauty spot</span> is from 1650s. <span class="foreign">Beauty parlor</span> is from 1894.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The sudden death of a young woman a little over a week ago in a down-town "beauty parlor" has served to direct public attention to those institutions and their methods. In this case, it seems, the operator painted on or injected into the patron's facial blemish a 4-per-cent cocaine solution and then applied an electrode, the sponge of which was saturated with carbolized water. ["The Western Druggist," October 1894]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Beauté du diable</span> (literally "devil's beauty") is used as a French phrase in English from 1825.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">

The happiest are not those who own all the best things, but those who can appreciate the <b>beauty</b> of life. </dt> <dd>最幸福的人们并不一定什么都是最好，只是他们懂得欣赏生活的美好。</dd> <p>来自金山词霸 每日一句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. Gregory was still enchanted with Shannon's youth and joy and <b>beauty</b>. </dt> <dd>格雷戈里仍为香农的朝气、快乐和美丽着迷。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4.
