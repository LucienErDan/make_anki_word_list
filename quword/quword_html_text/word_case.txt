                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/case">case</a> 情况，盒子</span><p>1.情况，来自词根cad, 掉落，词源同case, accident. </p><p> 2.盒子，来自拉丁词capsa, 盒子，词源同accept, capture.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/case">case</a></dt><dd><b>case: </b><span>[13] There are two distinct words <i>case </i>in English, both acquired via Old French from Latin and both members of very large families. <i>Case </i>‘circumstance’ was borrowed from Old French <i>cas</i>, which in turn came from Latin <i>cāsus </i>‘fall, chance’. This was formed from the base of the verb <i>cadere </i>‘fall’. The progression of senses is from the concrete ‘that which falls’ to the metaphorical ‘that which befalls, that which happens (by chance)’ (and English <i>chance </i>is also derived ultimately from Latin <i>cadere</i>).<br><br>Other related words in English include <i>accident</i>, <i>cadence</i>, <i>cadaver</i>, <i>cheat</i>, <i>chute</i>, <i>coincide</i>, <i>decadent</i>, <i>decay</i>, <i>deciduous</i>, and <i>occasion</i>. <i>Case </i>‘container’ comes via Old French <i>casse </i>from Latin <i>capsa </i>‘box’, a derivative of the verb <i>capere </i>‘hold’ (which is related to English <i>heave</i>).<br><br>At various points during its history it has produced offshoots which in English have become <i>capsule </i>[17], a diminutive form, <i>cash</i>, <i>chassis</i>, and perhaps <i>capsicum </i>[18] and <i>chase </i>‘engrave’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/accident" target="_blank">accident</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cadaver" target="_blank">cadaver</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cheat" target="_blank">cheat</a>, <a href="/etym/s/chute" target="_blank">chute</a>, <a href="/etym/s/decay" target="_blank">decay</a>, <a href="/etym/s/deciduous" target="_blank">deciduous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/occasion" target="_blank">occasion</a>, <a href="/etym/s/occident" target="_blank">occident</a>; <a href="/etym/s/capsicum" target="_blank">capsicum</a>, <a href="/etym/s/capsule" target="_blank">capsule</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cash" target="_blank">cash</a>, <a href="/etym/s/chassis" target="_blank">chassis</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/case">case (n.1)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., "what befalls one; state of affairs," from Old French <span class="foreign">cas</span> "an event, happening, situation, quarrel, trial," from Latin <span class="foreign">casus</span> "a chance, occasion, opportunity; accident, mishap," literally "a falling," from <span class="foreign">cas-</span>, past participle stem of <span class="foreign">cadere</span> "to fall, sink, settle down, decline, perish" (used widely: of the setting of heavenly bodies, the fall of Troy, suicides), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kad-</span> "to lay out, fall or make fall, yield, break up" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sad-</span> "to fall down," Armenian <span class="foreign">chacnum</span> "to fall, become low," perhaps also Middle Irish <span class="foreign">casar</span> "hail, lightning"). The notion being "that which falls" as "that which happens" (compare <a href="/etym/s/befall" target="_blank">befall</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "instance, example" is from c. 1300. Meaning "actual state of affairs" is from c. 1400. Given widespread extended and transferred senses in English in law (16c.), medicine (18c.), etc.; the grammatical sense (late 14c.) was in Latin. U.S. slang meaning "person" is from 1848. <span class="foreign">In case</span> "in the event" is recorded from mid-14c. <span class="foreign">Case history</span> is from 1879, originally medical; <span class="foreign">case study</span> "study of a particular case" is from 1879, originally legal.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/case">case (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"receptacle," early 14c., from Anglo-French and Old North French <span class="foreign">casse</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">chasse</span> "case, reliquary;" Modern French <span class="foreign">châsse</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">capsa</span> "box, repository" (especially for books), from <span class="foreign">capere</span> "to take, hold" (see <a href="/etym/s/capable" target="_blank">capable</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "outer protective covering" is from late 14c. Also used from 1660s with a sense "frame" (as in <span class="foreign">staircase</span>, <span class="foreign">casement</span>). Artillery sense is from 1660s, from <span class="foreign">case-shot</span> "small projectiles put in cases" (1620s). Its application in the printing trade (first recorded 1580s) to the two trays where compositors keep their types in separate compartments for easy access led to <span class="foreign">upper-case letter</span> for a capital (1862) and <span class="foreign">lower-case</span> for small letters.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; "The cases, or receptacles, for the type, which are always in pairs, and termed the 'upper' and the 'lower,' are formed of two oblong wooden frames, divided into compartments or boxes of different dimensions, the upper case containing ninety-eight and the lower fifty-four. In the upper case are placed the capital, small capital, and accented letters, also figures, signs for reference to notes &amp;c.; in the lower case the ordinary running letter, points for punctuation, spaces for separating the words, and quadrats for filling up the short lines." ["The Literary Gazette," Jan. 29, 1859]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/case">case (v.)</a></dt><dd>"enclose in a case," 1570s, from <a href="/etym/s/case" target="_blank">case</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Cased</span>; <a href="/etym/s/casing" target="_blank">casing</a>. Meaning "examine, inspect" (usually prior to robbing) is from 1915, American English slang, perhaps from the notion of giving a place a look on all sides (compare technical <span class="foreign">case</span> (v.) "cover the outside of a building with a different material," 1707).</dd></dl></div></div>
