                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/salt">salt</a> 盐，食盐</span><p>来自古英语 sealt,盐，来自 Proto-Germanic*saltom,盐，来自 PIE*sal,盐，词源同 halogen,saline.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/salt">salt</a></dt><dd><b>salt: </b><span>[OE] Salt was a key element in the diet of our Indo-European ancestors, and their word for it, *<i>sal</i>-, is the source of virtually all the modern European terms, including Russian <i>sol’</i>, Polish <i>sól</i>, Serbo-Croat <i>so</i>, Irish <i>salann</i>, and Welsh <i>halen</i>. Greek <i>háls </i>has given English <i>halogen </i>[19]. And Latin <i>sāl</i>, besides evolving into French <i>sel</i>, Italian <i>sale</i>, Spanish <i>sal</i>, and Romanian <i>sare</i>, has contributed an enormous range of vocabulary to English, including <i>salad</i>, <i>salary</i>, <i>saline </i>[15], <i>salsa</i>, <i>sauce</i>, <i>saucer</i>, and <i>sausage</i>.<br><br>Its Germanic descendant was *<i>salt</i>-, which has produced Swedish, Danish, and English <i>salt </i>and Dutch <i>zout</i>, and also lies behind English <i>silt </i>and <i>souse</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/halogen" target="_blank">halogen</a>, <a href="/etym/s/salad" target="_blank">salad</a>, <a href="/etym/s/salary" target="_blank">salary</a>, <a href="/etym/s/saline" target="_blank">saline</a>, <a href="/etym/s/salsa" target="_blank">salsa</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sauce" target="_blank">sauce</a>, <a href="/etym/s/saucer" target="_blank">saucer</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sausage" target="_blank">sausage</a>, <a href="/etym/s/silt" target="_blank">silt</a>, <a href="/etym/s/souse" target="_blank">souse</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/SALT">SALT (n.)</a></dt><dd>Cold War U.S.-U.S.S.R. nuclear weapons negotiations, 1968, acronym for <span class="foreign">Strategic Arms Limitation Talks</span> (which would make <span class="foreign">SALT talks</span> redundant, but the last element sometimes also is understood as <span class="foreign">treaty</span>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/salt">salt (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">sealt</span> "salt" (n.; also as an adjective, "salty, briny"), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*saltom</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Old Norse, Old Frisian, Gothic <span class="foreign">salt</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zout</span>, German <span class="foreign">Salz</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*sal-</span> (1) "salt" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">hals</span> "salt, sea," Latin <span class="foreign">sal</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">soli</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">salann</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">halen</span> "salt").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Modern chemistry sense is from 1790. Meaning "experienced sailor" is first attested 1840, in reference to the salinity of the sea. Salt was long regarded as having power to repel spiritual and magical evil. Many metaphoric uses reflect that this was once a rare and important resource, such as <span class="foreign">worth one's salt</span> (1830), <span class="foreign">salt of the earth</span> (Old English, after Matt. v:13). Belief that spilling salt brings bad luck is attested from 16c. To be <span class="foreign">above</span> (or <span class="foreign">below</span>) <span class="foreign">the salt</span> (1590s) refers to customs of seating at a long table according to rank or honor, and placing a large salt-cellar in the middle of the dining table.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Salt-lick</span> first recorded 1751; <span class="foreign">salt-marsh</span> is Old English <span class="foreign">sealtne mersc</span>; <span class="foreign">salt-shaker</span> is from 1882. <span class="foreign">Salt-and-pepper</span> "of dark and light color" first recorded 1915. To take something <span class="foreign">with a grain of salt</span> is from 1640s, from Modern Latin <span class="foreign">cum grano salis</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/salt">salt (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">sealtan</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*salto-</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/salt" target="_blank">salt</a> (n.)), and in part from the noun. Related: <span class="foreign">Salted</span>; <span class="foreign">salting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
