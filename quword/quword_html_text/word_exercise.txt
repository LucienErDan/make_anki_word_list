                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/exercise">exercise</a> 练习</span><p>ex-, 向外。-erc, 保护，限制，隐藏，词源同ark, arcane. 即去除限制，进行练习。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/exercise">exercise</a></dt><dd><b>exercise: </b><span>[14] The notion underlying <i>exercise </i>is of ‘removal of restraint’. It comes ultimately from Latin <i>exercēre</i>, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>ex</i>- ‘out of, from’ and <i>arcēre </i>‘restrain, enclose’ (source of English <i>arcane </i>and related to English <i>ark</i>). It has been speculated that this originally denoted the driving of draught animals out into the fields to plough, but however that may be, it soon developed the general senses ‘set to work, keep at work’ and ‘drill, practise’ which form the semantic basis of English <i>exercise</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/arcane" target="_blank">arcane</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ark" target="_blank">ark</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/exercise">exercise (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "condition of being in active operation; practice for the sake of training," from Old French <span class="foreign">exercice</span> (13c.) "exercise, execution of power; physical or spiritual exercise," from Latin <span class="foreign">exercitium</span> "training, exercise" (of soldiers, horsemen, etc.); "play;" in Medieval Latin also of arts, from <span class="foreign">exercitare</span>, frequentative of <span class="foreign">exercere</span> "keep busy, keep at work, oversee, engage busily; train, exercise; practice, follow; carry into effect; disturb, disquiet," literally "remove restraint," from <span class="foreign">ex-</span> "off" (see <a href="/etym/s/ex-" target="_blank">ex-</a>) + <span class="foreign">arcere</span> "keep away, prevent, enclose," from PIE <span class="foreign">*ark-</span> "to hold, contain, guard" (see <a href="/etym/s/arcane" target="_blank">arcane</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Original sense may have been driving farm animals to the field to plow. Meaning "physical activity for fitness, etc." first recorded in English late 14c. Also from late 14c. as "a carrying out of an action; a doing or practicing; a disciplinary task." In reference to written schoolwork from early 17c. The ending was abstracted for formations such as <span class="foreign">dancercise</span> (1967); <span class="foreign">jazzercise</span> (1977); and <span class="foreign">boxercise</span> (1985).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/exercise">exercise (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to employ, put into active use," from <a href="/etym/s/exercise" target="_blank">exercise</a> (n.); originally "to make use of;" also in regard to mental and spiritual training; sense of "engage in physical activity" is from 1650s. From late 14c. in sense of "train, drill, discipline, educate (someone); develop (a skill) by practice." Related: <span class="foreign">Exercised</span>; <span class="foreign">exercises</span>; <span class="foreign">exercising</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
