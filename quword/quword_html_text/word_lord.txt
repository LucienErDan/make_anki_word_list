                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/lord">lord</a> 主，贵族，阁下</span><p>来自古英语hlafweard,即管面包的人，一家之主，来自hlaf,面包，词源同loaf,weard,看管，词源同ward,guard.对应拉丁语Dominus,主，上帝。后用于敬称。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lord">lord</a></dt><dd><b>lord: </b><span>[OE] It is a measure of the centrality of bread to human society that the word <i>lord </i>denotes etymologically ‘guardian of the loaf’. It goes back to a primitive Old English *<i>khlaibward</i>, a compound formed from *<i>khlaib </i>‘loaf’ and *<i>ward </i>‘guardian, keeper’ (ancestor of modern English <i>ward</i>). This gradually developed in Old English via <i>hlāfweard </i>to <i>hlāford</i>, and in the 14th century it lost its middle /v/ to become the single-syllable word we know today. <i>Lady </i>was likewise originally based on the word <i>loaf</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/guard" target="_blank">guard</a>, <a href="/etym/s/loaf" target="_blank">loaf</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ward" target="_blank">ward</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lord">lord (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-13c., <span class="foreign">laverd</span>, <span class="foreign">loverd</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">hlaford</span> "master of a household, ruler, superior," also "God" (translating Latin <span class="foreign">Dominus</span>, though Old English <span class="foreign">drihten</span> was used more often), earlier <span class="foreign">hlafweard</span>, literally "one who guards the loaves," from <span class="foreign">hlaf</span> "bread, loaf" (see <a href="/etym/s/loaf" target="_blank">loaf</a> (n.)) + <span class="foreign">weard</span> "keeper, guardian" (see <a href="/etym/s/ward" target="_blank">ward</a> (n.)). Compare <a href="/etym/s/lady" target="_blank">lady</a> (literally "bread-kneader"), and Old English <span class="foreign">hlafæta</span> "household servant," literally "loaf-eater." Modern monosyllabic form emerged 14c. As an interjection from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Lord's Prayer</span> is from 1540s. <span class="foreign">Lord of the Flies</span> translates <a href="/etym/s/Beelzebub" target="_blank">Beelzebub</a> (q.v.) and was name of 1954 book by William Golding. To <span class="foreign">drink like a lord</span> is from 1620s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lord">lord (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "to exercise lordship," from <a href="/etym/s/lord" target="_blank">lord</a> (n.). Meaning "to play the lord, domineer" is late 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Lorded</span>; <span class="foreign">lording</span>. To <span class="foreign">lord it</span> is from 1570s.</dd></dl></div></div>
