                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stream">stream</a> 小河，溪流，水流，流动，飘扬</span><p>来自古英语 stream,小河，溪流，来自 Proto-Germanic*straumaz,水流，溪流，来自 PIE*sreu, 流动，词源同 serum,rheum.双字母-sr-在 Proto-Germanic 扩展为三字母-str-.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stream">stream</a></dt><dd><b>stream: </b><span>[OE] A <i>stream </i>is etymologically something that ‘flows’. The word comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>straumaz </i>(source also of German <i>strom</i>, Dutch <i>stroom</i>, Swedish <i>ström</i>, and Danish <i>strøm</i>). This in turn was derived from the Indo-European base *<i>sreu</i>- ‘flow’, which has also given English <i>catarrh</i>, <i>diarrhoea</i>, and <i>rheumatism</i>. Non-Germanic relatives of <i>stream </i>include Polish <i>strumyk </i>‘brook’ and Sanskrit <i>srotas</i>- ‘stream’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/catarrh" target="_blank">catarrh</a>, <a href="/etym/s/diarrhoea" target="_blank">diarrhoea</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rheumatism" target="_blank">rheumatism</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stream">stream (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., "to flow copiously," from <a href="/etym/s/stream" target="_blank">stream</a> (n.). Transitive sense "discharge in a stream" is from late 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Streamed</span>; <span class="foreign">streaming</span>. Compare German <span class="foreign">strömen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">stroomen</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">strömme</span>, all verbs from nouns.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stream">stream (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">stream</span> "a course of water," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*straumaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">strom</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">straumr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">strøm</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">ström</span>, Norwegian <span class="foreign">straum</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">stram</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">stroom</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">stroum</span>, German <span class="foreign">Strom</span> "current, river"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*sreu-</span> "to flow" (see <a href="/etym/s/rheum" target="_blank">rheum</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From early 12c. as "anything issuing from a source and flowing continuously." Meaning "current in the sea" (as in <span class="foreign">Gulf Stream</span>) is recorded from late 14c., as is the sense of "steady current in a river." <span class="foreign">Stream of consciousness</span> in lit crit first recorded 1930, originally in psychology (1855). <span class="foreign">Stream of thought</span> is from 1890.</dd></dl></div></div>
