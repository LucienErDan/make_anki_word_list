            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/wind">wind</a> 风</span><p>来自PIE*we,吹，词源同ventilate,wing.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/wind">wind</a> 蜿蜒</span><p>来自PIE*wendh,弯，转，缠绕，来自PIE*wei的扩大形式，词源同vine,vetch.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wind">wind</a></dt><dd><b>wind: </b><span>English has three distinct words <i>wind</i>. The noun, ‘moving air’ [OE], came from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>windaz</i>, which also produced German and Dutch <i>wind </i>and Swedish and Danish <i>vind</i>. This in turn went back to Indo- European *<i>went</i>-, whose other descendants include Latin <i>ventus </i>(source of English <i>vent</i>, <i>ventilate</i>, etc) and Welsh <i>gwynt</i>.<br><br>And *<i>went</i>itself was derived from the base *<i>we</i>- ‘blow’, source also of Greek <i>aétēs </i>‘wind’ and <i>áēr </i>‘air’ (from which English gets <i>air</i>), Sanskrit <i>vátas </i>‘wind’, and Russian <i>vejat’ </i>‘blow’. The now archaic verb <i>wind </i>‘blow a horn’ [16], for all that it rhymes with <i>wind </i>‘wrap round’, was derived from the noun <i>wind</i>. <i>Wind </i>‘wrap round’ [OE] originally meant ‘go in a particular direction’; ‘wrap’ did not emerge until the 14th century, via an intermediate ‘go in a circle’.<br><br>It came from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>windon </i>(source also of German and Dutch <i>winden</i>, Swedish <i>vinda</i>, and Danish <i>vinde</i>), which was formed from a variant of the base which produced English <i>wand</i>, <i>wander</i>, and <i>wend</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/air" target="_blank">air</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vent" target="_blank">vent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ventilate" target="_blank">ventilate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/weather" target="_blank">weather</a>, <a href="/etym/s/winnow" target="_blank">winnow</a>; <a href="/etym/s/wand" target="_blank">wand</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wander" target="_blank">wander</a>, <a href="/etym/s/went" target="_blank">went</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wind">wind (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"air in motion," Old English <span class="foreign">wind</span> "wind," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*windaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Old Frisian, Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">wind</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vindr</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">wind</span>, German <span class="foreign">Wind</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">winds</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*we-nt-o-</span> "blowing," from root <span class="foreign">*we-</span> "to blow" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">va-</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">aemi-</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">waian</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">wawan</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">wajan</span>, German <span class="foreign">wehen</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">vejati</span> "to blow;" Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vatah</span>, Avestan <span class="foreign">vata-</span>, Hittite <span class="foreign">huwantis</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">ventus</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">vetru</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vejas</span> "wind;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vetra</span> "tempest, storm;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">feth</span> "air;" Welsh <span class="foreign">gwynt</span>, Breton <span class="foreign">gwent</span> "wind").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Normal pronunciation evolution made this word rhyme with <span class="foreign">kind</span> and <span class="foreign">rind</span> (Donne rhymes it with <span class="foreign">mind</span>), but it shifted to a short vowel 18c., probably from influence of <span class="foreign">windy</span>, where the short vowel is natural. A sad loss for poets, who now must rhyme it only with <span class="foreign">sinned</span> and a handful of weak words. Symbolic of emptiness and vanity since late 13c.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; I have forgot much, Cynara! gone with the wind. [Ernest Dowson, 1896]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Meaning "breath" is attested from late Old English; especially "breath in speaking" (early 14c.), so <a href="/etym/s/long-winded" target="_blank">long-winded</a>, also "easy or regular breathing" (early 14c.), hence <span class="foreign">second wind</span> in the figurative sense (by 1830), an image from the sport of hunting.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Winds</span> "wind instruments of an orchestra" is from 1876. Figurative phrase <span class="foreign">which way the wind blows</span> for "the current state of affairs" is suggested from c. 1400. To <span class="foreign">get wind of</span> "receive information about" is by 1809, perhaps inspired by French <span class="foreign">avoir le vent de</span>. To <span class="foreign">take the wind out of (one's) sails</span> in the figurative sense (by 1883) is an image from sailing, where a ship without wind can make no progress. <span class="foreign">Wind-chill index</span> is recorded from 1939. <span class="foreign">Wind energy</span> from 1976. <span class="foreign">Wind vane</span> from 1725.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wind">wind (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"move by turning and twisting," Old English <span class="foreign">windan</span> "to turn, twist, plait, curl, brandish, swing" (class III strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">wand</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">wunden</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*windan</span> "to wind" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">windan</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vinda</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">winda</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">winden</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">wintan</span>, German <span class="foreign">winden</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">windan</span> "to wind"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*wendh-</span> "to turn, wind, weave" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">viere</span> "twist, plait, weave," <span class="foreign">vincire</span> "bind;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vyti</span> "twist, wind").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Related to <a href="/etym/s/wend" target="_blank">wend</a>, which is its causative form, and to <a href="/etym/s/wander" target="_blank">wander</a>. The past tense and past participle merged in Middle English. Meaning "to twine, entwine oneself around" is from 1590s; transitive sense of "turn or twist round and round (on something) is from c. 1300. Meaning "set a watch, clockwork, etc. in operating mode by tightening its spring" is from c. 1600. <span class="foreign">Wind down</span> "come to a conclusion" is recorded from 1952; <span class="foreign">wind up</span> "come to a conclusion" is from 1825; earlier in transitive sense "put (affairs) in order in advance of a final settlement" (1780). <span class="foreign">Winding sheet</span> "shroud of a corpse" is attested from early 15c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wind">wind (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to perceive by scent, get wind of," c. 1400, from <a href="/etym/s/wind" target="_blank">wind</a> (n.1). Of horns, etc., "make sound by blowing through," from 1580s. Meaning "tire, put out of breath; render temporarily breathless" is from 1802, originally in pugilism, in reference to the effect of a punch in the stomach. Related: <span class="foreign">Winded</span>; <span class="foreign">winding</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wind">wind (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"an act of winding round," 1825, from <a href="/etym/s/wind" target="_blank">wind</a> (v.1) . Earlier, "an apparatus for winding," late 14c., in which use perhaps from a North Sea Germanic word, such as Middle Dutch, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">winde</span> "windlass."</dd></dl></div></div>
