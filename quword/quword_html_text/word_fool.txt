                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fool">fool</a> 傻瓜</span><p>来自拉丁语follis, 风箱，皮袋，词源同bellows. 特指铁匠的鼓风袋，后来词义引申为夸夸其谈的人，笨蛋，傻瓜，等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fool">fool</a></dt><dd><b>fool: </b><span>[13] <i>Fool </i>comes via Old French <i>fol </i>from Latin <i>follis</i>, which originally meant ‘bellows’ (and may come ultimately from Indo-European *<i>bhel</i>-, which produced English <i>bellows</i>). In post-classical times it developed semantically via ‘windbag’ and ‘fatuous person’ to ‘idiot’. <i>Fool </i>‘dessert of puréed fruit and cream’ [16] appears to be the same word, applied (like <i>trifle</i>) to a light insubstantial dessert. <i>Folly </i>[13] comes from the Old French derivative <i>folie</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/folly" target="_blank">folly</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fool">fool (n.1)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., "silly, stupid, or ignorant person," from Old French <span class="foreign">fol</span> "madman, insane person; idiot; rogue; jester," also "blacksmith's bellows," also an adjective meaning "mad, insane" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">fou</span>), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">follus</span> (adj.) "foolish," from Latin <span class="foreign">follis</span> "bellows, leather bag" (see <a href="/etym/s/follicle" target="_blank">follicle</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sense evolution probably is from Vulgar Latin use of <span class="foreign">follis</span> in a sense of "windbag, empty-headed person." Compare also Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vatula-</span> "insane," literally "windy, inflated with wind." But some sources suggest evolution from Latin <span class="foreign">folles</span> "puffed cheeks" (of a buffoon), a secondary sense from plural of <span class="foreign">follis</span>. One makes the "idiot" sense original, the other the "jester" sense.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The word has in mod.Eng. a much stronger sense than it had at an earlier period; it has now an implication of insulting contempt which does not in the same degree belong to any of its synonyms, or to the derivative <span class="foreign">foolish</span>. [OED]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Also used in Middle English for "sinner, rascal, impious person" (late 13c.). Meaning "jester, court clown" in English is attested c. 1300, though it is not always possible to tell whether the reference is to a professional entertainer counterfeiting mental weakness or an amusing lunatic, and the notion of the <span class="foreign">fool sage</span> whose sayings are ironically wise is also in English from c. 1300. The French word probably also got into English via its borrowing in the Scandinavian languages of the vikings (Old Norse <span class="foreign">fol</span>, Old Danish <span class="foreign">fool</span>, <span class="foreign">fol</span>). &#13; <blockquote>&#13; There is no foole to the olde foole ["Proverbs of John Heywood," 1546]</blockquote>&#13; To <span class="foreign">make a fool of</span> (someone) "cause to appear ridiculous" is from 1620s (<span class="foreign">make fool</span> "to deceive, make (someone) appear a fool" is from early 15c.). <span class="foreign">Feast of Fools</span> (early 14c., from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">festum stultorum</span>) was the burlesque festival celebrated in some churches on New Year's Day in medieval times. <span class="foreign">Fool's gold</span> "iron pyrite" is from 1829. <span class="foreign">Fool's paradise</span> "illusory state of happiness" is from mid-15c. <span class="foreign">Fool-trap</span> is from 1690s. <span class="foreign">Foolosopher</span>, a useful insult, is in a 1549 translation of Erasmus. <span class="foreign">Fool's ballocks</span> is described in OED as "an old name" for the green-winged orchid. <span class="foreign">Fool-killer</span> "imaginary personage invested with authority to put to death anybody notoriously guilty of great folly" is from 1851, American English.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; <b>Fool killer</b>, a great American myth imagined by editors, who feign that his or its services are greatly needed, and frequently alluded to as being "around" or "in town" when some special act of folly calls for castigation. Whether the fool-killer be an individual or an instrument cannot always be gathered from the dark phraseology in which he or it is alluded to; but the weight of authority would sanction the impersonal interpretation. [Walsh, "Handy-Book of Literary Curiosities," 1892]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fool">fool (v.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "to be foolish, act the fool," from <a href="/etym/s/fool" target="_blank">fool</a> (n.1). The transitive meaning "make a fool of" is recorded from 1590s. Sense of "beguile, cheat" is from 1640s. Also as a verb 16c.-17c. was <span class="foreign">foolify</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Fooled</span>; <a href="/etym/s/fooling" target="_blank">fooling</a>. <span class="foreign">Fool around</span> is 1875 in the sense of "pass time idly," 1970s in sense of "have sexual adventures."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fool">fool (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "sinful, wicked; lecherous" (a <span class="foreign">fool woman</span> (c. 1300) was "a prostitute"), from <a href="/etym/s/fool" target="_blank">fool</a> (n.1). Meaning "foolish, silly" is mid-13c. In modern use considered U.S. colloquial.&#13; &#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fool">fool (n.2)</a></dt><dd>type of custard dish, 1590s, of uncertain origin. The food also was called <span class="foreign">trifle</span>, which may be the source of the name (via verb and noun senses of <a href="/etym/s/fool" target="_blank">fool</a>). OED utterly rejects derivation from Old French <span class="foreign">fole</span> "a pressing."</dd></dl></div></div>
