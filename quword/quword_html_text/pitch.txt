                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pitch">pitch</a> 用力扔，抛，投球，球场，上下颠簸，倾斜</span><p>来自中古英语pikken,刺，插，词源同peck,pike.引申词义扔，抛，投球，球场，倾斜等诸多词义。插入字母t,比较bake,batch,piece,patch.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pitch">pitch</a> 沥青，柏油</span><p>来自古英语pic,沥青，柏油，来自拉丁语pix,沥青，柏油，来自PIE*pik,松脂，松香，来自 PIE*pei,脂肪，松脂，汁液，词源同fat,pine.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pitch">pitch (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"resinous substance, wood tar," late 12c., <span class="foreign">pich</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">pic</span> "pitch," from a Germanic borrowing (Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">pik</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">pik</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">pek</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">pek</span>, German <span class="foreign">Pech</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">bik</span>) of Latin <span class="foreign">pix</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">picis</span>) "pitch," which according to Watkins is from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*pik-</span> "pitch" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">pissa</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">pikis</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">piklu</span> "pitch"), but according to Pokorny this is from the same PIE root as <a href="/etym/s/pine" target="_blank">pine</a> (n.). The English word was applied to pine resins from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Pitch-black</span> is attested from 1590s; <span class="foreign">pitch-dark</span> from 1680s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pitch">pitch (v.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "to thrust in, fasten, settle," probably from an unrecorded Old English <span class="foreign">*piccean</span>, related to <a href="/etym/s/prick" target="_blank">prick</a> (v.). The original past tense was <span class="foreign">pight</span>. Sense of "set upright," as in <span class="foreign">pitch a tent</span> (late 13c.), is from notion of "driving in" the pegs. Meaning to incline forward and downward" is from 1510s. Meaning "throw (a ball)" evolved late 14c. from that of "hit the mark." Musical sense is from 1670s. Of ships, "to plunge" in the waves, 1620s. To <span class="foreign">pitch in</span> "work vigorously" is from 1847, perhaps from farm labor. Related: <span class="foreign">Pitched</span>; <span class="foreign">pitching</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pitch">pitch (n.1)</a></dt><dd>1520s, "something that is pitched," from <a href="/etym/s/pitch" target="_blank">pitch</a> (v.1). Meaning "act of throwing" is attested from 1833. Meaning "act of plunging headfirst" is from 1762; sense of "slope, degree, inclination" is from 1540s; musical sense is from 1590s; but the connection of these is obscure. <span class="foreign">Sales pitch</span> in the modern commercial advertising sense is from 1943, American English, perhaps from the baseball sense.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pitch">pitch (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to cover with pitch," Old English <span class="foreign">pician</span>, from the source of <a href="/etym/s/pitch" target="_blank">pitch</a> (n.2).</dd></dl></div></div>
