                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/husband">husband</a> 丈夫</span><p>来自古英语husbonda,一家之主，男性首领，来自hus,屋子，词源同 house,-bonda,居住者，耕作者，农夫，来自PIE*bheue,是，存在，建造，词源同be,build,boor.后引申词义丈夫，而wife也由古英语词义女人引申为妻子。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/husband">husband</a></dt><dd><b>husband: </b><span>[OE] The Anglo-Saxons used <i>wer </i>‘man’ (as in <i>werewolf</i>) for ‘husband’, and not until the late 13th century was the word <i>husband </i>drafted in for ‘male spouse’. This had originally meant ‘master of a household’, and was borrowed from Old Norse <i>húsbóndi</i>, a compound formed from <i>hús </i>‘house’ and <i>bóndi</i>. <i>Bóndi </i>in turn was a contraction of an earlier <i>bóandi</i>, <i>búandi </i>‘dweller’, a noun use of the present participle of <i>bóa</i>, <i>búa </i>‘dwell’, This was derived from the Germanic base *<i>bū</i>- ‘dwell’, which also produced English <i>be</i>, <i>boor</i>, <i>booth</i>, <i>bound </i>‘intending to go’, <i>bower</i>, <i>build</i>, <i>burly</i>, <i>byelaw</i>, <i>byre</i>, and the -<i>bour </i>of <i>neighbour</i>.<br><br>The ancient link between ‘dwelling in a place’ and ‘farming the land’ comes out in <i>husbandman </i>[14] and <i>husbandry </i>[14], reflecting a now obsolete sense of <i>husband</i>, ‘farmer’. The abbreviated form <i>hubby </i>dates from the 17th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/be" target="_blank">be</a>, <a href="/etym/s/boor" target="_blank">boor</a>, <a href="/etym/s/booth" target="_blank">booth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/bower" target="_blank">bower</a>, <a href="/etym/s/build" target="_blank">build</a>, <a href="/etym/s/byre" target="_blank">byre</a>, <a href="/etym/s/house" target="_blank">house</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/husband">husband (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">husbonda</span> "male head of a household," probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">husbondi</span> "master of the house," from <span class="foreign">hus</span> "house" (see <a href="/etym/s/house" target="_blank">house</a> (n.)) + <span class="foreign">bondi</span> "householder, dweller, freeholder, peasant," from <span class="foreign">buandi</span>, present participle of <span class="foreign">bua</span> "to dwell" (see <a href="/etym/s/bower" target="_blank">bower</a>). Beginning late 13c., replaced Old English <span class="foreign">wer</span> as "married man," companion of <span class="foreign">wif</span>, a sad loss for English poetry. Slang shortening <span class="foreign">hubby</span> first attested 1680s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/husband">husband (v.)</a></dt><dd>"manage thriftily," early 15c., from <a href="/etym/s/husband" target="_blank">husband</a> (n.) in an obsolete sense of "steward" (mid-15c.). Related: <span class="foreign">Husbanded</span>; <span class="foreign">husbanding</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
