                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/guess">guess</a> 猜测</span><p>词源同get, 得到。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/guess">guess</a></dt><dd><b>guess: </b><span>[13] In the earliest records we have of the verb <i>guess</i>, it is used for ‘take aim’. The modern sense ‘estimate’ did not emerge until the mid- 14th century. It seems to be of Scandinavian origin, and probably comes ultimately from the same base as produced <i>get </i>(Old Norse <i>geta </i>meant ‘guess’ as well as ‘get’, and the semantic progression hinted at by the intermediate ‘take aim’ is probably via ‘lock on to something in one’s sights’ to ‘fix on a particular figure’ – by implication, without exact calculation). <i>Guesstimate</i>, a blend of <i>guess </i>and <i>estimate</i>, is a US coinage of the 1930s.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/get" target="_blank">get</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/guess">guess (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">gessen</span> "to infer from observation, perceive, find out; form an opinion, judge, decide, discern; evaluate, estimate the number, importance, etc. of," perhaps from Scandinavian (compare Middle Danish <span class="foreign">gitse</span>, <span class="foreign">getze</span> "to guess," Old Norse <span class="foreign">geta</span> "guess, get"), or from or influenced by Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gessen</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">gissen</span> "to guess," all from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*getan</span> "to get" (see <a href="/etym/s/get" target="_blank">get</a> (v.)). The prehistoric sense evolution then would be from "get," to "take aim at," to "to estimate." Meaning "to hit upon the right answer" is from 1540s. Spelling with <a href="/etym/s/gu-" target="_blank">gu-</a> is late 16c., sometimes attributed to Caxton and his early experience as a printer in Bruges. Related: <span class="foreign">Guessed</span>; <span class="foreign">guessing</span>. <span class="foreign">Guessing game</span> attested from 1650s. To <span class="foreign">keep (someone) guessing</span> "keep him in a state of suspense" is from 1896, American English.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; [T]he legitimate, English sense of this word is <span class="foreign">to conjecture</span>; but with us, and especially in New England, it is constantly used in common conversation instead of <span class="foreign">to believe</span>, <span class="foreign">to suppose</span>, <span class="foreign">to think</span>, <span class="foreign">to imagine</span>, <span class="foreign">to fancy</span>. [Bartlett, "Dictionary of Americanisms," 1848]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/guess">guess (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "indiscriminate conclusion, guesswork, doubtful supposition," from <a href="/etym/s/guess" target="_blank">guess</a> (v.). Mid-15c. as "considered opinion." Verbal shrug phrase <span class="foreign">your guess is as good as mine</span> attested from 1902.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. <b>Guess</b> what? I'm going to dinner at Mrs. Combley's tonight. </dt> <dd>你猜怎么着？今晚我要去康伯利夫人家里赴宴。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. It's a fair <b>guess</b> to say that the damage will be extensive. </dt> <dd>说损失会很严重想必猜得不错。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. Editors and contributors are trying to second-<b>guess</b> the future. </dt> <dd>编辑们和撰稿者们在试图预测未来。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. Browning's prediction is no better than a wild <b>guess</b>. </dt> <dd>勃朗宁的预测不过是妄加猜测而已。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5.
