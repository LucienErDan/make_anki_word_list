                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fine">fine</a> 好的，精细的，罚金</span><p>来自拉丁语finis, 终结，边界，词源同finish. 引申义接近极点的，最高程度的，精细的，好的。罚金义来自终结债务，解决争端等。词义演变参照sublime.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fine">fine</a></dt><dd><b>fine: </b><span>[12] Both the adjective and the noun <i>fine </i>have come a very long way since their beginnings in Latin <i>finis </i>‘end’. The etymological sense of the adjective is ‘finished’ – hence, ‘of high quality’. It comes via Old French <i>fin </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>fīnus</i>, an adjective formed from the Latin verb <i>fīnīre </i>‘limit, complete’ (source of English <i>finish</i>). (A derivative of *<i>finus </i>was the noun *<i>finitia</i>, from which ultimately English gets <i>finesse </i>[15].) The noun <i>fine </i>also comes from an Old French <i>fin</i>, this time a noun descended directly from Latin <i>fīnis</i>.<br><br>In medieval times this was used for ‘money to be paid at the completion of legal proceedings’ – hence the present-day sense ‘payment imposed as a punishment’. From the same ultimate source, but reflecting different aspects of it, come <i>confine </i>[16] and <i>define </i>[14] (‘limitation’) and <i>refine </i>[16] (‘high quality’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/confine" target="_blank">confine</a>, <a href="/etym/s/define" target="_blank">define</a>, <a href="/etym/s/final" target="_blank">final</a>, <a href="/etym/s/finance" target="_blank">finance</a>, <a href="/etym/s/finesse" target="_blank">finesse</a>, <a href="/etym/s/finish" target="_blank">finish</a>, <a href="/etym/s/refine" target="_blank">refine</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fine">fine (adj.)</a></dt><dd>mid-13c., "unblemished, refined, pure, free of impurities," also "of high quality, choice," from Old French <span class="foreign">fin</span> "perfected, of highest quality" (12c.), a back-formation from <span class="foreign">finire</span> or else from Latin <span class="foreign">finis</span> "that which divides, a boundary, limit, border, end" (see <a href="/etym/s/finish" target="_blank">finish</a> (v.)); hence "acme, peak, height," as in <span class="foreign">finis boni</span> "the highest good." The English word is from c. 1300 as "rich, valuable, costly;" also in a moral sense "true, genuine; faithful, constant." From late 14c. as "expertly fashioned, well or skillfully made," also, of cloth, "delicately wrought." Of weapons or edges, "sharp" from c. 1400. In reference to quality of gold and silver, late 15c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In French, the main meaning remains "delicate, intricately skillful;" in English since c. 1300 <span class="foreign">fine</span> has been also a general broad expression of admiration or approval, the equivalent of French <span class="foreign">beau</span> (as in <span class="foreign">fine arts</span>, 1767, translating French <span class="foreign">beaux-arts</span>). Related: <span class="foreign">Finer</span>; <span class="foreign">finest</span>. <span class="foreign">Fine print</span> is from 1861 as "type small and close-set;" by 1934 in the extended sense "qualifications and limitations of a deal."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fine">fine (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "termination, end; end of life," from Old French <span class="foreign">fin</span> "end, limit, boundary; death; fee, payment, finance, money" (10c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">finis</span> "end" (see <a href="/etym/s/finish" target="_blank">finish</a> (v.)), in Medieval Latin also "payment in settlement, fine or tax."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Modern meaning "exaction of money payment for an offense or dereliction" is via sense of "sum of money paid for exemption from punishment or to compensate for injury" (mid-14c., from the same sense in Anglo-French, late 13c.) and from phrases such as <span class="foreign">to make fine</span> "make one's peace, settle a matter" (c. 1300). Meaning "sum of money imposed as penalty for some offense" is first recorded 1520s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fine">fine (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 13c., "pay as a ransom or penalty," from <a href="/etym/s/fine" target="_blank">fine</a> (n.). Inverted meaning "to punish by pecuniary penalty" is from 1550s. Related: <span class="foreign">Fined</span>; <span class="foreign">fining</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
