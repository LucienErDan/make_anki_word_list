                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wine">wine</a></dt><dd><b>wine: </b><span>[OE] <i>Wine </i>comes from Latin <i>vīnum</i>. This was borrowed into prehistoric Germanic as *<i>wīnam</i>, which subsequently evolved into German <i>wein</i>, Dutch <i>wijn</i>, Swedish and Danish <i>vin</i>, and English <i>wine</i>. It also gave French <i>vin </i>and Italian and Spanish <i>vino</i>, and was extensively acquired by other Indo-European languages, including Russian and Serbo-Croat <i>vino</i>, Polish <i>wino</i>, Lithuanian <i>vynas</i>, and Welsh <i>gwin</i>.<br><br>The Latin word itself came from an ancient Mediterranean source, possibly non- Indo-European, which also produced Greek <i>oínos </i>‘wine’ (source of English <i>oenology </i>[19]), Albanian <i>vēne </i>‘wine’, and Armenian <i>gini </i>‘wine’. The same ancestral term also fed into the Semitic languages, giving Arabic <i>wain</i>, Hebrew <i>yayin</i>, and Assyrian <i>īnn </i>‘wine’.<br><br>Latin <i>vīnum </i>additionally gave English <i>vintage </i>and <i>vintner</i>, and its derivative <i>vīnea </i>‘vineyard, vine’ produced English <i>vine </i>[13].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/oenology" target="_blank">oenology</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vine" target="_blank">vine</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vintage" target="_blank">vintage</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vintner" target="_blank">vintner</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wine">wine (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">win</span> "wine," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*winam</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Old Frisian, Old High German <span class="foreign">win</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vin</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">wijn</span>, German <span class="foreign">Wein</span>), an early borrowing from Latin <span class="foreign">vinum</span> "wine," from PIE <span class="foreign">*woin-o-</span>, related to words for "wine" in Greek (<span class="foreign">oinos</span>), Armenian, Hittite, and non-Indo-European Georgian and West Semitic (Arabic <span class="foreign">wain</span>, Hebrew <span class="foreign">yayin</span>), probably from a lost Mediterranean language word <span class="foreign">*win-/*woin-</span> "wine."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Also from Latin <span class="foreign">vinum</span> are Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">vino</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">wino</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">vino</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vynas</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">gwin</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">fin</span>, Gaelic <span class="foreign">fion</span>. Essentially the same word as <a href="/etym/s/vine" target="_blank">vine</a> (q.v.). <span class="foreign">Wine snob</span> is recorded from 1951. <span class="foreign">Wine cellar</span> is from late 14c. <span class="foreign">Wine-cooler</span> is 1815 as "vessel in which bottled wine is kept cool;" by 1977 as a type of wine-based beverage.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/wine">wine (v.)</a></dt><dd>"entertain with wine," 1862, from <a href="/etym/s/wine" target="_blank">wine</a> (n.). Earlier "expend in drinking wine" (1620s). Related: <span class="foreign">Wined</span>; <span class="foreign">wining</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
