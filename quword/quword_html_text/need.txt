            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/need">need</a> 需要</span><p>词源不详。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/need">need</a></dt><dd><b>need: </b><span>[OE] <i>Need </i>is a widespread Germanic noun, with relatives also in German <i>not</i>, Dutch <i>nood</i>, Swedish <i>nöd</i>, and Danish <i>nød</i>. It comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>nauthiz</i>, whose non- Germanic relatives, such as Old Prussian <i>nautin </i>‘necessity, distress’ and Czech <i>nyti </i>‘languish’, reveal its darker past, in which the accent was on ‘distress’ and ‘straitened circumstances’ rather than just the desirability of having something (these connotations survive in German <i>not</i>, which means ‘misery, danger, emergency’ as well as ‘need’).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/need">need (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">nied</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">ned</span> (Mercian) "necessity, compulsion, duty; hardship, distress; errand, business," originally "violence, force," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*nauthiz</span>/<span class="foreign">*naudiz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">nod</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">nauðr</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ned</span>, Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">nood</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">not</span>, German <span class="foreign">Not</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">nauþs</span> "need"), probably cognate with Old Prussian <span class="foreign">nautin</span> "need," and perhaps with Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">nazda</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">nuzda</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">nędza</span> "misery, distress," from PIE <span class="foreign">*nau-</span> (1) "death, to be exhausted" (see <a href="/etym/s/narwhal" target="_blank">narwhal</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The more common Old English word for "need, necessity, want" was <span class="foreign">ðearf</span>, but they were connected via a notion of "trouble, pain," and the two formed a compound, <span class="foreign">niedðearf</span> "need, necessity, compulsion, thing needed." <span class="foreign">Nied</span> also might have been influenced by Old English <span class="foreign">neod</span> "desire, longing," which often was spelled the same. Common in Old English compounds, such as <span class="foreign">niedfaru</span> "compulsory journey," a euphemism for "death;" <span class="foreign">niedhæmed</span> "rape," the second element being an Old English word meaning "sexual intercourse;" <span class="foreign">niedling</span> "slave." Meaning "extreme poverty, destitution" is from c. 1200.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/need">need (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">neodian</span> "be necessary, be required (for some purpose); require, have need of," from the same root as <a href="/etym/s/need" target="_blank">need</a> (n.). Meaning "to be under obligation (to do something)" is from late 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Needed</span>; <span class="foreign">needing</span>. The adjectival phrase <span class="foreign">need-to-know</span> is attested from 1952. Dismissive phrase <span class="foreign">who needs it?</span>, popular from c. 1960, is a translated Yiddishism.</dd></dl></div></div>
