                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/martyr">martyr</a> 殉道者</span><p>来自拉丁语martyr,来自希腊语martys,见证者，-r,所有格后缀，来自PIE*smer,mer,记住，记忆，词源同memory,remember.后引申词义殉道的人，为教而牺牲的人。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/martyr">martyr</a></dt><dd><b>martyr: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, a <i>martyr </i>is a ‘witness’ – that was the original meaning of Greek <i>mártur</i>, which came ultimately from Indo-European *<i>mer </i>‘remember’ (source of English <i>memory</i>, <i>mourn</i>, <i>remember</i>, etc). In Christian usage, the notion of someone dying as a ‘witness’ to their faith led to the application of <i>mártur </i>to ‘martyr’, and it was in this sense that it passed via Latin <i>martyr </i>into Old English.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/memory" target="_blank">memory</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mourn" target="_blank">mourn</a>, <a href="/etym/s/remember" target="_blank">remember</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/martyr">martyr (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">martyr</span>, from Late Latin <span class="foreign">martyr</span>, from Doric Greek <span class="foreign">martyr</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">martys</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">martyros</span>), in Christian use "martyr," literally "witness," probably related to <span class="foreign">mermera</span> "care, trouble," from <span class="foreign">mermairein</span> "be anxious or thoughtful," from PIE <span class="foreign">*(s)mrtu-</span> (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">smarati</span> "remember," Latin <span class="foreign">memor</span> "mindful;" see <a href="/etym/s/memory" target="_blank">memory</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Adopted directly into most Germanic languages, but Norse substituted native formation <span class="foreign">pislarvattr</span>, literally "torture-witness." General sense of "constant sufferer" is from 1550s. <span class="foreign">Martyr complex</span> "exaggerated desire for self-sacrifice" is attested from 1920.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/martyr">martyr (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">martyrian</span>, from <span class="foreign">martyr</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/martyr" target="_blank">martyr</a> (n.)). Middle English also had a verb <span class="foreign">martyrize</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
