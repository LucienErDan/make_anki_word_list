                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/race">race</a> 比赛，竞赛，人种，种族</span><p>1.来自古英语 raes,跑动，跳，来自 Proto-Germanic*res,置换自 PIE*ers,移动，运动，词源同 errand.引申词义比赛，竞赛。 2.人种，种族，来自中古法语 razza,种族，世系，家族，可能来自拉丁语 radix,根，词源同 root,radish.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/race">race</a></dt><dd><b>race: </b><span>For such a common word – or rather two words, for ‘people, population’ [16] and ‘speed competition’ [13] are unrelated – surprisingly little is known about the origins of <i>race</i>. The former comes via French from Italian <i>razza</i>, but the antecedents of <i>razza </i>are obscure. The ‘running’ <i>race </i>originally meant ‘rush’, and was borrowed from Old Norse <i>rás </i>‘rush, running, race’ – again, of unknown origin.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/race">race (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"act of running," c. 1300, from Old Norse <span class="foreign">ras</span> "running, rush (of water)," cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">ræs</span> "a running, a rush, a leap, jump; a storming, an attack;" or else a survival of the Old English word with spelling influenced by the Old Norse one. The Norse and Old English words are from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*res-</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">rasen</span> "to rave, rage," German <span class="foreign">rasen</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">raesettan</span> "to rage" (of fire)), from a variant form of PIE <span class="foreign">*ers-</span> (1) "be in motion" (see <a href="/etym/s/err" target="_blank">err</a>). Originally a northern word, it became general in English c. 1550. Meaning "act of running" is from early 14c. Meaning "contest of speed" first recorded 1510s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/race">race (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"people of common descent," a word from the 16th century, from Middle French <span class="foreign">race</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">razza</span> "race, breed, lineage, family" (16c.), possibly from Italian <span class="foreign">razza</span>, of unknown origin (cognate with Spanish and Portuguese <span class="foreign">raza</span>). Etymologists say no connection with Latin <span class="foreign">radix</span> "root," though they admit this might have influenced the "tribe, nation" sense.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Original senses in English included "wines with characteristic flavor" (1520), "group of people with common occupation" (c. 1500), and "generation" (1540s). Meaning "tribe, nation, or people regarded as of common stock" is by 1560s. Modern meaning of "one of the great divisions of mankind based on physical peculiarities" is from 1774 (though as OED points out, even among anthropologists there never has been an accepted classification of these).<blockquote>Just being a Negro doesn't qualify you to understand the race situation any more than being sick makes you an expert on medicine. [Dick Gregory, 1964]</blockquote>&#13; In mid-20c. U.S. music catalogues, "Negro." Klein suggests these derive from Arabic <span class="foreign">ra's</span> "head, beginning, origin" (compare Hebrew <span class="foreign">rosh</span>). Old English <span class="foreign">þeode</span> meant both "race, folk, nation" and "language;" as a verb, <span class="foreign">geþeodan</span>, it meant "to unite, to join."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/race">race (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">rasen</span> "to rush," from a Scandinavian source akin to the source of <a href="/etym/s/race" target="_blank">race</a> (n.1), reinforced by the noun in English and by Old English cognate <span class="foreign">ræsan</span> "to rush headlong, hasten, enter rashly." Meaning "run swiftly" is from 1757. Meaning "run in competition against" is from 1809. Transitive sense of "cause to run" is from 1860. In reference to an engine, etc., "run with uncontrolled speed," from 1862. Related: <span class="foreign">Raced</span>; <span class="foreign">racing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/race">race (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"strong current of water," c. 1300, originally any forward movement or swift running, but especially of water, from Old Norse <span class="foreign">ras</span> "a rushing" (see <a href="/etym/s/race" target="_blank">race</a> (n.1)). Via Norman French the word entered French as <span class="foreign">ras</span>, which might have given English <span class="foreign">race</span> its specialized meaning of "channel of a stream" (especially an artificial one to a mill), which is recorded in English from 1560s.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. Before the <b>race</b>, he is fine. But afterwards he is worn out. </dt> <dd>赛前他状态很好，赛后就筋疲力尽了。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. Duke was soundly defeated in this month's Louisiana governor's <b>race</b>. </dt> <dd>杜克在本月的路易斯安那州长竞选中大败而归。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. Few jockeys continue <b>race</b>-riding beyond the age of 40. </dt> <dd>很少有职业赛马骑师超过40岁仍继续参加赛马的。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. It was peopled by a fiercely independent <b>race</b> of peace-loving Buddhists. </dt> <dd>那里住着一个与世隔绝的种族，人们都是爱好和平的佛教徒。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5.
