                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/proud">proud</a> 自豪的，得意的，自负的</span><p>来自古英语prud,卓越的，壮观的，傲慢的，高傲的，可能来自古法语prud,勇敢的，英勇的，缩写自拉丁语prodesse,有用的，起作用的，来自pro-,向前，在前，代替，esse,存在，成为，词源同essence.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/proud">proud</a></dt><dd><b>proud: </b><span>[OE] <i>Proud </i>was borrowed in the 10th century from Old French <i>prud</i>. This came from a Vulgar Latin *<i>prōdis</i>, a derivative of Latin <i>prōdesse </i>‘be beneficial’, which was a compound formed from <i>prōd</i>-, a variant of <i>prō</i>- ‘for’, and <i>esse </i>‘be’. The Old French adjective meant ‘good, brave’, and it is thought that the sense ‘having a high opinion of oneself’, which does not occur in Old French but is the earliest recorded in English, may reflect what the Anglo- Saxons thought of Norman nobles who referred to themselves as <i>prud barun </i>or <i>prud chevalier</i>.<br><br>A later form of Old French <i>prud </i>or <i>prod </i>was <i>prou</i>, whose derivative <i>proesce </i>‘bravery’ passed into English as <i>prowess </i>[13]; and English is also indebted to <i>prud </i>for <i>prude</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pride" target="_blank">pride</a>, <a href="/etym/s/prowess" target="_blank">prowess</a>, <a href="/etym/s/prude" target="_blank">prude</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/proud">proud (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late Old English <span class="foreign">prud</span>, <span class="foreign">prute</span> "excellent, splendid; arrogant, haughty," probably from Old French <span class="foreign">prud</span>, oblique case of adjective <span class="foreign">prouz</span> "brave, valiant" (11c., Modern French <span class="foreign">preux</span>; compare <span class="foreign">prud'homme</span> "brave man"), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">prode</span> "advantageous, profitable" (source also of Italian <span class="foreign">prode</span> "valiant"), a back-formation from Latin <span class="foreign">prodesse</span> "be useful," from <span class="foreign">pro-</span> "before, for, instead of" (see <a href="/etym/s/pro-" target="_blank">pro-</a>) + <span class="foreign">esse</span> "to be" (see <a href="/etym/s/essence" target="_blank">essence</a>). Also see <a href="/etym/s/pride" target="_blank">pride</a> (n.), <a href="/etym/s/prowess" target="_blank">prowess</a>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "elated by some act, fact, or thing" is from mid-13c. To <span class="foreign">do (someone) proud</span> attested by 1819. Related: <span class="foreign">Proudness</span>. "The <span class="foreign">-d-</span> in <span class="foreign">prodesse</span> is probably due to the influence of forms like <span class="foreign">red-eo-</span>, 'I go back,' <span class="foreign">red-imo-</span> 'I buy back,' etc." [OED]. The Old English form with <span class="foreign">-te</span> probably is from or influenced by <span class="foreign">pride</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sense of "have a high opinion of oneself," not found in Old French, might reflect the Anglo-Saxons' opinion of the Norman knights who called themselves "<span class="foreign">proud</span>." Old Norse <span class="foreign">pruðr</span>, probably from the same French source, had only the sense "brave, gallant, magnificent, stately" (compare Icelandic <span class="foreign">pruður</span>, Middle Swedish <span class="foreign">prudh</span>, Middle Danish <span class="foreign">prud</span>). Likewise a group of "pride" words in the Romance languages -- such as French <span class="foreign">orgueil</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">orgoglio</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">orgullo</span> -- are borrowings from Germanic, where they had positive senses (Old High German <span class="foreign">urgol</span> "distinguished").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Most Indo-European languages use the same word for "proud" in its good and bad senses, but in many the bad sense seems to be the earlier one. The usual way to form the word is by some compound of terms for "over" or "high" and words for "heart," "mood," "thought," or "appearance;" such as Greek <span class="foreign">hyperephanos</span>, literally "over-appearing;" Gothic <span class="foreign">hauhþuhts</span>, literally "high-conscience." Old English had <span class="foreign">ofermodig</span> "over-moody" ("mood" in Anglo-Saxon was a much more potent word than presently) and <span class="foreign">heahheort</span> "high-heart." Words for "proud" in other Indo-European languages sometimes reflect a physical sense of being swollen or puffed up; such as Welsh <span class="foreign">balch</span>, probably from a root meaning "to swell," and Modern Greek <span class="foreign">kamari</span>, from ancient Greek <span class="foreign">kamarou</span> "furnish with a vault or arched cover," with a sense evolution via "make an arch," to "puff out the chest," to "be puffed up" (compare English slang <a href="/etym/s/chesty" target="_blank">chesty</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
