                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/find">find</a> 找到</span><p>来自PIE*pent, 走，来自PIE*pod的鼻音形式，脚，词源同foot.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/find">find</a></dt><dd><b>find: </b><span>[OE] <i>Find </i>is a widespread Germanic verb, with relations in German (<i>finden</i>), Dutch (<i>vinden</i>), Swedish (<i>finna</i>), and Danish (<i>finde</i>). Further back in time, however, its ancestry is disputed. Some have connected it with various words for ‘path, way’ in Indo-European languages, such as Sanskrit <i>panthās </i>and Russian <i>put’</i>, and with related forms denoting ‘go, journey’, like Old Saxon <i>fāthi </i>‘going’ and Old High German <i>fendeo </i>‘walker’; others have suggested a link with Latin <i>petere </i>‘seek’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/find">find (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">findan</span> "come upon, meet with; discover; obtain by search or study" (class III strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">fand</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">funden</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*finthan</span> "to come upon, discover" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">findan</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">finda</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">finna</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vinden</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">findan</span>, German <span class="foreign">finden</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">finþan</span>), originally "to come upon."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Germanic word is from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pent-</span> "to tread, go" (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">fendeo</span> "pedestrian;" Sanskrit <span class="foreign">panthah</span> "path, way;" Avestan <span class="foreign">panta</span> "way;" Greek <span class="foreign">pontos</span> "open sea," <span class="foreign">patein</span> "to tread, walk;" Latin <span class="foreign">pons</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">pontis</span>) "bridge;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">poti</span> "path," <span class="foreign">peta</span> "heel;" Russian <span class="foreign">put'</span> "path, way").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To <span class="foreign">find out</span> "to discover by scrutiny" is from 1550s (Middle English had a verb, <span class="foreign">outfinden</span>, c. 1300).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/find">find (n.)</a></dt><dd>"person or thing discovered, discovery of something valuable," 1825, from <a href="/etym/s/find" target="_blank">find</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
