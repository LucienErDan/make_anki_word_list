                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/small">small</a> 小的，小号的，年幼的</span><p>来自古英语 smael,小的，狭窄的，苗条的，来自 Proto-Germanic*smalaz,小的，来自 PIE*smal, 小的。引申诸相关比喻义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/small">small</a></dt><dd><b>small: </b><span>[OE] <i>Small </i>comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>smalaz</i>, which in turn probably goes back ultimately to *<i>smel</i>-, a variant of the Indo- European base *<i>mel</i>- ‘grind’ (source of English <i>meal</i>, <i>mill</i>, etc). Etymologically, therefore, it could well denote ‘ground up into little bits’. Its Germanic relatives, such as German <i>schmal </i>and Dutch <i>smal</i>, have become specialized in meaning to ‘narrow’, but while English did start off down this semantic path, it has long since abandoned it.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/meal" target="_blank">meal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mill" target="_blank">mill</a>, <a href="/etym/s/molar" target="_blank">molar</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/small">small (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">smæl</span> "thin, slender, narrow; fine," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*smal-</span> "small animal; small" (cognates: Old Saxon, Danish, Swedish, Middle Dutch, Dutch, Old High German <span class="foreign">smal</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">smel</span>, German <span class="foreign">schmal</span> "narrow, slender," Gothic <span class="foreign">smalista</span> "smallest," Old Norse <span class="foreign">smali</span> "small cattle, sheep"), perhaps from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)melo-</span> "smaller animal" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">melon</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">mil</span> "a small animal;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">malu</span> "bad"). Original sense of "narrow" now almost obsolete, except in reference to waistline and intestines.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; My sister ... is as white as a lilly, and as small as a wand. [Shakespeare, "Two Gentlemen of Verona," 1591]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Sense of "not large, of little size" developed in Old English. Of children, "young," from mid-13c. Meaning "inferior in degree or amount" is from late 13c. Meaning "trivial, unimportant" is from mid-14c. Sense of "having little property or trade" is from 1746. That of "characterized by littleness of mind or spirit, base, low, mean" is from 1824. As an adverb by late 14c.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Small fry</span>, first recorded 1690s of little fish, 1885 of insignificant people. <span class="foreign">Small potatoes</span> "no great matter" first attested 1924; <span class="foreign">small change</span> "something of little value" is from 1902; <span class="foreign">small talk</span> "chit-chat, trifling conversation" (1751) first recorded in Chesterfield's "Letters." <span class="foreign">Small world</span> as a comment upon an unexpected meeting of acquaintances is recorded from 1895. <span class="foreign">Small-arms</span>, indicating those capable of being carried in the hand (contrasted to <span class="foreign">ordnance</span>) is recorded from 1710.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/small">small (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., "small person or animal," from <a href="/etym/s/small" target="_blank">small</a> (adj.). From c. 1300 as "persons of low rank" (opposed to <span class="foreign">great</span>); late 15c. as "the small part" of something (such as <span class="foreign">small of the back</span>, 1530s).</dd></dl></div></div>
