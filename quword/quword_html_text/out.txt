            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/out">out</a> 向外</span><p>来自古英语ut,向外，来自PIE*ud,向上，向外，离开，词源同utter.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/out">out</a></dt><dd><b>out: </b><span>[OE] <i>Out </i>is a widespread Germanic adverb (German <i>aus</i>, Dutch <i>uit</i>, Swedish <i>ut</i>, and Danish <i>ud </i>are its first cousins) which also has a relative on the far side of the Indo-European language area, Sanskrit <i>ud</i>- ‘out’. Its former comparative form still survives in <i>utter </i>‘complete’, and <i>utmost </i>and the verb <i>utter </i>are also closely related.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/utmost" target="_blank">utmost</a>, <a href="/etym/s/utter" target="_blank">utter</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/out">out (adv.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ut</span> "out, without, outside," common Germanic (Old Norse, Old Frisian, Old Saxon, Gothic <span class="foreign">ut</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">uut</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">uit</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">uz</span>, German <span class="foreign">aus</span>), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ud-</span> "up, out, up away" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">ut</span> "up, out," <span class="foreign">uttarah</span> "higher, upper, later, northern;" Avestan <span class="foreign">uz-</span> "up, out," Old Irish <span class="foreign">ud-</span> "out," Latin <span class="foreign">usque</span> "all the way to, without interruption," Greek <span class="foreign">hysteros</span> "the latter," Russian <span class="foreign">vy-</span> "out"). Meaning "into public notice" is from 1540s. As an adjective from c. 1200. Meaning "unconscious" is attested from 1898, originally in boxing. Sense of "not popular or modern" is from 1966. As a preposition from mid-13c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense in baseball (1860) was earlier in cricket (1746). Adverbial phrase <span class="foreign">out-and-out</span> "thoroughly" is attested from early 14c.; adjective usage is attested from 1813; <span class="foreign">out-of-the-way</span> (adj.) "remote, secluded" is attested from late 15c. <span class="foreign">Out-of-towner</span> "one not from a certain place" is from 1911. Shakespeare's <span class="foreign">It out-herods Herod</span> ("Hamlet") reflects Herod as stock braggart and bully in old religious drama and was widely imitated 19c. <span class="foreign">Out to lunch</span> "insane" is student slang from 1955; <span class="foreign">out of this world</span> "excellent" is from 1938; <span class="foreign">out of sight</span> "excellent, superior" is from 1891.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/out">out (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">utian</span> "expel, put out" (see <a href="/etym/s/out" target="_blank">out</a> (adv.)); used in many senses over the years. Meaning "to expose as a closet homosexual" is first recorded 1990 (as an adjective meaning "openly avowing one's homosexuality" it dates from 1970s; see <a href="/etym/s/closet" target="_blank">closet</a>); sense of "disclose to public view, reveal, make known" has been present since mid-14c.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Eufrosyne preyde Þat god schulde not outen hire to nowiht. [Legendary of St. Euphrosyne, c. 1350]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Related: <span class="foreign">Outed</span>; <span class="foreign">outing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/out">out (n.)</a></dt><dd>1620s, "a being out" (of something), from <a href="/etym/s/out" target="_blank">out</a> (adv.). From 1860 in baseball sense; from 1919 as "means of escape; alibi."</dd></dl></div></div>
