                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/side">side</a> 边，侧，面，方面</span><p>来自古英语 side,边，侧翼，腹部，来自 Proto-Germanic*sido,边，侧，来自 PIE*sei,投掷，发 送，悬垂，垂落。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/side">side</a></dt><dd><b>side: </b><span>[OE] The etymological meaning of <i>side </i>appears to be the ‘long’ surface of something (as opposed to the <i>ends </i>or the <i>top </i>or <i>bottom</i>, which are the ‘shorter’ or ‘narrower’ surfaces). The word goes back, together with German <i>seite</i>, Dutch <i>zijde</i>, Swedish <i>sida</i>, and Danish <i>side</i>, to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>sīthō</i>, which was probably derived from the adjective *<i>sīthaz </i>‘long, deep, low’ (source of Swedish <i>sid </i>‘long’).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/side">side (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">side</span> "flanks of a person, the long part or aspect of anything," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sithon</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sida</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">siða</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">side</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">sida</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">side</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zidje</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sita</span>, German <span class="foreign">Seite</span>), from adjective <span class="foreign">*sithas</span> "long" (source of Old English <span class="foreign">sid</span> "long, broad, spacious," Old Norse <span class="foreign">siðr</span> "long, hanging down"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*se-</span> "long, late" (see <a href="/etym/s/soiree" target="_blank">soiree</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Original sense preserved in <a href="/etym/s/countryside" target="_blank">countryside</a>. Figurative sense of "position or attitude of a person or set of persons in relation to another" (as in <span class="foreign">choosing sides</span>) first recorded mid-13c. Meaning "one of the parties in a transaction" is from late 14c.; sense in a sporting contest or game is from 1690s. Meaning "music on one side of a phonograph record" is first attested 1936. Phrase <span class="foreign">side by side</span> "close together and abreast" is recorded from c. 1200. <span class="foreign">Side-splitting</span> "affecting with compulsive laughter" is attested by 1825.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/side">side (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 15c., "to cut into sides" (of meat), from <a href="/etym/s/side" target="_blank">side</a> (n.). Meaning "to support one of the parties in a discussion, dispute, etc.," is first attested 1590s, from <a href="/etym/s/side" target="_blank">side</a> (n.) in the figurative sense; earlier to <span class="foreign">hold sides</span> (late 15c.). Related: <span class="foreign">Sided</span>; <span class="foreign">siding</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/side">side (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/side" target="_blank">side</a> (n.).</dd></dl></div></div>
