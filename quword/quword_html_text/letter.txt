            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/letter">letter</a> 信，字母</span><p>来自拉丁语litera,字母，文字，词源同literal.引申词义信件。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/letter">letter</a></dt><dd><b>letter: </b><span>[13] The distant ancestry of the word <i>letter </i>has never been satisfactorily explained. One possible candidate as a source that has been put forward is Greek <i>diphthérai </i>‘writing tablets’. But the earliest precursor that can be positively identified is Latin <i>littera</i>. This meant ‘alphabetic symbol’, or in the plural ‘document’ and ‘epistle’. English acquired it via Old French <i>lettre</i>. Also from Latin <i>littera </i>are English <i>literature </i>and <i>obliterate </i>[16], which means etymologically ‘remove letters’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/literature" target="_blank">literature</a>, <a href="/etym/s/obliterate" target="_blank">obliterate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/letter">letter (n.1)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "graphic symbol, alphabetic sign, written character," from Old French <span class="foreign">letre</span> (10c., Modern French <span class="foreign">lettre</span>) "character, letter; missive, note," in plural, "literature, writing, learning," from Latin <span class="foreign">littera</span> (also <span class="foreign">litera</span>) "letter of the alphabet," of uncertain origin, perhaps via Etruscan from Greek <span class="foreign">diphthera</span> "tablet," with change of <span class="foreign">d-</span> to <span class="foreign">l-</span> as in <a href="/etym/s/lachrymose" target="_blank">lachrymose</a>. In this sense it replaced Old English <span class="foreign">bocstæf</span>, literally "book staff" (compare German <span class="foreign">Buchstabe</span> "letter, character," from Old High German <span class="foreign">buohstab</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bok-staba-m</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Latin <span class="foreign">littera</span> also meant "a writing, document, record," and in &#13; plural <span class="foreign">litteræ</span> "a letter, epistle," a sense first attested in English early 13c., replacing Old English <span class="foreign">ærendgewrit</span>, literally "errand-writing." The Latin plural also meant "literature, books," and figuratively "learning, liberal education, schooling" (see <a href="/etym/s/letters" target="_blank">letters</a>). School <span class="foreign">letter</span> in sports, attested by 1908, were said to have been first awarded by University of Chicago football coach Amos Alonzo Stagg. Expression <span class="foreign">to the letter</span> "precisely" is from 1520s (earlier as <span class="foreign">after the letter</span>). <span class="foreign">Letter-perfect</span> is from 1845, originally in theater jargon, in reference to an actor knowing the lines exactly. <span class="foreign">Letter-press</span>, in reference to matter printed from relief surfaces, is from 1840.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/letter">letter (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to write in letters," 1660s, from <a href="/etym/s/letter" target="_blank">letter</a> (n.1). Earlier it meant "to instruct" (mid-15c.). Related: <a href="/etym/s/Lettered" target="_blank">Lettered</a>; <a href="/etym/s/lettering" target="_blank">lettering</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/letter">letter (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"one who lets" in any sense, c. 1400, agent noun from <a href="/etym/s/let" target="_blank">let</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
