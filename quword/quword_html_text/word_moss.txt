                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/moss">moss</a> 苔藓，地衣</span><p>来自古英语mos,沼泽，泥沼，来自PIE*meus,湿气，水气，词源同moist,must.引申词义苔藓，地衣。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/moss">moss</a></dt><dd><b>moss: </b><span>[OE] The prehistoric Germanic ancestor of <i>moss </i>was *<i>musam</i>. This had two distinct meanings: ‘swamp’ and ‘moss’. It is not altogether clear which was primary, but it seems more probable than not that ‘moss’ (a plant which frequents damp places) was derived from ‘swamp’. The only meaning recorded for its Old English descendant <i>mos </i>was ‘swamp’ (which survives in place-names), but no doubt ‘moss’ (not evidenced before the 14th century) was current too.<br><br>Words from the same ultimate source to have found their way into English include <i>mire </i>[14] (borrowed from Old Norse <i>mýrr </i>‘swamp’), <i>mousse </i>[19] (borrowed from French, which got it from Middle Low German <i>mos </i>‘moss’), and <i>litmus </i>[16] (whose Old Norse source <i>litmosi </i>meant literally ‘dye-moss’ – litmus is a dye extracted from lichens).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/litmus" target="_blank">litmus</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mire" target="_blank">mire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mousse" target="_blank">mousse</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/moss">moss (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">meos</span> "moss," related to <span class="foreign">mos</span> "bog," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*musan</span> (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">mios</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">mos</span>, German <span class="foreign">Moos</span>), also in part from Old Norse <span class="foreign">mosi</span> "moss, bog," and Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">mossa</span> "moss," from the same Germanic source, from PIE <span class="foreign">*meus-</span> "damp," with derivatives referring to swamps and swamp vegetation (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">muscus</span> "moss," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">musai</span> "mold, mildew," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">muchu</span> "moss").&#13; <blockquote>Selden Moseþ þe Marbelston þat men ofte treden. ["Piers Plowman," 1362]</blockquote>&#13; All the Germanic languages have the word in both senses, which is natural because moss is the characteristic plant of boggy places. It is impossible to say which sense is original. Scott (1805) revived 17c. <span class="foreign">moss-trooper</span> "freebooter infesting Scottish border marshes."</dd></dl></div></div>
