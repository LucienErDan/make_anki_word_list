                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/regular">regular</a> 规则的，惯常的</span><p>来自拉丁语 regula,规则，来自 regere,管理，统治，来自 PIE*reg,拉直，词源同 correct,right,regal.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/regular">regular</a></dt><dd><b>regular: </b><span>[14] <i>Regular </i>‘according to a rule’ is the most instantly recognizable English descendant of Latin <i>rēgula </i>‘rule’ (others include <i>rail </i>‘bar’ and <i>rule</i>). It goes back ultimately to the same Indo-European base as produced Latin <i>regere </i>‘rule’ (source of English <i>rector</i>, <i>regent</i>, etc) and <i>rēx </i>‘king’ (source of English <i>regal</i>, <i>royal</i>, etc). From it was derived the late Latin verb <i>rēgulāre</i>, which has given English <i>regulate </i>[17], and may also lie behind <i>rile </i>‘annoy’ [19], a variant of an earlier <i>roil </i>which was possibly imported via Old French <i>ruiler </i>‘mix mortar’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/rector" target="_blank">rector</a>, <a href="/etym/s/regent" target="_blank">regent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/regulate" target="_blank">regulate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rile" target="_blank">rile</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rule" target="_blank">rule</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/regular">regular (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">reguler</span> "ecclesiastical" (Modern French <span class="foreign">régulier</span>), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">regularis</span> "containing rules for guidance," from Latin <span class="foreign">regula</span> "rule," from PIE <span class="foreign">*reg-</span> "move in a straight line" (see <a href="/etym/s/regal" target="_blank">regal</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Earliest sense was of religious orders (the opposite of <span class="foreign">secular</span>). Extended from late 16c. to shapes, etc., that followed predictable or uniform patterns; sense of "normal" is from 1630s; meaning "real, genuine" is from 1821. Old English borrowed Latin <span class="foreign">regula</span> and nativized it as <span class="foreign">regol</span> "rule, regulation, canon, law, standard, pattern;" hence <span class="foreign">regolsticca</span> "ruler" (instrument); <span class="foreign">regollic</span> (adj.) "canonical, regular."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/regular">regular (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "member of a religious order," from <a href="/etym/s/regular" target="_blank">regular</a> (adj.). Sense of "soldier of a standing army" is from 1756. Meaning "regular customer" is from 1852; meaning "leaded gasoline" is from 1978.</dd></dl></div></div>
