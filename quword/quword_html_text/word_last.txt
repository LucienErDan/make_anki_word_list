                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/last">last</a> 最后的</span><p>缩写自latest,最后的，late的原最高级拼写形式。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/last">last</a> 坚持，持续，鞋楦</span><p>来自PIE*leis,踩踏，小径，犁沟，词源同learn,lore.引申词义坚持，持续，同时用于指鞋楦。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/last">last</a></dt><dd><b>last: </b><span>[OE] Modern English has three separate words <i>last</i>, two of which are related. The adjective, meaning ‘after all others’, originated in prehistoric Germanic as the superlative form of <i>late</i>; its modern Germanic relatives include German <i>letzt </i>and Dutch <i>laatst</i>. The verb <i>last </i>‘continue’ goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>laistjan </i>‘follow a track’, which also produced modern German <i>leisten </i>‘perform, afford’.<br><br>This was derived from *<i>laisti</i>-, as was ultimately the noun <i>last</i>, which in Old English meant ‘footprint’ (‘shoemaker’s model foot’ is a secondary development). The general semantic thread ‘following a track’ can be traced back further via Germanic *<i>lais</i>- (a variant of which gave English <i>learn</i>) to Indo-European *<i>leis</i>- (source of Latin <i>dēlīrāre</i>, literally ‘deviate from a straight track’, from which English gets <i>delirious </i>[18]).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/delirious" target="_blank">delirious</a>, <a href="/etym/s/late" target="_blank">late</a>, <a href="/etym/s/learn" target="_blank">learn</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/last">last (adj., adv.)</a></dt><dd>"following all others," from Old English <span class="foreign">latost</span> (adj.) and <span class="foreign">lætest</span> (adv.), superlative of <span class="foreign">læt</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/late" target="_blank">late</a>). Cognate with Old Frisian <span class="foreign">lest</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">laatst</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">laggost</span>, German <span class="foreign">letzt</span>. Meaning "most recent" is from c. 1200. The noun, "last person or thing," is c. 1200, from the adjective. <span class="foreign">Last hurrah</span> is from the title of Edwin O'Connor's 1956 novel. <span class="foreign">Last word</span> "final, definitive statement" is from 1650s. A dying person's <span class="foreign">last words</span> so called by 1740. As an adjective, <span class="foreign">last-minute</span> attested from 1913. <span class="foreign">Last-chance</span> (adj.) is from 1962.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/last">last (v.)</a></dt><dd>"endure, go on existing," from Old English <span class="foreign">læstan</span> "to continue, endure," earlier "accomplish, carry out," literally "to follow a track," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*laistjan</span> "to follow a track" (cognates: Gothic <span class="foreign">laistjan</span> "to follow," Old Frisian <span class="foreign">lasta</span> "to fulfill, to pay (duties)," German <span class="foreign">leisten</span> "to perform, achieve, afford"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*leis-</span> (1) "track, furrow" (see <a href="/etym/s/learn" target="_blank">learn</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Related to <a href="/etym/s/last" target="_blank">last</a> (n.), not to <span class="foreign">last</span> (adj.). Related: <span class="foreign">Lasted</span>; <span class="foreign">lasting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/last">last (n.)</a></dt><dd>"shoemaker's block," from Old English <span class="foreign">læste</span>, from <span class="foreign">last</span> "track, footprint, trace," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*laist-</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">leistr</span> "the foot," Middle Dutch, Dutch <span class="foreign">leest</span> "form, model, last," Old High German <span class="foreign">leist</span> "track, footprint," German <span class="foreign">Leisten</span> "last," Gothic <span class="foreign">laistjan</span> "to follow," Old English <span class="foreign">læran</span> "to teach"); see <a href="/etym/s/last" target="_blank">last</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
