                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/general">general</a> 一般的，通用的，将军</span><p>来自词根gen, 生育，词源同generate. 即同种类的，通用的。引申词义将军。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/general">general</a></dt><dd><b>general: </b><span>[13] <i>General </i>is one of a vast range of English words which go back ultimately to the prehistoric Indo-European base *<i>gen</i>-, *<i>gon</i>-, *<i>gn</i>-, denoting ‘produce’. Its Germanic offshoots include <i>kin</i>, <i>kind</i>, and probably <i>king</i>, but for sheer numbers it is the Latin descendants <i>genus </i>‘race, type’, <i>gēns </i>‘race, people’, <i>gignere </i>‘beget’, and <i>nāscī </i>‘be born’ (source of <i>nation</i>, <i>nature</i>, etc) that have been the providers.<br><br>From <i>genus </i>come <i>gender </i>[14] and its French-derived counterpart <i>genre </i>[19], <i>generate </i>[16], <i>generation </i>[13], <i>generic </i>[17], <i>generous</i>, and <i>genus </i>[16] itself. <i>Gēns </i>produced <i>genteel</i>, <i>gentile</i>, <i>gentle</i>, and <i>gentry</i>, while <i>gignere </i>was the source of <i>genital </i>[14], <i>genitive </i>[14], <i>gingerly </i>[16] (originally ‘daintily’, as if befitting someone of ‘noble birth’), <i>indigenous</i>, and <i>ingenuous</i>.<br><br>A separate Latin strand is represented by <i>genius </i>and <i>genie</i>, and its derivative <i>genial</i>, while Greek descendants of Indo-European *<i>gen</i>-, *<i>gon</i>- are responsible for <i>gene </i>[20], <i>genealogy </i>[13], <i>genesis </i>[OE], <i>genetic </i>[19], <i>genocide </i>[20] (apparently coined by the Polish-born American jurist Raphael Lemkin in 1944), and <i>gonorrhoea </i>[16] (literally ‘flow of semen’).<br><br>As for <i>general </i>itself, it comes via Old French <i>general </i>from Latin <i>generālis </i>‘of the genus or type (as a whole)’, particularly as contrasted with <i>speciālis </i>‘of the species’ (source of English <i>special</i>). The application of the noun <i>general </i>to ‘senior military officer’ originated in the 16th century as an abbreviation of the phrase <i>captain general </i>(where the <i>general </i>was an adjective), a translation of French <i>capitaine générale</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/gender" target="_blank">gender</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gene" target="_blank">gene</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genealogy" target="_blank">genealogy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/generate" target="_blank">generate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/generous" target="_blank">generous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genesis" target="_blank">genesis</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genetic" target="_blank">genetic</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genie" target="_blank">genie</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genital" target="_blank">genital</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genius" target="_blank">genius</a>, <a href="/etym/s/genocide" target="_blank">genocide</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gingerly" target="_blank">gingerly</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gonorrhoea" target="_blank">gonorrhoea</a>, <a href="/etym/s/indigenous" target="_blank">indigenous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ingenuous" target="_blank">ingenuous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/jaunty" target="_blank">jaunty</a>, <a href="/etym/s/kin" target="_blank">kin</a>, <a href="/etym/s/kind" target="_blank">kind</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/general">general (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "of wide application, generic, affecting or involving all" (as opposed to <span class="foreign">special</span> or <span class="foreign">specific</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">general</span> (12c.) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">generalis</span> "relating to all, of a whole class, generic" (contrasted with <span class="foreign">specialis</span>), from <span class="foreign">genus</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">generis</span>) "stock, kind" (see <a href="/etym/s/genus" target="_blank">genus</a>).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; What is <b>common</b> is of frequent occurrence.&#13; <br />What is <b>general</b> admits of comparatively few exceptions: the general opinion (the opinion of the majority); the general welfare.&#13; <br />[J.H.A. Günther, "English Synonyms Explained &amp; Illustrated," Groningen, Netherlands, 1904]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Used in forming titles from late 14c. with the sense "having general authority or jurisdiction, chief." Phrase <span class="foreign">in general</span> "without exception, in one body; as a rule, generally, not specifically" is from late 14c. <span class="foreign">General rule</span>, one applying to an art or science as a whole, is from c. 1400. <span class="foreign">General store</span> attested by 1810, American English, in reference to the range of goods sold; a <span class="foreign">general hospital</span> (1737) is one not restricted to one class of persons or type of disease.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/general">general (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "whole class of things or persons, a broad classification, a general truth," from <a href="/etym/s/general" target="_blank">general</a> (adj.). Meaning "commander of an army" is 1570s, shortening of <span class="foreign">captain general</span>, from Middle French <span class="foreign">capitaine général</span>. The English adjective was affixed to civic officer designations by late 14c. to indicate superior rank and extended jurisdiction.</dd></dl></div></div>
