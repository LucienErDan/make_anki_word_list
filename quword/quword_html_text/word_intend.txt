            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/intend">intend</a> 打算，计划</span><p>in-,进入，使，-tend,延伸，词源同tend,extend.引申词义打算，计划。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/intend">intend</a></dt><dd><b>intend: </b><span>[14] The Latin verb <i>intendere </i>(a compound formed from the prefix <i>in</i>- ‘towards’ and <i>tendere </i>‘stretch’) had a variety of metaphorical meanings, some of which have come through into English. Principal among them was ‘form a plan or purpose’, an extension of an earlier ‘direct or ‘stretch’ one’s thoughts towards something’, which has given English <i>intend </i>and the derived <i>intention </i>[14].<br><br>The noun <i>intent </i>[13] belongs with this group too, but the adjective <i>intent </i>[17] looks back to the earlier ‘direct one’s mind towards a particular thing’, and <i>intense </i>[14] comes from the even more literal ‘stretched tight’. A medieval Latin addition to the meanings of <i>intendere </i>was ‘understand’, which English adopted in the 14th century. It had largely died out in English by the end of the 17th century, but it has persisted in the Romance languages, and has even developed further to ‘hear’ (which is what French <i>entendre </i>means).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/intense" target="_blank">intense</a>, <a href="/etym/s/intention" target="_blank">intention</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tense" target="_blank">tense</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/intend">intend (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "direct one's attention to," from Old French <span class="foreign">entendre</span>, <span class="foreign">intendre</span> "to direct one's attention" (in Modern French principally "to hear"), from Latin <span class="foreign">intendere</span> "turn one's attention, strain," literally "stretch out, extend," from <span class="foreign">in-</span> "toward" (see <a href="/etym/s/in-" target="_blank">in-</a> (2)) + <span class="foreign">tendere</span> "to stretch" (see <a href="/etym/s/tenet" target="_blank">tenet</a>). Sense of "have as a plan" (late 14c.) was present in Latin. A Germanic word for this was <span class="foreign">ettle</span>, from Old Norse <span class="foreign">ætla</span> "to think, conjecture, propose," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ahta</span> "consideration, attention" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">eaht</span>, German <span class="foreign">acht</span>). <span class="foreign">Intended</span> (n.) "one's intended husband or wife" is from 1767.</dd></dl></div></div>
