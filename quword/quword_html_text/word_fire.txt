                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fire">fire</a> 火</span><p>来自PIE*paewr, 火，词源同pyre,empyrean. 该词在印欧语用来指无生命之火，而PIE*egni则用来指有生命之火，词源同ignite.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fire">fire</a></dt><dd><b>fire: </b><span>[OE] Appropriately enough for one of the mainsprings of human civilization, the word <i>fire </i>is widespread amongst Indo-European languages (although it is only one of two competing ‘fire’ strands, the other being represented in English by <i>ignite</i>). Among its relatives are Greek <i>pur </i>(whence English <i>pyre</i>, <i>pyrotechnic</i>, and, by a very circuitous route, <i>bureau</i>), Czech <i>pýr </i>‘embers’, Armenian <i>hūr</i>, and Hittite <i>pahhur</i>, pointing back to a prehistoric Indo-European *<i>pūr</i>. Its Germanic descendant was *<i>fūir</i>, from which came German <i>feuer</i>, Dutch <i>vuur</i>, and English <i>fire</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bureau" target="_blank">bureau</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pyre" target="_blank">pyre</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pyrotechnic" target="_blank">pyrotechnic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fire">fire (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fyr</span> "fire, a fire," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fur-i-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fiur</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fiur</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fürr</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">vuur</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fiur</span>, German <span class="foreign">Feuer</span> "fire"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*perjos</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*paəwr-</span> "fire" (cognates: Armenian <span class="foreign">hur</span> "fire, torch," Czech <span class="foreign">pyr</span> "hot ashes," Greek <span class="foreign">pyr</span>, Umbrian <span class="foreign">pir</span>, Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pu</span>, Hittite <span class="foreign">pahhur</span> "fire"). Current spelling is attested as early as 1200, but did not fully displace Middle English <span class="foreign">fier</span> (preserved in <a href="/etym/s/fiery" target="_blank">fiery</a>) until c. 1600.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; PIE apparently had two roots for fire: <span class="foreign">*paewr-</span> and <span class="foreign">*egni-</span> (source of Latin <span class="foreign">ignis</span>). The former was "inanimate," referring to fire as a substance, and the latter was "animate," referring to it as a living force (compare <a href="/etym/s/water" target="_blank">water</a> (n.1)).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Brend child fuir fordredeþ ["The Proverbs of Hendyng," c. 1250]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; English <span class="foreign">fire</span> was applied to "ardent, burning" passions or feelings from mid-14c. Meaning "discharge of firearms, action of guns, etc." is from 1580s. To be <span class="foreign">on fire</span> is from c. 1500 (<span class="foreign">in fire</span> attested from c. 1400, as is <span class="foreign">on a flame</span> "on fire"). To <span class="foreign">play with fire</span> in the figurative sense "risk disaster, meddle carelessly or ignorantly with a dangerous matter" is by 1861, from the common warning to children. Phrase <span class="foreign">where's the fire?</span>, said to one in an obvious hurry, is by 1917, American English.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Fire-bell</span> is from 1620s; <span class="foreign">fire-alarm</span> as a self-acting, mechanical device is from 1808 as a theoretical creation; practical versions began to appear in the early 1830s. <span class="foreign">Fire-escape</span> (n.) is from 1788 (the original so-called was a sort of rope-ladder disguised as a small settee); <span class="foreign">fire-extinguisher</span> is from 1826. A <span class="foreign">fire-bucket</span> (1580s) carries water to a fire. <span class="foreign">Fire-house</span> is from 1899; <span class="foreign">fire-hall</span> from 1867, <span class="foreign">fire-station</span> from 1828. <span class="foreign">Fire company</span> "men for managing a fire-engine" is from 1744, American English. <span class="foreign">Fire brigade</span> "firefighters organized in a body in a particular place" is from 1838. <span class="foreign">Fire department</span>, usually a branch of local government, is from 1805. <span class="foreign">Fire-chief</span> is from 1877; <span class="foreign">fire-ranger</span> from 1909.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Symbolic <span class="foreign">fire and the sword</span> is by c. 1600 (translating Latin <span class="foreign">flamma ferroque absumi</span>); earlier <span class="foreign">yron and fyre</span> (1560s), <span class="foreign">with suerd &amp; flawme</span> (mid-15c.), <span class="foreign">mid fure &amp; mid here</span> ("with fire and armed force"), c. 1200. <span class="foreign">Fire-breathing</span> is from 1590s. To <span class="foreign">set the river on fire</span>, "accomplish something surprising or remarkable" (usually with a negative and said of one considered foolish or incompetent) is by 1830, often with the name of a river, varying according to locality, but the original is <span class="foreign">set the Thames on fire</span> (1796). The hypothetical feat was mentioned as the type of something impossibly difficult by 1720; it circulated as a theoretical possibility under some current models of chemistry c. 1792-95, which may have contributed to the rise of the expression.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; [A]mong other fanciful modes of demonstrating the practicability of conducting the gas wherever it might be required, he anchored a small boat in the stream about 50 yards from the shore, to which he conveyed a pipe, having the end turned up so as to rise above the water, and forcing the gas through the pipe, lighted it just above the surface, observing to his friends "that he had now set the river on fire." ["On the Origins and Progress of Gas-lighting," in "Repertory of Patent Inventions," vol. III, London, 1827]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fire">fire (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">furen</span>, "arouse, enflame, excite" (a figurative use); literal sense of "set fire to" is attested from late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/fire" target="_blank">fire</a> (n.). The Old English verb <span class="foreign">fyrian</span> "to supply with fire" apparently did not survive into Middle English. Related: <span class="foreign">Fired</span>; <a href="/etym/s/firing" target="_blank">firing</a>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "expose to the effects of heat or fire" (of bricks, pottery, etc.) is from 1660s. Meaning "to discharge artillery or a firearm" (originally by application of fire) is from 1520s; extended sense of "to throw (as a missile)" is from 1580s. <span class="foreign">Fire away</span> in the figurative sense of "go ahead" is from 1775.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sense of "sack, dismiss from employment" is recorded by 1885 (with <span class="foreign">out</span>; 1887 alone) in American English. This probably is a play on the two meanings of <a href="/etym/s/discharge" target="_blank">discharge</a> (v.): "to dismiss from a position," and "to <span class="foreign">fire</span> a gun," influenced by the earlier general sense "throw (someone) out" of some place (1871). To <span class="foreign">fire out</span> "drive out by or as if by fire" (1520s) is in Shakespeare and Chapman. <span class="foreign">Fired up</span> "angry" is from 1824 (to <span class="foreign">fire up</span> "become angry" is from 1798).&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
