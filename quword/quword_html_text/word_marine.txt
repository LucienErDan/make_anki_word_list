                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/marine">marine</a> 海的</span><p>来自mare,海，-ine,形容词后缀。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marine">marine</a></dt><dd><b>marine: </b><span>[15] The Latin word for ‘sea’ was <i>mare </i>(borrowed into English in the 19th century as a term for any of the sea-like dark areas on the moon). It goes back to Indo-European *<i>mori</i>-, *<i>mari</i>-, which also produced Russian <i>more </i>‘sea’, Welsh <i>mor </i>‘sea’, and English <i>mere </i>‘lake’ (the <i>mer</i>- of <i>mermaid</i>). The Romance-language terms for ‘sea’ (French <i>mer</i>, Italian and Romanian <i>mare</i>, and Spanish <i>mar</i>) are descended from it. And its derived adjective, <i>marīnus</i>, has given English <i>marine </i>(and <i>mariner </i>[13]). <i>Maritime </i>[16] is another derivative. <i>Marina </i>[19] was borrowed from Italian.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/marinade" target="_blank">marinade</a>, <a href="/etym/s/maritime" target="_blank">maritime</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mere" target="_blank">mere</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marine">marine (adj.)</a></dt><dd>early 15c., "pertaining to the sea," from Middle French <span class="foreign">marin</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">marin</span> "of the sea, maritime," from Latin <span class="foreign">marinus</span> "of the sea," from <span class="foreign">mare</span> "sea, the sea, seawater," from PIE <span class="foreign">*mori-</span> "body of water, lake" (see <a href="/etym/s/mere" target="_blank">mere</a> (n.)). The Old English word was <span class="foreign">sælic</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marine">marine (n.)</a></dt><dd>14c., "seacoast;" see <a href="/etym/s/marine" target="_blank">marine</a> (adj.). Meaning "collective shipping of a country" is from 1660s. Meaning "soldier who serves on a ship" is from 1670s, a separate borrowing from French <span class="foreign">marine</span>, from the French adjective. Phrase <span class="foreign">tell that to the marines</span> (1806) originally was the first half of a retort expressing skepticism:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; "Upon my soul, sir," answered the lieutenant, "when I thought she scorned my passion, I wept like a child."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; "Belay there!" cried the captain; "you may tell that to the marines, but I'll be d----d if the sailors will believe it." ["John Moore," "The Post-Captain; or, the Wooden Walls Well Manned," 1805]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The book, a rollicking sea romance/adventure novel, was popular in its day and the remark is a recurring punch line in it (repeated at least four times). It was written by naval veteran John Davis (1774-1854) but published under the name John Moore. Walsh records that, "The marines are among the 'jolly' jack-tars a proverbially gullible lot, capable of swallowing any yarn, in size varying from a yawl-boat to a full-rigged frigate."&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
