                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/excise">excise</a> 消费税</span><p>来自拉丁语accensum, 评估，估税，来自词根cens,评估，审查，词源同census. 因词义与excise有所重合，因而拼写受excise影响俗化。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/excise">excise</a> 切除，删除</span><p>ex-, 向外。-cis, 砍，切，词源同decide, circumcise.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/excise">excise</a></dt><dd><b>excise: </b><span>English has two words <i>excise</i>. The one meaning ‘tax’ [15] is essentially a Dutch usage. English borrowed it in the late 15th century from Middle Dutch <i>excijs</i>, which came via Old French <i>acceis </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>accēnsum</i>, a compound noun formed from the Latin prefix <i>ad</i>- ‘against, to’ and <i>cēnsus </i>‘tax’ (source of English <i>census </i>[17]).<br><br>At first it was used broadly for any ‘tax’, but in 1643 (following the example of Holland) it was officially adopted as the term for a tax imposed on certain forms of goods (originally domestically produced or imported, but since the 19th century only domestically produced – the tax on imports being termed <i>customs duty</i>). Dr Johnson in his <i>Dictionary </i>1755 defined <i>excise </i>as ‘a hateful tax levied upon commodities, and ajudged not by the common judges of property, but by wretches hired by those to whome excise is paid’. <i>Excise </i>‘cut out’ [16] comes from the past participle of Latin <i>excīdere</i>, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>ex</i>- ‘out’ and <i>caedere </i>‘cut’ (source also of English <i>concise, decide</i>, and <i>incision</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/census" target="_blank">census</a>, <a href="/etym/s/concise" target="_blank">concise</a>, <a href="/etym/s/decide" target="_blank">decide</a>, <a href="/etym/s/incision" target="_blank">incision</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/excise">excise (n.)</a></dt><dd>"tax on goods," late 15c., from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">excijs</span> (early 15c.), apparently altered from <span class="foreign">accijs</span> "tax" (by influence of Latin <span class="foreign">excisus</span> "cut out or removed," see <a href="/etym/s/excise" target="_blank">excise</a> (v.)), traditionally from Old French <span class="foreign">acceis</span> "tax, assessment" (12c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*accensum</span>, ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">ad-</span> "to" (see <a href="/etym/s/ad-" target="_blank">ad-</a>) + <span class="foreign">census</span> "tax, census" (see <a href="/etym/s/census" target="_blank">census</a>). English got the word, and the idea for the tax, from Holland.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/excise">excise (v.)</a></dt><dd>"cut out," 1570s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">exciser</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">excisus</span>, past participle of <span class="foreign">excidere</span> "cut out, cut down, cut off," from <span class="foreign">ex-</span> "out" (see <a href="/etym/s/ex-" target="_blank">ex-</a>) + <span class="foreign">-cidere</span>, comb. form of <span class="foreign">caedere</span> "to cut down" (see <a href="/etym/s/-cide" target="_blank">-cide</a>). Related: <span class="foreign">Excised</span>; <span class="foreign">excising</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
