                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/marshal">marshal</a> 元帅，指挥，安排</span><p>来自古法语mareschal,军事统帅，总管，来自mare,母马，-schal,仆人，来自PIE*skel,分开，分类，词源同scale,shell.其原义为养马的仆人，马夫，弼马温，后词义扩大，引申词义皇室总管，军队统帅，元帅。比较minister.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marshal">marshal</a></dt><dd><b>marshal: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, a <i>marshal </i>is a ‘horse-servant’. The word goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>markhaskalkaz </i>‘groom’, a compound based on *<i>markhaz </i>‘horse’ (source of English <i>mare </i>[OE]) and *<i>skalkaz </i>‘servant’. This was borrowed into late Latin as <i>mariscalcus</i>, and passed from there via Old French <i>mareschal </i>into English. In the course of its journey its status gradually rose, and by the time it reached English it denoted a ‘high officer of state’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/mare" target="_blank">mare</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marshal">marshal (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 13c. as a surname; mid-13c. as "high officer of the royal court;" from Old French <span class="foreign">mareschal</span> "commanding officer of an army; officer in charge of a household" (Modern French <span class="foreign">maréchal</span>), originally "stable officer, horse tender, groom" (Frankish Latin <span class="foreign">mariscaluis</span>) from Frankish <span class="foreign">*marhskalk</span> or a similar Germanic word, literally "horse-servant" (compare Old High German <span class="foreign">marahscalc</span> "groom," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">maerschalc</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*markhaz</span> "horse" (see <a href="/etym/s/mare" target="_blank">mare</a> (n.1)) + <span class="foreign">*skalkaz</span> "servant" (source of Old English <span class="foreign">scealc</span> "servant, retainer, member of a crew," Dutch <span class="foreign">schalk</span> "rogue, wag," Gothic <span class="foreign">skalks</span> "servant").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">horsþegn</span>. From c. 1300 as "stable officer;" early 14c. as "military commander, general in the army." For development history, compare <a href="/etym/s/constable" target="_blank">constable</a>. Also from Germanic are Italian <span class="foreign">scalco</span> "steward," Spanish <span class="foreign">mariscal</span> "marshal."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/marshal">marshal (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 15c., "to tend (horses)," from <a href="/etym/s/marshal" target="_blank">marshal</a> (n.). Meaning "to arrange, place in order" is from mid-15c.; that of "to arrange for fighting" is from mid-15c. Figurative use by 1690s. Related: <span class="foreign">Marshaled</span>; <span class="foreign">marshaling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
