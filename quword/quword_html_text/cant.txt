                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cant">cant</a> 仿善言辞，倾斜</span><p>1.伪善言辞，词源同chant, 唱，吟唱。</p><p> 2.倾斜，来自PIE *kemb, 弯，转，词源同camber, change,decant.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cant">cant</a></dt><dd><b>cant: </b><span>English has two separate words <i>cant</i>. The older, ‘oblique angle’ [14], originally meant ‘edge’, and appears to have come via Middle Low German <i>kant </i>or Middle Dutch <i>cant</i>, both meaning ‘edge’ or ‘corner’, from Vulgar Latin *<i>canto</i>, a descendant of Latin <i>cantus </i>‘iron tyre’. which was probably of Celtic origin (Welsh <i>cant </i>means ‘rim’).<br><br>The accusative case of the Vulgar Latin word, *<i>cantōnem</i>, was the source of English <i>canton </i>[16], originally ‘corner, section’, now ‘territorial division’; while its Italian descendant, <i>canto</i>, may be the source of Italian <i>cantina </i>‘cellar’, from which English got <i>canteen </i>[18]. <i>Cant </i>‘thieves’ jargon’ or ‘hypocritical talk’ [16] was probably originally a specific application of the Latin verb <i>cantāre </i>‘sing’ (source also of English <i>chant</i>, <i>canto</i>, <i>cantor</i>, <i>cantata</i>, and <i>canticle</i>).<br><br>It is usually assumed that the usage derives from an ironic transference of the singing of church congregations or choirs to the wheedling ‘song’ of beggars and (by association) thieves.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/canteen" target="_blank">canteen</a>, <a href="/etym/s/canton" target="_blank">canton</a>; <a href="/etym/s/cantata" target="_blank">cantata</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cantor" target="_blank">cantor</a>, <a href="/etym/s/chant" target="_blank">chant</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cant">cant (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"insincere talk," 1709, earlier it was slang for "whining of beggars" (1640s), from the verb in this sense (1560s), from Old North French <span class="foreign">canter</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">chanter</span>) "to sing, chant," from Latin <span class="foreign">cantare</span>, frequentative of <span class="foreign">canere</span> "to sing" (see <a href="/etym/s/chant" target="_blank">chant</a> (v.)). Sense in English developed after 1680 to mean the jargon of criminals and vagabonds, thence applied contemptuously by any sect or school to the phraseology of its rival.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; ... Slang is universal, whilst Cant is restricted in usage to certain classes of the community: thieves, vagrom men, and -- well, their associates. ... Slang boasts a quasi-respectability denied to Cant, though Cant is frequently more enduring, its use continuing without variation of meaning for many generations. [John S. Farmer, Forewords to "Musa Pedestris," 1896]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cant">cant (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"slope, slant," late 14c., Scottish, "edge, brink," from Old North French <span class="foreign">cant</span> "corner" (perhaps via Middle Low German <span class="foreign">kante</span> or Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">kant</span>), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*canthus</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">cantus</span> "iron tire of a wheel," possibly from a Celtic word meaning "rim of wheel, edge" (compare Welsh <span class="foreign">cant</span> "bordering of a circle, tire, edge," Breton <span class="foreign">cant</span> "circle"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*kam-bo-</span> "corner, bend," from root <span class="foreign">*kemb-</span> "to bend, turn, change" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">kanthos</span> "corner of the eye," Russian <span class="foreign">kutu</span> "corner").</dd></dl></div></div>
