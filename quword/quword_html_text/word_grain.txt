                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/grain">grain</a> 谷物，颗粒</span><p>来自PIE*gre-no, 生长，词源同grass, corn. 后指谷物。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grain">grain</a></dt><dd><b>grain: </b><span>[13] <i>Grain </i>comes via Old French from Latin <i>grānum </i>‘seed’. Its prehistoric Indo- European ancestor was *<i>grnóm</i>, literally ‘worndown particle’, which also produced English <i>corn</i>, and it has given English a remarkably wide range of related forms: not just obvious derivatives like <i>granary </i>[16], <i>granule </i>[17], and <i>ingrained </i>[16], but also <i>garner </i>[12] (originally a noun derived from Latin <i>grānārium </i>‘granary’), <i>gram </i>‘chick-pea’ [18] (from the Portuguese descendant of <i>grānum</i>, now mainly encountered in ‘gram flour’), <i>grange</i>, <i>granite</i>, <i>gravy</i>, <i>grenade</i>, and the second halves of <i>filigree </i>and <i>pomegranate</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/filigree" target="_blank">filigree</a>, <a href="/etym/s/garner" target="_blank">garner</a>, <a href="/etym/s/granary" target="_blank">granary</a>, <a href="/etym/s/granite" target="_blank">granite</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gravy" target="_blank">gravy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/grenade" target="_blank">grenade</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ingrained" target="_blank">ingrained</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pomegranate" target="_blank">pomegranate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grain">grain (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "a small, hard seed," especially of one of the cereal plants, also as a collective singular, "seed of wheat and allied grasses used as food;" also "something resembling grain; a hard particle of other substances" (salt, sand, later gunpowder, etc.), from Old French <span class="foreign">grain</span>, <span class="foreign">grein</span> (12c.) "seed, grain; particle, drop; berry; grain as a unit of weight," from Latin <span class="foreign">granum</span> "seed, a grain, small kernel," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*gre-no-</span> "grain" (see <a href="/etym/s/corn" target="_blank">corn</a> (n.1)). From late 14c. as "a species of cereal plant." In the U.S., where <span class="foreign">corn</span> has a specialized sense, it is the general word (used of wheat, rye, oats, barley, etc.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Figuratively, "the smallest possible quantity," from late 14c. From early 15c. in English as the smallest unit of weight (originally the weight of a plump, dry grain of wheat or barley from the middle of the ear). From late 14c as "roughness of surface; a roughness as of grains." In reference to wood, "quality due to the character or arrangement of its fibers," 1560s; hence, <span class="foreign">against the grain</span> (1650), a metaphor from carpentry: cutting across the fibers of the wood is more difficult than cutting along them.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Earliest sense of the word in English was "scarlet dye made from insects" (early 13c.), a sense also in the Old French collateral form <span class="foreign">graine</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/kermes" target="_blank">kermes</a> for the evolution of this sense, which was frequent in Middle English; also compare <a href="/etym/s/engrain" target="_blank">engrain</a>. In Middle English <span class="foreign">grain</span> also could mean "seed of flowers; pip of an apple, grape, etc.; a berry, legume, nut." <span class="foreign">Grain alcohol</span> attested by 1854.</dd></dl></div></div>
