                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. 该词是由 antiphon 简化、音变而来。<br />
2. anti- => ant-, ph => f => h, o => e, n => m.<br />
3. 其本义为：轮流吟唱的圣歌、颂歌、赞美诗等。<br />
4. antiphon: anti- "over against" + phon- "voice" => "a verse or song sung responsively", 其简单直译就是：对唱、唱对台戏，由此引申为轮流吟唱的作品。<br />
5. 该词的含义在其本已的基础上逐步泛化，也就是泛指各种圣歌、颂歌、赞歌、赞美诗、甚至是国歌。<br />
6. Anthem：俺颂。俺来歌颂上帝----圣歌，赞美歌。<br />
7. 【记】an theme 主旋律,主题曲；音：俺圣母，天主教徒圣歌就是唱“俺圣母”</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/anthem">anthem</a> 国歌</span><p>来自希腊词antiphona传入古英语的变体。前缀anti-, 相对。词根phone, 声音，歌。指对歌，神圣的音乐。后在英语里特指国歌。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/anthem">anthem</a></dt><dd><b>anthem: </b><span>[OE] <i>Anthem </i>is ultimately an alteration of <i>antiphon </i>‘scriptural verse said or sung as a response’ (which was independently reborrowed into English from ecclesiastical Latin in the 15th century). It comes from Greek <i>antíphōnos </i>‘responsive’, a compound formed from <i>anti</i>- ‘against’ and <i>phōné </i>‘sound’ (source of English <i>phonetic</i>, <i>telephone</i>, etc).<br><br>By the time it had become established in Old English, <i>antiphon </i>had already developed to <i>antefn</i>, and gradually the /v/ sound of the <i>f </i>became assimilated to the following <i>n</i>, producing <i>antemne </i>and eventually <i>antem</i>. The spelling with <i>th </i>begins to appear in the 15th century, perhaps influenced by Old French <i>anthaine</i>; it gradually altered the pronunciation.<br><br>The meaning ‘antiphon’ died out in the 18th century, having been succeeded by ‘piece of choral church music’ and more generally ‘song of praise’. The specific application to a ‘national song’ began in the 19th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/antiphon" target="_blank">antiphon</a>, <a href="/etym/s/phonetic" target="_blank">phonetic</a>, <a href="/etym/s/telephone" target="_blank">telephone</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/anthem">anthem (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ontemn</span>, <span class="foreign">antefn</span>, "a composition (in prose or verse) sung antiphonally," from Late Latin <span class="foreign">antefana</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">antiphona</span> "verse response" (see <a href="/etym/s/antiphon" target="_blank">antiphon</a>). Sense evolved to "a composition set to sacred music" (late 14c.), then "song of praise or gladness" (1590s). Used in reference to the English national song (technically, as OED points out, a hymn) and extended to those of other nations. Modern spelling is from late 16c., perhaps an attempt to make the word look more Greek.</dd></dl></div></div>
