                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/trap">trap</a> 陷阱，罗网，圈套</span><p>来自古英语 traeppe,陷阱，罗网，来自 Proto-Germanic*trep,踩踏，来自 PIE*der,走，跑，步 行，词源同 trip,tramp,tread.词义通俗化为圈套。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/trap">trap</a></dt><dd><b>trap: </b><span>[OE] The precise origins of <i>trap </i>are obscure. It goes back to an Old English <i>træppe</i>, and it has various relatives in the modern Germanic and Romance languages – Flemish <i>trape</i>, French <i>trappe</i>, Portuguese <i>trapa</i>, for instance – but its ultimate ancestry has never been unravelled. Its application to a small carriage emerged in the 19th century; it may be short for <i>rattle-trap </i>‘rickety vehicle’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/trap">trap (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "ensnare (an animal), catch in a trap; encircle; capture," from <a href="/etym/s/trap" target="_blank">trap</a> (n.) or from Old English <span class="foreign">betræppan</span>. Figurative use is slightly earlier (late 14c.). Related: <span class="foreign">Trapped</span>; <span class="foreign">trapping</span>.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/trap">trap (n.)</a></dt><dd>"contrivance for catching unawares," late Old English <span class="foreign">træppe</span>, <span class="foreign">treppe</span> "snare, trap," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*trep-</span> (cognates: Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">trappe</span> "trap, snare"), related to Germanic words for "stair, step, tread" (Middle Dutch, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">trappe</span>, <span class="foreign">treppe</span>, German <span class="foreign">Treppe</span> "step, stair," English <a href="/etym/s/tread" target="_blank">tread</a> (v.)), and probably literally "that on or into which one steps," from PIE <span class="foreign">*dreb-</span>, extended form of root <span class="foreign">*der-</span> (1), an assumed base of words meaning "to run, walk, step." Probably akin to Old French <span class="foreign">trape</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">trampa</span> "trap, pit, snare," but the exact relationship is uncertain.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "deceitful practice, device or contrivance to betray one" is first recorded c. 1400. Meaning "U-shaped section of a drain pipe" is from 1833. Slang meaning "mouth" is from 1776. <span class="foreign">Speed trap</span> recorded from 1908. <span class="foreign">Trap door</span> "door in a floor or ceiling" (often hidden and leading to a passageway or secret place) is first attested late 14c.</dd></dl></div></div>
