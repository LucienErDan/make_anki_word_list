                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mirror">mirror</a> 镜子，反映</span><p>来自古法语mirer,观察，沉思，照镜子，来自拉丁语mirari,惊叹，吃惊，词源同smile,miracle.因镜子在古代社会常常带有神秘和迷信色彩，甚至打碎一面镜子都会被认为是不祥之兆。引申词义反映，反射。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mirror">mirror</a></dt><dd><b>mirror: </b><span>[13] <i>Mirror </i>belongs to a small family of English words which illustrate how a Latin term originally signifying ‘wonder at’ weakened (presumably via ‘stare in wonder at’) to ‘look at’. Etymologically, a <i>mirror </i>is something you ‘look at’ yourself in. The word comes via Old French <i>mirour </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>mīrātōrium</i>, a derivative of *<i>mirāre </i>‘look at’.<br><br>This was closely related to classical Latin <i>mīrārī </i>‘wonder at’ (a derivative of <i>mīrus </i>‘wonderful’), which passed into Old French as <i>mirer </i>‘look at’, source of English <i>mirage </i>[19]. Based on <i>mīrārī </i>were Latin <i>mīrābilis </i>‘wonderful’ (source of English <i>marvel </i>[13]) and <i>mīrāculum </i>‘something to be wondered at’ (source of English <i>miracle </i>[12]).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/marvel" target="_blank">marvel</a>, <a href="/etym/s/miracle" target="_blank">miracle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mirage" target="_blank">mirage</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mirror">mirror (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., from Old French <span class="foreign">mireoir</span> "a reflecting glass, looking glass; observation, model, example," earlier <span class="foreign">miradoir</span> (11c.), from <span class="foreign">mirer</span> "look at" (oneself in a mirror), "observe, watch, contemplate," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*mirare</span> "to look at," variant of Latin <span class="foreign">mirari</span> "to wonder at, admire" (see <a href="/etym/s/miracle" target="_blank">miracle</a>). Figurative usage is attested from c. 1300. Used in divination since classical and biblical times; mirrors in modern England are the subject of at least 14 known superstitions, according to folklorists. Belief that breaking one brings bad luck is attested from 1777. The Spanish cognate, <span class="foreign">mirador</span> (from <span class="foreign">mirar</span> "to look, look at, behold"), has come to mean "watch tower." <span class="foreign">Mirror ball</span> attested from 1968.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mirror">mirror (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to reflect," 1590s, from <a href="/etym/s/mirror" target="_blank">mirror</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Mirrored</span>; <span class="foreign">mirroring</span>. The Middle English verb <span class="foreign">mirouren</span> (early 15c.) meant "to be a model" (for conduct, behavior, etc.), while <span class="foreign">miren</span> (mid-14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">mirer</span>) meant "to look in a mirror."</dd></dl></div></div>
