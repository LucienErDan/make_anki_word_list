                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spring">spring</a> 蹦，跳，跃</span><p>来自古英语 springan,蹦，跳，跃，来自 Proto-Germanic*springana,弹，跳，冲出，来自 PIE*spergh, 弹，跳，快速移动，可能来自 PIE*sper,播撒，展开，词源同 spread,spark.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spring">spring</a></dt><dd><b>spring: </b><span>[OE] The noun <i>spring </i>and the verb <i>spring </i>come from the same source: the Indo-European base *<i>sprengh</i>-, which denoted ‘rapid movement’. Of its Germanic verbal descendants, German and Dutch <i>springen</i>, like English <i>spring</i>, have moved on semantically to ‘jump’, but Swedish <i>springa </i>‘run’ has stayed closer to its roots. The noun <i>spring </i>in Old English times denoted the place where a stream ‘rises’ from the ground, which soon evolved metaphorically into ‘source, origin’ in general.<br><br>The notion of ‘rising’ was also applied figuratively to the ‘beginning of the day’ and to the ‘emergence of new growth’, and the latter led in the 16th century, via the expression <i>spring of the year</i>, to the use of <i>spring </i>for the ‘season following winter’ (replacing the previous term <i>Lent</i>).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spring">spring (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">springan</span> "to leap, burst forth, fly up; spread, grow," (class III strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">sprang</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">sprungen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sprengan</span> (cognates: Old Norse, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">springa</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">springhen</span>, Dutch Related: <span class="foreign">springen</span>, Old Saxon and Old High German <span class="foreign">springan</span>, German <span class="foreign">springen</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*sprengh-</span>, nasalized form of root <span class="foreign">*spergh-</span> "to move, hasten, spring" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sprhayati</span> "desires eagerly," Greek <span class="foreign">sperkhesthai</span> "to hurry").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In Middle English, it took on the role of causal <span class="foreign">sprenge</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">sprengan</span> (as still in <span class="foreign">to spring a trap</span>, etc.). Meaning "to cause to work or open," by or as by a spring mechanism, is from 1828.Meaning "to announce suddenly" (usually with <span class="foreign">on</span>) is from 1876. Meaning "to release" (from imprisonment) is from 1900. Slang meaning "to pay" (for a treat, etc.) is recorded from 1906.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spring">spring (n.1)</a></dt><dd>season following winter, the vernal season, c. 1400, earlier <span class="foreign">springing time</span> (late 14c.), which replaced <a href="/etym/s/Lent" target="_blank">Lent</a>, the Old English word. From <a href="/etym/s/spring" target="_blank">spring</a> (v.); also see <a href="/etym/s/spring" target="_blank">spring</a> (n.3). The notion is of the "spring of the year," when plants begin to rise (as in <span class="foreign">spring of the leaf</span>, 1520s), from the noun in its old sense of "action or time of rising or springing into existence." It was used of sunrise, the waxing of the moon, rising tides, etc.; compare 14c. <span class="foreign">spring of dai</span> "sunrise," <span class="foreign">spring of mone</span> "moonrise," late Old English <span class="foreign">spring</span> "carbuncle, pustule."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Other Germanic languages tend to take words for "fore" or "early" as their roots for the season name (Danish <span class="foreign">voraar</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">voorjaar</span>, literally "fore-year;" German <span class="foreign">Frühling</span>, from Middle High German <span class="foreign">vrueje</span> "early"). In 15c. English, the season also was <span class="foreign">prime-temps</span>, after Old French <span class="foreign">prin tans, tamps prim</span> (French <span class="foreign">printemps</span>, which replaced <span class="foreign">primevère</span> 16c. as the common word for spring), from Latin <span class="foreign">tempus primum</span>, literally "first time, first season."&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Spring fever</span> is from 1843 as "surge of romantic feelings;" earlier of a type of disease or head-cold prevalent in certain places in spring; Old English had <span class="foreign">lenctenadle</span>. First record of <span class="foreign">spring cleaning</span> in the domestic sense is by 1843 (in ancient Persia, the first month, corresponding to March-April, was <span class="foreign">Adukanaiša</span>, which apparently means "Irrigation-Canal-Cleaning Month;" Kent, p.167). <span class="foreign">Spring chicken</span> "small roasting chicken" (usually 11 to 14 weeks) is recorded from 1780; transferred sense of "young person" first recorded 1906. Baseball <span class="foreign">spring training</span> attested by 1889, earlier of militias, etc.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spring">spring (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"source of a stream or river, flow of water rising to the surface of the earth from below," Old English <span class="foreign">spring</span> "spring, source, sprinkling," from <a href="/etym/s/spring" target="_blank">spring</a> (v.) on the notion of the water "bursting forth" from the ground. Rarely used alone in Old English, appearing more often in compounds, such as <span class="foreign">wyllspring</span> "wellspring," <span class="foreign">espryng</span> "water spring." Figurative sense of "source or origin of something" is attested from early 13c. Cognate with Old High German <span class="foreign">sprung</span> "source of water," Middle High German <span class="foreign">sprinc</span> "leap, jump; source of water."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spring">spring (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"act of springing or leaping," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/spring" target="_blank">spring</a> (v.). The elastic wire coil that returns to its shape when stretched is so called from early 15c., originally in clocks and watches. As a device in carriages, coaches, etc., it is attested from 1660s.</dd></dl></div></div>
