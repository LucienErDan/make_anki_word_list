                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/coast">coast</a> 海岸</span><p>来自拉丁语costa,肋骨，边，词源同costal,accost.词义由岸边引申为海岸。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/coast">coast</a></dt><dd><b>coast: </b><span>[13] Latin <i>costa </i>meant ‘rib’ (hence the English medical term <i>intercostal </i>‘between the ribs’), but also more generally ‘flank, side’. It was in this sense that it passed into Old French as <i>coste</i>, and subsequently into English. The modern meaning ‘seashore’ (which had already developed in Old French) arises from the shore being thought of as the ‘side’ or ‘edge’ of the land (compare <i>seaside</i>).<br><br>Amongst the senses of the French word little represented in English is ‘hillside, slope’; it was however adopted in North America for a ‘slope down which one slides on a sledge’, and came to be used in the mid 19th century as a verb meaning ‘sledge down such a slope’. That was the source of the modern verbal sense ‘freewheel’. The <i>coster </i>of <i>costermonger </i>[16] was originally <i>costard</i>, a variety of apple named from its prominent ‘ribs’.<br><br>And another hidden relative is <i>cutlet </i>[18], borrowed from French <i>côtelette</i>, literally ‘little rib’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/costermonger" target="_blank">costermonger</a>, <a href="/etym/s/cutlet" target="_blank">cutlet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/intercostal" target="_blank">intercostal</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/coast">coast (n.)</a></dt><dd>"margin of the land," early 14c.; earlier "rib as a part of the body" (early 12c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">coste</span> "rib, side, flank; slope, incline;" later "coast, shore" (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">côte</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">costa</span> "a rib," perhaps related to a root word for "bone" (compare Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kosti</span> "bone," also see <a href="/etym/s/osseous" target="_blank">osseous</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Latin <span class="foreign">costa</span> developed a secondary sense in Medieval Latin of "the shore," via notion of the "side" of the land, as well as "side of a hill," and this passed into Romanic (Italian <span class="foreign">costa</span> "coast, side," Spanish <span class="foreign">cuesta</span> "slope," <span class="foreign">costa</span> "coast"), but only in the Germanic languages that borrowed it is it fully specialized in this sense (Dutch <span class="foreign">kust</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">kust</span>, German <span class="foreign">Küste</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">kyst</span>). French also used this word for "hillside, slope," which led to verb meaning "sled downhill," first attested 1775 in American English. Expression <span class="foreign">the coast is clear</span> (16c.) is an image of landing on a shore unguarded by enemies.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/coast">coast (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to skirt, to go around the sides, to go along the border" of something (as a ship does the coastline), from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">costien</span>, from the French source of <a href="/etym/s/coast" target="_blank">coast</a> (n.). The meaning "sled downhill," first attested 1775 in American English, is a separate borrowing. Of motor vehicles, "to move without thrust from the engine," by 1925; figurative use, of persons, "not to exert oneself," by 1934. Related: <span class="foreign">Coasted</span>; <span class="foreign">coasting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
