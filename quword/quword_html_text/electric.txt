                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/electric">electric</a> 电的</span><p>来自拉丁文electrum, 琥珀。因古人发现琥珀经摩擦后可以吸附其它物体，因此就借用该拉丁词表示电。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/electric">electric (adj.)</a></dt><dd>1640s, first used in English by physician Sir Thomas Browne (1605-1682), apparently coined as Modern Latin <span class="foreign">electricus</span> (literally "resembling amber") by English physicist William Gilbert (1540-1603) in treatise <span class="foreign">"De Magnete"</span> (1600), from Latin <span class="foreign">electrum</span> "amber," from Greek <span class="foreign">elektron</span> "amber" (Homer, Hesiod, Herodotus), also "pale gold" (a compound of 1 part silver to 4 of gold); which is of unknown origin.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Vim illam electricam nobis placet appellare</span> [Gilbert]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Originally the word described substances which, like amber, attract other substances when rubbed. Meaning "charged with electricity" is from 1670s; the physical force so called because it first was generated by rubbing amber. In many modern instances, the word is short for <a href="/etym/s/electrical" target="_blank">electrical</a>. Figurative sense is attested by 1793. <span class="foreign">Electric light</span> is from 1767. <span class="foreign">Electric toothbrush</span> first recorded 1936; <span class="foreign">electric blanket</span> in 1930. <span class="foreign">Electric typewriter</span> is from 1958. <span class="foreign">Electric guitar</span> is from 1938; <span class="foreign">electric organ</span> coined as the name of a hypothetical future instrument in 1885.</dd></dl></div></div>
