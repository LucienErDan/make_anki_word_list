            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bust">bust</a> 半身像，胸部，打碎</span><p>1.胸部，词源不详。</p><p> 2.打碎，burst 变体，字母r 脱落。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bust">bust</a></dt><dd><b>bust: </b><span>There are two different words <i>bust </i>in English. The one meaning ‘break’ [18] is simply an alteration of <i>burst</i>. <i>Bust </i>‘sculpture of head and chest’ [17] comes via French <i>buste </i>from Italian <i>busto </i>‘upper body’, of uncertain origin (Latin had the temptingly similar <i>bustum </i>‘monument on a tomb’, but this does not seem to fit in with the word’s primary sense ‘upper body’).<br><br>In English, application of the word to the human chest probably developed in the 18th century (one of the earliest examples is from Byron’s <i>Don Juan </i>1819: ‘There was an Irish lady, to whose bust I ne’er saw justice done’), although as late as the early 19th century it could still be used with reference to men’s chests, and had not become particularized to female breasts: ‘His naked bust would have furnished a model for a statuary’, Washington Irving, <i>A tour on the prairies </i>1835.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bust">bust (n.1)</a></dt><dd>1690s, "sculpture of upper torso and head," from French <span class="foreign">buste</span> (16c.), from Italian <span class="foreign">busto</span> "upper body," from Latin <span class="foreign">bustum</span> "funeral monument, tomb," originally "funeral pyre, place where corpses are burned," perhaps shortened from <span class="foreign">ambustum</span>, neuter of <span class="foreign">ambustus</span> "burned around," past participle of <span class="foreign">amburere</span> "burn around, scorch," from <span class="foreign">ambi-</span> "around" + <span class="foreign">urere</span> "to burn." Or perhaps from Old Latin <span class="foreign">boro</span>, the early form of classical Latin <span class="foreign">uro</span> "to burn." Sense development in Italian is probably from Etruscan custom of keeping dead person's ashes in an urn shaped like the person when alive. Meaning "bosom" is by 1884.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bust">bust (n.2)</a></dt><dd>variant of <a href="/etym/s/burst" target="_blank">burst</a> (n.), 1764, American English. For loss of <span class="foreign">-r-</span>, compare <a href="/etym/s/ass" target="_blank">ass</a> (n.2). Originally "frolic, spree;" sense of "sudden failure" is from 1842. Meaning "police raid or arrest" is from 1938. Phrase <span class="foreign">______ or bust</span> as an emphatic expression attested by 1851 in British depictions of Western U.S. dialect. Probably from earlier expression <span class="foreign">bust (one's) boiler</span>, by late 1840s, a reference to steamboat boilers exploding when driven too hard.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bust">bust (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to burst," 1806, variant of <a href="/etym/s/burst" target="_blank">burst</a> (v.); for loss of <span class="foreign">-r-</span>, compare <a href="/etym/s/ass" target="_blank">ass</a> (n.2). Meaning "go bankrupt" is from 1834. Meaning "break into" is from 1859. The slang meaning "demote" (especially in a military sense) is from 1918; that of "place under arrest" is from 1953 (earlier "to raid" from Prohibition). In card games, "to go over a score of 21," from 1939. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Busted" target="_blank">Busted</a>; <span class="foreign">busting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
