                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/set">set</a> 放置，设置，布置，安排</span><p>来自古英语 settan,使坐下，放置，建造，来自 Proto-Germanic*satjan,使坐下，来自 PIE*sed, 坐下，词源同 sit,session.引申诸相关词义。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/set">set</a> 一套，一副，一组</span><p>来自古法语 sette,顺序，次序，来自拉丁语 secta,追随，组织，过去分词格于 sequi,跟随，追 随，词源同 sect,sequence. 字母 c 脱落，比较 saint,sanctify.引申词义集中的东西，一套，一</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/set">set</a></dt><dd><b>set: </b><span>English has two words <i>set</i>. The verb [OE] is simply the causative version of <i>sit</i>. That is to say, etymologically it means ‘cause to sit’. It comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>satjan </i>(source also of German <i>setzen</i>, Dutch <i>zetten</i>, Swedish <i>sätta</i>, and Danish <i>sætte</i>), which was a causative variant of *<i>setjan</i>, ancestor of English <i>sit</i>. <i>Set </i>‘group’ [14] is essentially the same word as <i>sect</i>.<br><br>It comes via Old French <i>sette </i>from Latin <i>secta</i>, source of English <i>sect</i>. It originally meant strictly a ‘group of people’, and its far broader modern application, which emerged in the 16th century, is no doubt due to association with the verb <i>set </i>and the notion of ‘setting’ things together.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/sit" target="_blank">sit</a>; <a href="/etym/s/sect" target="_blank">sect</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/set">set (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">settan</span> (transitive) "cause to sit, put in some place, fix firmly; build, found; appoint, assign," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*(bi)satjan</span> "to cause to sit, set" (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">setja</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">sätta</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">settian</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">setta</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zetten</span>, German <span class="foreign">setzen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">satjan</span>), causative form of PIE <span class="foreign">*sod-</span>, variant of <span class="foreign">*sed-</span> (1) "to sit" (see <a href="/etym/s/sit" target="_blank">sit</a> (v.)). Also see <a href="/etym/s/set" target="_blank">set</a> (n.2).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Intransitive sense from c. 1200, "be seated." Used in many disparate senses by Middle English; sense of "make or cause to do, act, or be; start" and that of "mount a gemstone" attested by mid-13c. Confused with <span class="foreign">sit</span> since early 14c. Of the sun, moon, etc., "to go down," recorded from c. 1300, perhaps from similar use of the cognates in Scandinavian languages. To <span class="foreign">set (something) on</span> "incite to attack" (c. 1300) originally was in reference to hounds and game.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/set">set (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"fixed," c. 1200, <span class="foreign">sett</span>, past participle of <span class="foreign">setten</span> "to set" (see <a href="/etym/s/set" target="_blank">set</a> (v.)). Meaning "ready, prepared" first recorded 1844.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/set">set (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"collection of things," mid-15c., from Old French <span class="foreign">sette</span> "sequence," variant of <span class="foreign">secte</span> "religious community," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">secta</span> "retinue," from Latin <span class="foreign">secta</span> "a following" (see <a href="/etym/s/sect" target="_blank">sect</a>). "[I]n subsequent developments of meaning influenced by SET <span class="foreign">v</span>.1 and apprehended as equivalent to 'number set together'" [OED]. The noun <span class="foreign">set</span> was in Middle English, but only in the sense of "religious sect" (late 14c.), which likely is the direct source of some modern meanings, such as "group of persons with shared status, habits, etc." (1680s).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "complete collection of pieces" is from 1680s. Meaning "group of pieces musicians perform at a club during 45 minutes" (more or less) is from c. 1925, though it is found in a similar sense in 1580s. <span class="foreign">Set piece</span> is from 1846 as "grouping of people in a work of visual art;" from 1932 in reference to literary works.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/set">set (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"act of setting; condition of being set" (of a heavenly body), mid-14c., from <a href="/etym/s/set" target="_blank">set</a> (v.) or its identical past participle. Many disparate senses collect under this word because of the far-flung meanings assigned to the verb: &#13; <br /><br />"Action of hardening," 1837; also "manner or position in which something is set" (1530s), hence "general movement, direction, tendency" (1560s); "build, form" (1610s), hence "bearing, carriage" (1855); "action of fixing the hair in a particular style" (1933).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; "Something that has been set" (1510s), hence the use in tennis (1570s) and the theatrical meaning "scenery for an individual scene in a play, etc.," recorded from 1859. Other meanings OED groups under "miscellaneous technical senses" include "piece of electrical apparatus" (1891, first in telegraphy); "burrow of a badger" (1898). Old English had <span class="foreign">set</span> "seat," in plural "camp; stable," but OED finds it "doubtful whether this survived beyond OE." Compare <a href="/etym/s/set" target="_blank">set</a> (n.1).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Set</span> (n.1) and <span class="foreign">set</span> (n.2) are not always distinguished in dictionaries; OED has them as two entries, Century Dictionary as one. The difference of opinion seems to be whether the <span class="foreign">set</span> meaning "group, grouping" (here (n.2)) is a borrowing of the unrelated French word that sounds like the native English one, or a borrowing of the sense only, which was absorbed into the English word.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Set">Set</a></dt><dd>Egyptian god, from Greek <span class="foreign">Seth</span>, from Egyptian <span class="foreign">Setesh</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
