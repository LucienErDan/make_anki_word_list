                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. aff(阿福) + ray => 小狗阿福经常为了晒太阳争光线而打架、吵架。<br />
2. afraid => affray.<br />
3. The underlying meaning of the word is thus 'take away someone's peace'.<br />
4. free => afraid, affray.<br />
5. *exfray => *esfray => *effray (同化) => *affray (应该是误写、误传所致).<br />
6. afraid 原本是 affray 的过去分词。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/affray">affray</a> 打架</span><p>前缀af-为ex- 变体。词根fray, 平和，友爱，同free, friend. 原意为忧虑，后指破坏和平，打架。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/affray">affray</a></dt><dd><b>affray: </b><span>[14] <i>Affray </i>is a word of mixed Germanic and Romance origin. The noun comes from the verb, ‘alarm’ (now obsolete, but still very much with us in the form of its past participle, <i>afraid</i>), which was borrowed into English from Anglo- Norman <i>afrayer </i>and Old French <i>effreer </i>and <i>esfreer</i>. These go back to a hypothetical Vulgar Latin verb *<i>exfridāre</i>, which was composed of the Latin prefix <i>ex</i>- ‘out’ and an assumed noun *<i>fridus</i>, which Latin took from the Frankish *<i>frithuz </i>‘peace’ (cognate with German <i>friede </i>‘peace’, and with the name <i>Frederick</i>). The underlying meaning of the word is thus ‘take away someone’s peace’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/afraid" target="_blank">afraid</a>, <a href="/etym/s/belfry" target="_blank">belfry</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/affray">affray (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "state of alarm produced by a sudden disturbance," from Old French <span class="foreign">effrei</span>, <span class="foreign">esfrei</span> "disturbance, fright," from <span class="foreign">esfreer</span> (v.) "to worry, concern, trouble, disturb," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*exfridare</span>, a hybrid word meaning literally "to take out of peace," from Latin <span class="foreign">ex-</span> "out of" (see <a href="/etym/s/ex-" target="_blank">ex-</a>) + Frankish <span class="foreign">*frithu</span> "peace," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*frithuz</span> "peace, consideration, forbearance" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">frithu</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">friðu</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fridu</span> "peace, truce"), from suffixed form of PIE root <span class="foreign">*pri-</span> "to be friendly, love" (see <a href="/etym/s/free" target="_blank">free</a> (adj.)). Meaning "breach of the peace, riotous fight in public" is from late 15c. Related verb <span class="foreign">afrey</span> (early 14c.) survives almost exclusively in its past participle, <a href="/etym/s/afraid" target="_blank">afraid</a> (q.v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
