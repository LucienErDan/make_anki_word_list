            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/east">east</a> 东方的</span><p>来自PIE*aus, 光照，照耀，词源同aurora, aureate.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/east">east</a></dt><dd><b>east: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, <i>east </i>is the point of the compass at which the sun rises (and hence is a parallel formation to <i>orient</i>, which comes from a Latin word originally meaning ‘rising’). It goes back to an Indo-European base *<i>aus</i>-, source of a range of terms meaning not only ‘east’ but also ‘dawn’; Latin <i>aurora</i>, for instance, and Greek <i>aúōs</i>, had both senses.<br><br>Its Germanic descendant, *<i>austo</i>-, produced German <i>ost</i>, Dutch <i>oosten</i>, Swedish <i>öster</i>, and English <i>east </i>(which was subsequently borrowed by French as <i>est</i>). It was also the source of *<i>Austron</i>, the name of a goddess of the prehistoric Germanic peoples, originally the dawn-goddess, whose festival occurred in spring. In Old English her name was <i>Ēastre</i>, which is generally taken to be the ultimate source of English <i>Easter </i>(German <i>Ostern </i>‘Easter’ has a parallel origin).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/easter" target="_blank">easter</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/east">east</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">east</span>, <span class="foreign">eastan</span> (adj., adv.) "east, easterly, eastward;" <span class="foreign">easte</span> (n.), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*aust-</span> "east," literally "toward the sunrise" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ast</span> "east," <span class="foreign">aster</span> "eastward," Dutch <span class="foreign">oost</span> Old Saxon <span class="foreign">ost</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">ostan</span>, German <span class="foreign">Ost</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">austr</span> "from the east"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*aus-</span> (1) "to shine," especially of the dawn (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">ushas</span> "dawn;" Greek <span class="foreign">aurion</span> "morning;" Old Irish <span class="foreign">usah</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">auszra</span> "dawn;" Latin <span class="foreign">aurora</span> "dawn," <span class="foreign">auster</span> "south;" see <a href="/etym/s/aurora" target="_blank">aurora</a>). The east is the direction in which dawn breaks. For theory of shift in the geographical sense in Latin, see <a href="/etym/s/Australia" target="_blank">Australia</a>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As one of the four cardinal points of the compass, from c. 1200. Meaning "the eastern part of the world" (from Europe) is from c. 1300. Cold War use of <span class="foreign">East</span> for "communist states" first recorded 1951. French <span class="foreign">est</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">este</span> are borrowings from Middle English, originally nautical. The <span class="foreign">east wind</span> in Biblical Palestine was scorching and destructive (as in Ezek. xvii:10); in New England it is bleak, wet, unhealthful. <span class="foreign">East End</span> of London so called by 1846; <span class="foreign">East Side</span> of Manhattan so called from 1871; <span class="foreign">East Indies</span> (India and Southeast Asia) so called 1590s to distinguish them from the West Indies.</dd></dl></div></div>
