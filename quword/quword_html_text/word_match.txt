                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/match">match</a> 火柴</span><p>来自中古英语macche,灯芯或烛芯，来自希腊语myxa,鼻涕，灯芯，来自PIE*meug,黏的，滑的，词源同mucus,mucous.比喻用法，把悬荡的灯芯比喻成小孩的鼻涕。后引申词义用于点火的浸油小木条，最后指火柴。拼写比较bake,batch.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/match">match</a> 匹配，配对，比赛</span><p>来自古英语maecca,伴侣，妻子，丈夫，来自Proto-Germanic*makon,匹配，来自PIE*mag,揉，捏，词源同make,massage.后引申词义比赛，竞争等，即能匹敌的对手。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/match">match</a></dt><dd><b>match: </b><span>There are two unrelated words <i>match </i>in English, of which the older is ‘counterpart’ [OE]. This goes back to an Old English <i>gemæcca </i>‘mate’, whose ancestry can be traced to a prehistoric *<i>gamakjon</i>, a word based on the collective prefix *<i>ga</i>- and *<i>mak</i>- ‘fit’ (source of English <i>make</i>). Its etymological meaning is thus ‘fitting well together’.<br><br>The use of the word as a verb emerged in the 14th century. <i>Match </i>‘ignitable stick’ [14] originally meant ‘wick’. It comes via Old French <i>meiche </i>from Latin <i>myxa </i>‘lamp nozzle’. The first record of its modern use for ‘ignitable stick’ comes from 1831 (the synonymous <i>lucifer </i>is exactly contemporary, but had virtually died out by the end of the 19th century).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/make" target="_blank">make</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/match">match (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"stick for striking fire," late 14c., <span class="foreign">macche</span>, "wick of a candle or lamp," from Old French <span class="foreign">meiche</span> "wick of a candle," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*micca/*miccia</span> (source also of Catalan <span class="foreign">metxa</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">mecha</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">miccia</span>), probably ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">myxa</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">myxa</span> "lamp wick," originally "mucus," based on notion of wick dangling from the spout of a lamp like snot from a nostril, from PIE root <span class="foreign">*meug-</span> "slimy, slippery" (see <a href="/etym/s/mucus" target="_blank">mucus</a>). Modern spelling is from mid-15c. (English <a href="/etym/s/snot" target="_blank">snot</a> also had a secondary sense of "snuff of a candle, burnt part of a wick" from late 14c., surviving at least to late 19c. in northern dialects.)&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "piece of cord or splinter of wood soaked in sulfur, used for lighting fires, lamps, candles, etc." is from 1530. First used 1831 for the modern type of wooden friction match, and competed with <a href="/etym/s/lucifer" target="_blank">lucifer</a> for much of 19c. as the name for this invention.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/match">match (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"one of a pair, an equal," Old English <span class="foreign">mæcca</span>, "companion, mate, one of a pair, wife, husband, one suited to another, an equal," from <span class="foreign">gemæcca</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gamakon</span> "fitting well together" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gimaco</span> "fellow, equal," Old High German <span class="foreign">gimah</span> "comfort, ease," Middle High German <span class="foreign">gemach</span> "comfortable, quiet," German <span class="foreign">gemach</span> "easy, leisurely"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*mak-/*mag-</span> "to fit" (see <a href="/etym/s/make" target="_blank">make</a> (v.)). Middle English sense of "matching adversary, person able to contend with another" (c. 1300) led to sporting meaning "contest," first attested 1540s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/match">match (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to join one to another" (originally especially in marriage), late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/match" target="_blank">match</a> (n.2). Meaning "to place (one) in conflict with (another)" is from c. 1400. That of "to pair with a view to fitness" is from 1520s; that of "to be equal to" is from 1590s. Related: <span class="foreign">Matched</span>; <span class="foreign">matching</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
