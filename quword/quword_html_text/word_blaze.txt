                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/blaze">blaze</a> 燃烧</span><p>来自PIE *bhel, 燃烧，发光，同blank.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blaze">blaze</a></dt><dd><b>blaze: </b><span>There are three distinct words <i>blaze </i>in English. The commonest, meaning ‘fire, flame’ [OE], comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>blasōn</i>. Its original signification was ‘torch’ (in the sense, of course, of a burning piece of wood or bunch of sticks), but by the year 1000 the main current meaning was established. The precise source of <i>blaze </i>‘light-coloured mark or spot’ [17] is not known for certain, but there are several cognate forms in other Germanic languages, including Old Norse <i>blesi </i>and German <i>blässe</i>; perhaps the likeliest candidate as far as <i>blaze </i>is concerned is Middle Low German <i>bles</i>.<br><br>The verbal usage, as in ‘blaze a trail’ (that is, by making conspicuous marks on trees) originated in the mid 18th century. The related German adjective <i>blass </i>‘pale’, which originally meant ‘shining’, points up the fact that ultimately these two words <i>blaze </i>are related, the primeval sense ‘shining’ having diverged on the one hand through ‘pale’, on the other through ‘glowing, burning’.<br><br>The third <i>blaze</i>, ‘proclaim’ [14], as in ‘blaze abroad’, is now seldom encountered. It originally meant ‘blow a trumpet’, and comes ultimately from the Indo-European base *<i>bhlā</i>- (source of <i>blow</i>). Its immediate source in English was Middle Dutch <i>blāsen</i>. Despite its formal and semantic similarity, it does not appear to have any connection with <i>blazon </i>[13], which comes from Old French <i>blason </i>‘shield’, a word of unknown origin.<br><br>A <i>blazer </i>[19] got its name from being a brightly coloured jacket (from <i>blaze </i>meaning ‘fire, flame’). It originated among English university students in the late 19th century. According to a correspondent in the <i>Daily News </i>22 August 1889, the word was originally applied specifically to the red jackets worn by members of the ‘Lady Margaret, St John’s College, Cambridge, Boat Club’.<br><br>But by the 1880s its more general application had become widely established: in the <i>Durham University Journal </i>of 21 February 1885 we read that ‘the latest novelty … for the river is flannels, a blazer, and spats’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/blow" target="_blank">blow</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blaze">blaze (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"bright flame, fire," Old English <span class="foreign">blæse</span> "a torch, flame, firebrand, lamp," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*blas-</span> "shining, white" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">blas</span> "white, whitish," Middle High German <span class="foreign">blas</span> "bald," originally "white, shining," Old High German <span class="foreign">blas-ros</span> "horse with a white spot," Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">bles</span>, German <span class="foreign">Blesse</span> "white spot," <span class="foreign">blass</span> "pale, whitish"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bhel-</span> (1) "to shine, flash, burn" (see <a href="/etym/s/bleach" target="_blank">bleach</a> (v.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blaze">blaze (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"light-colored mark or spot," 1630s, northern English dialect, probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">blesi</span> "white spot on a horse's face" (from the same root as <a href="/etym/s/blaze" target="_blank">blaze</a> (n.1)). A Low German cognate of the Norse word also has been suggested as the source. Applied 1660s in American English to marks cut on tree trunks to indicate a track; thus the verb meaning "to mark a trail;" first recorded 1750, American English. Related: <span class="foreign">Blazed</span>; <span class="foreign">blazing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blaze">blaze (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"make public" (often in a bad sense, boastfully), late 14c., perhaps from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">blasen</span> "to blow" (on a trumpet), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*blaes-an</span> (cognates: German <span class="foreign">blasen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">-blesan</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhle-</span>, variant of root <span class="foreign">*bhel-</span> (2) "to blow, inflate, swell" (see <a href="/etym/s/bole" target="_blank">bole</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blaze">blaze (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to burn brightly or vigorously," c. 1200, from <a href="/etym/s/blaze" target="_blank">blaze</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Blazed</span>; <span class="foreign">blazing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/blaze">blaze (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"to mark" (a tree, a trail), 1750, American English; see <a href="/etym/s/blaze" target="_blank">blaze</a> (n.2).</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/blaze">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
