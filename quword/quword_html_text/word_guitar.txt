                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/guitar">guitar</a> 吉它</span><p>来自希腊语kithara , 词源同sitar, zither.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/guitar">guitar</a></dt><dd><b>guitar: </b><span>[17] The Greek <i>kithárā </i>was a stringed musical instrument of the lyre family, which has bequeathed its name to a variety of successors. Via Latin <i>cithara </i>came English <i>citole </i>[14], a medieval stringed instrument, and German <i>zither </i>(borrowed by English in the 19th century), while Arabic took it over as <i>qītār </i>and passed it on to Spanish as <i>guitarra</i>.<br><br>French adopted it in the form <i>guitare </i>(which eventually superseded the earlier <i>guiterne</i>), and it eventually reached English. (The history of <i>guiterne</i>, incidentally, is not entirely clear, although it is obviously a member of the <i>kithárā </i>family. English acquired it as <i>gittern </i>[14], applied to an early form of guitar, and it seems to have been blended with Latin <i>cithara </i>to produce English <i>cithern </i>or <i>cittern </i>[16], the name of a plucked stringed instrument of Renaissance times.)</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/zither" target="_blank">zither</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/guitar">guitar (n.)</a></dt><dd>lute-like musical instrument, 1620s, from French <span class="foreign">guitare</span>, which was altered by Spanish and Provençal forms from Old French <span class="foreign">guiterre</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">guiterne</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">cithara</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">kithara</span> "cithara," a triangular seven-stringed musical instrument related to the lyre, perhaps from Persian <span class="foreign">sihtar</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/sitar" target="_blank">sitar</a>). The name reached English several times, including <span class="foreign">giterne</span> (early 14c., from Old French), in reference to various stringed, guitar-like instruments; the modern word is also directly from Spanish <span class="foreign">guitarra</span> (14c.), which ultimately is from the Greek. The Arabic word is perhaps from Spanish or Greek, though often the relationship is said to be the reverse. The modern guitar is one of a large class of instruments used in all countries and ages but particularly popular in Spain and periodically so in France and England. Other 17c, forms of the word in English include <span class="foreign">guittara</span>, <span class="foreign">guitarra</span>, <span class="foreign">gittar</span>, and <span class="foreign">guitarre</span>. Compare <a href="/etym/s/zither" target="_blank">zither</a>, <a href="/etym/s/gittern" target="_blank">gittern</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
