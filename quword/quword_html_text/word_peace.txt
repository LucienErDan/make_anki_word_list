                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/peace">peace</a> 和平，平静</span><p>来自古法语pais,和平，平和，安静，来自拉丁语pacem,协议，条款，和平条约，来自PIE*pag,固定，词源同page,pact,pacify.词义由固定引申为解决争端，订立条约，引申词义和平，平静。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/peace">peace</a></dt><dd><b>peace: </b><span>[12] The etymological notion underlying <i>peace </i>is of ‘fastening’, so as to achieve a ‘stable’ condition. The word comes via Anglo-Norman <i>pes </i>from Latin <i>pāx </i>‘peace’, which was derived from the same base, *<i>pāk</i>- ‘fasten’, as lies behind English <i>pact</i>, and is closely related to <i>pagan</i>, <i>page</i>, <i>pale </i>‘stake’, and <i>pole </i>‘stick’. Derivatives of Latin <i>pāx </i>or its Old French descendant to reach English include <i>appease </i>[16], <i>pacific </i>[16], <i>pacify </i>[15], and <i>pay</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/appease" target="_blank">appease</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pacific" target="_blank">pacific</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pact" target="_blank">pact</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pagan" target="_blank">pagan</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pale" target="_blank">pale</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pay" target="_blank">pay</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pole" target="_blank">pole</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/peace">peace (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-12c., "freedom from civil disorder," from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">pes</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">pais</span> "peace, reconciliation, silence, permission" (11c., Modern French <span class="foreign">paix</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">pacem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">pax</span>) "compact, agreement, treaty of peace, tranquility, absence of war" (source of Provençal <span class="foreign">patz</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">paz</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">pace</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*pag-</span>/<span class="foreign">*pak-</span> "fasten," related to <span class="foreign">pacisci</span> "to covenant or agree" (see <a href="/etym/s/pact" target="_blank">pact</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">frið</span>, also <span class="foreign">sibb</span>, which also meant "happiness." Modern spelling is 1500s, reflecting vowel shift. Sense in <span class="foreign">peace of mind</span> is from c. 1200. Used in various greetings from c. 1300, from Biblical Latin <span class="foreign">pax</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">eirene</span>, which were used by translators to render Hebrew <span class="foreign">shalom</span>, properly "safety, welfare, prosperity."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "quiet" is attested by 1300; meaning "absence or cessation of war or hostility" is attested from c. 1300. As a type of hybrid tea rose (developed 1939 in France by François Meilland), so called from 1944. Native American <span class="foreign">peace pipe</span> is first recorded 1760. <span class="foreign">Peace-officer</span> attested from 1714. <span class="foreign">Peace offering</span> is from 1530s. Phrase <span class="foreign">peace with honor</span> first recorded 1607 (in "Coriolanus"). The U.S. <span class="foreign">Peace Corps</span> was set up March 1, 1962. <span class="foreign">Peace sign</span>, both the hand gesture and the graphic, attested from 1968.</dd></dl></div></div>
