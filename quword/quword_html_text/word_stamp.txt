                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stamp">stamp</a> 邮票，印章，印花，痕迹，烙印，跺脚，猛踩</span><p>来 自 中 古 英 语 stampen, 踩 踏 ， 捣 ， 来 自 古 英 语 stempan, 捣 ， 碾 碎 ， 来 自 Proto-Germanic*stampijana,踩踏，击打，来自 PIE*stemp,踩踏，践踏，鼻音化自 PIE*stebh, 踩踏，词源同 step,stomp,stump.最终来自 PIE*sta,站立，词源同 stand.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stamp">stamp</a></dt><dd><b>stamp: </b><span>[12] <i>Stamp </i>originally meant ‘crush into small pieces, pound’. The sense ‘slam the foot down’ did not emerge until the 14th century, and ‘imprint with a design by pressure’ (which forms the semantic basis of <i>postage stamp </i>[19]) is as recent as the 16th century. The word comes, probably via an unrecorded Old English *<i>stampian</i>, from prehistoric Germanic *<i>stampōjan </i>(source also of German <i>stampfen</i>, Dutch <i>stampen</i>, Swedish <i>stampa</i>, and Danish <i>stampe</i>).<br><br>This was derived from the noun *<i>stampaz </i>‘pestle’, which was formed from the base *<i>stamp</i>- (a non-nasalized version of which, *<i>stap</i>-, lies behind English <i>step</i>). The Germanic verb was borrowed into Vulgar Latin as *<i>stampīre</i>, whose past participle has given English, via Mexican Spanish, <i>stampede </i>[19].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/stampede" target="_blank">stampede</a>, <a href="/etym/s/step" target="_blank">step</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stamp">stamp (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">stempan</span> "to pound in a mortar," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stamp-</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">stappa</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">stampe</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">stampen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">stampfon</span>, German <span class="foreign">stampfen</span> "to stamp with the foot, beat, pound," German <span class="foreign">Stampfe</span> "pestle"), from nasalized form of PIE root <span class="foreign">*stebh-</span> "to support, place firmly on" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">stembein</span> "to trample, misuse;" see <a href="/etym/s/staff" target="_blank">staff</a> (n.)). The vowel altered in Middle English, perhaps by influence of Scandinavian forms.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "strike the foot forcibly downwards" is from mid-14c. The meaning "impress or mark (something) with a die" is first recorded 1550s. Italian <span class="foreign">stampa</span> "stamp, impression," Spanish <span class="foreign">estampar</span> "to stamp, print," French <span class="foreign">étamper</span> (13c., Old French <span class="foreign">estamper</span>) "to stamp, impress" are Germanic loan-words. Related: <span class="foreign">Stamped</span>; <span class="foreign">stamping</span>. To <span class="foreign">stamp out</span> originally was "extinguish a fire by stamping on it;" attested from 1851 in the figurative sense. <span class="foreign">Stamping ground</span> "one's particular territory" (1821) is from the notion of animals. A <span class="foreign">stamped addressed envelope</span> (1873) was one you enclosed in a letter to speed or elicit a reply.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stamp">stamp (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-15c., "instrument for crushing, stamping tool," from <a href="/etym/s/stamp" target="_blank">stamp</a> (v.). Especially "instrument for making impressions" (1570s). Meaning "downward thrust or blow with the foot, act of stamping" is from 1580s. Sense of "official mark or imprint" (to certify that duty has been paid on what has been printed or written) dates from 1540s; transferred 1837 to designed, pre-printed adhesive labels issued by governments to serve the same purpose as impressed stamps. German <span class="foreign">Stempel</span> "rubber stamp, brand, postmark" represents a diminutive form. <span class="foreign">Stamp-collecting</span> is from 1862 (compare <a href="/etym/s/philately" target="_blank">philately</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
