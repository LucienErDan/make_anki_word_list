            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cat">cat</a> 猫</span><p>来自拉丁词cattus,猫。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cat">cat</a></dt><dd><b>cat: </b><span>[OE] The word <i>cat </i>seems to have appeared on the European scene, in the form of Latin <i>catta </i>or <i>cattus</i>, around 1000 AD (the previous Latin word was <i>fēlēs</i>, source of English <i>feline</i>). No one is completely sure where it came from (although given the domestic cat’s origins in Egypt, it is likely to have been an Egyptian word), but it soon spread north and west through Europe. The Latin word reached English via Germanic *<i>kattuz</i>, later backed up by Anglo-Norman <i>cat</i>, a variant of Old French <i>chat</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cat">cat (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">catt</span> (c. 700), from West Germanic (c. 400-450), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kattuz</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">katte</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">köttr</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kat</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kazza</span>, German <span class="foreign">Katze</span>), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">cattus</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The near-universal European word now, it appeared in Europe as Latin <span class="foreign">catta</span> (Martial, c. 75 C.E.), Byzantine Greek <span class="foreign">katta</span> (c. 350) and was in general use on the continent by c. 700, replacing Latin <span class="foreign">feles</span>. Probably ultimately Afro-Asiatic (compare Nubian <span class="foreign">kadis</span>, Berber <span class="foreign">kadiska</span>, both meaning "cat"). Arabic <span class="foreign">qitt</span> "tomcat" may be from the same source. Cats were domestic in Egypt from c. 2000 B.C.E., but not a familiar household animal to classical Greeks and Romans. The nine lives have been proverbial since at least 1560s.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Late Latin word also is the source of Old Irish and Gaelic <span class="foreign">cat</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">kath</span>, Breton <span class="foreign">kaz</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">gatto</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">gato</span>, French <span class="foreign">chat</span> (12c.). Independent, but ultimately from the same source are words in the Slavic group: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kotuka</span>, <span class="foreign">kotel'a</span>, Bulgarian <span class="foreign">kotka</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">koška</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">kot</span>, along with Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kate</span> and non-Indo-European Finnish <span class="foreign">katti</span>, which is from Lithuanian.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Extended to lions, tigers, etc. c. 1600. As a term of contempt for a woman, from early 13c. Slang sense of "prostitute" is from at least c. 1400. Slang sense of "fellow, guy," is from 1920, originally in U.S. Black English; narrower sense of "jazz enthusiast" is recorded from 1931.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Cat's paw</span> (1769, but <span class="foreign">cat's foot</span> in the same sense, 1590s) refers to old folk tale in which the monkey tricks the cat into pawing chestnuts from a fire; the monkey gets the nuts, the cat gets a burnt paw. <span class="foreign">Cat bath</span> "hurried or partial cleaning" is from 1953. <span class="foreign">Cat burglar</span> is from 1907, so called for stealth. <span class="foreign">Cat-witted</span> "small-minded, obstinate, and spiteful" (1670s) deserved to survive. For <span class="foreign">Cat's meow, cat's pajamas</span>, see <a href="/etym/s/bee%27s_knees" target="_blank">bee's knees</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/CAT">CAT</a></dt><dd>1975, medical acronym for <span class="foreign">computerized axial tomography</span> or something like it. Related: <span class="foreign">CAT scan</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
