                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/naked">naked</a> 裸体的</span><p>来自PIE*nogw,裸体的，词源同nude,gymnasium.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/naked">naked</a></dt><dd><b>naked: </b><span>[OE] <i>Naked </i>goes back ultimately to Indo- European *<i>nogw</i>- ‘unclothed’, which also produced Latin <i>nūdus </i>(source of English <i>nude </i>[16]) and Russian <i>nagój </i>‘naked’. The past participial form derived from this, *<i>nogwedhos</i>, passed into prehistoric Germanic as *<i>naquethaz</i>, which has subsequently differentiated to German <i>nackt</i>, Dutch <i>naakt</i>, Swedish <i>naken</i>, Danish <i>nøgen</i>, and English <i>naked</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/nude" target="_blank">nude</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/naked">naked (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">nacod</span> "nude, bare; empty," also "not fully clothed," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*nakwadaz</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">nakad</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">naket</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">naakt</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">nackot</span>, German <span class="foreign">nackt</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">nökkviðr</span>, Old Swedish <span class="foreign">nakuþer</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">naqaþs</span> "naked"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*nogw-</span> "naked" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">nagna</span>, Hittite <span class="foreign">nekumant-</span>, Old Persian <span class="foreign">*nagna-</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">gymnos</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">nudus</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">nuogas</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">nagu-</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">nagoi</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">nocht</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">noeth</span> "bare, naked"). Related: <span class="foreign">Nakedly</span>; <span class="foreign">nakedness</span>. Applied to qualities, actions, etc., from late 14c. (first in "The Cloud of Unknowing"); phrase <span class="foreign">naked truth</span> is from 1585, in Alexander Montgomerie's "The Cherry and the Slae":&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Which thou must (though it grieve thee) grant&#13; <br />I trumped never a man.&#13; <br />But truely told the naked trueth,&#13; <br />To men that meld with mee,&#13; <br />For neither rigour, nor for rueth,&#13; <br />But onely loath to lie.&#13; <br />[Montgomerie, 1585]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Phrase <span class="foreign">naked as a jaybird</span> (1943) was earlier <span class="foreign">naked as a robin</span> (1879, in a Shropshire context); the earliest known comparative based on it was <span class="foreign">naked as a needle</span> (late 14c.). <span class="foreign">Naked eye</span> is from 1660s, unnecessary in the world before telescopes and microscopes.</dd></dl></div></div>
