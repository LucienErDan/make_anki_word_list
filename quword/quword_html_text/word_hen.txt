                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hen">hen</a> 母鸡</span><p>来自古英语henn,来自PIE*kan,唱，吟唱，词源同chant,cantor.引申词义打鸣的鸟，即公鸡，参照古英语hana,公鸡。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hen">hen</a></dt><dd><b>hen: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, a <i>hen </i>is a ‘singing bird’. The word goes back ultimately to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>khanon </i>‘male fowl, cock’, which was related to the Latin verb <i>canere </i>‘sing’ (source of English <i>chant</i>). In the West Germanic dialects a feminine form developed, *<i>khannjō</i>, which has become German <i>henne</i>, Dutch <i>hen</i>, Swedish <i>höna</i>, Danish <i>høne</i>, and English <i>hen</i>. (The original masculine form survives in German <i>hahn</i>, Dutch <i>haan</i>, and Swedish and Danish <i>hane</i>, but English has given it up – the Old English word was <i>hana</i>, and if it had survived to the present day it would probably be *<i>hane</i>.) The metaphorical extension of the term to any female bird took place in the 14th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/chant" target="_blank">chant</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hen">hen (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">henn</span>, from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*hannjo</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">henn</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">henne</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">henna</span>), fem. of <span class="foreign">*han(e)ni</span> "male fowl, cock" (source of Old English <span class="foreign">hana</span> "cock"), literally "bird who sings (for sunrise)," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kan-</span> "to sing" (see <a href="/etym/s/chant" target="_blank">chant</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The original masculine word survives in German (<span class="foreign">Hahn</span> "cock"), Swedish, Danish, etc. German also has a generic form, <span class="foreign">Huhn</span>, for either gender of the bird. Extension to "female of any bird species" is early 14c. in English. <span class="foreign">Hen</span> as slang for "woman" dates from 1620s; hence <span class="foreign">hen party</span> "gathering of women," first recorded 1887. To be <span class="foreign">mad as a wet hen</span> is from 1823, but the figure was used to indicate other states:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Some, on the contrary, are viciously opposite to these, who act so tamely and so coldly, that when they ought to be angry, to thunder and lighten, as one may say, they are no fuller of Heat, than a wet Hen, as the Saying is; .... ["Life of Mr. Thomas Betterton," London, 1710]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; Orth. Out upon you for a dastardly Fellow; you han't the Courage of a wet Hen. ["A Sermon Preached at St. Mary-le-Bow, March 27, 1704"]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">As wanton as a wet hen</span> is in "Scots Proverbs" (1813). Among Middle English proverbial expressions was <span class="foreign">nice as a nonne hen</span> "over-refined, fastidiously wanton" (c. 1500); to <span class="foreign">singen so hen in snowe</span> "sing miserably," literally "sing like a hen in snow" (c. 1200). <span class="foreign">Hen's teeth</span> as a figure of scarceness is attested by 1838.</dd></dl></div></div>
