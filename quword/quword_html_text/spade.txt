                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spade">spade</a> 锹，铲</span><p>来自古英语 spadu,锹，铲，来自 Proto-Germanic*spadan,锹，铲，来自 PIE*spe-dh-,扩展自*spe, 长木条，词源同 spoon,spay.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/spade">spade</a> 黑桃</span><p>扑克牌花色，来自拉丁语 spatha,宽刀，阔剑，来自希腊语 spathe,宽刀，阔剑，来自 PIE*spe-dh-, 扩展自*spe,长木条，词源同 spoon,spay.后用于指扑克牌的花色黑桃。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spade">spade</a></dt><dd><b>spade: </b><span>English has two words <i>spade</i>, but they are ultimately related. <i>Spade </i>for digging [OE] comes from a Low German source, which also produced Dutch <i>spade</i>. This went back to, or shared a common source with, Greek <i>spáthē </i>‘broad blade’, which was borrowed into Latin as <i>spatha </i>‘broad flat instrument’ (source of the English botanical term <i>spathe </i>[18]).<br><br>This in turn passed into Italian as <i>spada </i>‘broad sword’, whose plural <i>spade </i>gave English the playingcard symbol <i>spade </i>[16]. The corresponding French term is <i>épée </i>‘sword’, adopted by English as a fencing term in the 19th century; and its Old French precursor <i>espee </i>is the ultimate source of English <i>spay </i>[15]. The diminutive form of Latin <i>spatha </i>was <i>spathula</i>, from which English gets <i>spatula </i>[16].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/spathe" target="_blank">spathe</a>, <a href="/etym/s/spatula" target="_blank">spatula</a>, <a href="/etym/s/spay" target="_blank">spay</a>, <a href="/etym/s/spoon" target="_blank">spoon</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spade">spade (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"tool for digging," Old English <span class="foreign">spadu</span> "spade," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spadan</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">spada</span> "a spade," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">spade</span> "a sword," Old Saxon <span class="foreign">spado</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">spade</span>, German <span class="foreign">Spaten</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*spe-dh-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*spe-</span> (2) "long, flat piece of wood" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">spathe</span> "wooden blade, paddle," Old English <span class="foreign">spon</span> "chip of wood, splinter," Old Norse <span class="foreign">spann</span> "shingle, chip;" see <a href="/etym/s/spoon" target="_blank">spoon</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; "A spade differs from a two-handed shovel chiefly in the form and thickness of the blade" [Century Dictionary]. To <span class="foreign">call a spade a spade</span> "use blunt language, call things by right names" (1540s) translates a Greek proverb (known to Lucian), <span class="foreign">ten skaphen skaphen legein</span> "to call a bowl a bowl," but Erasmus mistook Greek <span class="foreign">skaphe</span> "trough, bowl" for a derivative of the stem of <span class="foreign">skaptein</span> "to dig," and the mistake has stuck [see OED].</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/spade">spade (n.2)</a></dt><dd>black figure on playing cards," 1590s, probably from Italian <span class="foreign">spade</span>, plural of <span class="foreign">spada</span> "the ace of spades," literally "sword, spade," from Latin <span class="foreign">spatha</span> "broad, flat weapon or tool," from Greek <span class="foreign">spathe</span> "broad blade" (see <a href="/etym/s/spade" target="_blank">spade</a> (n.1)). Phrase <span class="foreign">in spades</span> "in abundance" first recorded 1929 (Damon Runyon), probably from bridge, where spades are the highest-ranking suit.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The invitations to the musicale came sliding in by pairs and threes and spade flushes. [O.Henry, "Cabbages &amp; Kings," 1904]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Derogatory meaning "black person" is 1928, from the color of the playing card symbol.</dd></dl></div></div>
