            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. *ster- / *star- "stiff, rigid" => starve.<br />
2. Starve means etymologically 'be stiff', the 'stiffness' of a corpse led to its use for 'die'.<br />
3. But later, it gradually weakened and narrowed down in meaning to 'dying from cold' and 'dying from hunger'.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/starve">starve</a> 使挨饿，饥饿</span><p>来自古英语 steorfan,死去，死掉，字面意思为身体变僵硬，来自 Proto-Germanic*sterban,变僵 硬，来自 PIE*ster,僵的，硬的，固定的，词源同 startle,sterile.后词义弱化为慢慢饿死，再弱 化为使挨饿，饥饿。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/starve">starve</a></dt><dd><b>starve: </b><span>[OE] <i>Starve </i>means etymologically ‘be stiff’ – it goes back to a prehistoric Germanic base *<i>star</i>-, *<i>ster</i>- ‘be stiff’, which also produced English <i>starch</i>, <i>stare</i>, etc. The ‘stiffness’ of a corpse led to its use for ‘die’ – a meaning which it retains in the related German <i>sterben </i>and Dutch <i>sterven</i>. In English, however, from the 12th century onwards, <i>starve </i>gradually narrowed down in meaning to ‘dying from cold’ (which survived into the modern era in northern dialects) and ‘dying from hunger’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/starch" target="_blank">starch</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stare" target="_blank">stare</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stork" target="_blank">stork</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/starve">starve (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">steorfan</span> "to die" (past tense <span class="foreign">stearf</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">storfen</span>), literally "become stiff," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sterban</span> "be stiff" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">sterva</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sterban</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">sterven</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sterban</span> "to die," Old Norse <span class="foreign">stjarfi</span> "tetanus"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ster-</span> (1) "stiff, rigid" (see <a href="/etym/s/stereo-" target="_blank">stereo-</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The conjugation became weak in English by 16c. The sense narrowed to "die of cold" (14c.); transitive meaning "to kill with hunger" is first recorded 1520s (earlier <span class="foreign">to starve of hunger</span>, early 12c.). Intransitive sense of "to die of hunger" dates from 1570s. German cognate <span class="foreign">sterben</span> retains the original sense of the word, but the English has come so far from its origins that <span class="foreign">starve to death</span> (1910) is now common.</dd></dl></div></div>
