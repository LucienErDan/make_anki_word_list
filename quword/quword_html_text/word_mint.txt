                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mint">mint</a> 薄荷</span><p>来自拉丁文menta,薄荷，来自希腊语Minthe,薄荷仙子，在古希腊神话中，原为泉水仙子，因引诱冥王Hades,被冥后Persephone变成了薄荷树。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mint">mint</a> 铸造，铸币，创造</span><p>来自拉丁文moneta,铸币，硬币，词源同money,monetary.引申词义创造或发明。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mint">mint</a></dt><dd><b>mint: </b><span>[OE] English has two completely unconnected words <i>mint</i>. The ‘money factory’ comes ultimately from Latin <i>monēta </i>‘mint, money’ (source also of English <i>money</i>). It was borrowed into prehistoric West Germanic as *<i>munita</i>, which in due course produced Old English <i>mynet</i>. This denoted ‘coin’ (as its modern German relative <i>münze </i>still does), and it was not until the 15th century that the modern sense ‘place where money is made’ emerged. <i>Mint </i>the plant originated in Greek <i>mínthē</i>, and reached English via Latin <i>mentha </i>(source of <i>menthol </i>[19], a German coinage) and prehistoric West Germanic *<i>minta</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/money" target="_blank">money</a>; <a href="/etym/s/menthol" target="_blank">menthol</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mint">mint (n.1)</a></dt><dd>aromatic herb, Old English <span class="foreign">minte</span> (8c.), from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*minta</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">minta</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">mente</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">minza</span>, German <span class="foreign">Minze</span>), a borrowing from Latin <span class="foreign">menta</span>, <span class="foreign">mentha</span> "mint," from Greek <span class="foreign">minthe</span>, personified as a nymph transformed into an herb by Proserpine, probably a loan-word from a lost Mediterranean language.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mint">mint (n.2)</a></dt><dd>place where money is coined, early 15c., from Old English <span class="foreign">mynet</span> "coin, coinage, money" (8c.), from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*munita</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">munita</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">menote</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">munte</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">munizza</span>, German <span class="foreign">münze</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">moneta</span> "mint" (see <a href="/etym/s/money" target="_blank">money</a>). Earlier word for "place where money is coined" was <span class="foreign">minter</span> (early 12c.). General sense of "a vast sum of money" is from 1650s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mint">mint (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to stamp metal to make coins," 1540s, from <a href="/etym/s/mint" target="_blank">mint</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Minted</span>; <span class="foreign">minting</span>. <span class="foreign">Minter</span> "one who stamps coins to create money" is from early 12c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mint">mint (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"perfect" (like a freshly minted coin), 1887 (in <span class="foreign">mint condition</span>), from <a href="/etym/s/mint" target="_blank">mint</a> (n.2).</dd></dl></div></div>
