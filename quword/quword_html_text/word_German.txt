                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/German">German</a> 德国的</span><p>来自拉丁语Germanus, 最早来自古罗马执政官凯撒的使用。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/german">german (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"of the same parents or grandparents," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">germain</span> "own, full; born of the same mother and father; closely related" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">germanus</span> "full, own (of brothers and sisters); one's own brother; genuine, real, actual, true," related to <span class="foreign">germen</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">germinis</span>) "sprout, bud," of uncertain origin; perhaps dissimilated from PIE <span class="foreign">*gen(e)-men-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*gene-</span> "to give birth, beget" (see <a href="/etym/s/genus" target="_blank">genus</a>). Your <span class="foreign">cousin-german</span> (also <span class="foreign">first cousin</span>) is the son or daughter of an uncle or aunt; your children and your first cousin's are <span class="foreign">second cousins</span> to one another; to you, your first cousin's children are <span class="foreign">first cousin once removed</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/German">German (n.)</a></dt><dd>"a native of Germany," 1520s, from Latin <span class="foreign">Germanus</span> (adjective and noun, plural <span class="foreign">Germani</span>), first attested in writings of Julius Caesar, who used <span class="foreign">Germani</span> to designate a group of tribes in northeastern Gaul, of unknown origin. Probably originally the name of an individual tribe, but Gaulish (Celtic) origins have been proposed, from words perhaps originally meaning "noisy" (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">garim</span> "to shout") or "neighbor" (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">gair</span> "neighbor"). Middle English had <span class="foreign">Germayns</span> (plural, late 14c.), but only in the sense "ancient Teuton, member of the Germanic tribes." The earlier English word was <span class="foreign">Almain</span> (early 14c.; see <a href="/etym/s/Alemanni" target="_blank">Alemanni</a>) or <a href="/etym/s/Dutch" target="_blank">Dutch</a>. &#13; <blockquote>&#13; Þe empere passede from þe Grees to þe Frenschemen and to þe Germans, þat beeþ Almayns. [John of Trevisa, translation of Higdon's <span class="foreign">Polychronicon</span>, 1387]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Their name for themselves, <span class="foreign">die Deutschen</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/Dutch" target="_blank">Dutch</a>), dates from 12c. Roman writers also used <span class="foreign">Teutoni</span> as a German tribal name, and writers in Latin after about 875 commonly refer to the German language as <span class="foreign">teutonicus</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/Teutonic" target="_blank">Teutonic</a>). Meaning "the German language" in English is from 1748. <span class="foreign">High German</span> (1823 in English) and <span class="foreign">Low German</span> as a division of dialects is geographical: High German (from 16c. established as the literary language) was the German spoken in the upland regions in southern Germany, Low German (often including Dutch, Frisian, Flemish), also called <a href="/etym/s/Plattdeutsch" target="_blank">Plattdeutsch</a> was spoken in the regions near the North Sea. In the U.S. <span class="foreign">German</span> also was used of descendants of settlers from Germany.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/German">German (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"of or pertaining to Germany or the Germans," 1550s, from <a href="/etym/s/German" target="_blank">German</a> (n.). <span class="foreign">German shepherd</span> as a breed of dog (1922) is short for <span class="foreign">German shepherd dog</span> (1889), which translates German <span class="foreign">deutscher Schäferhund</span>. <span class="foreign">German Ocean</span> as an old name for the North Sea translates Ptolemy. <span class="foreign">German measles</span> attested by 1856. <span class="foreign">German-American</span> is from 1880. <span class="foreign">German Reformed</span> church is from 1812.</dd></dl></div></div>
