                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sign">sign</a> 手势，标记，符号，印章，图章，签字，签署</span><p>来自拉丁语 signum,符号，标志，图章，可能来自 PIE*sekw,砍，切，词源同 segment,score,shear. 引申诸相关比喻义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sign">sign</a></dt><dd><b>sign: </b><span>[13] <i>Sign </i>comes via Old French <i>signe </i>from Latin <i>signum </i>‘mark’. It already had the meaning ‘mark denoting something’ in Latin, and it was in this sense that it entered English, gradually ousting the native word <i>token</i>. The verb <i>sign </i>goes back ultimately to the Latin derivative <i>signāre </i>‘mark’. English acquired it in the 14th century, and first used it for ‘write one’s name’ in the 15th century.<br><br>Other related forms in English include <i>assign </i>[14], <i>consign </i>[15], <i>design</i>, <i>ensign </i>[14], <i>insignia </i>[17], <i>resign </i>[14] (in which the prefix <i>re</i>- has the force of ‘un-’), <i>seal </i>‘wax impression, fastening’, <i>signal</i>, <i>signatory </i>[17], <i>signature </i>[16], <i>signet </i>[14], <i>significant </i>[16], and <i>signify </i>[13].<br><br>The ultimate source of Latin <i>signum </i>is uncertain. It was once assumed to go back to the Indo-European base *<i>sek</i>- ‘cut’ (source of English <i>saw</i>, <i>section</i>, etc), as if it denoted etymologically a ‘cut mark’, but now Indo-European *<i>seq</i>- ‘point out’, hence ‘say, tell’ (source of English <i>say</i>) is viewed as a more likely ancestor.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/assign" target="_blank">assign</a>, <a href="/etym/s/consign" target="_blank">consign</a>, <a href="/etym/s/design" target="_blank">design</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ensign" target="_blank">ensign</a>, <a href="/etym/s/insignia" target="_blank">insignia</a>, <a href="/etym/s/resign" target="_blank">resign</a>, <a href="/etym/s/seal" target="_blank">seal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/signal" target="_blank">signal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/signature" target="_blank">signature</a>, <a href="/etym/s/significant" target="_blank">significant</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sign">sign (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 13c., "gesture or motion of the hand," especially one meant to communicate something, from Old French <span class="foreign">signe</span> "sign, mark," from Latin <span class="foreign">signum</span> "identifying mark, token, indication, symbol; proof; military standard, ensign; a signal, an omen; sign in the heavens, constellation," according to Watkins, literally "standard that one follows," from PIE <span class="foreign">*sekw-no-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*sekw-</span> (1) "to follow" (see <a href="/etym/s/sequel" target="_blank">sequel</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Ousted native <a href="/etym/s/token" target="_blank">token</a>. Meaning "a mark or device having some special importance" is recorded from late 13c.; that of "a miracle" is from c. 1300. Zodiacal sense in English is from mid-14c. Sense of "characteristic device attached to the front of an inn, shop, etc., to distinguish it from others" is first recorded mid-15c. Meaning "token or signal of some condition" (late 13c.) is behind <span class="foreign">sign of the times</span> (1520s). In some uses, the word probably is a shortening of <a href="/etym/s/ensign" target="_blank">ensign</a>. <span class="foreign">Sign language</span> is recorded from 1847; earlier <span class="foreign">hand-language</span> (1670s).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sign">sign (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "to make the sign of the cross," from Old French <span class="foreign">signier</span> "to make a sign (to someone); to mark," from Latin <span class="foreign">signare</span> "to set a mark upon, mark out, designate; mark with a stamp; distinguish, adorn;" figuratively "to point out, signify, indicate," from <span class="foreign">signum</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/sign" target="_blank">sign</a> (n.)). Sense of "to mark, stamp" is attested from mid-14c.; that of "to affix one's name" is from late 15c. Meaning "to communicate by hand signs" is recorded from 1700. Related: <span class="foreign">Signed</span>; <span class="foreign">signing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
