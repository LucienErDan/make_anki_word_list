            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/little">little</a> 小的</span><p>来自PIE*leud,弯，转，蜷缩，变小，词源同lout,little.引申词义弱小的，小人物的，后词义通用化。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/little">little</a></dt><dd><b>little: </b><span>[OE] <i>Little </i>goes back to the prehistoric West Germanic base *<i>lut</i>-, which also produced Dutch <i>luttel </i>and may have been the source of the Old English verb <i>lūtan </i>‘bow down’. Some have detected a link with Old English <i>lot </i>‘deceit’, Old Norse <i>lýta </i>‘dishonour, blame’, Russian <i>ludit</i>’ ‘deceive’, and Serbo-Croat <i>lud </i>‘foolish’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/little">little (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">lytel</span> "not large, not much; short in distance or time; unimportant," also used in late Old English as a noun, "small piece; a short time," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*lutilla-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">luttil</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">luttel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">luzzil</span>, German <span class="foreign">lützel</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">leitils</span> "little"), perhaps originally a diminutive of the root of Old English <span class="foreign">lyt</span> "little, few," from PIE <span class="foreign">*leud-</span> "small." "Often synonymous with <span class="foreign">small</span>, but capable of emotional implications which <span class="foreign">small</span> is not" [OED].&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Phrase <span class="foreign">the little woman</span> "wife" attested from 1795. <span class="foreign">Little people</span> "the faeries" is from 1726; as "children," it is attested from 1752; as "ordinary people" (opposed to the great), it is attested from 1827. <span class="foreign">Little Neck</span> clams (1884) are so called for <span class="foreign">Little Neck</span>, Long Island, a "neck" of land on the island's North Shore. <span class="foreign">Little by little</span> is from late 15c. (<span class="foreign">litylle be litille</span>). <span class="foreign">Little green men</span> "space aliens" is from 1950. <span class="foreign">Little black dress</span> is from 1939.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; At the beginning of summer, smart women who stay in town like to wear sheer "little black dresses." Because most "little black dresses" look alike, retailers struggle each year to find something which will make them seem new. ["Life," June 13, 1939]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Little Orphan Annie</span> originally was (as <span class="foreign">Little Orphant Annie</span>) the character in James Whitcomb Riley's 1885 poem, originally titled "Elf Child." The U.S. newspaper comic strip created by Harold Gray (1894-1968) debuted in 1924 in the New York "Daily News."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; LITTLE Orphant Annie's come to our house to stay,&#13; <br />An' wash the cups an' saucers up, an' brush the crumbs away,&#13; <br />An' shoo the chickens off the porch, an' dust the hearth, an' sweep,&#13; <br />An' make the fire, an' bake the bread, an' earn her board-an'-keep;&#13; <br />An' all us other childern, when the supper-things is done,&#13; <br />We set around the kitchen fire an' has the mostest fun&#13; <br />A-list'nin' to the witch-tales 'at Annie tells about,&#13; <br />An' the Gobble-uns 'at gits you&#13; <br />Ef you&#13; <br />Don't&#13; <br />Watch&#13; <br />Out!&#13; <br /><br />[Riley, "Elf Child"]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/little">little (v.)</a></dt><dd>OE <span class="foreign">lytlian</span>, from root of <a href="/etym/s/little" target="_blank">little</a> (adj.).</dd></dl></div></div>
