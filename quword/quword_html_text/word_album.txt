                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. alb- + -um (拉丁语后缀).<br />
2. 该词本意为：白色，洁白(white color, whiteness)。<br />
3. 在古代，使用这个词表示罗马教皇在空白的牌匾上记录一年中重大的事情，后来由此被德国学者借用过去表示由同事、同僚在空白册子上签了名的签名册，后来含义由此泛化为表示记录下来、收集起来供收藏、纪念的各种册、集、簿等。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/album">album</a> 相册</span><p>来自词根alb, 白色。原指古时候用来登记姓名，记录重大事件的白板。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/album">album</a></dt><dd><b>album: </b><span>[17] Latin <i>albus </i>‘white’ has been the source of a variety of English words: <i>alb </i>‘ecclesiastical tunic’ [OE], <i>albedo </i>‘reflective power’ [19], <i>Albion </i>[13], an old word for <i>Britain</i>, probably with reference to its white cliffs, <i>albumen </i>‘white of egg’ [16], and <i>auburn</i>, as well as <i>albino</i>. <i>Album </i>is a nominalization of the neuter form of the adjective, which was used in classical times for a blank, or white, tablet on which public notices were inscribed.<br><br>Its original adoption in the modern era seems to have been in Germany, where scholars kept an <i>album amicorum </i>‘album of friends’ in which to collect colleagues’ signatures. This notion of an autograph book continues in Dr Johnson’s definition of <i>album </i>in his <i>Dictionary </i>1755: ‘a book in which foreigners have long been accustomed to insert the autographs of celebrated people’, but gradually it became a repository for all sorts of souvenirs, including in due course photographs.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/alb" target="_blank">alb</a>, <a href="/etym/s/albedo" target="_blank">albedo</a>, <a href="/etym/s/albino" target="_blank">albino</a>, <a href="/etym/s/albumen" target="_blank">albumen</a>, <a href="/etym/s/auburn" target="_blank">auburn</a>, <a href="/etym/s/daub" target="_blank">daub</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/album">album (n.)</a></dt><dd>1650s, from Latin <span class="foreign">album</span> "white color, whiteness," neuter of <span class="foreign">albus</span> "white" (see <a href="/etym/s/alb" target="_blank">alb</a>). In classical times "a blank tablet on which the Pontifex Maximus registered the principal events of the year; a list of names." Revived 16c. by German scholars whose custom was to keep an <span class="foreign">album amicorum</span> of colleagues' signatures; meaning then expanded into "book to collect souvenirs." According to Johnson, "a book in which foreigners have long been accustomed to insert autographs of celebrated people." Photographic albums first recorded 1859. Meaning "long-playing gramophone record" is by 1951, because the sleeves they came in resembled large albums.</dd></dl></div></div>
