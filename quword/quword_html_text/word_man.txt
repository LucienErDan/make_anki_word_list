                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/man">man</a> 男子，人，人类</span><p>来自古英语man,人类，男人，女人，来自Proto-Germanic*manwaz,来自PIE*man,人，进一步来自PIE*men,思考，词源同mind,mania.后不再用于女人义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/man">man</a></dt><dd><b>man: </b><span>[OE] <i>Man </i>is a widespread Germanic word (with relatives in German <i>mann </i>‘man’ and <i>mensch </i>‘person’, Dutch and Swedish <i>man </i>‘man’, Danish <i>mand </i>‘man’, and Swedish <i>menniska </i>‘person’), and connections have even been found outside Germanic (Sanskrit, for instance, had <i>mánu</i>- ‘man’). But no decisive evidence has been found for an ultimate Indo- European source.<br><br>Among the suggestions put forward have been links with a base *<i>men</i>- ‘think’ or ‘breathe’, or with Latin <i>manus </i>‘hand’. The etymologically primary sense of the word is ‘human being, person’, and that is what it generally meant in Old English: the sexes were generally distinguished by <i>wer </i>‘man’ (which survives probably in <i>werewolf </i>and is related to <i>world</i>) and <i>wīf </i>(source of modern English <i>wife</i>) or <i>cwene </i>‘woman’.<br><br>But during the Middle English and early modern English periods ‘male person’ gradually came to the fore, and today ‘person’ is decidedly on the decline (helped on its way by those who feel that the usage discriminates against women). <i>Woman </i>originated in Old English as a compound of <i>wīf </i>‘woman, female’ and <i>man </i>‘person’. <i>Manikin </i>[17] was borrowed from Dutch <i>manneken</i>, a diminutive form of <i>man </i>‘man’; and <i>mannequin </i>[18] is the same word acquired via French.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/manikin" target="_blank">manikin</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mannequin" target="_blank">mannequin</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/man">man (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">man</span>, <span class="foreign">mann</span> "human being, person (male or female); brave man, hero; servant, vassal," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*manwaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Swedish, Dutch, Old High German <span class="foreign">man</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mann</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">maðr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">mand</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">manna</span> "man"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*man-</span> (1) "man" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">manuh</span>, Avestan <span class="foreign">manu-</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">mozi</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">muzh</span> "man, male").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Plural <span class="foreign">men</span> (German <span class="foreign">Männer</span>) shows effects of <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/imutate.php">i-mutation</a>. Sometimes connected to root <span class="foreign">*men-</span> "to think" (see <a href="/etym/s/mind" target="_blank">mind</a>), which would make the ground sense of <span class="foreign">man</span> "one who has intelligence," but not all linguists accept this. Liberman, for instance, writes, "Most probably <span class="foreign">man</span> 'human being' is a secularized divine name" from <span class="foreign">Mannus</span> [Tacitus, <span class="foreign">"Germania,"</span> chap. 2], "believed to be the progenitor of the human race."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; So I am as he that seythe, `Come hyddr John, my man.' [1473]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Sense of "adult male" is late (c. 1000); Old English used <span class="foreign">wer</span> and <span class="foreign">wif</span> to distinguish the sexes, but <span class="foreign">wer</span> began to disappear late 13c. and was replaced by <span class="foreign">man</span>. Universal sense of the word remains in <a href="/etym/s/mankind" target="_blank">mankind</a> and <a href="/etym/s/manslaughter" target="_blank">manslaughter</a>. Similarly, Latin had <span class="foreign">homo</span> "human being" and <span class="foreign">vir</span> "adult male human being," but they merged in Vulgar Latin, with <span class="foreign">homo</span> extended to both senses. A like evolution took place in Slavic languages, and in some of them the word has narrowed to mean "husband." PIE had two stems: <span class="foreign">*uiHro</span> "freeman" (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vira-</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">vyras</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">vir</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">fer</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">wair</span>) and <span class="foreign">*hner</span> "man," a title more of honor than <span class="foreign">*uiHro</span> (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">nar-</span>, Armenian <span class="foreign">ayr</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">ner</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">aner</span>).&#13; <blockquote>&#13; MANTRAP, a woman's commodity. [Grose, "Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue," London, 1785]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Man</span> also was in Old English as an indefinite pronoun, "one, people, they." The chess pieces so called from c. 1400. As an interjection of surprise or emphasis, first recorded c. 1400, but especially popular from early 20c. <span class="foreign">Man-about-town</span> is from 1734; <span class="foreign">the Man</span> "the boss" is from 1918. To be <span class="foreign">man or mouse</span> "be brave or be timid" is from 1540s. <span class="foreign">Men's Liberation</span> first attested 1970.<blockquote>At the kinges court, my brother, Ech man for himself. [Chaucer, "Knight's Tale," c. 1386]</blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/man">man (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">mannian</span> "to furnish (a fort, ship, etc.) with a company of men," from <a href="/etym/s/man" target="_blank">man</a> (n.). Meaning "to take up a designated position on a ship" is first recorded 1690s. Meaning "behave like a man, act with courage" is from c. 1400. To <span class="foreign">man (something) out</span> is from 1660s. Related: <span class="foreign">Manned</span>; <span class="foreign">manning</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
