                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/witch">witch</a> 女巫，巫婆</span><p>来自古英语wicca,巫师，巫术，来自PIE*weik,选择，献祭，牺牲，词源同victim,Wicca。引申词义通灵，做法，召唤鬼神。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/witch">witch</a></dt><dd><b>witch: </b><span>[OE] The close Germanic relatives of <i>witch </i>have died out, but it seems that it may be related to German <i>weihen </i>‘consecrate’ and even, distantly, to English <i>victim </i>(etymologically ‘someone killed in a religious ritual’), so the word’s underlying signification is of a ‘priestess’. <i>Wicked </i>was derived from Old English <i>wicca </i>‘wizard’, the masculine form of <i>wicce</i>, ancestor of modern English <i>witch</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/wicked" target="_blank">wicked</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/witch">witch (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">wicce</span> "female magician, sorceress," in later use especially "a woman supposed to have dealings with the devil or evil spirits and to be able by their cooperation to perform supernatural acts," fem. of Old English <span class="foreign">wicca</span> "sorcerer, wizard, man who practices witchcraft or magic," from verb <span class="foreign">wiccian</span> "to practice witchcraft" (compare Low German <span class="foreign">wikken</span>, <span class="foreign">wicken</span> "to use witchcraft," <span class="foreign">wikker</span>, <span class="foreign">wicker</span> "soothsayer").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; OED says of uncertain origin; Liberman says "None of the proposed etymologies of <span class="foreign">witch</span> is free from phonetic or semantic difficulties." Klein suggests connection with Old English <span class="foreign">wigle</span> "divination," and <span class="foreign">wig, wih</span> "idol." Watkins says the nouns represent a Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wikkjaz</span> "necromancer" (one who wakes the dead), from PIE <span class="foreign">*weg-yo-</span>, from <span class="foreign">*weg-</span> (2) "to be strong, be lively" (see <a href="/etym/s/wake" target="_blank">wake</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; That <span class="foreign">wicce</span> once had a more specific sense than the later general one of "female magician, sorceress" perhaps is suggested by the presence of other words in Old English describing more specific kinds of magical craft. In the Laws of Ælfred (c.890), witchcraft was specifically singled out as a woman's craft, whose practitioners were not to be suffered to live among the West Saxons:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Ða fæmnan þe gewuniað onfon gealdorcræftigan &amp; scinlæcan &amp; wiccan, ne læt þu ða libban."&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The other two words combined with it here are <span class="foreign">gealdricge</span>, a woman who practices "incantations," and <span class="foreign">scinlæce</span> "female wizard, woman magician," from a root meaning "phantom, evil spirit." Another word that appears in the Anglo-Saxon laws is <span class="foreign">lyblæca</span> "wizard, sorcerer," but with suggestions of skill in the use of drugs, because the root of the word is <span class="foreign">lybb</span> "drug, poison, charm." <span class="foreign">Lybbestre</span> was a fem. word meaning "sorceress," and <span class="foreign">lybcorn</span> was the name of a certain medicinal seed (perhaps wild saffron). Weekley notes possible connection to Gothic <span class="foreign">weihs</span> "holy" and German <span class="foreign">weihan</span> "consecrate," and writes, "the priests of a suppressed religion naturally become magicians to its successors or opponents." In Anglo-Saxon glossaries, <span class="foreign">wicca</span> renders Latin <span class="foreign">augur</span> (c. 1100), and <span class="foreign">wicce</span> stands for "pythoness, <span class="foreign">divinatricem</span>." In the "Three Kings of Cologne" (c. 1400) <span class="foreign">wicca</span> translates <span class="foreign">Magi</span>:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Þe paynyms ... cleped þe iij kyngis <span class="foreign">Magos</span>, þat is to seye wicchis.&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The glossary translates Latin <span class="foreign">necromantia</span> (<span class="foreign">"demonum invocatio"</span>) with <span class="foreign">galdre, wiccecræft</span>. The Anglo-Saxon poem called "Men's Crafts" has <span class="foreign">wiccræft</span>, which appears to be the same word, and by its context means "skill with horses." In a c. 1250 translation of "Exodus," <span class="foreign">witches</span> is used of the Egyptian midwives who save the newborn sons of the Hebrews: "Ðe wicches hidden hem for-ðan, Biforen pharaun nolden he ben." <span class="foreign">Witch</span> in reference to a man survived in dialect into 20c., but the fem. form was so dominant by 1601 that <span class="foreign">men-witches</span> or <span class="foreign">he-witch</span> began to be used. Extended sense of "old, ugly, and crabbed or malignant woman" is from early 15c; that of "young woman or girl of bewitching aspect or manners" is first recorded 1740. <span class="foreign">Witch doctor</span> is from 1718; applied to African magicians from 1836.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; At this day it is indifferent to say in the English tongue, 'she is a witch,' or 'she is a wise woman.' [Reginald Scot, "The Discoverie of Witchcraft," 1584]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
