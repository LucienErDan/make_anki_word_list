                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/ride">ride</a> 骑马，骑车，乘坐</span><p>来自古英语 ridan,骑马，乘坐，来自 Proto-Germanic*ridan,骑行，来自 PIE*reidh,骑行，词源 同 road,raid.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ride">ride</a></dt><dd><b>ride: </b><span>[OE] <i>Ride </i>is a widespread Germanic verb, with close relatives in German <i>reiten</i>, Dutch <i>rijden</i>, Swedish <i>rida</i>, and Danish <i>ride</i>. It apparently has connections in the Celtic languages – Irish <i>rīadaim </i>‘ride’ and Gaulish <i>rēda </i>‘chariot’, for instance – but its ultimate provenance is unclear.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/raid" target="_blank">raid</a>, <a href="/etym/s/road" target="_blank">road</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ride">ride (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">ridan</span> "sit or be carried on" (as on horseback), "move forward; rock; float, sail" (class I strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">rad</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">riden</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ridan</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">riða</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">ridan</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">rida</span> "to ride," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">riden</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">rijden</span>, Old High Germn <span class="foreign">ritan</span>, German <span class="foreign">reiten</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*reidh-</span> "to ride" (cognates: Old Irish <span class="foreign">riadaim</span> "I travel," Old Gaulish <span class="foreign">reda</span> "chariot"). Common to Celtic and Germanic, perhaps a loan word from one to the other.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "heckle" is from 1912; that of "have sex with (a woman)" is from mid-13c.; that of "dominate cruelly" is from 1580s. To <span class="foreign">ride out</span> "endure (a storm, etc.) without great damage" is from 1520s. To <span class="foreign">ride shotgun</span> is 1963, from custom of having an armed man beside the driver on the stagecoach in Old West movies to ward off trouble. To <span class="foreign">ride shank's mare</span> "walk" is from 1846 (see <a href="/etym/s/shank" target="_blank">shank</a> (n.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ride">ride (n.)</a></dt><dd>1759, "journey on the back of a horse or in a vehicle," from <a href="/etym/s/ride" target="_blank">ride</a> (v.); slang meaning "a motor vehicle" is recorded from 1930; sense of "amusement park device" is from 1934. Meaning "act of sexual intercourse" is from 1937. To <span class="foreign">take (someone) for a ride</span> "tease, mislead, cheat," is first attested 1925, American English, possibly from underworld sense of "take on a car trip with intent to kill" (1927). Phrase <span class="foreign">go along for the ride</span> in the figurative sense "join in passively" is from 1956. A <span class="foreign">ride cymbal</span> (1956) is used by jazz drummers for keeping up continuous rhythm, as opposed to a <span class="foreign">crash cymbal</span> (<span class="foreign">ride</span> as "rhythm" in jazz slang is recorded from 1936).</dd></dl></div></div>
