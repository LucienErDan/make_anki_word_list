                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/cross">cross</a> 十字，叉字</span><p>来自拉丁词crux, 十字架，刑罚用具，来自PIE*sker，弯，转，词源同ring,curve.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cross">cross</a></dt><dd><b>cross: </b><span>[OE] When the Anglo-Saxons embraced Christianity they acquired <i>cros</i>, in the first instance from Old Irish <i>cross</i>. The word’s ultimate source was Latin <i>crux</i>, which may have been of Phoenician origin (although some have connected it with Latin <i>curvus </i>‘bent’). (<i>Crux </i>itself was borrowed into English in the 18th century.) The cross’s shape formed the basis of the adjectival, adverbial, and verbal uses of the word, and also of <i>across</i>. (The notion of ‘crossing’ also lies behind <i>cruise </i>[17] a probable borrowing from the Dutch <i>kruisen </i>‘cross’.) Derivatives of the Latin word include <i>crucial </i>[18], <i>crucible </i>[15], <i>crucifix </i>[13] (from late Latin <i>crucifixus</i>, literally ‘fixed to a cross’), <i>crusade </i>[16], and <i>excruciate </i>[16].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/crucial" target="_blank">crucial</a>, <a href="/etym/s/crucible" target="_blank">crucible</a>, <a href="/etym/s/crucifix" target="_blank">crucifix</a>, <a href="/etym/s/crusade" target="_blank">crusade</a>, <a href="/etym/s/excruciate" target="_blank">excruciate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cross">cross (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">cros</span> "instrument of Christ's crucifixion; symbol of Christianity" (mid-10c.), from Old Irish <span class="foreign">cros</span>, probably via Scandinavian, from Latin <span class="foreign">crux</span> (accusative <span class="foreign">crucem</span>, genitive <span class="foreign">crucis</span>) "stake, cross" on which criminals were impaled or hanged (originally a tall, round pole); hence, figuratively, "torture, trouble, misery." The word is possibly of Phoenician origin. Replaced Old English <a href="/etym/s/rood" target="_blank">rood</a>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Also from Latin <span class="foreign">crux</span> are Italian <span class="foreign">croce</span>, French <span class="foreign">croix</span>, Spanish and Portuguese <span class="foreign">cruz</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kruis</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kreuz</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; By c. 1200 as "ornamental likeness of the cross, something resembling or in the form of a cross; sign of the cross made with the right hand or with fingers." From mid-14c. as "small cross with a human figure attached; a crucifix;" late 14c. as "outdoor structure or monument in the form of a cross." Also late 14c. as "a cross formed by two lines drawn or cut on a surface; two lines intersecting at right angles; the shape of a cross without regard to religious signification." From late 12c. as a surname.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From c. 1200 in the figurative sense "the burden of a Christian; suffering; a trial or affliction; penance in Christ's name," from Matt. x.38, xvi.24, etc. Theological sense "crucifixion and death of Christ as a necessary part of his mission" is from late 14c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As "a mixing of breeds in the production of animals" from 1760, hence broadly "a mixture of the characteristics of two different things." In pugilism, 1906, from the motion of the blow (1880s as a verb; <span class="foreign">cross-counter</span> (n.) is from 1883).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cross">cross (adj.)</a></dt><dd>developed in early Modern English from the adverb (see <a href="/etym/s/cross" target="_blank">cross</a> (adv.)). Earliest sense is "falling athwart, lying athwart the main direction" (1520s). Meaning "intersecting, lying athwart each other" is from c. 1600.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "adverse, opposed, contrary, opposite" is from 1560s; of persons, "peevish, ill-tempered," from 1630s, probably from the earlier senses of "contrary, athwart," especially with reference to winds and sailing ships. A 19c. emphatic form was <span class="foreign">cross as two sticks</span> (1807), punning on the verb.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Cross-purposes</span> "contradictory intentions" is from 1660s. <span class="foreign">Cross-legged</span> is from 1520s; <span class="foreign">cross-grained</span> is from 1670s of wood; as "opposed in nature or temper" from 1640s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cross">cross (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "make the sign of a cross," from <a href="/etym/s/cross" target="_blank">cross</a> (n.) and in part from French <span class="foreign">croiser</span>. Sense of "to go across, pass from side to side of, pass over" is from c. 1400; that of "to cancel by drawing crossed lines over" is from mid-15c.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From late 14c. as "lie across; intersect;" also "place (two things) crosswise of each other; lay one thing across another." From early 15c. as "mark a cross on." Also in Middle English in now-archaic sense "crucify" (mid-14c.), hence, figuratively, <span class="foreign">crossed</span> "carrying a cross of affliction or penance." Meaning "thwart, obstruct, hinder, oppose" is from 1550s; that of "cause to interbreed" is from 1754. In telegraphy, electricity, etc., in reference to accidental contact of two wires on different circuits or different parts of a circuit that allows part of the current to flow from one to the other, from 1884. Meaning "to cheat" is by 1823.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Cross my heart</span> as a vow is from 1898. To <span class="foreign">cross over</span> as euphemistic for "to die" is from 1930. To <span class="foreign">cross (someone's) path</span> is from 1818. Of ideas, etc., to <span class="foreign">cross (someone's) mind</span> is from 1768; the notion is of something entering the mind as if passing athwart it. Related: <span class="foreign">Crossed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/crossing" target="_blank">crossing</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/cross">cross (adv.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "to the side," from <span class="foreign">on cros</span>, variant of <a href="/etym/s/across" target="_blank">across</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
