                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/please">please</a> 请，使满意</span><p>来自placere,抚慰，安抚，来自PIE*plak,平的，来自PIE*pele,展开，放平，词源同plan,place.引申词义使满意，使愉悦，并用于词义请。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/please">please</a></dt><dd><b>please: </b><span>[14] <i>Please </i>is at the centre of a small family of English words that go back to Latin <i>placēre </i>‘please’ (a derivative of the same base as produced <i>plācāre </i>‘calm, appease’, source of English <i>implacable </i>[16] and <i>placate </i>[17]). Related English words that started life in Latin include <i>complacent</i>, <i>placebo</i>, and <i>placid </i>[17]. It reached English via Old French <i>plaisir</i>, and other derivatives picked up via Old French or Anglo-Norman are <i>plea</i>, <i>plead</i>, <i>pleasant </i>[14], and <i>pleasure </i>[14] (originally a noun use of the verb <i>plaisir</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/complacent" target="_blank">complacent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/implacable" target="_blank">implacable</a>, <a href="/etym/s/placate" target="_blank">placate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/placebo" target="_blank">placebo</a>, <a href="/etym/s/placid" target="_blank">placid</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plea" target="_blank">plea</a>, <a href="/etym/s/plead" target="_blank">plead</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pleasant" target="_blank">pleasant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pleasure" target="_blank">pleasure</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/please">please (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "to be agreeable," from Old French <span class="foreign">plaisir</span> "to please, give pleasure to, satisfy" (11c., Modern French <span class="foreign">plaire</span>, the form of which is perhaps due to analogy of <span class="foreign">faire</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">placere</span> "to be acceptable, be liked, be approved," related to <span class="foreign">placare</span> "to soothe, quiet" (source of Spanish <span class="foreign">placer</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">piacere</span>), possibly from PIE <span class="foreign">*plak-e-</span> "to be calm," via notion of still water, etc., from root <span class="foreign">*plak-</span> (1) "to be flat" (see <a href="/etym/s/placenta" target="_blank">placenta</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "to delight" in English is from late 14c. Inverted use for "to be pleased" is from c. 1500, first in Scottish, and paralleling the evolution of synonymous <a href="/etym/s/like" target="_blank">like</a> (v.). Intransitive sense (<span class="foreign">do as you please</span>) first recorded c. 1500; imperative use (<span class="foreign">please do this</span>), first recorded 1620s, was probably a shortening of <span class="foreign">if it please (you)</span> (late 14c.). Related: <a href="/etym/s/Pleased" target="_blank">Pleased</a>; <span class="foreign">pleasing</span>; <span class="foreign">pleasingly</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Verbs for "please" supply the stereotype polite word ("<span class="foreign">Please</span> come in," short for <span class="foreign">may it please you to ...</span>) in many languages (French, Italian), "But more widespread is the use of the first singular of a verb for 'ask, request' " [Buck, who cites German <span class="foreign">bitte</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">proszę</span>, etc.]. Spanish <span class="foreign">favor</span> is short for <span class="foreign">hace el favor</span> "do the favor." Danish has in this sense <span class="foreign">vær saa god</span>, literally "be so good."</dd></dl></div></div>
