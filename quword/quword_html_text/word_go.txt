                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/go">go</a> 走</span><p>来自PIE*ghe, 释放，走。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/go">go</a></dt><dd><b>go: </b><span>[OE] <i>Go </i>is an ancient verb, traceable back to a prehistoric Indo-European base *<i>ghēi</i>- or *<i>ghē</i>-. This seems to have been relatively unproductive outside the Germanic languages (Sanskrit <i>hā-, hī</i>- ‘leave’ and Greek <i>kikhánō </i>‘reach’ may be descendants of it), but it has provided the basic word for ‘move along, proceed’ in all the Germanic languages, including German <i>gehen</i>, Dutch <i>gaan</i>, Swedish <i>gā</i>, Danish <i>gaa</i>, and English <i>go</i>. In Old and Middle English its past tense was <i>ēode</i>, later <i>yode</i>, a word of uncertain origin, but from about 1500 this was replaced by <i>went</i>, originally the past tense of <i>wend</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/go">go (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">gan</span> "to advance, walk; depart, go away; happen, take place; conquer; observe, practice, exercise," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*gaian</span> (cognates: Old Saxon, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">gan</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gaen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">gaan</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">gan</span>, German <span class="foreign">gehen</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ghe-</span> "to release, let go" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">jihite</span> "goes away," Greek <span class="foreign">kikhano</span> "I reach, meet with"), but there does not seem to be general agreement on a list of cognates.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; A defective verb throughout its recorded history; the Old English past tense was <span class="foreign">eode</span>, a word of uncertain origin but evidently once a different verb (perhaps connected to Gothic <span class="foreign">iddja</span>); it was replaced 1400s by <span class="foreign">went</span>, past tense of <span class="foreign">wenden</span> "to direct one's way" (see <a href="/etym/s/wend" target="_blank">wend</a>). In northern England and Scotland, however, <span class="foreign">eode</span> tended to be replaced by <span class="foreign">gaed</span>, a construction based on <span class="foreign">go</span>. In modern English, only <a href="/etym/s/be" target="_blank">be</a> and <span class="foreign">go</span> take their past tenses from entirely different verbs.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The word in its various forms and combinations takes up 45 columns of close print in the OED. Meaning "cease to exist" is from c. 1200; that of "to appear" (with reference to dress, appearance, etc.) is from late 14c.; that of "to be sold" is from early 15c. Meaning "to be known" (with <span class="foreign">by</span>) is from 1590s; that of "pass into another condition or state" is from 1580s. From c. 1600 as "to wager," hence also "to stand treat," and to <span class="foreign">go (someone) better</span> in wagering (1864). Meaning "say" emerged 1960s in teen slang. Colloquial meaning "urinate or defecate" attested by 1926, euphemistic (compare Old English <span class="foreign">gong</span> "a privy," literally "a going").&#13; &#13; To <span class="foreign">go back on</span> "prove faithless to" is from 1859; to <span class="foreign">go under</span> in the figurative sense "to fail" is from 1849. To <span class="foreign">go places</span> "be successful" is by 1934.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/go">go (n.)</a></dt><dd>1727, "action of going," from use of <a href="/etym/s/go" target="_blank">go</a> (v.) to start a race, etc. Meaning "an incident, an occurrence, affair, piece of business" is from 1796. Meaning "power of going, dash, vigor" is from 1825, colloquial, originally of horses. The sense of "an attempt, a try or turn at doing something" (as in <span class="foreign">give it a go</span>, <span class="foreign">have a go at</span>) is from 1825 (earlier it meant "a delivery of the ball in skittles," 1773). Meaning "something that goes, a success" is from 1876. Phrase <span class="foreign">on the go</span> "in constant motion" is from 1843. Phrase <span class="foreign">from the word go</span> "from the beginning" is by 1834. <span class="foreign">The go</span> "what is in fashion" is from 1793. <span class="foreign">No go</span> "of no use" is from 1825.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/go">go (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"in order," 1951, originally in aerospace jargon, from <a href="/etym/s/go" target="_blank">go</a> (v.).</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/go">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. No matter where you <b>go</b> in life or how old you get, there's always something new to learn about. After all, life is full of surprises. </dt> <dd>不管你生活在哪里，你有多少岁，总有新东西要学习，毕竟，生活总是充满惊喜。</dd> <p>来自金山词霸 每日一句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. We'll <b>go</b> to a meeting in Birmingham and come straight back. </dt> <dd>我们将去伯明翰参加会议，然后马上回来。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. It's a long way to <b>go</b> for two people in their seventies. </dt> <dd>对于两个七十几岁的人来说，这段路太远了。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. His wife wasn't feeling too well and she wanted to <b>go</b> home. </dt> <dd>他的妻子感到有些不舒服，想要回家。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5.
