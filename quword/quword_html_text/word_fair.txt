                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fair">fair</a> 美丽的，漂亮的，公平的</span><p>缩写自古英语faeger, 美丽的，漂亮的，来自PIE*pek, 使美丽，漂亮。词义由美丽引申为公平，公正。比较very, 原义为真实的，even, 原义为平的。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fair">fair</a> 集市，庙会</span><p>来自拉丁语feriae, 宗教节目，宗教集会，词源同festival.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fair">fair</a></dt><dd><b>fair: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>fair</i>, one Germanic and the other Romance. The older, meaning ‘beautiful’ [OE], comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>fagraz</i>, which survives also in Swedish <i>fager </i>‘beautiful’. It derived from a base *<i>fag</i>-, which seems originally to have meant ‘fitting, suitable’ (a variant of it was the ultimate source of <i>fake </i>and possibly also of the now archaic noun <i>fig </i>‘clothes, array’, as in ‘in full fig’).<br><br>Of its main present-day meanings, ‘just, equitable’ developed in the 14th century and ‘not dark’ in the mid 16th century. <i>Fair </i>‘festive event’ [13] comes from Old French <i>feire</i>. This was a descendant of late Latin <i>fēria</i>, a singular use of a noun which in classical times had been used in the plural, <i>fēriae</i>, for ‘holiday’. A close relative of <i>fēriae </i>was the adjective <i>festus </i>‘joyous’, source of English <i>feast, festival, festoon</i>, and <i>fête</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fake" target="_blank">fake</a>, <a href="/etym/s/feast" target="_blank">feast</a>, <a href="/etym/s/festival" target="_blank">festival</a>, <a href="/etym/s/festoon" target="_blank">festoon</a>, <a href="/etym/s/f%C3%AAte" target="_blank">fête</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fig" target="_blank">fig</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fair">fair (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fæger</span> "pleasing to the sight (of persons and body features, also of objects, places, etc.); beautiful, handsome, attractive," of weather, "bright, clear, pleasant; not rainy," also in late Old English "morally good," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fagraz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fagar</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fagr</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">fager</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fagar</span> "beautiful," Gothic <span class="foreign">fagrs</span> "fit"), perhaps from PIE <span class="foreign">*pek-</span> (1) "to make pretty" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">puošiu</span> "I decorate").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The meaning in reference to weather preserves the oldest sense "suitable, agreeable" (opposed to <a href="/etym/s/foul" target="_blank">foul</a> (adj.)). Of the main modern senses of the word, that of "light of complexion or color of hair and eyes, not dusky or sallow" (of persons) is from c. 1200, <span class="foreign">faire</span>, contrasted to <span class="foreign">browne</span> and reflecting tastes in beauty. From early 13c. as "according with propriety; according with justice," hence "equitable, impartial, just, free from bias" (mid-14c.).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Of wind, "not excessive; favorable for a ship's passage," from late 14c. Of handwriting from 1690s. From c. 1300 as "promising good fortune, auspicious." Also from c. 1300 as "above average, considerable, sizable." From 1860 as "comparatively good."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The sporting senses (<span class="foreign">fair ball</span>, <span class="foreign">fair catch</span>, etc.) began to appear in 1856. <span class="foreign">Fair play</span> is from 1590s but not originally in sports. <span class="foreign">Fair-haired</span> in the figurative sense of "darling, favorite" is from 1909. First record of <span class="foreign">fair-weather friends</span> is from 1736 (in a letter from Pope published that year, written in 1730). <span class="foreign">The fair sex</span> "women" is from 1660s, from the "beautiful" sense (<span class="foreign">fair</span> as a noun meaning "a woman" is from early 15c.). <span class="foreign">Fair game</span> "legitimate target" is from 1776, from hunting.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Others, who have not gone to such a height of audacious wickedness, have yet considered common prostitutes as fair game, which they might pursue without restraint. ["Advice from a Father to a Son, Just Entered into the Army and about to Go Abroad into Action," London, 1776]&#13; </blockquote>&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fair">fair (n.)</a></dt><dd>"a stated market in a town or city; a regular meeting to buy, sell, or trade," early 14c., from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">feyre</span> (late 13c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">feire</span>, <span class="foreign">faire</span> "fair, market; feast day," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*feria</span> "holiday, market fair," from Latin <span class="foreign">feriae</span> "religious festivals, holidays," related to <span class="foreign">festus</span> "solemn, festive, joyous" (see <a href="/etym/s/feast" target="_blank">feast</a> (n.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fair">fair (adv.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fægere</span> "beautifully," from <span class="foreign">fæger</span> "beautiful" (see <a href="/etym/s/fair" target="_blank">fair</a> (adj.)). From c. 1300 as "honorably;" mid-14c. as "correctly; direct;" from 1510s as "clearly." <span class="foreign">Fair and square</span> is from c. 1600. <span class="foreign">Fair-to-middling</span> is from 1829, of livestock markets.</dd></dl></div></div>
