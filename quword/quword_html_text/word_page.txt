                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1、pag- + -e.<br />
2、该词的来源有两种说法；其一是：individual sheets of paper "fastened" into a book, pages "fastened" into a book. 其二是：vines fastened by stakes and formed into a trellis, which led to sense of "columns of writing on a scroll." When books replaced scrolls, the word continued to be used.<br />
3、该词还有第二层含义，其来源也有两种说法；其一是：from Medieval Latin pagius "servant," perhaps ultimately from Greek paidion "boy, lad," diminutive of pais (genitive paidos) "child". 其二是：in Latin pagus "countryside," in sense of "boy from the rural regions" (see pagan).</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/page">page</a> 页码，书页</span><p>来自拉丁语pagina,书页，把书页固定成书，来自PIE*pag,固定，词源同fang,pact.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/page">page</a> 门童，仆人，受训骑士，呼叫，传唤</span><p>来自拉丁语pagus,乡村，村民，来自PIE*pag,固定，标记，词源同fang,pact.后用于贵族家庭 担任童子或门童的从农村出来的小男孩以及受训期的小骑士。引申词义呼叫，传唤，传召等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/page">page</a></dt><dd><b>page: </b><span>English has two nouns <i>page</i>. The one that now denotes ‘boy servant’ originally meant simply ‘boy’ [13]. It was borrowed from Old French <i>page</i>, itself an adaptation of Italian <i>paggio</i>. This is generally assumed to have come from Greek <i>paidíon</i>, a diminutive form of <i>pais </i>‘boy, child’ (source of English <i>encyclopedia</i>, <i>paediatric </i>[19], <i>paedophilia </i>[20], <i>pedagogue </i>[14], <i>pederast </i>[18], etc). <i>Page </i>of a book [15] depends ultimately on the notion of ‘fastening’.<br><br>It comes via Old French <i>page </i>from Latin <i>pāgina</i>, a derivative of the base *<i>pāg</i>- ‘fix’ (source also of English <i>pagan</i>, <i>pale </i>‘stake’, etc). This was used for ‘vine-stakes fastened together into a trellis’, which perhaps inspired its metaphorical application to a ‘column of writing’ in a scroll. When books replaced scrolls, <i>pāgina </i>was transferred to ‘page’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/encyclopedia" target="_blank">encyclopedia</a>, <a href="/etym/s/paediatric" target="_blank">paediatric</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedagogue" target="_blank">pedagogue</a>; <a href="/etym/s/pagan" target="_blank">pagan</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pale" target="_blank">pale</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pole" target="_blank">pole</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/page">page (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"sheet of paper," 1580s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">page</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">pagene</span> "page, text" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">pagina</span> "page, leaf of paper, strip of papyrus fastened to others," related to <span class="foreign">pagella</span> "small page," from <span class="foreign">pangere</span> "to fasten," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pag-</span> "to fix" (see <a href="/etym/s/pact" target="_blank">pact</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Earlier <span class="foreign">pagne</span> (12c.), directly from Old French. Usually said to be from the notion of individual sheets of paper "fastened" into a book. Ayto and Watkins offer an alternative theory: vines fastened by stakes and formed into a trellis, which led to sense of "columns of writing on a scroll." When books replaced scrolls, the word continued to be used. Related: <span class="foreign">Paginal</span>. <span class="foreign">Page-turner</span> "book that one can't put down" is from 1974.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/page">page (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"youth, lad, boy of the lower orders," c. 1300, originally also "youth preparing to be a knight," from Old French <span class="foreign">page</span> "a youth, page, servant" (13c.), possibly via Italian <span class="foreign">paggio</span> (Barnhart), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">pagius</span> "servant," perhaps ultimately from Greek <span class="foreign">paidion</span> "boy, lad," diminutive of <span class="foreign">pais</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">paidos</span>) "child."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; But OED considers this unlikely and points instead to Littré's suggestion of a source in Latin <span class="foreign">pagus</span> "countryside," in sense of "boy from the rural regions" (see <a href="/etym/s/pagan" target="_blank">pagan</a>). Meaning "youth employed as a personal attendant to a person of rank" is first recorded mid-15c.; this was transferred from late 18c. to boys who did personal errands in hotels, clubs, etc., also in U.S. legislatures.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/page">page (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to summon or call by name," 1904, from <a href="/etym/s/page" target="_blank">page</a> (n.2), on the notion of "to send a page after" someone. Related: <span class="foreign">Paged</span>; <span class="foreign">paging</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/page">page (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to turn pages," 1620s, from <a href="/etym/s/page" target="_blank">page</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Paged</span>; <span class="foreign">paging</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
