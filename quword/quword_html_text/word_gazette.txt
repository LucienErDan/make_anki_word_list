                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gazette">gazette</a> 报纸</span><p>来自意大利威尼斯方言gazeta, 喜鹊，拟声词，模仿喜鹊的叫声，同时也指威尼斯当地的小铜币。与报纸的联系存在争议，一种说法是把报童比做小喜鹊，另一种说法是一张报纸刚好值一小铜币而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gazette">gazette</a></dt><dd><b>gazette: </b><span>[17] If the <i>Sun </i>or the <i>Mirror </i>were called the <i>22p</i>, they would be echoing the origins of the word <i>gazette</i>. In Renaissance Venice, a ‘newspaper’ was termed casually <i>gazeta de la novita </i>(<i>gazeta </i>for short), literally a ‘pennyworth of news’ – for a <i>gazeta </i>was the name of a small Venetian copper coin (probably a diminutive form of <i>gazza </i>‘magpie’).<br><br>Italian took the word over as <i>gazzetta</i>, and passed it on to English via French. The verbal use of <i>gazette</i>, ‘announce a military promotion officially’, arises from the practice of printing such announcements in the British government newspaper, the <i>London Gazette </i>(first published in the 17th century). The derived <i>gazeteer </i>[17], ultimately from Italian <i>gazzettiere</i>, originally meant ‘journalist’.<br><br>Its current sense ‘index of places’ was inspired by Laurence Echard’s <i>The Gazetteer’s; or a Newsman’s Interpreter: Being a Geographical Index </i>1693.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gazette">gazette (n.)</a></dt><dd>"newspaper," c. 1600, from French <span class="foreign">gazette</span> (16c.), from Italian <span class="foreign">gazzetta</span>, Venetian dialectal <span class="foreign">gazeta</span> "newspaper," also the name of a small copper coin, literally "little magpie," from <span class="foreign">gazza</span>; applied to the monthly newspaper (<span class="foreign">gazeta de la novità</span>) published in Venice by the government, either from its price or its association with the bird (typical of false chatter), or both. First used in English 1665 for the paper issued at Oxford, whither the court had fled from the plague.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The coin may have been so called for its marking; Gamillscheg writes the word is from French <span class="foreign">gai</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/jay" target="_blank">jay</a>). The general story of the origin of the word is broadly accepted, but there are many variations in the details:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; We are indebted to the Italians for the idea of newspapers. The title of their <span class="foreign">gazettas</span> was, perhaps, derived from <span class="foreign">gazzera</span>, a magpie or chatterer; or, more probably, from a farthing coin, peculiar to the city of Venice, called <span class="foreign">gazetta</span>, which was the common price of the newspapers. Another etymologist is for deriving it from the Latin <span class="foreign">gaza</span>, which would colloquially lengthen into <span class="foreign">gazetta</span>, and signify a little treasury of news. The Spanish derive it from the Latin <span class="foreign">gaza</span>, and likewise their <span class="foreign">gazatero</span>, and our <span class="foreign">gazetteer</span>, for a writer of the <span class="foreign">gazette</span> and, what is peculiar to themselves, <span class="foreign">gazetista</span>, for a lover of the gazette. [Isaac Disraeli, "Curiosities of Literature," 1835]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br /><b>Gazzetta</b> It., Sp. <span class="foreign">gazeta</span>, Fr. E. <span class="foreign">gazette</span>; prop. the name of a Venetian coin (from <span class="foreign">gaza</span>), so in Old English. Others derive <span class="foreign">gazette</span> from <span class="foreign">gazza</span> a magpie, which, it is alleged, was the emblem figured on the paper; but it does not appear on any of the oldest Venetian specimens preserved at Florence. The first newspapers appeared at Venice about the middle of the 16th century during the war with Soliman II, in the form of a written sheet, for the privilege of reading which a <span class="foreign">gazzetta</span> (= a <span class="foreign">crazia</span>) was paid. Hence the name was transferred to the news-sheet. [T.C. Donkin, "Etymological Dictionary of the Romance Languages" (based on Diez), 1864]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; GAZETTE. A paper of public intelligence and news of divers countries, first printed at Venice, about the year 1620, and so called (some say) because <span class="foreign">una gazetta</span>, a small piece of Venetian coin, was given to buy or read it. Others derive the name from <span class="foreign">gazza</span>, Italian for magpie, <span class="foreign"> i.e.</span> chatterer.--<span class="foreign">Trusler</span>. A gazette was printed in France in 1631; and one in Germany in 1715. [Haydn's "Dictionary of Dates," 1857]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gazette">gazette (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to announce in the Gazette," 1670s; see <a href="/etym/s/gazette" target="_blank">gazette</a> (n.). The three official journals were published in Britain from c. 1665, twice weekly, and contained lists of appointments, promotions, public notices, etc. Hence, to be <span class="foreign">gazetted</span> was "to be named to a command, etc."</dd></dl></div></div>
