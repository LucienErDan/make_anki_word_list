                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/let">let</a> 让，出租</span><p>来自PIE*le,松开，松手，容许，词源同late,lenient,lassitude.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/let">let</a></dt><dd><b>let: </b><span>[OE] English has two distinct verbs <i>let</i>, of diametrically opposite meaning, but they are probably ultimately related. The one meaning ‘allow’ goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>lǣt</i>- (source also of German <i>lassen </i>and Dutch <i>laten</i>) which, like the related <i>late</i>, is connected with a range of words denoting ‘slowness’ or ‘weariness’. It therefore appears that the underlying etymological meaning of <i>let </i>is ‘let go of something because one is too tired to hold on to it’.<br><br>By the time the verb reached Old English this had developed to ‘leave behind’ and ‘omit to do’, senses now defunct, as well as to ‘allow’. A close relative of the base *<i>lǣt</i>- was *<i>lat</i>-, direct ancestor of English <i>late</i>. From this was formed the Germanic verb *<i>latjan</i>, which gave English its other verb <i>let</i>, meaning ‘prevent’, now largely obsolete except as a noun, in the phrase <i>without let or hindrance </i>or as a tennis term.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/late" target="_blank">late</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/let">let (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">lætan</span> "to allow to remain; let go, leave, depart from; leave undone; to allow; bequeath," also "to rent" (class VII strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">let</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">læten</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*letan</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">latan</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">leta</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">laten</span>, German <span class="foreign">lassen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">letan</span> "to leave, let"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*le-</span> (2) "to let go, slacken" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">lassus</span> "faint, weary," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">leisti</span> "to let, to let loose;" see <a href="/etym/s/lenient" target="_blank">lenient</a>). If that derivation is correct, the primary sense would be "let go through weariness, neglect."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Of blood, from late Old English. To <span class="foreign">let (something) slip</span> originally (1520s) was a reference to hounds on a leash; figurative use from 1540s. To <span class="foreign">let (someone) off</span> "allow to go unpunished" is from 1814. To <span class="foreign">let on</span> "reveal, divulge" is from 1725; to <span class="foreign">let up</span> "cease, stop" is from 1787. <span class="foreign">Let alone</span> "not to mention" is from 1812.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/let">let (n.)</a></dt><dd>"stoppage, obstruction" (obsolete unless in legal contracts), late 12c., from archaic verb <span class="foreign">letten</span> "to hinder," from Old English <span class="foreign">lettan</span> "hinder, delay," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*latjan</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">lettian</span> "to hinder," Old Norse <span class="foreign">letja</span> "to hold back," Old High German <span class="foreign">lezzen</span> "to stop, check," Gothic <span class="foreign">latjan</span> "to hinder, make late," Old English <span class="foreign">læt</span> "sluggish, slow, late"); see <a href="/etym/s/late" target="_blank">late</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/let">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. She said her future husband would not <b>let</b> her make another film. </dt> <dd>她说她的未婚夫不会让她再拍电影了. (此句用wouldnot <b>let</b>, 意思是“阻止”)</dd> <p>来自《用法词典》</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. Well, at any rate, <b>let</b> me thank you for all you did. </dt> <dd>好吧，不管怎样，还是要感谢你所做的一切。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. I <b>let</b> the horse drop his head to crop the spring grass. </dt> <dd>我让马低下头啃吃春天的青草。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. If they value these data, <b>let</b> them pay for them. </dt> <dd>他们要是看重这些数据，就让他们出钱买。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. He's pissed. <b>Let</b>'s get out of his way before he starts spewing. </dt> <dd>他很恼火。趁他还没有发作，我们赶快离他远点儿。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/let">let 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
