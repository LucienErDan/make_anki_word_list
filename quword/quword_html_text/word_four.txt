                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/four">four</a></dt><dd><b>four: </b><span>[OE] The distant Indo-European ancestor of English <i>four </i>was *<i>qwetwōr</i>-, which also produced Latin <i>quattuor </i>(whence French <i>quatre</i>, Italian <i>quattro</i>, Spanish <i>cuatro</i>, etc), Greek <i>téssares</i>, Sanskrit <i>catvāras</i>, Russian <i>chetvero</i>, and Welsh <i>pedwar</i>. Its Germanic descendant was *<i>petwor</i>-, from which come German and Dutch <i>vier</i>, Swedish <i>fyra</i>, and English <i>four</i>.<br><br>Amongst the word’s perhaps more surprising relatives in English are <i>carfax </i>‘crossroads’ (ultimately from a post-classical Latin compound which meant literally ‘four forks’), <i>quarantine </i>(denoting etymologically a ‘period of 40 days’), <i>quire </i>‘set of four sheets of paper’, and <i>trapeze </i>(literally ‘four-footed’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/carfax" target="_blank">carfax</a>, <a href="/etym/s/quarantine" target="_blank">quarantine</a>, <a href="/etym/s/quire" target="_blank">quire</a>, <a href="/etym/s/trapeze" target="_blank">trapeze</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/four">four (n., adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">feower</span> "four; four times," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fedwor-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fiuwar</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fiower</span>, <span class="foreign">fiuwer</span>, Frankish <span class="foreign">*fitter-</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vier</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fior</span>, German <span class="foreign">vier</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fjorir</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">fire</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">fyra</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fidwor</span> "four"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*kwetwer-</span> "four" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">catvarah</span>, Avestan <span class="foreign">čathwaro</span>, Persian <span class="foreign">čatvar</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">tessares</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">quattuor</span>, Oscan <span class="foreign">petora</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">četyre</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">keturi</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">cethir</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">pedwar</span>). The phonetic evolution of the Germanic forms has not been fully explained; Watkins explains the <span class="foreign">-f-</span> as being from the following number (Modern English <span class="foreign">five</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To be <span class="foreign">on all fours</span> is from 1719; earlier <span class="foreign">on all four</span> (14c.). <span class="foreign">Four-letter word</span> as a euphemism for one of the short words generally regarded as offensive or objectionable is attested from 1923; <span class="foreign">four-letter man</span> is recorded from 1920 (apparently as a euphemism for <span class="foreign">a shit</span>). Compare Latin <span class="foreign">homo trium litterarum</span>, literally "three-letter man," a euphemism for <span class="foreign">fur</span> "a thief." A <span class="foreign">four-in-hand</span> (1793) was a carriage drawn by four horses driven by one person; in the sense of "loosely tied necktie" it is attested from 1892. To study <span class="foreign">The History of the Four Kings</span> (1760, compare French <span class="foreign">Livres des Quatre Rois</span>) contains an old euphemistic slang phrase for "a pack of cards," from the time when card-playing was considered a wicked pastime for students. Slang <span class="foreign">4-1-1</span> "essential information" (by 1993) is from the telephone number called to get customer information. The <span class="foreign">four-color problem</span> so called from 1879. The <span class="foreign">four-minute mile</span> was attained 1954.</dd></dl></div></div>
