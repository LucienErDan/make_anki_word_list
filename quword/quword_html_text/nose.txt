            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/nose">nose</a> 鼻子</span><p>来自PIE*nas,鼻子，可能来自拟声词，词源同nasal,nostril.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/nose">nose</a></dt><dd><b>nose: </b><span>[OE] <i>Nose </i>is the English member of a widespread family of ‘nose’-words that trace their ancestry back to Indo-European *<i>nas</i>-. This has produced Latin <i>nāsus </i>(source of English <i>nasal </i>[17]), Sanskrit <i>nás</i>, Lithuanian <i>nósis</i>, and Russian, Polish, Czech, and Serbo-Croat <i>nos</i>. Its Germanic descendant has differentiated into German <i>nase</i>, Dutch <i>neus</i>, Swedish <i>näsa</i>, Danish <i>næse</i>, and English <i>nose</i>. <i>Nozzle </i>[17] and <i>nuzzle </i>[15] are probably derived from <i>nose</i>, and <i>ness </i>‘promontory, headland’ [OE] (now encountered only in place-names) is related to it.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/nasal" target="_blank">nasal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ness" target="_blank">ness</a>, <a href="/etym/s/nostril" target="_blank">nostril</a>, <a href="/etym/s/nozzle" target="_blank">nozzle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/nuzzle" target="_blank">nuzzle</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/nose">nose (v.)</a></dt><dd>"perceive the smell of," 1570s; "pry, search," 1640s, from <a href="/etym/s/nose" target="_blank">nose</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Nosed</span>; <span class="foreign">nosing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/nose">nose (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">nosu</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*nusus</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">nös</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">nose</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">neus</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">nasa</span>, German <span class="foreign">Nase</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*nas-</span> "nose" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">nasa</span>, Old Persian <span class="foreign">naham</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">nasu</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">nosis</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">nasus</span> "nose"). Used of any prominent or projecting part from 1530s. (<span class="foreign">nose cone</span> in the space rocket sense is from 1949). Used to indicate "something obvious" from 1590s. Meaning "odor, scent" is from 1894.&#13; <blockquote>Kiv, It could bee no other then his owne manne, that had thrust his nose so farre out of ioynte. ["Barnabe Riche His Farewell to Military Profession," 1581]</blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Pay through the nose</span> (1670s) seems to suggest "bleed." Many extended meanings are from the horse-racing sense of "length of a horse's nose," as a measure of distance between two finishers (1908). To <span class="foreign">turn up one's nose</span> "show disdain" is from 1818 (earlier <span class="foreign">hold up one's nose</span>, 1570s); similar notion in <span class="foreign">look down one's nose</span> (1921). To say something is <span class="foreign">under (one's) nose</span> "in plain view" is from 1540s.</dd></dl></div></div>
