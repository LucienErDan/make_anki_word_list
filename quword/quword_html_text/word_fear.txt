                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fear">fear</a> 害怕</span><p>来自PIE*per, 向前，尝试，词源同far, peril, experience. 由尝试引申词义风险，害怕。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fear">fear</a></dt><dd><b>fear: </b><span>[OE] ‘Being frightened’ seems to be a comparatively recent development in the semantic history of the word <i>fear</i>. In Old English times the verb meant ‘be afraid’, but the noun meant ‘sudden terrible event, danger’, and it did not develop its modern sense – possibly under the influence of the verb – until the 13th century (the Old English nouns for ‘fear’ were <i>ege </i>and <i>fyrhto</i>, source of modern English <i>fright</i>).<br><br>Related words, such as German <i>gefahr </i>and Dutch <i>gevaar</i>, both meaning ‘danger’, confirm that this is the earlier sense (as would Latin <i>perīculum </i>‘danger’ – source of English <i>peril </i>– if, as has been suggested, it too is connected). Taking the search wider, possible links with Latin <i>perītus </i>‘experienced’, Greek <i>peráō </i>‘go through’, and English <i>fare </i>‘go’ point to an underlying meaning ‘what one undergoes, experience’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/peril" target="_blank">peril</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fear">fear (n.)</a></dt><dd>Middle English <span class="foreign">fere</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">fær</span> "calamity, sudden danger, peril, sudden attack," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*feraz</span> "danger" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">far</span> "ambush," Old Norse <span class="foreign">far</span> "harm, distress, deception," Dutch <span class="foreign">gevaar</span>, German <span class="foreign">Gefahr</span> "danger"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*per-</span> "to try, risk," a form of verbal root <span class="foreign">*per-</span> (3) "to lead, pass over" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">periculum</span> "trial, risk, danger;" Greek <span class="foreign">peria</span> "trial, attempt, experience," Old Irish <span class="foreign">aire</span> "vigilance," Gothic <span class="foreign">ferja</span> "watcher"); related to <span class="foreign">*per-</span> (1) "forward, through" (see <a href="/etym/s/per" target="_blank">per</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "state of being afraid, uneasiness caused by possible danger" developed by late 12c. Some Old English words for "fear" as we now use it were <span class="foreign">fyrhto</span>, <span class="foreign">fyrhto</span>; as a verb, <span class="foreign">ondrædan</span>. Meaning "feeling of dread and reverence for God" is from c. 1400. To <span class="foreign">put the fear of God</span> (into someone) "intimidate, cause to cower" is by 1888, from the common religious phrase; the extended use was often at first in colonial contexts:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Thus then we seek to put "the fear of God" into the natives at the point of the bayonet, and excuse ourselves for the bloody work on the plea of the benefits which we intend to confer afterwards. [Felix Adler, "The Religion of Duty," 1905]&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fear">fear (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">færan</span> "to terrify, frighten," from a Proto-Germanic verbal form of the root of <a href="/etym/s/fear" target="_blank">fear</a> (n.). Cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">faron</span> "to lie in wait," Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vaeren</span> "to fear," Old High German <span class="foreign">faren</span> "to plot against," Old Norse <span class="foreign">færa</span> "to taunt."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Originally transitive in English; long obsolete in this sense but somewhat revived in digital gaming via "fear" spells, which matches the old sense "drive away by fear," attested early 15c. Meaning "feel fear" is late 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Feared</span>; <span class="foreign">fearing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
