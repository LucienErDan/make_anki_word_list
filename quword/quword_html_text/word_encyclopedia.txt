                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/encyclopedia">encyclopedia</a> 百科全书</span><p>en-, 进入，使。-cycle, 圈。-ped, 小孩，教育，词源同few, pedagogy,pedophilia.原指教育小孩的通识教育丛书，后指百科全书。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/encyclopedia">encyclopedia</a></dt><dd><b>encyclopedia: </b><span>[16] Etymologically, <i>encyclopedia </i>means ‘general education’. It is a medieval formation, based on the Greek phrase <i>egkúklios paideíā </i>(<i>egkúklios</i>, a compound adjective formed from the prefix <i>en</i>- ‘in’ and <i>kúklos </i>‘circle’ – source of English <i>cycle </i>– meant originally ‘circular’, and hence ‘general’, and is the ultimate source of English <i>encyclical </i>[17]; <i>paideíā </i>‘education’ was a derivative of <i>país </i>‘boy, child’, which has given English <i>paederast </i>[18], <i>paedophilia </i>[20], <i>pedagogue </i>[14], <i>pedant </i>[16], and <i>paediatrician </i>[20]).<br><br>This referred to the general course of education which it was customary to give a child in classical Greece, and after it was merged into a single word <i>egkuklopaideíā </i>and transmitted via medieval Latin <i>encyclopedia </i>into English, it retained that meaning at first. However, in the 17th century the term began to be applied to compendious reference works (the first, or at least the one which did most to establish the name, was perhaps that of J H Alsted in 1632).<br><br>The <i>Encyclopedia Britannica </i>was first published in 1768.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cycle" target="_blank">cycle</a>, <a href="/etym/s/encyclical" target="_blank">encyclical</a>, <a href="/etym/s/paederast" target="_blank">paederast</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedagogue" target="_blank">pedagogue</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedant" target="_blank">pedant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pediatrician" target="_blank">pediatrician</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/encyclopedia">encyclopedia (n.)</a></dt><dd>1530s, "general course of instruction," from Modern Latin <span class="foreign">encyclopaedia</span> (c. 1500), thought to be a false reading by Latin authors of Greek <span class="foreign">enkyklios paideia</span> taken as "general education," but literally "training in a circle," i.e. the "circle" of arts and sciences, the essentials of a liberal education; from <span class="foreign">enkyklios</span> "circular," also "general" (from <span class="foreign">en</span> "in;" see <a href="/etym/s/in" target="_blank">in</a> + <span class="foreign">kyklos</span> "circle;" see <a href="/etym/s/cycle" target="_blank">cycle</a> (n.)) + <span class="foreign">paideia</span> "education, child-rearing," from <span class="foreign">pais</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">paidos</span>) "child" (see <a href="/etym/s/pedo-" target="_blank">pedo-</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Modern sense of "reference work arranged alphabetically" is from 1640s, often applied specifically to the French <span class="foreign">"Encyclopédie ou Dictionnaire raisonné des Sciences, des Arts, et des Métiers"</span> (1751-65). Related: <span class="foreign">Encyclopedist</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
