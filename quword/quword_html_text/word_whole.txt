                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/whole">whole</a> 全部的</span><p>来自古英语hal,整个的，完整的，健康的，来自PIE*kailo,整个的，词源同health,holy,hollow.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/whole">whole</a></dt><dd><b>whole: </b><span>[OE] <i>Whole </i>is at the centre of a tightlyknit family of English words descended from prehistoric Germanic *<i>khailaz </i>‘undamaged’ (the other members – <i>hail </i>‘salute’, <i>hale</i>, <i>hallow</i>, <i>heal</i>, <i>health</i>, and <i>holy </i>– have have branched off in different semantic directions, but <i>whole </i>has stayed fairly close to its source). The Germanic form, which also produced German <i>heil</i>, Dutch <i>heel</i>, and Swedish and Danish <i>hel</i>, went back to an Indo-European *<i>qoilos</i>, source also of Russian <i>celyj </i>‘whole’ and Welsh <i>coel </i>‘good omen’. <i>Hale </i>[13] originated as a northern variant of <i>whole </i>(whose <i>wh</i>- spelling emerged in the 16th century).<br><br>The compound <i>wholesome </i>was probably formed in Old English, but it is not recorded until the 12th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/hail" target="_blank">hail</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hale" target="_blank">hale</a>, <a href="/etym/s/hallow" target="_blank">hallow</a>, <a href="/etym/s/heal" target="_blank">heal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/health" target="_blank">health</a>, <a href="/etym/s/holy" target="_blank">holy</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/whole">whole (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hal</span> "entire, whole; unhurt, uninjured, safe; healthy, sound; genuine, straightforward," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*haila-</span> "undamaged" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">hel</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">heill</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hal</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">hiel</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">heel</span>, Old High German, German <span class="foreign">heil</span> "salvation, welfare"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*kailo-</span> "whole, uninjured, of good omen" (cognates: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">celu</span> "whole, complete;" see <a href="/etym/s/health" target="_blank">health</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The spelling with <a href="/etym/s/wh-" target="_blank">wh-</a> developed early 15c. The sense in <span class="foreign">whole number</span> is from early 14c. <span class="foreign">Whole milk</span> is from 1782. <span class="foreign">On the whole</span> "considering all facts or circumstances" is from 1690s. For phrase <span class="foreign">whole hog</span>, see <a href="/etym/s/hog" target="_blank">hog</a> (n.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/whole">whole (n.)</a></dt><dd>"entire body or company; the full amount," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/whole" target="_blank">whole</a> (adj.).</dd></dl></div></div>
