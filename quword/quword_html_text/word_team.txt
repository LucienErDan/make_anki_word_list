                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/team">team</a> 团队，队伍</span><p>来自古英语 team,分娩，后代，家族，种族，来自 Proto-Germanic*taumaz,拉出，引导，来自 PIE*deuk,拉出，引申，词源同 duct,duke,tow.后引申词义团队，队伍。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/team">team</a></dt><dd><b>team: </b><span>[OE] The etymological notion underlying the word <i>team </i>is ‘pulling’. It goes back ultimately to the Indo-European base *<i>deuk</i>- ‘pull’, which also produced Latin <i>dūcere </i>‘pull, lead’ (source of English <i>abduct</i>, <i>duke</i>, etc). Its Germanic descendant was *<i>taukh</i>-. From this was derived a noun *<i>taugmaz</i>, whose later form *<i>taumaz </i>gave English <i>team</i>.<br><br>This originally denoted a group of animals harnessed together to ‘pull’ a load, but the modern sense ‘group of people acting together’ did not emerge from this until the 16th century. Another strand in the meaning of the base is ‘giving birth, off-spring’ (presumably based on the notion of children being ‘drawn’ forth from the womb). This has now disappeared from <i>team</i>, but traces of it can still be detected in the related <i>teem </i>[OE], whose modern connotations of ‘abundance’ go back to an earlier ‘bring forth offspring prolifically’.<br><br>From the same source come English <i>tie </i>and <i>tow</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/abduct" target="_blank">abduct</a>, <a href="/etym/s/duct" target="_blank">duct</a>, <a href="/etym/s/duke" target="_blank">duke</a>, <a href="/etym/s/educate" target="_blank">educate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/teem" target="_blank">teem</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tie" target="_blank">tie</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tow" target="_blank">tow</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/team">team (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">team</span> "descendant, family, race, line; child-bearing, brood; company, band; set of draft animals yoked together," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*tau(h)maz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">taumr</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">tam</span> "bridle; progeny, line of descent," Dutch <span class="foreign">toom</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">zoum</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zaum</span> "bridle"), probably literally "that which draws," from PIE <span class="foreign">*douk-mo-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*deuk-</span> "to pull" (see <a href="/etym/s/duke" target="_blank">duke</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Applied in Old English to groups of persons working together for some purpose, especially "group of people acting together to bring suit;" modern sense of "persons associated in some joint action" is from 1520s. <span class="foreign">Team spirit</span> is recorded from 1928. <span class="foreign">Team player</span> attested from 1886, originally in baseball.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/team">team (v.)</a></dt><dd>1550s, "to harness beasts in a team," from <a href="/etym/s/team" target="_blank">team</a> (n.). From 1841 as "drive a team." The meaning "to come together as a team" (usually with <span class="foreign">up</span>) is attested from 1932. Transitive sense "to use (something) in conjunction" (with something else) is from 1948. Related: <span class="foreign">Teamed</span>; <span class="foreign">teaming</span>. The Old English verb, <span class="foreign">teaman</span>, <span class="foreign">tieman</span>, is attested only in the sense "bring forth, beget, engender, propagate."</dd></dl></div></div>
