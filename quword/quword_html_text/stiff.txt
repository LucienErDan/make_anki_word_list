2. PIE *stipos-, from root *steip- "press together, pack, cram" => stone, stipe, constipation, stevedore.<br />
3. => Latin stipare "pack down, press," stipes "post, tree trunk".<br />
4. stiffen => stiff.<br />
5. akin to stifle, steeve.<br />
6. 林正英的电影：《僵尸先生》：《Mr. Stiff Corpse》。<br />
7. 谐音“是呆夫、死敌缚”。<br />

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/stiff">stiff</a> 硬的，僵硬的，生硬的，艰难的</span><p>来自古英语 stif,硬的，僵硬的，来自 Proto-Germanic*stifaz,僵硬的，无弹性的，来自 PIE*steip, 压紧，打包，塞满，可能来自 PIE*steu,推，挤，击打，词源同 steep,stoop,stuff.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stiff">stiff</a></dt><dd><b>stiff: </b><span>[OE] <i>Stiff </i>goes back to prehistoric Germanic *<i>stīfaz </i>‘inflexible’, source also of German <i>steif</i>, Dutch <i>stiff</i>, Swedish <i>styf</i>, and Danish <i>stiv</i>. This in turn was descended from an Indo-European *<i>stīpos</i>, a derivative of the same base as produced Latin <i>stīpāre </i>‘press, pack’ (source of English <i>constipate </i>and <i>stevedore</i>), Latvian <i>stipt </i>‘stiffen’, and Lithuanian <i>stiprùs </i>‘strong’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/constipation" target="_blank">constipation</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stevedore" target="_blank">stevedore</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stiff">stiff (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">stif</span> "rigid, inflexible," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stifaz</span> "inflexible" (cognates: Dutch <span class="foreign">stijf</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">stif</span>, German <span class="foreign">steif</span> "stiff;" Old Norse <span class="foreign">stifla</span> "choke"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*stipos-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*steip-</span> "press together, pack, cram" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">styayate</span> "coagulates," <span class="foreign">stima</span> "slow;" Greek <span class="foreign">stia</span>, <span class="foreign">stion</span> "small stone," <span class="foreign">steibo</span> "press together;" Latin <span class="foreign">stipare</span> "pack down, press," <span class="foreign">stipes</span> "post, tree trunk;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">stipti</span> "stiffen," <span class="foreign">stiprus</span> "strong;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">stena</span> "wall"). Of battles and competitions, from mid-13c.; of liquor, from 1813. To <span class="foreign">keep a stiff upper lip</span> is attested from 1815. Related: <span class="foreign">Stiffly</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stiff">stiff (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to make stiff," from <a href="/etym/s/stiff" target="_blank">stiff</a> (adj.). Meaning "fail to tip" is from 1939, originally among restaurant and hotel workers, probably from <span class="foreign">stiff</span> (n.), perhaps in slang sense of "corpse" (because dead men pay no tips), or from the "contemptible person" sense. Extended by 1950 to "cheat."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/stiff">stiff (n.)</a></dt><dd>"corpse, dead body," 1859, slang, from <a href="/etym/s/stiff" target="_blank">stiff</a> (adj.) which had been associated with notion of rigor mortis since c. 1200. Meaning "working man" first recorded 1930, from earlier genitive sense of "contemptible person," but sometimes merely "man, fellow" (1882). Slang meaning "something or someone bound to lose" is 1890 (originally of racehorses), from notion of "corpse."</dd></dl></div></div>
