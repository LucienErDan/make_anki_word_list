                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/kind">kind</a> 种类，友好的</span><p>词源同kin,亲属，亲戚。即同民族的，同种族的，引申词义同类的，相似的，以及友好的，亲切的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/kind">kind</a></dt><dd><b>kind: </b><span>[OE] <i>Kind </i>the noun and <i>kind </i>the adjective are ultimately the same word, but they split apart in pre-historic times. Their common source was Germanic *<i>kunjam</i>, the ancestor of English <i>kin</i>. From it, using the collective prefix *<i>ga</i>- and the abstract suffix *-<i>diz</i>, was derived the noun *<i>gakundiz</i>, which passed into Old English as <i>gecynde </i>‘birth, origin, nature, race’.<br><br>The prefix <i>ge</i>- disappeared in the early Middle English period. Germanic *<i>gakundiz </i>formed the basis of an adjective, *<i>gakundjaz</i>, which in Old English converged with its source to produce <i>gecynde</i>. It meant ‘natural, innate’, but gradually progressed via ‘of noble birth’ and ‘well-disposed by nature’ to (in the 14th century) ‘benign, compassionate’ (a semantic development remarkably similar to that of the distantly related <i>gentle</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/kin" target="_blank">kin</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/kind">kind (n.)</a></dt><dd>"class, sort, variety," from Old English <span class="foreign">gecynd</span> "kind, nature, race," related to <span class="foreign">cynn</span> "family" (see <a href="/etym/s/kin" target="_blank">kin</a>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kundjaz</span> "family, race," from PIE <span class="foreign">*gene-</span> "to give birth, beget" (see <a href="/etym/s/genus" target="_blank">genus</a>). Ælfric's rendition of "the Book of Genesis" into Old English came out <span class="foreign">gecyndboc</span>. The prefix disappeared 1150-1250. No exact cognates beyond English, but it corresponds to adjective endings such as Goth <span class="foreign">-kunds</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">-kund</span>. Also in English as a suffix (<a href="/etym/s/mankind" target="_blank">mankind</a>, etc.). Other earlier, now obsolete, senses in English included "character, quality derived from birth" and "manner or way natural or proper to anyone." Use in phrase <span class="foreign">a kind of</span> (1590s) led to colloquial extension as adverb (1804) in phrases such as <span class="foreign">kind of stupid</span> ("a kind of stupid (person)").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/kind">kind (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"friendly, deliberately doing good to others," from Old English <span class="foreign">gecynde</span> "natural, native, innate," originally "with the feeling of relatives for each other," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kundi-</span> "natural, native," from <span class="foreign">*kunjam</span> "family" (see <a href="/etym/s/kin" target="_blank">kin</a>), with collective prefix <span class="foreign">*ga-</span> and abstract suffix <span class="foreign">*-iz</span>. Sense development from "with natural feelings," to "well-disposed" (c. 1300), "benign, compassionate" (c. 1300).</dd></dl></div></div>
