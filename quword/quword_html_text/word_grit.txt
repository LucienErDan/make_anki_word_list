                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/grit">grit</a> 沙粒，盐粒</span><p>来自PIE*ghrei, 刮，擦，磨，词源同gravel, grind. 指沙粒，盐粒。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grit">grit</a></dt><dd><b>grit: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, <i>grit </i>is ‘something produced by pounding’. Prehistoric Indo- European *<i>ghrēu</i>- denoted ‘rub, pound, crush’, and from it came Germanic *<i>greutam </i>‘tiny particles of crushed or pounded rock’, hence ‘sand, gravel’. Its modern descendants include English <i>grit </i>and German <i>griess </i>‘gravel, grit, coarse sand’, and it was also used in the formation of the Old English word for ‘pearl’, <i>meregrot</i>, literally ‘sea-pebble’, an alteration of Latin <i>margarīta </i>‘pearl’. <i>Groats </i>‘husked grain’ [OE] comes from the same source.<br><br>The sense ‘determination, resolve’ originated in the USA in the early 19th century, presumably as a metaphorical extension of <i>grit </i>meaning ‘hard sandstone’ (as in <i>millstone grit</i>).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/groats" target="_blank">groats</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grit">grit (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">greot</span> "sand, dust, earth, gravel," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*greutan</span> "tiny particles of crushed rock" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">griot</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">gret</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">grjot</span> "rock, stone," German <span class="foreign">Grieß</span> "grit, sand"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ghreu-</span> "rub, grind" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">grudas</span> "corn, kernel," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">gruda</span> "clod"). Sense of "pluck, spirit, firmness of mind" first recorded American English, 1808.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; If he hadn't a had the clear grit in him, and showed teeth and claws, they'd a nullified him so, you wouldn't have see'd a grease spot of him no more. [Thomas Chandler Haliburton, "Sam Slick in England," 1843] &#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/grit">grit (v.)</a></dt><dd>"make a grating sound," 1762, probably from <a href="/etym/s/grit" target="_blank">grit</a> (n.). Meaning "to grate, grind" is from 1797. Related: <span class="foreign">Gritted</span>; <span class="foreign">gritting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
