                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/dog">dog</a> 狗</span><p>词源不详，神秘词。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dog">dog</a></dt><dd><b>dog: </b><span>[11] <i>Dog </i>is one of the celebrated mystery words of English etymology. It appears once in late Old English, in the <i>Prudentius glosses</i>, where it translates Latin <i>canis</i>, but its use does not seem to have proliferated until the 13th century, and it did not replace the native <i>hound </i>as the main word for the animal until the 16th century. It has no known relatives of equal antiquity in other European languages, although several borrowed it in the 16th and 17th centuries for particular sorts of ‘dog’: German <i>dogge </i>‘large dog, such as a mastiff’, for instance, French <i>dogue </i>‘mastiff’, and Swedish <i>dogg </i>‘bulldog’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dog">dog (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">docga</span>, a late, rare word, used in at least one Middle English source in reference to a powerful breed of canine. The word forced out Old English <span class="foreign">hund</span> (the general Germanic and Indo-European word; see <a href="/etym/s/canine" target="_blank">canine</a>) by 16c. and subsequently was picked up in many continental languages (French <span class="foreign">dogue</span> (16c.), Danish <span class="foreign">dogge</span>, German <span class="foreign">Dogge</span> (16c.)), but the origin remains one of the great mysteries of English etymology.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Many expressions -- <span class="foreign">a dog's life</span> (c. 1600), <span class="foreign">go to the dogs</span> (1610s), etc. -- reflect earlier hard use of the animals as hunting accessories, not pampered pets. In ancient times, "the dog" was the worst throw in dice (attested in Greek, Latin, and Sanskrit, where the word for "the lucky player" was literally "the dog-killer"), which plausibly explains the Greek word for "danger," <span class="foreign">kindynas</span>, which appears to be "play the dog."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Slang meaning "ugly woman" is from 1930s; that of "sexually aggressive man" is from 1950s. Adjectival phrase <span class="foreign">dog-eat-dog</span> attested by 1850s. <span class="foreign">Dog tag</span> is from 1918. To <span class="foreign">dog-ear</span> a book is from 1650s; <span class="foreign">dog-eared</span> in extended sense of "worn, unkempt" is from 1894. <blockquote>Notwithstanding, as a dog hath a day, so may I perchance have time to declare it in deeds. [Princess Elizabeth, 1550]&#13; </blockquote>Phrase <span class="foreign">put on the dog</span> "get dressed up" (1934) may look back to the stiff stand-up shirt collars that in the 1890s were the height of male fashion (and were known as <span class="foreign">dog-collars</span> at least from 1883), with reference to collars worn by dogs. The common Spanish word for "dog," <span class="foreign">perro</span>, also is a mystery word of unknown origin, perhaps from Iberian. A group of Slavic "dog" words (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pisu</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">pies</span>, Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">pas</span>) likewise are of unknown origin.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dog">dog (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to track like a dog," 1510s, see <a href="/etym/s/dog" target="_blank">dog</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Dogged</span>; <span class="foreign">dogging</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
