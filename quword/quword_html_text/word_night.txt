                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/night">night</a> 夜晚</span><p>来自PIE*nekwt,夜晚，词源同nocturnal,equinox.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/night">night</a></dt><dd><b>night: </b><span>[OE] <i>Night </i>is the English member of an ancient Indo-European family of ‘night’-words, represented in virtually all the modern European languages. The ancestral form was *<i>nokt</i>-, and from this have come Greek <i>núx</i>, Latin <i>nox </i>(source of English <i>nocturnal </i>[15] and <i>nocturne </i>[19], and forerunner of French <i>nuit</i>, Italian <i>notte</i>, and Spanish <i>noche</i>), Welsh <i>nos</i>, Latvian <i>nakts</i>, and Russian <i>noch’</i>. The Germanic descendant of *<i>nokt</i>- was *<i>nakht</i>-, source of modern German and Dutch <i>nacht</i>, Swedish <i>natt</i>, Danish <i>nat</i>, and English <i>night</i>. The only exception to the general European picture is modern Irish <i>oidhche </i>‘night’, a word of unknown origin.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/nocturnal" target="_blank">nocturnal</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/night">night (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">niht</span> (West Saxon <span class="foreign">neaht</span>, Anglian <span class="foreign">næht</span>, <span class="foreign">neht</span>) "night, darkness;" the vowel indicating that the modern word derives from oblique cases (genitive <span class="foreign">nihte</span>, dative <span class="foreign">niht</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*nakht-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Old High German <span class="foreign">naht</span>, Old Frisian and Dutch <span class="foreign">nacht</span>, German <span class="foreign">Nacht</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">natt</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">nahts</span>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Germanic words are from PIE <span class="foreign">*nekwt-</span> "night" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">nuks</span> "a night," Latin <span class="foreign">nox</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">nochd</span>, Sanskrit <span class="foreign">naktam</span> "at night," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">naktis</span> "night," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">nosti</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">noch'</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">henoid</span> "tonight"), according to Watkins, probably from a verbal root <span class="foreign">*neg-</span> "to be dark, be night." For spelling with <span class="foreign">-gh-</span> see <a href="/etym/s/fight" target="_blank">fight</a>.<blockquote>The fact that the Aryans have a common name for night, but not for day (q.v.), is due to the fact that they reckoned by nights. [Weekley]</blockquote>Compare German <span class="foreign">Weihnachten</span> "Christmas." In early times, the day was held to begin at sunset, so Old English <span class="foreign">monanniht</span> "Monday night" was the night before Monday, or what we would call <span class="foreign">Sunday night</span>. The Greeks, by contrast, counted their days by mornings.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; To <span class="foreign">work nights</span> preserves the Old English genitive of time. <span class="foreign">Night shift</span> is attested from 1710 in the sense of "garment worn by a woman at night" (see <a href="/etym/s/shift" target="_blank">shift</a> (n.1)); meaning "gang of workers employed after dark" is from 1839. <span class="foreign">Night soil</span> "excrement" (1770) is so called because it was removed (from cesspools, etc.) after dark. <span class="foreign">Night train</span> attested from 1838. <span class="foreign">Night life</span> "habitual nocturnal carousing" attested from 1852.</dd></dl></div></div>
