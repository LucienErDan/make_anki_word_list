                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1.  金酒有许多称呼：香港、广东地区称为毡酒；台北称为琴酒，又因其含有特殊的杜松子味道，所以又被称为杜松子酒。<br />
2. 金酒，又名叫杜松子酒（geneva）或琴酒，最先由荷兰生产，在英国大量生产后闻名于世，是世界第一大类的烈酒。<br />
3. “金酒”是该词的音译，毡酒、琴酒也是该词的音译。<br />
4. engine => gin.<br />
5. One is short of engine, the other is short of geneva.<br />
6. geneva => gin.<br />
7. juniper => *genever, *genevre => geneva (remodelled and influenced on the basis of the name of the Swiss city) => gin.<br />
8. begin => gin.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gin">gin</a> 杜松子酒</span><p>词源同juniper, 杜松，刺柏。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/gin">gin</a> 轨棉机</span><p>来自engine的缩写。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gin">gin</a></dt><dd><b>gin: </b><span><i>Gin </i>‘alcoholic drink’ [18] and <i>gin </i>‘trap’ [13] are different words, but both originated as abbreviations. The latter comes from Old French <i>engin </i>(source of English <i>engine</i>), while the former is short for <i>geneva</i>. This now obsolete term for the spirit was borrowed via Dutch <i>genever </i>from Old French <i>genevre</i>, a derivative of Latin <i>jūniperus </i>‘juniper’ (juniper being the chief flavouring agent of gin). English <i>geneva </i>was remodelled on the basis of the name of the Swiss city.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/engine" target="_blank">engine</a>; <a href="/etym/s/juniper" target="_blank">juniper</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gin">gin (n.1)</a></dt><dd>type of distilled drinking alcohol, 1714, shortening of <span class="foreign">geneva</span>, altered (by influence of the name of the Swiss city, with which it has no connection) from Dutch <span class="foreign">genever</span> "gin," literally "juniper" (because the alcohol was flavored with its berries), from Old French <span class="foreign">genevre</span> "the plant juniper" (12c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*jeniperus</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">juniperus</span> "juniper" (see <a href="/etym/s/juniper" target="_blank">juniper</a>). <span class="foreign">Gin and tonic</span> is attested by 1873; <span class="foreign">gin-sling</span> by 1790; <span class="foreign">gin-fizz</span> (with lemon juice and aerated water) is from 1878. <span class="foreign">Gin-mill</span>, U.S. slang for "low-class tavern or saloon where spirits are drunk" (1872) might be a play on the senses from <a href="/etym/s/gin" target="_blank">gin</a> (n.2). British <span class="foreign">gin-palace</span> "gaudily decorated tavern or saloon where spirits are drunk" is from 1831.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The card game <span class="foreign">gin rummy</span> first attested 1941 (described in "Life" that year as the latest Hollywood fad); OED lists it with the entries for the liquor, but the sense connection seems obscure other than as a play on <a href="/etym/s/rummy" target="_blank">rummy</a>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gin">gin (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"machine for separating cotton from seeds," 1796, American English, used earlier of other machineries, especially of war or torture, from Middle English <span class="foreign">gin</span> "ingenious device, contrivance" (c. 1200), from Old French <span class="foreign">gin</span> "machine, device, scheme," shortened form of <span class="foreign">engin</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/engine" target="_blank">engine</a>). The verb in this sense is recorded from 1789. Related: <span class="foreign">Ginned</span>; <span class="foreign">ginning</span>. Middle English had <span class="foreign">ginful</span> "ingenious, crafty; guileful, treacherous" (c. 1300).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gin">gin (v.1)</a></dt><dd>in slang phrase <span class="foreign">gin up</span> "enliven, make more exciting," 1887 (<span class="foreign">ginning</span> is from 1825), perhaps a special use of the verb associated with <a href="/etym/s/gin" target="_blank">gin</a> (n.2) "engine," but perhaps rather or also from <span class="foreign">ginger up</span> in the same sense (1849), which is from <a href="/etym/s/ginger" target="_blank">ginger</a> in sense of "spice, pizzazz;" specifically in reference to the treatment described in the 1811 edition of Grose's slang dictionary under the entry for <span class="foreign">feague</span>:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; ... to put ginger up a horse's fundament, and formerly, as it is said, a live eel, to make him lively and carry his tail well; it is said, a forfeit is incurred by any horse-dealer's servant, who shall shew a horse without first feaguing him. Feague is used, figuratively, for encouraging or spiriting one up.&#13; </blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/gin">gin (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to begin," c. 1200, <span class="foreign">ginnen</span>, shortened form of <span class="foreign">beginnen</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/begin" target="_blank">begin</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
