            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/season">season</a> 季节，当季，添加调料</span><p>来自古法语 seson,播种季节，播种时间，来自拉丁语 satio,播种，过去分词名词格于 serere,</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/season">season</a></dt><dd><b>season: </b><span>[13] A <i>season </i>is etymologically a time of ‘sowing seeds’. The word comes via Old French <i>seson </i>from Latin <i>satiō </i>‘act of sowing’, a derivative of <i>satus</i>, the past participle of <i>serere </i>‘sow, plant’ (which went back to the same Indo- European base that produced English <i>seed</i>, <i>semen</i>, and <i>sow</i>). In post-classical times ‘act of sowing’ evolved into ‘time for sowing’, and by the time it reached Old French it had developed further to any ‘suitable time’.<br><br>The application to ‘any of the four main divisions of the year’ emerged in English in the 14th century. The use of <i>season </i>as a verb, meaning ‘add flavourings to’, had its beginnings in post-classical Latin, and arose as the result of a progression from ‘sow’ through ‘ripen’ to ‘cook thoroughly or well’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/seed" target="_blank">seed</a>, <a href="/etym/s/semen" target="_blank">semen</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sow" target="_blank">sow</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/season">season (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "a period of the year," with reference to weather or work, also "proper time, suitable occasion," from Old French <span class="foreign">seison</span>, <span class="foreign">saison</span> "season, date; right moment, appropriate time" (Modern French <span class="foreign">saison</span>) "a sowing, planting," from Latin <span class="foreign">sationem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">satio</span>) "a sowing, planting," noun of action from past participle stem of <span class="foreign">serere</span> "to sow" (see <a href="/etym/s/sow" target="_blank">sow</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense shifted in Vulgar Latin from "act of sowing" to "time of sowing," especially "spring, regarded as the chief sowing season." In Old Provençal and Old French (and thus in English), this was extended to "season" in general. In other Indo-European languages, generic "season" (of the year) words typically are from words for "time," sometimes with a word for "year" (as in Latin <span class="foreign">tempus (anni)</span>, German <span class="foreign">Jahrzeit</span>). Of game (as in <span class="foreign">out of season</span>) from late 14c. Spanish <span class="foreign">estacion</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">stagione</span> are unrelated, being from Latin <span class="foreign">statio</span> "station."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "time of year during which a place is most frequented" is from 1705. <span class="foreign">Season ticket</span> is attested from 1820.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/season">season (v.)</a></dt><dd>"improve the flavor of by adding spices," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">assaisoner</span> "to ripen, season," from <span class="foreign">a-</span> "to" (see <a href="/etym/s/ad-" target="_blank">ad-</a>) + root of <a href="/etym/s/season" target="_blank">season</a> (n.) on the notion of fruit becoming more palatable as it ripens. Applied to timber by 1540s. In 16c., it also meant "to copulate with."</dd></dl></div></div>
