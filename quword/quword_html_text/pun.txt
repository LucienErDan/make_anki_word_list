                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. 对比：pan, pen, pin, pon-, pun.<br />
2. 谐音“判”---判官----判双关-----判一语双关----判词一语双关。<br />
3. pound => pun.<br />
4. p- + -un (字母u、n正好是双向倒置，它们就像是两个通道，但是通道的末尾都被堵死了、被关上了，是不是非常形象地表达了“双关”的意境啊) => pun <= 盼双关。<br />
5. p- (pipe) + -un (形象“双关”) =》pun: 双向的管道都被关了。<br />
6. 段子手 =》 punster.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pun">pun</a> 双关语</span><p>词源不详，可能缩写自拉丁语punctum,点，刺点，词源同point,punch.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pun">pun</a></dt><dd><b>pun: </b><span>[17] Snappy monosyllables produced by breaking off a piece of a longer word were all the rage in late 17th- and early 18th-century England (<i>mob </i>is a well-known example), and it is thought that <i>pun </i>may be one of them. It seems to be short for <i>pundigrion</i>, a short-lived fanciful 17th- and 18th-century term for a ‘pun’ or ‘quibble’ which may have been adapted from Italian <i>puntiglio </i>‘nice point, quibble’ (source of English <i>punctilious</i>).</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pun">pun (n.)</a></dt><dd>1660s (first attested in Dryden), of uncertain origin, perhaps from <span class="foreign">pundigron</span>, which is perhaps a humorous alteration of Italian <span class="foreign">puntiglio</span> "equivocation, trivial objection," diminutive of Latin <span class="foreign">punctum</span> "point." This is pure speculation. The verb also is attested from 1660s. Related: <span class="foreign">Punned</span>; <span class="foreign">punning</span>.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Pun</span> was prob. one of the clipped words, such as <span class="foreign">cit</span>, <span class="foreign">mob</span>, <span class="foreign">nob</span>, <span class="foreign">snob</span>, which came into fashionable slang at or after the Restoration. [OED]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
