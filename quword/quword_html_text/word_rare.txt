                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/rare">rare</a> 罕有的，罕见的</span><p>来自拉丁语 rarus,稀疏的，薄的，松散的，来自 PIE*ere,分开，网，词源同 retina,reticulate. 引申词义罕有的，罕见的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rare">rare</a></dt><dd><b>rare: </b><span><i>Rare </i>‘uncommon’ [15] and <i>rare </i>‘underdone’ [17] are not the same word. The former was borrowed from Latin <i>rārus</i>, which originally signified ‘having a loose texture, widely separated’ – hence ‘scarce’. It is not known what its ultimate source is. The latter is an alteration of the now obsolete <i>rear </i>‘underdone’ (originally used mainly of eggs: ‘They had at their dinner rear eggs’, <i>Book of the knight of the tower </i>1450), which goes back to Old English <i>hrēr </i>– again of unknown origin.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rare">rare (adj.1)</a></dt><dd>"unusual," late 14c., "thin, airy, porous;" mid-15c., "few in number and widely separated, sparsely distributed, seldom found;" from Old French <span class="foreign">rere</span> "sparse" (14c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">rarus</span> "thinly sown, having a loose texture; not thick; having intervals between, full of empty spaces," from PIE <span class="foreign">*ra-ro-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*ere-</span> "to separate; adjoin" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">rte</span> "besides, except," <span class="foreign">viralah</span> "distant, tight, rare;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">rediku</span> "rare," Old Hittite <span class="foreign">arhaš</span> "border," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">irti</span> "to be dissolved"). "Few in number," hence, "unusual." Related: <span class="foreign">Rareness</span>. In chemistry, <span class="foreign">rare earth</span> is from 1818.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rare">rare (adj.2)</a></dt><dd>"undercooked," 1650s, variant of Middle English <span class="foreign">rere</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">hrere</span> "lightly cooked," probably related to <span class="foreign">hreran</span> "to stir, move, shake, agitate," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hrorjan</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hrera</span> "to stir, move," Old Saxon <span class="foreign">hrorian</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">roeren</span>, German <span class="foreign">rühren</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hroera</span>), from PIE base <span class="foreign">*kere-</span> "to mix, confuse; cook" (cognates: Greek <span class="foreign">kera-</span> "to mix," <span class="foreign">krasis</span> "mixture"). Originally of eggs, not recorded in reference to meat until 1784, and according to OED, in this sense "formerly often regarded as an Americanism, although it was current in many English dialects ...."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rare">rare (v.)</a></dt><dd>"rise up," 1833, dialectal variant of <a href="/etym/s/rear" target="_blank">rear</a> (v.1). Sense of "eager" (in <span class="foreign">raring to go</span>) first recorded 1909. Related: <span class="foreign">Rared</span>; <span class="foreign">raring</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
