                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/garrison">garrison</a> 卫戍部队</span><p>来自PIE*wer, 保护，防御，词源同warrant, weir. 原指城堡守卫，后指卫戍部队。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/garrison">garrison</a></dt><dd><b>garrison: </b><span>[13] The notion underlying <i>garrison </i>is of ‘protection’. Its ultimate source was Germanic *<i>war</i>-, denoting ‘caution’, which also produced English <i>ward</i>, <i>warn</i>, <i>wary</i>, and the -<i>ware </i>of <i>beware</i>. This produced the verb *<i>warjan </i>‘protect, defend’, which Old French borrowed as <i>garir </i>(the related <i>garer </i>gave English <i>garage</i>). From it was derived the Old French noun <i>garison </i>‘defence, protection’, from which English borrowed <i>garrison</i>. The concrete senses ‘fortress’, and hence ‘detachment of troops in such a fortress’, developed in the 15th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/beware" target="_blank">beware</a>, <a href="/etym/s/garage" target="_blank">garage</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ward" target="_blank">ward</a>, <a href="/etym/s/warn" target="_blank">warn</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wary" target="_blank">wary</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/garrison">garrison (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to place troops in," 1560s, from <a href="/etym/s/garrison" target="_blank">garrison</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Garrisoned</span>; <span class="foreign">garrisoning</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/garrison">garrison (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "store, treasure," from Old French <span class="foreign">garison</span> "defense, protection, safety, security; crops, food; salvation; healing, recovery, cure" (Modern French <span class="foreign">guérison</span> "cure, recovery, healing") from <span class="foreign">garir</span> "defend" (see <a href="/etym/s/garret" target="_blank">garret</a>). Meaning "fortified stronghold" is from early 15c.; that of "body of troops in a fortress" is from mid-15c., a sense taken over from Middle English <span class="foreign">garnison</span> "body of armed men stationed in a fort or town to guard it" (late 14c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">garnison</span> "provision, munitions," from <span class="foreign">garnir</span> "to furnish, provide" (see <a href="/etym/s/garnish" target="_blank">garnish</a> (v.)).</dd></dl></div></div>
