                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pupil">pupil</a> 小学生</span><p>来自拉丁语pupillus,小男孩，来自pupus的小词形式，来自pupus,男孩，可能来自PIE*pup,鼓起，膨胀，拟声词，引申词义生长，或来自PIE*pau,小的，少的，词源同few,pedagogue.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pupil">pupil</a> 瞳孔，瞳仁</span><p>来自拉丁语pupilla,小女孩，来自pupillus 的阴性格。因反射在瞳孔的人像如同小女孩而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pupil">pupil</a></dt><dd><b>pupil: </b><span>[14] Latin <i>pūpus </i>and <i>pūpa </i>meant respectively ‘boy’ and ‘girl’ (<i>pūpa </i>was applied by the Swedish naturalist Linnaeus to ‘chrysalises’, the underlying link being ‘undeveloped creature’, and English adopted it as <i>pupa </i>[19]). The diminutive derivatives <i>pūpillus </i>and <i>pūpilla </i>denoted ‘orphan’, a sense which remained with <i>pūpill</i>- as it passed via Old French <i>pupille </i>into English as <i>pupil</i>. ‘Person being taught’ did not emerge until the 16th century.<br><br>The application of the word to the ‘black aperture in the eye’, which reached English in the mid-16th century, goes back to Latin <i>pūpilla</i>, which was also used for ‘doll’ – the notion being that if you stand close to someone and look into their eyes, you can see yourself reflected in the pupils like a little ‘doll’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pupa" target="_blank">pupa</a>, <a href="/etym/s/puppet" target="_blank">puppet</a>, <a href="/etym/s/puppy" target="_blank">puppy</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pupil">pupil (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"student," late 14c., originally "orphan child, ward," from Old French <span class="foreign">pupille</span> (14c.) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">pupillus</span> (fem. <span class="foreign">pupilla</span>) "orphan child, ward, minor," diminutive of <span class="foreign">pupus</span> "boy" (fem. <span class="foreign">pupa</span> "girl"), probably related to <span class="foreign">puer</span> "child," possibly from PIE <span class="foreign">*pup-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*pu-</span> "to swell, inflate." Meaning "disciple, student" first recorded 1560s. Related: <span class="foreign">Pupillary</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pupil">pupil (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"center of the eye," early 15c. (in English in Latin form from late 14c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">pupille</span> (14c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">pupilla</span>, originally "little girl-doll," diminutive of <span class="foreign">pupa</span> "girl; doll" (see <a href="/etym/s/pupil" target="_blank">pupil</a> (n.1)), so called from the tiny image one sees of himself reflected in the eye of another. Greek used the same word, <span class="foreign">kore</span> (literally "girl"), to mean both "doll" and "pupil of the eye;" and compare obsolete <span class="foreign">baby</span> "small image of oneself in another's pupil" (1590s), source of 17c. colloquial expression <span class="foreign">to look babies</span> "stare lovingly into another's eyes."<blockquote>Self-knowledge can be obtained only by looking into the mind and virtue of the soul, which is the diviner part of a man, as we see our own image in another's eye. [Plato, <span class="foreign">"Alcibiades,"</span> I.133]</blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
