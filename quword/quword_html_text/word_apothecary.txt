                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/apothecary">apothecary</a> 药商</span><p>前缀apo-, 从，从...离开。词根thec, 放置，词源同单词do, 放置，做。指放商品的店铺，后特指药店。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/apothecary">apothecary</a></dt><dd><b>apothecary: </b><span>[14] Originally, an apothecary was simply a shopkeeper – the word comes via Old French from late Latin <i>apothēcārius</i>, which was based on Greek <i>apothékē </i>‘storehouse’ (source, via French, of <i>boutique </i>[18] and via Spanish of <i>bodega </i>[19]), a derivative of the verb <i>apotithénai </i>‘put away’ (formed from the prefix <i>apo</i>- ‘away’ and the verb <i>tithénai </i>‘put’ – source of <i>thesis</i>).<br><br>By the time the word entered English it was reserved to shopkeepers who sold non-perishable groceries, such as spices – and herbal and other remedies. Gradually, apothecaries began to specialize more and more in drugs, so that in 1617 a formal separation took place between the Apothecaries’ Company of London and the Grocers’ Company. <i>Apothecary </i>remained the general term for a ‘druggist’ until about 1800, when <i>chemist </i>began to take over.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bodega" target="_blank">bodega</a>, <a href="/etym/s/boutique" target="_blank">boutique</a>, <a href="/etym/s/thesis" target="_blank">thesis</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/apothecary">apothecary (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., "shopkeeper, especially one who stores, compounds, and sells medicaments," from Old French <span class="foreign">apotecaire</span> (13c., Modern French <span class="foreign">apothicaire</span>), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">apothecarius</span> "storekeeper," from Latin <span class="foreign">apotheca</span> "storehouse," from Greek <span class="foreign">apotheke</span> "barn, storehouse," literally "a place where things are put away," from <span class="foreign">apo-</span> "away" (see <a href="/etym/s/apo-" target="_blank">apo-</a>) + <span class="foreign">tithenai</span> "to put, to place" (see <a href="/etym/s/theme" target="_blank">theme</a>). Same root produced French <span class="foreign">boutique</span> and Spanish <span class="foreign">bodega</span>. Cognate compounds produced Sanskrit <span class="foreign">apadha-</span> "concealment," Old Persian <span class="foreign">apadana-</span> "palace."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Drugs and herbs being among the chief items of non-perishable goods, the meaning narrowed 17c. to "druggist" (Apothecaries' Company of London separated from the Grocers' in 1617). Apothecaries formerly were notorious for "the assumed gravity and affectation of knowledge generally put on by the gentlemen of this profession, who are commonly as superficial in their learning as they are pedantic in their language" [Francis Grose, "A Classical Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue," 1796]. Hence, <span class="foreign">Apothecary's Latin</span>, barbarously mangled, also known as <span class="foreign">Dog Latin</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
