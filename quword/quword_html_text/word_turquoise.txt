                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/turquoise">turquoise</a> 绿松石</span><p>来自古法语 pierre turqueise,来自土耳其的石头，来自 pierre,石头，词源同 petrol,turqueise,土 耳其的，词源同 Turkish.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/turquoise">turquoise</a></dt><dd><b>turquoise: </b><span>[14] <i>Turquoise </i>is etymologically the ‘Turkish’ stone. The word was borrowed from Old French <i>turqueise</i>, short for <i>pierre turqueise </i>‘Turkish stone’. The stone was so called because it was first found in Turkestan. The present-day form of the word, which dates from the 16th century, is due to the influence of modern French <i>turquoise</i>. It was first used as a colour adjective in the late 16th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/turquoise">turquoise (n.)</a></dt><dd>greenish-blue precious stone, 1560s, from Middle French, replacing Middle English <span class="foreign">turkeis</span>, <span class="foreign">turtogis</span> (late 14c.), from Old French fem. adjective <span class="foreign">turqueise</span> "Turkish," in <span class="foreign">pierre turqueise</span> "Turkish stone," so called because it was first brought to Europe from Turkestan or some other Turkish dominion. Cognate with Spanish <span class="foreign">turquesa</span>, Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">(lapis) turchesius</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">turcoys</span>, German <span class="foreign">türkis</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">turkos</span>. As an adjective, 1570s. As a color name, attested from 1853. "Chemically it is a hydrated phosphate of aluminum and copper" [Flood].</dd></dl></div></div>
