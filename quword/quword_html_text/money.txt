                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/money">money</a> 钱，财富</span><p>来自拉丁语moneta,硬币，铸币，铸币处，来自古罗马时期铸造钱币的地方Juno Moneta之庙。Juno为古罗马神话天后，天父Jupiter之妻，她在古罗马神话中为劝诫神，教导神，妇女保护神，同时也为钱财守护神，所以人们选择在她的庙宇里铸造钱币，Moneta为她的称号之一，字面意思为劝告，劝勉，警告，词源同admonish,monitor.比较dollar.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/money">money</a></dt><dd><b>money: </b><span>[13] An epithet used in ancient Rome for the goddess Juno was <i>Monēta </i>(derived by some etymologists in the past from the Latin verb <i>monēre </i>‘advise, warn’, although this is now regarded as rather dubious). The name was also applied to her temple in Rome, which contained a mint. And so in due course <i>monēta </i>came to mean ‘mint’ (a sense retained in English <i>mint</i>, which goes back via a circuitous route to <i>monēta</i>), then ‘stamp for coining’, and finally ‘coin’ – the meaning transmitted via Old French <i>moneie </i>to English <i>money</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/mint" target="_blank">mint</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/money">money (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-13c., "coinage, metal currency," from Old French <span class="foreign">monoie</span> "money, coin, currency; change" (Modern French <span class="foreign">monnaie</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">moneta</span> "place for coining money, mint; coined money, money, coinage," from <span class="foreign">Moneta</span>, a title or surname of the Roman goddess Juno, in or near whose temple money was coined; perhaps from <span class="foreign">monere</span> "advise, warn" (see <a href="/etym/s/monitor" target="_blank">monitor</a> (n.)), with the sense of "admonishing goddess," which is sensible, but the etymology is difficult. Extended early 19c. to include paper money.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; It had been justly stated by a British writer that the power to make a small piece of paper, not worth one cent, by the inscribing of a few names, to be worth a thousand dollars, was a power too high to be entrusted to the hands of mortal man. [John C. Calhoun, speech, U.S. Senate, Dec. 29, 1841]&#13; <br /><br /><hr /><br />&#13; I am not interested in money but in the things of which money is the symbol. [Henry Ford]</blockquote>&#13; To <span class="foreign">make money</span> "earn pay" is first attested mid-15c. Highwayman's threat <span class="foreign">your money or your life</span> first attested 1841. Phrase <span class="foreign">in the money</span> (1902) originally meant "one who finishes among the prize-winners" (in a horse race, etc.). The challenge to <span class="foreign">put (one's) money where (one's) mouth is</span> is first recorded 1942, American English. <span class="foreign">money-grub</span> "one who is sordidly intent on amassing money" is from 1768. The image of money burning a hole in someone's pocket is attested from 1520s.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
