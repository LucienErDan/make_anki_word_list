                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tire">tire</a> 使疲劳，疲倦</span><p>来自古英语 teorian,耗尽，停止，厌倦，来自 PIE*deu-s,*deu,缺乏，缺少，滞后，由该 PIE 词衍生前缀 dys-,坏的，差的，如 dysfunction,dyslexia.引申词义使疲劳，厌倦。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tire">tire</a> 轮胎</span><p>来自中古英语 tire,装备，缩写自 attire,装备，准备，盛装，词源同 tier,tirade.该词原指马车或 战车轮子的外边缘铁板，后引申词义轮胎。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tire">tire</a></dt><dd><b>tire: </b><span>[OE] <i>Tire </i>is something of a mystery word. It was relatively common in the Old English period (where it originally meant ‘fail, cease, come to an end’ – ‘become weary’ is a secondary development), but then it disappeared, to return in the 14th century. Nor is anything certain known about its pre-English ancestry, although it may go back to the Indo-European base *<i>deus</i>- (source also of Sanskrit <i>dosa</i>- ‘fault, lack’). The use of the past participle <i>tired </i>as an adjective dates from the late 14th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tire">tire (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to weary," also "to become weary," Old English <span class="foreign">teorian</span> (Kentish <span class="foreign">tiorian</span>) "to fail, cease; become weary; make weary, exhaust," of uncertain origin; according to Watkins possibly from a PIE <span class="foreign">*deu-s-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*deu-</span> (1) "to lack, be wanting." Related: <a href="/etym/s/Tired" target="_blank">Tired</a>; <span class="foreign">tiring</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tire">tire (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 15c., "iron plates forming a rim of a carriage wheel," probably from <span class="foreign">tire</span> "equipment, dress, covering" (c. 1300), a shortened form of <a href="/etym/s/attire" target="_blank">attire</a> (n.). The notion is of the tire as the dressing of the wheel. The original spelling was <span class="foreign">tyre</span>, which had shifted to <span class="foreign">tire</span> in 17c.-18c., but since early 19c. <span class="foreign">tyre</span> has been revived in Great Britain and become standard there. Rubber ones, for bicycles (later automobiles) are from 1877. A <span class="foreign">tire-iron</span> originally was one of the iron plates; as a device for separating a tire from a wheel, by 1909.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tire">tire (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"furnish with a tire," 1899, from <a href="/etym/s/tire" target="_blank">tire</a> (n.).</dd></dl></div></div>
