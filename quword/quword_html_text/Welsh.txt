                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/Welsh">Welsh</a> 威尔士</span><p>来自Proto-Germanic*walhaz,凯尔特部落名，凯尔特人，后词义通用化，用于指外国人，不固定的罗马人，高卢人，不列颠人等非日耳曼人，直至后来指现代意义上的威尔士人。大部分现代凯尔特语学者认为该词原义为猎鹰，词源同falcon, 也有学者偏向于词义为狼，词源同wolf. 词义演变比较frank.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/welsh">welsh</a> 赖帐</span><p>来自Welsh,威尔干人。赖帐义可能来自英国人的对威尔士的调侃或开涮。比较Dutch courage,French letter.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/Welsh">Welsh (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">Wielisc</span>, <span class="foreign">Wylisc</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">Welisc</span>, <span class="foreign">Wælisc</span> (Anglian and Kentish) "foreign; British (not Anglo-Saxon), Welsh; not free, servile," from <span class="foreign">Wealh</span>, <span class="foreign">Walh</span> "Celt, Briton, Welshman, non-Germanic foreigner;" in Tolkien's definition, "common Gmc. name for a man of what we should call Celtic speech," but also applied in Germanic languages to speakers of Latin, hence Old High German <span class="foreign">Walh</span>, <span class="foreign">Walah</span> "Celt, Roman, Gaulish," and Old Norse <span class="foreign">Val-land</span> "France," <span class="foreign">Valir</span> "Gauls, non-Germanic inhabitants of France" (Danish <span class="foreign">vælsk</span> "Italian, French, southern"); from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*Walkhiskaz</span>, from a Celtic tribal name represented by Latin <span class="foreign">Volcæ</span> (Caesar) "ancient Celtic tribe in southern Gaul."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As a noun, "the Britons," also "the Welsh language," both from Old English. The word survives in <span class="foreign">Wales</span>, <a href="/etym/s/Cornwall" target="_blank">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/etym/s/Walloon" target="_blank">Walloon</a>, <a href="/etym/s/walnut" target="_blank">walnut</a>, and in surnames <span class="foreign">Walsh</span> and <span class="foreign">Wallace</span>. Borrowed in Old Church Slavonic as <span class="foreign">vlachu</span>, and applied to the Rumanians, hence <span class="foreign">Wallachia</span>. Among the English, <span class="foreign">Welsh</span> was used disparagingly of inferior or substitute things (such as <span class="foreign">Welsh cricket</span> "louse" (1590s); <span class="foreign">Welsh comb</span> "thumb and four fingers" (1796), and compare <a href="/etym/s/welch" target="_blank">welch</a> (v.)). <span class="foreign">Welsh rabbit</span> is from 1725, also perverted by folk-etymology as <span class="foreign">Welsh rarebit</span> (1785).</dd></dl></div></div>
