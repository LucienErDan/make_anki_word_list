                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/church">church</a> 教堂</span><p>来自PIE*keue, 膨胀，增强，词源同cumulative,accumulate. 词义由膨胀外延到强大，强壮，上帝，上帝的世俗所在，其方言变体kirk.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/church">church</a></dt><dd><b>church: </b><span>[OE] Etymologically, a <i>church </i>is the ‘Lord’s house’. Its ultimate source is Greek <i>kūrios </i>‘lord, master’ (perhaps most familiar nowadays from the words of the choral mass <i>kyrie eleison </i>‘lord have mercy’). The adjective derived from this was <i>kūriakós</i>, whose use in the phrase ‘house of the lord’ led to its use as a noun, <i>kūrikón</i>. The medieval Greek form, <i>kūrkón </i>‘house of worship’ was borrowed into West Germanic as *<i>kirika</i>, producing eventually German <i>kirche </i>and English <i>church</i>. The Scots form <i>kirk </i>comes from Old Norse <i>kirkja</i>, which in turn was borrowed from Old English.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/kirk" target="_blank">kirk</a>, <a href="/etym/s/kyrie" target="_blank">kyrie</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/church">church (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">cirice</span>, <span class="foreign">circe</span> "church, public place of worship; Christians collectively," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kirika</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">kirika</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">kirkja</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">zerke</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">kerke</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kerk</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kirihha</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kirche</span>), probably [see note in OED] from Greek <span class="foreign">kyriake (oikia)</span>, <span class="foreign">kyriakon doma</span> "Lord's (house)," from <span class="foreign">kyrios</span> "ruler, lord," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*keue-</span> "to swell" ("swollen," hence "strong, powerful"); see <a href="/etym/s/cumulus" target="_blank">cumulus</a>. Phonetic spelling from c. 1200, established by 16c. For vowel evolution, see <a href="/etym/s/bury" target="_blank">bury</a>. As an adjective from 1570s.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Greek <span class="foreign">kyriakon</span> (adj.) "of the Lord" was used of houses of Christian worship since c.300, especially in the East, though it was less common in this sense than <span class="foreign">ekklesia</span> or <span class="foreign">basilike</span>. An example of the direct Greek-to-Germanic progress of many Christian words, via the Goths; it probably was used by West Germanic people in their pre-Christian period.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Also picked up by Slavic, probably via Germanic (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">criky</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">cerkov</span>). Finnish <span class="foreign">kirkko</span>, Estonian <span class="foreign">kirrik</span> are from Scandinavian. Romance and Celtic languages use variants of Latin <span class="foreign">ecclesia</span> (such as French <span class="foreign">église</span>, 11c.).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Church-bell</span> was in late Old English. <span class="foreign">Church-goer</span> is from 1680s. <span class="foreign">Church key</span> is early 14c.; slang use for "can or bottle opener" is by 1954, probably originally U.S. college student slang. <span class="foreign">Church-mouse</span>, proverbial in many languages for its poverty, is 1731 in English.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/church">church (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to bring or lead to church," mid-14c., from <a href="/etym/s/church" target="_blank">church</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Churched</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
