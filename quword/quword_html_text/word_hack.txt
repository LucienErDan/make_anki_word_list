                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hack">hack</a> 砍，劈，非法侵入计算机</span><p>来自PIE*keg,砍，劈，钩，词源同hook,haggle,hew.引申词义侵入别人的计算机，即黑掉。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/hack">hack</a> 供人骑的马，出租车，雇佣文人</span><p>缩写自Hackney,伦敦附近地名，在13世纪开始就成为著名的老马养殖场，提供老马或马车出租，后引申词义妓女，苦力，雇佣文人等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack</a></dt><dd><b>hack: </b><span>English has two distinct words <i>hack</i>. By far the older, ‘cut savagely or randomly’ [OE], goes back via Old English <i>haccian </i>to a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>khak</i>-, also reproduced in German <i>hacken </i>and Dutch <i>hakken</i>. It perhaps originated in imitation of the sound of chopping. <i>Hack </i>‘worn-out horse’ [17] is short for <i>hackney </i>(as in <i>hackney carriage</i>), a word in use since the 14th century in connection with hired horses.<br><br>It is thought that this may be an adaptation of the name of <i>Hackney</i>, now an inner-London borough but once a village on the northeastern outskirts of the capital where horses were raised before being taken into the city for sale or hire. Most rented horses being past their best from long and probably ill usage, <i>hackney </i>came to mean ‘broken-down horse’ and hence in general ‘drudge’.<br><br>This quickly became respecified to ‘someone who writes for hire, and hence unimaginatively’, which influenced the development of <i>hackneyed </i>‘trite’ [18]. The modern sense of <i>hacker</i>, ‘someone who gains unauthorized access to computer records’, comes from a slightly earlier ‘one who works like a hack – that is, very hard – at writing and experimenting with software’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to cut roughly, cut with chopping blows," c. 1200, from verb found in stem of Old English <span class="foreign">tohaccian</span> "hack to pieces," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*hakkon</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hackia</span> "to chop or hack," Dutch <span class="foreign">hakken</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hacchon</span>, German <span class="foreign">hacken</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*keg-</span> "hook, tooth" (see <a href="/etym/s/hook" target="_blank">hook</a> (n.)). Perhaps influenced by Old Norse <span class="foreign">höggva</span> "to hew, cut, strike, smite" (which is unrelated, from PIE <span class="foreign">*kau-</span> "to hew, strike;" see <a href="/etym/s/hew" target="_blank">hew</a>). Slang sense of "cope with" (as in <span class="foreign">can't hack it</span>) is first recorded in American English 1955, with a sense of "get through by some effort," as a jungle (phrase <span class="foreign">hack after</span> "keep working away at" is attested from late 14c.). To <span class="foreign">hack around</span> "waste time" is U.S. slang, by 1955, perhaps originally of golfers or cabbies. Related: <span class="foreign">Hacked</span>; <span class="foreign">hacking</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"person hired to do routine work," c. 1700, ultimately short for <span class="foreign">hackney</span> "an ordinary horse, horse for general service (especially for driving or riding, as opposed to war, hunting, or hauling)," c. 1300. This word is probably from the place name <a href="/etym/s/Hackney" target="_blank">Hackney</a>, Middlesex. Apparently nags were raised on the pastureland there in early medieval times. Extended sense of "horse for hire" (late 14c.) led naturally to "broken-down nag," and also "prostitute" (1570s) and "a drudge" (1540s), especially a literary one, one who writes according to direction or demand. Sense of "carriage for hire" (1704) led to modern slang for "taxicab." As an adjective, 1734, from the noun. <span class="foreign">Hack writer</span> is first recorded 1826, though <span class="foreign">hackney writer</span> is at least 50 years earlier. <span class="foreign">Hack-work</span> is recorded from 1851.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"illegally enter a computer system," by 1984; apparently a back-formation from <a href="/etym/s/hacker" target="_blank">hacker</a>. Related: <span class="foreign">Hacked</span>; <span class="foreign">hacking</span> (1975 in this sense). Earlier verb senses were "to make commonplace" (1745), "make common by everyday use" (1590s), "use (a horse) for ordinary riding" (1560s), all from <a href="/etym/s/hack" target="_blank">hack</a> (n.2).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"tool for chopping," early 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/hack" target="_blank">hack</a> (v.1); cognates: Danish <span class="foreign">hakke</span> "mattock," German <span class="foreign">Hacke</span> "pickax, hatchet, hoe." Meaning "a cut, notch" is from 1570s. Meaning "an act of cutting" is from 1836; figurative sense of "a try, an attempt" is first attested 1898.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"to cough with a short, dry cough," 1802, perhaps from <a href="/etym/s/hack" target="_blank">hack</a> (v.1) on the notion of being done with difficulty, or else imitative.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"hired, mercenary," 1812, from <a href="/etym/s/hack" target="_blank">hack</a> (n.2).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/hack">hack (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"a short, hard cough," 1885, from <a href="/etym/s/hack" target="_blank">hack</a> (v.3).</dd></dl></div></div>
