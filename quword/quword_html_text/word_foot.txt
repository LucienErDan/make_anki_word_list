                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/foot">foot</a> 脚</span><p>来自PIE*ped, 脚，词源同biped, pedestal.用做测量单位英尺，因约略等于成人脚长而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/foot">foot</a></dt><dd><b>foot: </b><span>[OE] <i>Foot </i>traces its ancestry back to Indo- European *<i>pōd</i>-, *<i>ped</i>-, which provided the word for ‘foot’ in most modern Indo-European languages (the exceptions are the Slavic languages, whose ‘foot’ – words, such as Russian <i>noga </i>and Czech <i>noha</i>, come from a source that meant ‘claw’, and the Celtic languages – such as Welsh <i>troed </i>and Irish <i>troigh</i>).<br><br>Descendants include Greek <i>poús </i>‘foot’ (whence English <i>antipodes</i>, <i>pew</i>, <i>podium </i>[18], and <i>tripod</i>, literally ‘three-footed’, a formation mirrored exactly by Latin <i>trivet </i>[15] and Hindi <i>teapoy </i>[19]), Persian <i>pāē </i>or <i>pay </i>(whence English <i>pyjama</i>), Sanskrit <i>pádas </i>‘foot’ (source of <i>pie </i>‘unit of Indian currency’), and Lithuanian <i>pedà </i>‘footstep’, but the most fruitful of all from the point of view of the English lexicon has been Latin <i>pēs</i>, source of <i>impede</i>, <i>pawn </i>‘chess piece’, <i>pedal</i>, <i>pedestal</i>, <i>pedestrian</i>, <i>pedicure</i>, <i>pedigree</i>, <i>pedometer</i>, <i>peon</i>, <i>pioneer</i>, <i>quadruped</i>, <i>vamp</i>, and <i>velocipede </i>(it also, of course, gave French <i>pied</i>, Italian <i>piede</i>, and Spanish <i>pie</i>).<br><br>Its Germanic descendant was *<i>fōr</i>-, which produced German <i>fuss</i>, Dutch <i>voet</i>, Swedish <i>fot</i>, Danish <i>fod</i>, and English <i>foot</i>. Other related forms in English include <i>pilot </i>and <i>trapeze</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/antipodes" target="_blank">antipodes</a>, <a href="/etym/s/impede" target="_blank">impede</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pawn" target="_blank">pawn</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedal" target="_blank">pedal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedestal" target="_blank">pedestal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedestrian" target="_blank">pedestrian</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pedigree" target="_blank">pedigree</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pilot" target="_blank">pilot</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pioneer" target="_blank">pioneer</a>, <a href="/etym/s/podium" target="_blank">podium</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pyjamas" target="_blank">pyjamas</a>, <a href="/etym/s/quadruped" target="_blank">quadruped</a>, <a href="/etym/s/trapeze" target="_blank">trapeze</a>, <a href="/etym/s/tripod" target="_blank">tripod</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vamp" target="_blank">vamp</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/foot">foot (n.)</a></dt><dd>"terminal part of the leg of a vertebrate animal," Old English <span class="foreign">fot</span> "foot," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fot</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fot</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fot</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fotr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">fod</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">fot</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">voet</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fuoz</span>, German <span class="foreign">Fuß</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fotus</span> "foot"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ped-</span> (1) "a foot" (cognates: Avestan <span class="foreign">pad-</span>; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pad-</span>, accusative <span class="foreign">padam</span> "foot;" Greek <span class="foreign">pos</span>, Attic <span class="foreign">pous</span>, genitive <span class="foreign">podos</span>; Latin <span class="foreign">pes</span>, genitive <span class="foreign">pedis</span> "foot;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">padas</span> "sole," <span class="foreign">peda</span> "footstep"). Plural form <span class="foreign">feet</span> is an instance of <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/imutate.php">i-mutation</a>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The linear measure was in Old English (the exact length has varied over time), this being considered the length of a man's foot; a unit of measure used widely and anciently. In this sense the plural is often <span class="foreign">foot</span>. The current inch and foot are implied from measurements in 12c. English churches (Flinders Petrie, "Inductive Metrology"), but the most usual length of a "foot" in medieval England was the foot of 13.2 inches common throughout the ancient Mediterranean. The Anglo-Saxon foot apparently was between the two. All three correspond to units used by the Romans, and possibly all three lengths were picked up by the Anglo-Saxons from the Romano-Britons. "That the Saxon units should descend to mediæval times is most probable, as the Normans were a ruling, and not a working, class." [Flinders Petrie, 1877]. The medieval <span class="foreign">Paul's Foot</span> (late 14c.) was a measuring standard cut into the base of a column at the old St. Paul's cathedral in London. The metrical <span class="foreign">foot</span> (late Old English, translating Latin <span class="foreign">pes</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">pous</span> in the same sense) is commonly taken to represent one rise and one fall of a foot: keeping time according to some, dancing according to others.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In Middle English also "a person" (c. 1200), hence <span class="foreign">non-foot</span> "nobody." Meaning "bottom or lowest part of anything eminent or upright" is from c. 1200. Of a bed, grave, etc., from c. 1300. <span class="foreign">On foot</span> "by walking" is from c. 1300. To <span class="foreign">get off on the wrong foot</span> is from 1905 (the <span class="foreign">right foot</span> is by 1907); to <span class="foreign">put one's best foot foremost</span> first recorded 1849 (Shakespeare has <span class="foreign">the better foot before</span>, 1596); Middle English had <span class="foreign">evil-foot</span> (adv.) "through mischance, unluckily." To put one's <span class="foreign">foot in (one's) mouth</span> "say something stupid" is attested by 1942; the expression <span class="foreign">put (one's) foot in</span> something "make a mess of it" is from 1823. To have <span class="foreign">one foot in the grave</span> "be near death" is from 1844. Colloquial exclamation <span class="foreign">my foot!</span> expressing "contemptuous contradiction" [OED] is attested by 1923, probably euphemistic for <span class="foreign">my ass</span> in the same sense, which dates to 1796 (also see <a href="/etym/s/eyewash" target="_blank">eyewash</a>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/foot">foot (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "to dance," also "to move or travel on foot," from <a href="/etym/s/foot" target="_blank">foot</a> (n.). From mid-15c. as "make a footing or foundation." To <span class="foreign">foot</span> a bill "pay the entirety of" is attested from 1848, from the process of tallying the expenses and writing the figure at the bottom ("foot") of the sheet; <span class="foreign">foot</span> (v.) as "add up and set the sum at the foot of" is from late 15c. (compare <a href="/etym/s/footnote" target="_blank">footnote</a> (n.)). The Old English verb <span class="foreign">gefotian</span> meant "to hasten up." Related: <span class="foreign">Footed</span>; <a href="/etym/s/footing" target="_blank">footing</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
