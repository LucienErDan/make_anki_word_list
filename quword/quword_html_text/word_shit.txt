                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/shit">shit</a> 屎，胡扯</span><p>来自古英语 scitan,屎，粪便，来自 Proto-Germanic*skitana,排便，来自 skei,切，分开，词源 同 shed,sheath,science.词源比较 turd.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shit">shit</a></dt><dd><b>shit: </b><span>[14] The verb <i>shit </i>is an alteration (due to the influence of the past participle <i>shitten</i>) of an earlier <i>shite </i>[OE]. This, like German <i>scheissen</i>, Dutch <i>schijten</i>, Swedish <i>skita</i>, and Danish <i>skide</i>, goes back to a prehistoric Germanic base *<i>skīt</i>-, which in turn was descended from Indo- European *<i>skheid</i>- ‘split, divide, separate’ (source of English <i>schism </i>and <i>schist </i>– the underlying notion being of ‘separation’ from the body, and hence ‘discharge’ from the body. The noun <i>shit</i>, a derivative of the verb, is first recorded in the 16th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/schism" target="_blank">schism</a>, <a href="/etym/s/schist" target="_blank">schist</a>, <a href="/etym/s/schizophrenia" target="_blank">schizophrenia</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shit">shit (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">scitan</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skit-</span> (cognates: North Frisian <span class="foreign">skitj</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">schijten</span>, German <span class="foreign">scheissen</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*skei-</span> "to cut, split, divide, separate" (see <a href="/etym/s/shed" target="_blank">shed</a> (v.)). The notion is of "separation" from the body (compare Latin <span class="foreign">excrementum</span>, from <span class="foreign">excernere</span> "to separate," Old English <span class="foreign">scearn</span> "dung, muck," from <span class="foreign">scieran</span> "to cut, shear;" see <a href="/etym/s/sharn" target="_blank">sharn</a>). It is thus a cousin to <a href="/etym/s/science" target="_blank">science</a> and <a href="/etym/s/conscience" target="_blank">conscience</a>.&#13; <br /><br /><a href="http://www.etymonline.com/baloney.php"><b>"Shit" is not an acronym</b></a>. The notion that it is a recent word might be partly because it was taboo from c. 1600 and rarely appeared in print (neither Shakespeare nor the KJV has it), and even in "vulgar" publications of the late 18c. it is disguised by dashes. It drew the wrath of censors as late as 1922 ("Ulysses" and "The Enormous Room"), scandalized magazine subscribers in 1957 (a Hemingway story in "Atlantic Monthly") and was omitted from some dictionaries as recently as 1970 ("Webster's New World").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Extensive slang usage; meaning "to lie, to tease" is from 1934; that of "to disrespect" is from 1903. <span class="foreign">Shite</span>, now a jocular or slightly euphemistic and chiefly British variant of the noun, formerly a dialectal variant, reflects the vowel in the Old English verb (compare German <span class="foreign">scheissen</span>); the modern verb has been influenced by the noun. <span class="foreign">Shat</span> is a humorous past tense form, not etymological, first recorded 18c. To <span class="foreign">shit bricks</span> "be very frightened" attested by 1961. The connection between fear and involuntary defecation has generated expressions in English since 14c. (the image also is in Latin), and probably also is behind <span class="foreign">scared shitless</span> (1936).&#13; <blockquote>Alle þe filþ of his magh ['maw'] salle breste out atte his fondament for drede. [<span class="foreign">"Cursor Mundi,"</span> early 14c.]</blockquote></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shit">shit (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">scitte</span> "purging, diarrhea," from source of <a href="/etym/s/shit" target="_blank">shit</a> (v.). Sense of "excrement" dates from 1580s (Old English had <span class="foreign">scytel</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">shitel</span> for "dung, excrement;" the usual 14c. noun seems to have been <a href="/etym/s/turd" target="_blank">turd</a>). Use for "obnoxious person" is since at least 1508; meaning "misfortune, trouble" is attested from 1937. <span class="foreign">Shit-faced</span> "drunk" is 1960s student slang; <span class="foreign">shit list</span> is from 1942. <span class="foreign">Up shit creek</span> "in trouble" is from 1937 (compare <a href="/etym/s/salt_river" target="_blank">salt river</a>). To <span class="foreign">not give a shit</span> "not care" is from 1922. Pessimistic expression <span class="foreign">Same shit different day</span> attested by 1989. <span class="foreign">Shitticism</span> is Robert Frost's word for scatological writing.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The expression [<span class="foreign">the shit hits the fan</span>] is related to, and may well derive from, an old joke. A man in a crowded bar needed to defecate but couldn't find a bathroom, so he went upstairs and used a hole in the floor. Returning, he found everyone had gone except the bartender, who was cowering behind the bar. When the man asked what had happened, the bartender replied, 'Where were you when the shit hit the fan?' [Hugh Rawson, "Wicked Words," 1989]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/shit">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. This is a load of <b>shit</b>. </dt> <dd>这是一堆废物。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. <b>Shit</b>! I've lost my keys! </dt> <dd>他妈的！我把钥匙丢了！</dd> <p>来自《权威词典》</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. The little kid stepped right in a pile of dog <b>shit</b>. </dt> <dd>这小孩正好踩在一堆狗屎上.</dd> <p>来自《简明英汉词典》</p> </dl><dl><dt>4.
