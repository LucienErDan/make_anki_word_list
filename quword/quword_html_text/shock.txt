                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/shock">shock</a> 震惊，震动，震荡，休克</span><p>可能来自中古荷兰语 schokken,摇动，摇晃，猛拉，猛推，来自 Proto-Germanic*skakana,摇动， 颤动，来自 PIE*skek,摇动，摇晃，词源同 shake.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shock">shock</a></dt><dd><b>shock: </b><span>English has two words <i>shock </i>in current general usage. <i>Shock </i>‘heavy blow, unpleasant surprise’ [16] was borrowed from French <i>choc</i>, a derivative of the verb <i>choquer </i>‘strike’, whose origins are unknown. <i>Shock </i>‘thick shaggy mass of hair’ [19] is a nominalization of an earlier adjective <i>shock </i>‘thick and shaggy’ [17], but it is not clear where this came from. It has been linked with the obsolete <i>shough</i>, which referred to a sort of dog, and another possibility is that it is connected with the now little used <i>shock </i>‘stack of sheaves of corn’ [14]. This was probably borrowed from Middle Dutch or Middle Low German <i>schok</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shock">shock (n.1)</a></dt><dd>1560s, "violent encounter of armed forces or a pair of warriors," a military term, from Middle French <span class="foreign">choc</span> "violent attack," from Old French <span class="foreign">choquer</span> "strike against," probably from Frankish, from a Proto-Germanic imitative base (compare Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">schokken</span> "to push, jolt," Old High German <span class="foreign">scoc</span> "jolt, swing").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "a sudden blow" is from 1610s; meaning "a sudden and disturbing impression on the mind" is from 1705. Sense of "feeling of being (mentally) shocked" is from 1876. Medical sense is attested from 1804 (it also once meant "seizure, stroke," 1794). <span class="foreign">Shock-absorber</span> is attested from 1906 (short form <span class="foreign">shocks</span> attested by 1961); <span class="foreign">shock wave</span> is from 1907. <span class="foreign">Shock troops</span> (1917) translates German <span class="foreign">stoßtruppen</span> and preserves the word's original military sense. <span class="foreign">Shock therapy</span> is from 1917; <span class="foreign">shock treatment</span> from 1938.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shock">shock (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"bundle of grain," early 14c., from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">schok</span> "shock of corn," originally "group of sixty," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skukka-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">skok</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">schok</span> "sixty pieces; shock of corn;" German <span class="foreign">schock</span> "sixty," <span class="foreign">Hocke</span> "heap of sheaves"). In 16c.-17c. English the word sometimes meant "60-piece lot," from trade with the Dutch.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shock">shock (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"thick mass of hair," 1819, from earlier <span class="foreign">shock</span> (adj.) "having thick hair" (1680s), and a noun sense of "lap dog having long, shaggy hair" (1630s), from <span class="foreign">shough</span> (1590s), the name for this type of dog, which was said to have been brought originally from Iceland; the word is perhaps from the source of <a href="/etym/s/shock" target="_blank">shock</a> (n.2), or from an Old Norse variant of <a href="/etym/s/shag" target="_blank">shag</a> (n.). <span class="foreign">Shock-headed Peter</span> was used in 19c. translations for German <span class="foreign">Struwwelpeter</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shock">shock (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to come into violent contact, strike against suddenly and violently," 1570s, now archaic or obsolete, from <a href="/etym/s/shock" target="_blank">shock</a> (n.1). Meaning "to give (something) an electric shock" is from 1746; sense of "to offend, displease" is first recorded 1690s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/shock">shock (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"arrange (grain) in a shock," mid-15c., from <a href="/etym/s/shock" target="_blank">shock</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Shocked</span>; <span class="foreign">shocking</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. The violence in her tone gave Alistair a <b>shock</b>. </dt> <dd>她语气之强烈令阿利斯泰尔深感震惊。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. It was quite a <b>shock</b> to see my face on that screen! </dt> <dd>从那个屏幕上看到我的脸，真让人惊愕不已。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. The documentary left me in a state of <b>shock</b>. </dt> <dd>那部纪录片使我大为震惊。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. The <b>shock</b> waves of the earthquake were felt in Teheran. </dt> <dd>地震带来的冲击波在德黑兰也能感觉到。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/shock">shock 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
