                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/deer">deer</a> 鹿</span><p>来自PIE*dheu, 呼吸，水气，烟雾，词源同fume. 原指不确定的野生动物，后词义固定为鹿。参照animal, 动物，原义为呼吸，参照venison, 鹿肉，原指不确定的野生动物肉。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/deer">deer</a></dt><dd><b>deer: </b><span>[OE] In Old English, <i>dēor </i>meant ‘animal’ in general, as opposed to ‘human being’ (as its modern Germanic relatives, German <i>tier</i>, Dutch <i>dier</i>, and Swedish <i>djur</i>, still do). Apparently connected forms in some other Indo-European languages, such as Lithuanian <i>dusti </i>‘gasp’ and Church Slavonic <i>dychati </i>‘breathe’, suggest that it comes via a prehistoric Germanic *<i>deuzom </i>from Indo-European *<i>dheusóm</i>, which meant ‘creature that breathes’ (English <i>animal </i>and Sanskrit <i>prānin</i>- ‘living creature’ have similar semantic origins).<br><br>Traces of specialization in meaning to ‘deer’ occur as early as the 9th century (although the main Old English word for ‘deer’ was <i>heorot</i>, source of modern English <i>hart</i>), and during the Middle English period it became firmly established, driving out ‘animal’ by the 15th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/deer">deer (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">deor</span> "animal, beast," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*deuzam</span>, the general Germanic word for "animal" (as opposed to man), but often restricted to "wild animal" (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">diar</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">dier</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">dyr</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">tior</span>, German <span class="foreign">Tier</span> "animal," Gothic <span class="foreign">dius</span> "wild animal," also see <a href="/etym/s/reindeer" target="_blank">reindeer</a>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*dheusom</span> "creature that breathes," from root <span class="foreign">*dheu-</span> (1) "cloud, breath" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">dusti</span> "gasp," <span class="foreign">dvesti</span> "gasp, perish;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">dychati</span> "breathe").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; For prehistoric sense development, compare Latin <span class="foreign">animal</span> from <span class="foreign">anima</span> "breath"). Sense specialization to a specific animal began in Old English (usual Old English for what we now call a deer was <span class="foreign">heorot</span>; see <a href="/etym/s/hart" target="_blank">hart</a>), common by 15c., now complete. Probably via hunting, deer being the favorite animal of the chase (compare Sanskrit <span class="foreign">mrga-</span> "wild animal," used especially for "deer"). <span class="foreign">Deer-lick</span> is first attested 1778, in an American context.</dd></dl></div></div>
