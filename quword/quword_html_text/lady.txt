                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/lady">lady</a> 夫人，女士</span><p>来自古英语hlaefdige,准备面包的人，女主人，hlaef,面包，词源同loaf,-dige,揉捏，词源同dough,dairy.后用于尊称夫人，女士。比较lord.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lady">lady</a></dt><dd><b>lady: </b><span>[OE] Originally, the term <i>lady </i>denoted a ‘kneader of bread’. It comes from Old English <i>hloefdige</i>, a compound formed from <i>hlǣf </i>‘bread’ (ancestor of modern English <i>loaf</i>) and an element *<i>dig</i>- ‘knead’ (related to English <i>dough</i>). It is a measure of the symbolic (and actual) importance of bread in medieval households that (like <i>lord</i>, also a derivative of <i>loaf</i>) <i>lady </i>came, as a provider of bread, to be applied to someone in a position of authority within a house.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/dairy" target="_blank">dairy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/dough" target="_blank">dough</a>, <a href="/etym/s/loaf" target="_blank">loaf</a>, <a href="/etym/s/lord" target="_blank">lord</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/lady">lady (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">lafdi, lavede</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">hlæfdige</span> "mistress of a household, wife of a lord," literally "one who kneads bread," from <span class="foreign">hlaf</span> "bread" (see <a href="/etym/s/loaf" target="_blank">loaf</a>) + <span class="foreign">-dige</span> "maid," related to <span class="foreign">dæge</span> "maker of dough" (see <a href="/etym/s/dey" target="_blank">dey</a> (n.1); also compare <a href="/etym/s/lord" target="_blank">lord</a> (n.)). The medial <span class="foreign">-f-</span> disappeared 14c. Not found outside English except where borrowed from it.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "woman of superior position in society" is c. 1200; "woman whose manners and sensibilities befit her for high rank in society" is from 1861 (<span class="foreign">ladylike</span> in this sense is from 1580s, and <span class="foreign">ladily</span> from c. 1400). Meaning "woman as an object of chivalrous love" is from early 14c. Used commonly as an address to any woman since 1890s. Applied in Old English to the Holy Virgin, hence many extended usages in plant names, place names, etc., from genitive singular <span class="foreign">hlæfdigan</span>, which in Middle English merged with the nominative, so that <span class="foreign">lady-</span> often represents <span class="foreign">(Our) Lady's</span>, as in <a href="/etym/s/ladybug" target="_blank">ladybug</a>. <span class="foreign">Ladies' man</span> first recorded 1784. <span class="foreign">Lady of pleasure</span> recorded from 1640s.</dd></dl></div></div>
