                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/district">district</a></dt><dd><b>district: </b><span>[17] <i>District </i>started life as the past participle of the verb which gave English <i>distrain </i>[13] and <i>strain</i>. It came via French <i>district </i>from medieval Latin <i>districtus</i>; this meant literally ‘seized, compelled’, and hence was used as a noun in the sense ‘seizure of offenders’, and hence ‘exercise of justice’, and finally ‘area in which justice is so exercised (in the feudal system)’.<br><br>This was the word’s meaning when it was first borrowed into English, and it was not really until the early 18th century that its much more general modern application developed. <i>Districtus </i>was the past participle of Latin <i>distringere</i>, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>dis</i>- ‘apart’ and <i>stringere </i>‘pull tight’ (source of English <i>strain</i>, <i>strict</i>, <i>stringent</i>, <i>stress</i>, etc).<br><br>In classical times it meant ‘draw apart, detain, hinder’, but by the Middle Ages this had moved on to ‘seize, compel’, which were the main senses in which it entered English as <i>distrain </i>(via Old French <i>destreindre</i>). Latin <i>districtus </i>was also the source of a Vulgar Latin noun *<i>districtia </i>‘narrowness’, which passed via Old French <i>destresse </i>into English as <i>distress </i>[13].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/distrain" target="_blank">distrain</a>, <a href="/etym/s/distress" target="_blank">distress</a>, <a href="/etym/s/strain" target="_blank">strain</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stress" target="_blank">stress</a>, <a href="/etym/s/strict" target="_blank">strict</a>, <a href="/etym/s/stringent" target="_blank">stringent</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/district">district (n.)</a></dt><dd>1610s, "territory under the jurisdiction of a lord or officer," from French <span class="foreign">district</span> (16c.), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">districtus</span> "restraining of offenders, jurisdiction," then under the feudal system "area of jurisdiction," noun use of past participle of Latin <span class="foreign">distringere</span> "hinder, detain" (see <a href="/etym/s/distress" target="_blank">distress</a>). Used vaguely of "any tract of land" from 1712. <span class="foreign">District attorney</span> attested by 1789, American English.</dd></dl></div></div>
