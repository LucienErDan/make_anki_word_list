                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/count">count</a></dt><dd><b>count: </b><span>There are two distinct words <i>count </i>in English. <i>Count </i>‘enumerate’ [14] comes ultimately from Latin <i>computāre </i>‘calculate’ (source of English <i>compute</i>). It came into English from Old French <i>conter</i>, which had, via the notion of ‘adding up and rendering an account’, developed the sense ‘tell a story’ (preserved in English in the derivatives <i>account </i>and <i>recount</i>).<br><br>The derivative <i>counter </i>[14] began life as medieval Latin <i>computātōrium </i>‘place of accounts’, and entered English via Anglo- Norman <i>counteour</i>. Its modern sense ‘surface for transactions in a shop’ does not seem to have become firmly established until the early 19th century, although it was applied to similar objects in banks from the late 17th century. The noble title <i>count </i>[16] comes via Old French <i>conte </i>from Latin <i>comes</i>, which originally meant ‘companion, attendant’ (it was a compound noun, formed from the prefix <i>com</i>- ‘with’ and <i>īre </i>‘go’, and so its underlying etymological meaning is ‘one who goes with another’).<br><br>In the Roman empire it was used for the governor of a province, and in Anglo- Norman it was used to translate English <i>earl</i>. It has never been used as an English title, but the feminine form <i>countess </i>was adopted for the wife of an earl in the 12th century (and <i>viscount </i>was borrowed from Anglo-Norman <i>viscounte </i>in the 14th century). The Latin derivative <i>comitātus </i>was originally a collective noun denoting a ‘group of companions’, but with the development of meaning in <i>comes </i>it came to mean first ‘office of a governor’ and latterly ‘area controlled by a governor’.<br><br>In England, this area was the ‘shire’, and so <i>county </i>[14], acquired via Anglo-Norman <i>counte</i>, came to be a synonym for ‘shire’. Another descendant of Latin <i>comes </i>is <i>concomitant </i>[17], from the present participle of late Latin <i>concomitārī</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/account" target="_blank">account</a>, <a href="/etym/s/compute" target="_blank">compute</a>, <a href="/etym/s/putative" target="_blank">putative</a>, <a href="/etym/s/recount" target="_blank">recount</a>; <a href="/etym/s/concomitant" target="_blank">concomitant</a>, <a href="/etym/s/county" target="_blank">county</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/count">count (v.)</a></dt><dd>mid-14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">conter</span> "add up," but also "tell a story," from Latin <span class="foreign">computare</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/compute" target="_blank">compute</a>). Related: <span class="foreign">Counted</span>; <span class="foreign">counting</span>. Modern French differentiates <span class="foreign">compter</span> "to count" and <span class="foreign">conter</span> "to tell," but they are cognates.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/count">count (n.)</a></dt><dd>title of nobility, c. 1300, from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">counte</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">conte</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">comitem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">comes</span>) "companion, attendant," the Roman term for a provincial governor, from <span class="foreign">com-</span> "with" (see <a href="/etym/s/com-" target="_blank">com-</a>) + stem of <span class="foreign">ire</span> "to go" (see <a href="/etym/s/ion" target="_blank">ion</a>). The term was used in Anglo-French to render Old English <span class="foreign">eorl</span>, but the word was never truly naturalized and mainly was used with reference to foreign titles.</dd></dl></div></div>
