                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tear">tear</a> 眼泪</span><p>来自古英语 tear,眼泪，来自 Proto-Germanic*tagr,眼泪，来自 PIE*dakru,眼泪，词源同 lachrymose, 流泪的。字母 d,l 音变，中间字母 g 脱落。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/tear">tear</a> 撕裂，撕碎</span><p>来自古英语teran,撕碎，撕扯，来自Proto-Germanic*teran,撕碎，撕扯，来自PIE*der,撕开， 皮肤，词源同dermis,dermotology,turd.可能来自PIE*da,分开，词源同deal,time.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tear">tear</a></dt><dd><b>tear: </b><span>English has two separate words <i>tear</i>, both of ancient ancestry. The sort of <i>tear </i>that one weeps [OE] goes back (together with its Germanic relatives German <i>träne</i>, Dutch <i>traan</i>, Swedish <i>tår</i>, and Danish <i>taare</i>) to prehistoric Indo- European *<i>dakru</i>-, a word of uncertain origin which also produced Welsh <i>deigryn </i>and Latin <i>lacrima </i>(source of English <i>lachrymal </i>[16] and <i>lachrymose </i>[17]). <i>Tear </i>‘rip’ [OE] comes from an Indo- European base *<i>der</i>- ‘tear’, which also produced Russian <i>drat</i>’ and Polish <i>drzeć </i>‘tear’.<br><br>The base *<i>der</i>- denoted the concept of ‘flaying’ as well as ‘tearing’, in which sense it produced English <i>turd </i>and Greek <i>dérma </i>‘skin’ (source of English <i>dermatitis, epidermis</i>, etc).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/lachrymose" target="_blank">lachrymose</a>; <a href="/etym/s/dermatitis" target="_blank">dermatitis</a>, <a href="/etym/s/epidermis" target="_blank">epidermis</a>, <a href="/etym/s/turd" target="_blank">turd</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tear">tear (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"fluid drop from the eye," Old English <span class="foreign">tear</span> "tear, drop, nectar, what is distilled in drops," from earlier <span class="foreign">teahor</span>, <span class="foreign">tæhher</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*tahr-</span>, <span class="foreign">*tagr-</span> (cognates: Old Norse, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">tar</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">zahar</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zähre</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">tagr</span> "tear"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*dakru-</span> (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">lacrima</span>, Old Latin <span class="foreign">dacrima</span>, Irish <span class="foreign">der</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">deigr</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">dakryma</span>). To be <span class="foreign">in tears</span> "weeping" is from 1550s. <span class="foreign">Tear gas</span> first recorded 1917.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tear">tear (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"act of ripping or rending," 1660s, from <a href="/etym/s/tear" target="_blank">tear</a> (v.1). Old English had <span class="foreign">ter</span> (n.) "tearing, laceration, thing torn."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tear">tear (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"pull apart," Old English <span class="foreign">teran</span> "to tear, lacerate" (class IV strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">tær</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">toren</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*teran</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">terian</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">teren</span> "to consume," Old High German <span class="foreign">zeran</span> "to destroy," German <span class="foreign">zehren</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">ga-tairan</span> "to tear, destroy"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*der-</span> (2) "to split, peel, flay," with derivatives referring to skin and leather (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">drnati</span> "cleaves, bursts," Greek <span class="foreign">derein</span> "to flay," Armenian <span class="foreign">terem</span> "I flay," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">dera</span> "to burst asunder," Breton <span class="foreign">darn</span> "piece").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Old English past tense survived long enough to get into Bible translations as <span class="foreign">tare</span> before giving place 17c. to <span class="foreign">tore</span>, which is from the old past participle <span class="foreign">toren</span>. Sense of "to pull by force" (away from some situation or attachment) is attested from late 13c. To <span class="foreign">be torn between</span> two things (desires, loyalties, etc.) is from 1871.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/tear">tear (v.2)</a></dt><dd>early 15c., "shed tears," 1650s, "fill with tears" mainly in American English, from <a href="/etym/s/tear" target="_blank">tear</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Teared</span>; <span class="foreign">tearing</span>. Old English verb <span class="foreign">tæherian</span>, <span class="foreign">tearian</span> "to weep" did not survive into Middle English.</dd></dl></div></div>
