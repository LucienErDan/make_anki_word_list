                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/rich">rich</a> 富有的，丰富的</span><p>来自中古英语 riche,有权的，有钱的，来自古英语 rice,有权的，来自 PIE*reg,统治，管理， 词源同 regal,regulate.引申诸相关比喻义。词义的演变体现了权钱的关系。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rich">rich</a></dt><dd><b>rich: </b><span>[OE] The original meaning of <i>rich </i>was ‘mighty, noble’. It goes back ultimately to the Indo-European base *<i>reg</i>- ‘move in a straight line’, hence ‘direct’, hence ‘rule’, source of English <i>right</i>, Latin <i>rēx </i>‘king’ (ancestor of English <i>regal</i>, <i>royal</i>, etc), and Latin <i>regere </i>‘rule’ (ancestor of English <i>regent</i>, <i>regiment</i>, etc).<br><br>The Old Celtic equivalent of Latin <i>rēx </i>was <i>rīx </i>‘king’. This was borrowed into prehistoric Germanic, where it subsequently evolved into German <i>reich</i>, Dutch <i>rijk</i>, Swedish <i>rik</i>, Danish <i>rig</i>, and English <i>rich</i>. (It was also taken over by the Romance languages, giving French <i>riche</i>, Italian <i>ricco</i>, etc.) The sense ‘mighty, noble’ survived in English into the late Middle Ages, but ‘wealthy’ had started to develop in Germanic, and eventually saw off ‘mighty’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/regal" target="_blank">regal</a>, <a href="/etym/s/right" target="_blank">right</a>, <a href="/etym/s/royal" target="_blank">royal</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/rich">rich (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">rice</span> "strong, powerful; great, mighty; of high rank," in later Old English "wealthy," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*rikijaz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">rikr</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">rik</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">rig</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">rike</span> "wealthy, mighty," Dutch <span class="foreign">rijk</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">rihhi</span> "ruler, powerful, rich," German <span class="foreign">reich</span> "rich," Gothic <span class="foreign">reiks</span> "ruler, powerful, rich"), borrowed from a Celtic source akin to Gaulish <span class="foreign">*rix</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">ri</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">rig</span>) "king," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*reg-</span> "move in a straight line," hence, "direct, rule" (see <a href="/etym/s/rex" target="_blank">rex</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The form of the word was influenced in Middle English by Old French <span class="foreign">riche</span> "wealthy, magnificent, sumptuous," which is, with Spanish <span class="foreign">rico</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">ricco</span>, from Frankish <span class="foreign">*riki</span> "powerful," or some other cognate Germanic source.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Old English also had a noun, <span class="foreign">rice</span> "rule, reign, power, might; authority; empire." The evolution of the word reflects a connection between wealth and power in the ancient world. Of food and colors, from early 14c.; of sounds, from 1590s. Sense of "entertaining, amusing" is recorded from 1760. The noun meaning "the wealthy" was in Old English.</dd></dl></div></div>
