                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/to">to</a> 朝，向</span><p>来自古英语 to,朝向，在某方向，来自 West-Germanic*to,朝向，来自 PIE*do,指示代词词干， 朝，向，衍生前缀 de-.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/to">to</a></dt><dd><b>to: </b><span>[OE] <i>To </i>comes from a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>tō</i>, which also produced German <i>zu </i>and Dutch <i>toe</i>. This went back ultimately to an Indo-European *<i>do</i>, which also produced Russian <i>do </i>‘to’. <i>Too </i>is historically the same word as <i>to</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/too" target="_blank">too</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/to">to (prep.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">to</span> "in the direction of, for the purpose of, furthermore," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*to</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">to</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">too</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">zuo</span>, German <span class="foreign">zu</span> "to"), from PIE pronominal base <span class="foreign">*do-</span> "to, toward, upward" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">donec</span> "as long as," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">do</span> "as far as, to," Greek suffix <span class="foreign">-de</span> "to, toward," Old Irish <span class="foreign">do</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">da-</span>), from demonstrative <span class="foreign">*de-</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Not found in Scandinavian, where the equivalent of <a href="/etym/s/till" target="_blank">till</a> (prep.) is used. In Old English, the preposition (<span class="foreign">go to town</span>) leveled with the adverb (<span class="foreign">the door slammed to</span>) except where the adverb retained its stress (<span class="foreign">tired and hungry too</span>); there it came to be written with <span class="foreign">-oo</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/too" target="_blank">too</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The nearly universal use of <span class="foreign">to</span> with infinitives (<span class="foreign">to sleep, to dream</span>, etc.) arose in Middle English out of the Old English dative use of <span class="foreign">to</span>, and it helped drive out the Old English inflectional endings (though in this use <span class="foreign">to</span> itself is a mere sign, without meaning).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Commonly used as a prefix in Middle English (<span class="foreign">to-hear</span> "listen to," etc.), but few of these survive (<span class="foreign">to-do</span>, <span class="foreign">together</span>, and time references such as <span class="foreign">today</span>, <span class="foreign">tonight</span>, <span class="foreign">tomorrow</span> -- Chaucer also has <span class="foreign">to-yeere</span>). <span class="foreign">To and fro</span> "side to side" is attested from mid-14c. Phrase <span class="foreign">what's it to you</span> "how does that concern you?" (1819) is a modern form of an old question:&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Huæd is ðec ðæs?&#13; <br />[John xxi:22, in Lindisfarne Gospel, c.950]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>

            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">

It has nothing <b>to</b> do with age. </dt> <dd>美是一种态度，与年龄无关。</dd> <p>来自金山词霸</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. The best thing <b>to</b> do when entering unknown territory is smile. </dt> <dd>踏入未知地带最好的对策就是微笑。</dd> <p>来自美剧《凯莉日记》</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. No matter where you go in life or how old you get, there's always something new <b>to</b> learn about. After all, life is full of surprises. </dt> <dd>不管你生活在哪里，你有多少岁，总有新东西要学习，毕竟，生活总是充满惊喜。</dd> <p>来自金山词霸 每日一句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5.
