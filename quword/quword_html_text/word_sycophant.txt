                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sycophant">sycophant</a> 告密者，诽谤者，谄媚者，拍马者</span><p>来自拉丁语 sycopanta,来自希腊语 sykophantes,告密者，诽谤者，字面意思为展示无花果的人， 来自 sykon,无花果，-phant,显现，展示，词源同 epiphany,phantasm.其词源说法不一，其中两</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sycophant">sycophant</a></dt><dd><b>sycophant: </b><span>[16] <i>Sycophants </i>are etymologically ‘fig-showers’. The word comes via Latin <i>sychophanta </i>from Greek <i>súkophántēs</i>, a compound formed from <i>súkon </i>‘fig’ and -<i>phántēs </i>‘shower’, a derivative of <i>phaínein </i>‘show’ (source of English <i>fancy</i>, <i>phantom</i>, etc). <i>Súkon </i>(which probably came from a Semitic source that also produced Latin <i>ficus </i>‘fig’, source of English <i>fig</i>) was used metaphorically for ‘cunt’, and hence for an ‘indecent gesture made by putting the thumb into the mouth or between two fingers’.<br><br>People who grassed on criminals were said to ‘show them the fig’ – ‘show them two fingers’, as it might be expressed in modern English. And so the term <i>súkophántēs </i>came to be used for an ‘informer’, and eventually, via ‘one who ingratiates himself by informing’, for a ‘flatterer’ or ‘toady’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/fancy" target="_blank">fancy</a>, <a href="/etym/s/phantom" target="_blank">phantom</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sycamore" target="_blank">sycamore</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sycophant">sycophant (n.)</a></dt><dd>1530s (in Latin form <span class="foreign">sycophanta</span>), "informer, talebearer, slanderer," from Middle French <span class="foreign">sycophante</span> and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">sycophanta</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">sykophantes</span> "false accuser, slanderer," literally "one who shows the fig," from <span class="foreign">sykon</span> "fig" (see <a href="/etym/s/fig" target="_blank">fig</a>) + <span class="foreign">phainein</span> "to show" (see <a href="/etym/s/phantasm" target="_blank">phantasm</a>). "Showing the fig" was a vulgar gesture made by sticking the thumb between two fingers, a display which vaguely resembles a fig, itself symbolic of a vagina (<span class="foreign">sykon</span> also meant "vulva"). The modern accepted explanation is that prominent politicians in ancient Greece held aloof from such inflammatory gestures, but privately urged their followers to taunt their opponents. The sense of "mean, servile flatterer" is first recorded in English 1570s.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; The explanation, long current, that it orig. meant an informer against the unlawful exportation of figs cannot be substantiated. [OED]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
