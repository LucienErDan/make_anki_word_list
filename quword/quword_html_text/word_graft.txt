                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/graft">graft</a> 嫁接，行贿</span><p>来自PIE*gerbh, 刮，刻，切，词源同carve,graph. 用于植物学术语嫁接，即把切下来的一种植物移植到另一种植物上。俚语义行贿，即切下留作己用。比较 bribe.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/graft">graft</a></dt><dd><b>graft: </b><span>[15] <i>Graft</i>, in its original sense ‘plant part inserted into a living plant’ (the application to skin and other animal tissue is a late 19thcentury development), came from its resemblance in shape to a pencil. Greek <i>graphíon </i>meant ‘writing implement, stylus’ (it was a derivative of the verb <i>gráphein </i>‘write’, source of English <i>graphic</i>). It passed via Latin <i>graphium </i>into Old French as <i>grafe</i>, gradually changing in its precise application with the advance of writing technology.<br><br>By the time it reached Old French it denoted a ‘pencil’, and it was then that the resemblance to two artificially united plant stems was noted and the metaphor born. English took the word over as <i>graff </i>in the late 14th century (it actually survived in that form into the 19th century), and within a hundred years had added a -<i>t </i>to the end to give modern English <i>graft</i>. <i>Graft </i>‘corruption’, first recorded in mid 19th-century America, may be the same word, perhaps derived from the notion of a <i>graft </i>as an ‘insertion’, hence ‘something extra, on the side’. <i>Graft </i>‘hard work’ [19], on the other hand, is probably a different word, perhaps based on the English dialect verb <i>graft </i>‘dig’, an alteration of <i>grave </i>‘dig’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/graphic" target="_blank">graphic</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/graft">graft (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"shoot inserted into another plant," late 15c. alteration of Middle English <span class="foreign">graff</span> (late 14c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">graife</span> "grafting knife, carving tool; stylus, pen," from Latin <span class="foreign">graphium</span> "stylus," from Greek <span class="foreign">grapheion</span> "stylus," from <span class="foreign">graphein</span> "to write" (see <a href="/etym/s/-graphy" target="_blank">-graphy</a>). So called probably on resemblance of a stylus to the pencil-shaped shoots used in grafting. The terminal <span class="foreign">-t-</span> in the English word is not explained. Surgical sense is from 1871.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/graft">graft (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"corruption," 1865, perhaps 1859, American English, perhaps from British slang <span class="foreign">graft</span> "one's occupation" (1853), which is perhaps from the identical word meaning "a ditch, moat," literally "a digging" (1640s), from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">graft</span>, from <span class="foreign">graven</span> "to dig" (see <a href="/etym/s/grave" target="_blank">grave</a> (v.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/graft">graft (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 15c., "insert a shoot from one tree into another," from <a href="/etym/s/graft" target="_blank">graft</a> (n.1). Figurative use by 1530s. Surgical sense by 1868. Related: <span class="foreign">Grafted</span>; <span class="foreign">grafting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
