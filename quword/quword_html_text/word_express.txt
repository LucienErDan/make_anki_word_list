            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/express">express</a> 表达</span><p>ex-, 向外。press, 压，推。即推出，表达。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/express">express</a></dt><dd><b>express: </b><span>[14] Something that is <i>expressed </i>is literally ‘pressed out’. The word comes via Old French from Vulgar Latin *<i>expressāre</i>, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>ex</i>- ‘out’ and <i>pressāre </i>‘press’. Its meaning developed metaphorically from ‘press out’ to ‘form by pressure’ (presumably applied originally to modelling in clay or some similar substance, and subsequently to sculpture and then painting), and finally to ‘make known in words’.<br><br>The Vulgar Latin verb was in fact moving in on territory already occupied by its classical Latin forerunner <i>exprimere </i>(source of French <i>exprimer </i>‘express’ and perhaps of English <i>sprain </i>[17]). The past participle of this was <i>expressus</i>, used adjectivally for ‘prominent, distinct, explicit’. Old French took it over as <i>expres </i>and passed it on to English in the 14th century.<br><br>By now its meaning was moving towards ‘intended for a particular purpose’, and in the 19th century it was applied to ‘special’ trains (as in ‘football specials’). It did not take long, however, for this to slip via ‘train for people wanting to go to a particular place, and therefore not stopping anywhere else’ to ‘fast train’. Hence the modern sense of <i>express</i>, ‘fast’, was born.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/espresso" target="_blank">espresso</a>, <a href="/etym/s/press" target="_blank">press</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sprain" target="_blank">sprain</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/express">express (v.1)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "represent in visual arts; put into words," from Old French <span class="foreign">espresser</span>, <span class="foreign">expresser</span> "press, squeeze out; speak one's mind" (Modern French <span class="foreign">exprimer</span>), Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">expressare</span>, frequentative of Latin <span class="foreign">exprimere</span> "represent, describe, portray, imitate, translate," literally "to press out" (source also of Italian <span class="foreign">espresso</span>); the sense evolution here perhaps is via an intermediary sense such as "clay, etc., that under pressure takes the form of an image," from <span class="foreign">ex-</span> "out" (see <a href="/etym/s/ex-" target="_blank">ex-</a>) + <span class="foreign">pressare</span> "to press, push," from Latin <span class="foreign">premere</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/press" target="_blank">press</a> (v.1)). Related: <span class="foreign">Expressed</span>; <span class="foreign">expresses</span>; <span class="foreign">expressing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/express">express (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "stated explicitly, not implied, clearly made known" from Old French <span class="foreign">espres</span>, <span class="foreign">expres</span> (13c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">expressus</span> "clearly presented, distinct, articulated precisely," past participle of <span class="foreign">exprimere</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/express" target="_blank">express</a> (v.)). Also late 14c. as an adverb, "specially, on purpose;" it also doubled as an adverb in Old French. An <span class="foreign">express</span> train (1841) originally was one that ran to a certain station.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/express">express (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to send by express service," 1716, from <a href="/etym/s/express" target="_blank">express</a> (n.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/express">express (n.)</a></dt><dd>1610s, "special messenger," from <a href="/etym/s/express" target="_blank">express</a> (adj.). Sense of "business or system for sending money or parcels" is by 1794.</dd></dl></div></div>
