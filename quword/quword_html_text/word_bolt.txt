                                        <div class="row" id="yd-word-pron">英 [bəʊlt]
美 [bolt]</div>
                                        <div class="row" id="yd-word-meaning"><ul>
<li>n. 螺栓，螺钉；闪电，雷电；门闩；弩箭</li>
<li>vt. 筛选；囫囵吞下；（把门、窗等）闩上；突然说出，脱口说出</li>
<li>vi. （门窗等）闩上，拴住；冲出，跳出；（马等的）脱缰；囫囵吞下</li>
<li>adv. 突然地；像箭似地；直立地</li>
</ul></div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bolt">bolt</a> 螺栓，闪电</span><p>来自PIE *bheld, 击，打。可能同bill, 击，咬，鸟嘴。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bolt">bolt</a></dt><dd><b>bolt: </b><span>[OE] In Old English, a bolt was an arrow, particularly of the short stout variety used in crossbows (hence the phrase <i>shoot one’s bolt</i>). The more familiar modern sense ‘fastening pin’ developed in the 13th century. The verbal sense ‘make a quick escape’ comes from the notion of firing a projectile. The word appears in other Germanic languages (for instance German <i>bolz </i>‘bolt’), but its ultimate origin is unknown.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bolt">bolt (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">bolt</span> "short, stout arrow with a heavy head;" also "crossbow for throwing bolts," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bultas</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">bolti</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">bolt</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">bout</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bolzen</span>), perhaps from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bheld-</span> "to knock, strike" (cognates: Lithuanian <span class="foreign">beldu</span> "I knock," <span class="foreign">baldas</span> "pole for striking").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Applied since Middle English to other short metal rods (especially those with knobbed ends). From the notion of an arrow's flight comes the <span class="foreign">lightning bolt</span> (1530s). A <span class="foreign">bolt</span> of canvas (c. 1400) was so called for its shape. Adverbial phrase <span class="foreign">bolt upright</span> is from late 14c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bolt">bolt (v.)</a></dt><dd>from <a href="/etym/s/bolt" target="_blank">bolt</a> (n.) in its various senses; from a crossbow arrow's quick flight comes the meaning "to spring, to make a quick start" (early 13c.). Via the notion of runaway horses, this came to mean "to leave suddenly" (early 19c.). Meaning "to gulp down food" is from 1794. The meaning "to secure by means of a bolt" is from 1580s. Related: <span class="foreign">Bolted</span>; <span class="foreign">bolting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
