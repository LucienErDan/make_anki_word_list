                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/muscle">muscle</a> 肌肉</span><p>来自拉丁语musculus,小老鼠，肌肉，来自mus,老鼠，词源同mouse,-cul,小词后缀。因古人认为鼓起的肌肉形如跳跃的小老鼠而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/muscle">muscle</a></dt><dd><b>muscle: </b><span>[16] Ultimately, <i>muscle </i>and <i>mussel </i>[OE] are the same word, and both owe their origin to a supposed resemblance to a mouse. They go back to Latin <i>mūsculus</i>, literally ‘little mouse’, a diminutive form of <i>mūs </i>‘mouse’, which was applied to the shellfish because of a similarity in shape and colour, and to ‘muscle’ because the shape and movement of certain muscles beneath the skin, such as the biceps, reminded people of a mouse.<br><br>Latin <i>mūsculus </i>‘mussel’ was borrowed into Old English as <i>muscle </i>or <i>muxle</i>; the -<i>ss</i>spelling began to emerge in the 15th century, inspired by Middle Low German <i>mussel </i>(which came from *<i>muscula</i>, a Vulgar Latin feminization of Latin <i>mūsculus </i>and source of French <i>moule </i>‘mussel’) and reinforced in the 16th century by the introduction via Old French of <i>muscle </i>for ‘muscle’.<br><br>The notion of resemblance to a mouse also lies behind English <i>musk</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/mouse" target="_blank">mouse</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mussel" target="_blank">mussel</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/muscle">muscle (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., from Middle French <span class="foreign">muscle</span> "muscle, sinew" (14c.) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">musculus</span> "a muscle," literally "little mouse," diminutive of <span class="foreign">mus</span> "mouse" (see <a href="/etym/s/mouse" target="_blank">mouse</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; So called because the shape and movement of some muscles (notably biceps) were thought to resemble mice. The analogy was made in Greek, too, where <span class="foreign">mys</span> is both "mouse" and "muscle," and its comb. form gives the medical prefix <a href="/etym/s/myo-" target="_blank">myo-</a>. Compare also Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">mysi</span> "mouse," <span class="foreign">mysica</span> "arm;" German <span class="foreign">Maus</span> "mouse; muscle," Arabic <span class="foreign">'adalah</span> "muscle," <span class="foreign">'adal</span> "field mouse." In Middle English, <span class="foreign">lacerte</span>, from the Latin word for "lizard," also was used as a word for a muscle.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Musclez &amp; lacertez bene one selfe þing, Bot þe muscle is said to þe fourme of mouse &amp; lacert to þe fourme of a lizard. [Guy de Chauliac, "Grande Chirurgie," c. 1425]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Hence <span class="foreign">muscular</span> and <span class="foreign">mousy</span> are relatives, and a Middle English word for "muscular" was <span class="foreign">lacertous</span>, "lizardy." Figurative sense of "force, violence, threat of violence" is 1930, American English. <span class="foreign">Muscle car</span> "hot rod" is from 1969.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/muscle">muscle (v.)</a></dt><dd>1913, "to accomplish by strength," from <a href="/etym/s/muscle" target="_blank">muscle</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Muscled</span>; <span class="foreign">muscling</span>. To <span class="foreign">muscle in</span> is 1929 in underworld slang.</dd></dl></div></div>
