                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/date">date</a> 日期，约会，海枣</span><p>1.日期，来自拉丁语datum, 给予，来自PIE*do, 给予，词源同donate, dowry. 来自古罗马时期的书信习惯，在结果处写上datum, 附上年月日，因此引申该词义。</p><p> 2.海枣，来自希腊语dactylos, 手指，词源同date, pterodactyl. 因形似手指而得名。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/date">date</a></dt><dd><b>date: </b><span><i>Date </i>‘time of an event’ and <i>date </i>‘fruit’ are distinct words in English, and perhaps unexpectedly the latter [13] entered the language a century before the former. It came via Old French <i>date </i>and Latin <i>dactylus </i>from Greek <i>dáktulos</i>, which originally meant literally ‘finger’ or ‘toe’. The term was originally applied from the supposed resemblance of a date to a little brown finger or toe. <i>Date </i>‘time’ [14] was acquired from Old French <i>date</i>, a descendant of medieval Latin <i>data</i>, which represented a nominal use of the feminine form of Latin <i>datus</i>, the past participle of the verb <i>dare </i>‘give’.<br><br>It originated in such phrases as <i>data Romae </i>‘given at Rome’, the ancient Roman way of dating letters. (<i>Data </i>‘information’ [17], on the other hand, is the plural of the neuter form of the past participle, <i>datum</i>.) Among the wide range of other English words descended from Latin <i>dare </i>(which can be traced back ultimately to an Indo- European base *<i>dō</i>-) are <i>antidote </i>[15] (etymologically ‘what is given against something’), <i>condone </i>[19], <i>dado </i>[17] (a borrowing from Italian, ‘cube’), <i>dative </i>[15], <i>donation </i>[15], <i>dice</i>, <i>dowry </i>and <i>endow </i>(both ultimately from Latin <i>dōs </i>‘dowry’, a relative of <i>dare</i>), <i>edit</i>, and <i>pardon </i>[13].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pterodactyl" target="_blank">pterodactyl</a>; <a href="/etym/s/antidote" target="_blank">antidote</a>, <a href="/etym/s/condone" target="_blank">condone</a>, <a href="/etym/s/data" target="_blank">data</a>, <a href="/etym/s/dative" target="_blank">dative</a>, <a href="/etym/s/dice" target="_blank">dice</a>, <a href="/etym/s/donation" target="_blank">donation</a>, <a href="/etym/s/edit" target="_blank">edit</a>, <a href="/etym/s/endow" target="_blank">endow</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pardon" target="_blank">pardon</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/date">date (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"time," early 14c., from Old French <span class="foreign">date</span> (13c.) "date, day; time," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">data</span>, noun use of fem. singular of Latin <span class="foreign">datus</span> "given," past participle of <span class="foreign">dare</span> "to give, grant, offer," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*do-</span> "to give" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">dadati</span> "gives," <span class="foreign">danam</span> "offering, present;" Old Persian <span class="foreign">dadatuv</span> "let him give," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">dati</span> "give," <span class="foreign">dani</span> "tribute;" Latin <span class="foreign">donum</span> "gift;" Greek <span class="foreign">didomi</span>, <span class="foreign">didonai</span>, "to give, offer," <span class="foreign">doron</span> "gift;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">duonis</span> "gift," Old Irish <span class="foreign">dan</span> "gift, endowment, talent," Welsh <span class="foreign">dawn</span> "gift"). &#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Roman convention of closing every article of correspondence by writing "given" and the day and month -- meaning perhaps "given to messenger" -- led to <span class="foreign">data</span> becoming a term for "the time (and place) stated." (a Roman letter would include something along the lines of <span class="foreign">datum Romae pridie Kalendas Maias</span> -- "given at Rome on the last day of April."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/date">date (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"liaison," 1885, gradually evolving from <a href="/etym/s/date" target="_blank">date</a> (n.1) in its general sense of "appointment;" romantic sense by 1890s. Meaning "person one has a date with" is from 1925.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/date">date (n.2)</a></dt><dd>the fruit, late 13c., from Old French <span class="foreign">date</span>, from Old Provençal <span class="foreign">datil</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">dactylus</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">daktylos</span> "date," originally "finger, toe;" so called because of fancied resemblance between oblong fruit of the date palm and human digits. Possibly from a Semitic source (compare Hebrew <span class="foreign">deqel</span>, Aramaic <span class="foreign">diqla</span>, Arabic <span class="foreign">daqal</span> "date palm") and assimilated to the Greek word for "finger."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/date">date (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"have a romantic liaison;" 1902, from <a href="/etym/s/date" target="_blank">date</a> (n.3). Related: <span class="foreign">Dated</span>; <span class="foreign">dating</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/date">date (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to mark (a document) with the date," late 14c., from <a href="/etym/s/date" target="_blank">date</a> (n.1). Meaning "to assign to or indicate a date" (of an event) is from c. 1400. Meaning "to mark as old-fashioned" is from 1895. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Dated" target="_blank">Dated</a>; <span class="foreign">dating</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
