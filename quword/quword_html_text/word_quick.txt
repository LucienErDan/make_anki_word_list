                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/quick">quick</a> 快速的，活的，有生命的</span><p>来自古英语cwic,活的，有生命的，来自PIE*gweie,存活，生命，词源同zoo,vival.引申词义 快速的，敏捷的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quick">quick</a></dt><dd><b>quick: </b><span>[OE] Originally <i>quick </i>meant ‘alive’ (as in the now fossilized phrase <i>the quick and the dead</i>); it was not until the 13th century that the sense ‘rapid’ began to emerge. It goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>kwikwaz </i>(which also produced Swedish <i>kvick </i>‘rapid’); and this was descended from an Indo-European base *<i>gwej</i>-, which branched out into Latin <i>vīvus </i>‘alive’ (source of English <i>vivid</i>), Greek <i>bíos </i>‘life’ (source of English <i>biology</i>), Welsh <i>byw </i>‘alive’, Russian <i>zhivoj </i>‘alive’, etc.<br><br>The <i>couch </i>of <i>couch grass </i>[16] is a variant of the now seldom encountered <i>quitch</i>, whose Old English ancestor <i>cwice </i>may be related to <i>quick </i>(the allusion presumably being to its vigorous growth).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/biology" target="_blank">biology</a>, <a href="/etym/s/vivid" target="_blank">vivid</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quick">quick (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">cwic</span> "living, alive, animate," and figuratively, of mental qualities, "rapid, ready," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kwikwaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">quik</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">kvikr</span> "living, alive," Dutch <span class="foreign">kwik</span> "lively, bright, sprightly," Old High German <span class="foreign">quec</span> "lively," German <span class="foreign">keck</span> "bold"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*gweie-</span> (1) "to live" (see <a href="/etym/s/bio-" target="_blank">bio-</a>). Sense of "lively, swift" developed by late 12c., on notion of "full of life."&#13; <blockquote>&#13; NE <span class="foreign">swift</span> or the now more common <span class="foreign">fast</span> may apply to rapid motion of any duration, while in <span class="foreign">quick</span> (in accordance with its original sense of 'live, lively') there is a notion of 'sudden' or 'soon over.' We speak of a <span class="foreign">fast</span> horse or runner in a race, a <span class="foreign">quick</span> starter but not a <span class="foreign">quick</span> horse. A somewhat similar feeling may distinguish NHG <span class="foreign">schnell</span> and <span class="foreign">rasch</span> or it may be more a matter of local preference. [Buck]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; Of persons, "mentally active," from late 15c. Also in Middle English used of soft soils, gravel pits, etc. where the ground is shifting and yielding (mid-14c., compare <a href="/etym/s/quicksand" target="_blank">quicksand</a>). As an adverb from c. 1300. To <span class="foreign">be quick about</span> something is from 1937. <span class="foreign">Quick buck</span> is from 1946, American English. <span class="foreign">Quick-change artist</span> (1886) originally was an actor expert in playing different roles in the same performance of a show. <span class="foreign">Quick-witted</span> is from 1520s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quick">quick (n.)</a></dt><dd>"living persons," Old English <span class="foreign">cwic</span>, from <a href="/etym/s/quick" target="_blank">quick</a> (adj.); frequently paired with the dead, as in Old English <span class="foreign">cwicum &amp; deadum</span>. <span class="foreign">The quick</span> "tender part of the flesh" (under a nail, etc.) is from 1520s, as is the figurative use of it.</dd></dl></div></div>
