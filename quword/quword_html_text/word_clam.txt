                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. “蛤蜊”本身很像该词的近似音译。青岛人将其念着“gala”，据说源于四本当年殖民统治时日语发音的影响，而日语发音就是该英语单词发音的近似音译。<br />
2. 谐音“壳耐磨”----想到这个谐音就直到它是贝壳类动物---蛤蜊外部有两片坚硬的贝壳，当然很耐磨了。<br />
3. clamp => clam.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/clam">clam</a> 蛤蜊</span><p>词源同clamp,夹子，夹具。后用以指蛤蜊之类的双壳软体动物。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/clam">clam</a></dt><dd><b>clam: </b><span>[OE] Old English <i>clam </i>meant ‘something for tying up or fastening, fetter’; it can be traced back to a prehistoric Germanic base *<i>klam</i>-, which also produced <i>clamp </i>[14] and is related to <i>climb</i>. There is a gap in the word’s history in early Middle English times, but it reappears at the end of the 14th century in the sense ‘clamp’, and in the 16th century it was applied, originally in Scotland, to the mollusc which now bears the name, apparently on the grounds that its two shells close like the jaws of a clamp or vice.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/clamp" target="_blank">clamp</a>, <a href="/etym/s/climb" target="_blank">climb</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/clam">clam (n.)</a></dt><dd>bivalve mollusk, c. 1500, in <span class="foreign">clam-shell</span>, originally Scottish, apparently a particular use from Middle English <span class="foreign">clam</span> "pincers, vice, clamp" (late 14c.), from Old English <span class="foreign">clamm</span> "bond, fetter, grip, grasp," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*klam-</span> "to press or squeeze together" (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">klamma</span> "cramp, fetter, constriction," German <span class="foreign">Klamm</span> "a constriction"). If this is right then the original reference is to the shell. <span class="foreign">Clam-chowder</span> attested from 1822. To be <span class="foreign">happy as a clam</span> is from 1833, but the earliest uses do not elaborate on the notion behind it, unless it be self-containment.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/clam">clam (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to dig for clams," 1630s, American English, from <a href="/etym/s/clam" target="_blank">clam</a> (n.). <span class="foreign">Clam up</span> "be quiet" is 1916, American English, but <span class="foreign">clam</span> was used in this sense as an interjection mid-14c.</dd></dl></div></div>
