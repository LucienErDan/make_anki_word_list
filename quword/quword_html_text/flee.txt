                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/flee">flee</a> 逃避</span><p>来自PIE*pleu, 飞，词源同fly. 引申词义飞走。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flee">flee</a></dt><dd><b>flee: </b><span>[OE] <i>Flee</i>, like its close relatives German <i>fliehe</i>, Dutch <i>vlieden</i>, and Swedish and Danish <i>fly</i>, comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>thleukhan</i>, a word of unknown origin. In Old English, <i>flee </i>and <i>fly </i>had the same past tense and past participle (and indeed the same derivatives, represented in modern English by <i>flight</i>), and this, together with a certain similarity in meaning, has led to the two verbs being associated and often confused, but there is no reliable evidence that they are etymologically connected.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/flee">flee (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fleon</span>, <span class="foreign">flion</span> "take flight, fly from, avoid, escape" (contracted class II strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">fleah</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">flogen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fleuhan</span> "to run away" (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">fliohan</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">flöja</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">flia</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vlieden</span>, German <span class="foreign">fliehen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">þliuhan</span> "to flee"), probably from PIE <span class="foreign">*pleuk-</span>, extended form of root <span class="foreign">*pleu-</span> "to flow" (see <a href="/etym/s/pluvial" target="_blank">pluvial</a>). Also compare <a href="/etym/s/fly" target="_blank">fly</a> (v.2).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Weak past tense and past participle <span class="foreign">fled</span> emerged in Middle English under influence of Scandinavian. Old English had a transitive form, <span class="foreign">geflieman</span> "put to flight, banish, drive away," which came in handy in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle. Related: <span class="foreign">fled</span>; <span class="foreign">Fleeing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
