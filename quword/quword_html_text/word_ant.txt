                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/ant">ant</a> 蚂蚁</span><p>来自古英语aemette,蚂蚁，来自West Germanic*amaitjo,砍下，来自*ai,离开，去掉，*mai,砍， 切，词源同maim.字面意思即砍下，切开，引申词义分段，成段，用于指蚂蚁。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ant">ant</a></dt><dd><b>ant: </b><span>[OE] The word <i>ant </i>appears to carry the etymological sense ‘creature that cuts off or bites off’. Its Old English form, <i>æmette</i>, was derived from a hypothetical Germanic compound *<i>aimaitjōn</i>, formed from the prefix *<i>ai</i>- ‘off, away’ and the root *<i>mait</i>- ‘cut’ (modern German has the verb <i>meissen </i>‘chisel, carve’): thus, ‘the biter’.<br><br>The Old English word later developed along two distinct strands: in one, it became <i>emmet</i>, which survived into the 20th century as a dialectal form; while in the other it progressed through <i>amete </i>and <i>ampte </i>to modern English <i>ant</i>. If the notion of ‘biting’ in the naming of the ant is restricted to the Germanic languages (German has <i>ameise</i>), the observation that it and its nest smell of urine has been brought into play far more widely.<br><br>The Indo-European root *<i>meigh</i>-, from which ultimately we get <i>micturate </i>‘urinate’ [18], was also the source of several words for ‘ant’, including Greek <i>múrmēx </i>(origin of English <i>myrmecology </i>‘study of ants’, and also perhaps of <i>myrmidon </i>[14] ‘faithful follower’, from the <i>Myrmidons</i>, a legendary Greek people who loyally followed their king Achilles in the Trojan war, and who were said originally to have been created from ants), Latin <i>formīca </i>(hence English <i>formic acid </i>[18], produced by ants, and <i>formaldehyde </i>[19]), and Danish <i>myre</i>.<br><br>It also produced Middle English <i>mire </i>‘ant’, the underlying meaning of which was subsequently reinforced by the addition of <i>piss </i>to give <i>pismire</i>, which again survived dialectally into the 20th century.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/ant">ant (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1500, from Middle English <span class="foreign">ampte</span> (late 14c.), from Old English <span class="foreign">æmette</span> "ant," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*amaitjo</span> (cognates: Old High German <span class="foreign">ameiza</span>, German <span class="foreign">Ameise</span>) from a compound of bases <span class="foreign">*ai-</span> "off, away" + <span class="foreign">*mai-</span> "cut," from PIE <span class="foreign">*mai-</span> "to cut" (cognates: <a href="/etym/s/maim" target="_blank">maim</a>). Thus the insect's name is, etymologically, "the biter off."&#13; <blockquote>As þycke as ameten crepeþ in an amete hulle [chronicle of Robert of Gloucester, 1297]</blockquote>&#13; <a href="/etym/s/Emmet" target="_blank">Emmet</a> survived into 20c. as an alternative form. <span class="foreign">White ant</span> "termite" is from 1729. To have <span class="foreign">ants in one's pants</span> "be nervous and fidgety" is from 1934, made current by a popular song; <a href="/etym/s/antsy" target="_blank">antsy</a> embodies the same notion.</dd></dl></div></div>
