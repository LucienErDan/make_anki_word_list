                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/like">like</a> 喜欢，相似，类似</span><p>缩写自古英语gelic,同样，相似，ge-,一起，-lic,身体，形体，并衍生后缀-ly.引申词义同类，相似，喜欢。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/like">like</a></dt><dd><b>like: </b><span>English has a diverse group of words spelled <i>like</i>, but they all come ultimately from the same source. This was prehistoric Germanic *<i>līkam </i>‘appearance, form, body’ (source also of the <i>lych</i>- of English <i>lych-gate </i>[15], which originally signified the gate through which a coffin was carried into a churchyard). From it was derived the verb *<i>līkōjan</i>, which passed into English as <i>like</i>.<br><br>It originally meant ‘please’, but by the 12th century had done a semantic somersault to ‘find pleasing’. The same Germanic *<i>likam </i>produced English <i>alike</i>, literally ‘similar in appearance’, whose Old Norse relative <i>líkr </i>was borrowed into English as the adjective <i>like </i>[12]. Its adverbial and prepositional uses developed in the later Middle Ages. Also from Old Norse came the derived adjective <i>likely </i>[13].<br><br>English <i>each </i>and <i>such </i>were formed from the ancestor of <i>like</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/each" target="_blank">each</a>, <a href="/etym/s/such" target="_blank">such</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/like">like (adj.)</a></dt><dd>"having the same characteristics or qualities" (as another), Middle English shortening of Old English <span class="foreign">gelic</span> "like, similar," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*galika-</span> "having the same form," literally "with a corresponding body" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gilik</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">gelijk</span>, German <span class="foreign">gleich</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">galeiks</span> "equally, like"), a compound of <span class="foreign">*ga-</span> "with, together" + Germanic base <span class="foreign">*lik-</span> "body, form; like, same" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">lic</span> "body," German <span class="foreign">Leiche</span> "corpse," Danish <span class="foreign">lig</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">lik</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">lijk</span> "body, corpse"). Analogous, etymologically, to Latin <span class="foreign">conform</span>. The modern form (rather than <span class="foreign">*lich</span>) may be from a northern descendant of the Old English word's Norse cognate, <span class="foreign">glikr</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Formerly with comparative <span class="foreign">liker</span> and superlative <span class="foreign">likest</span> (still in use 17c.). The preposition (c. 1200) and the adverb (c. 1300) both are from the adjective. As a conjunction, first attested early 16c. The word has been used as a postponed filler (<span class="foreign">"going really fast, like"</span>) from 1778; as a presumed emphatic (<span class="foreign">"going, like, really fast"</span>) from 1950, originally in counterculture slang and bop talk. Phrase <span class="foreign">more like it</span> "closer to what is desired" is from 1888.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/like">like (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "a similar thing" (to another), from <a href="/etym/s/like" target="_blank">like</a> (adj.).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/like">like (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">lician</span> "to please, be sufficient," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*likjan</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">lika</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">likia</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">lihhen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">leikan</span> "to please"), from <span class="foreign">*lik-</span> "body, form; like, same."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The basic meaning seems to be "to be like" (see <a href="/etym/s/like" target="_blank">like</a> (adj.)), thus, "to be suitable." <span class="foreign">Like</span> (and <span class="foreign">dislike</span>) originally flowed the other way: <span class="foreign">It likes me,</span> where we would say <span class="foreign">I like it</span>. The modern flow began to appear late 14c. (compare <a href="/etym/s/please" target="_blank">please</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
