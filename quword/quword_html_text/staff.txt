            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/staff">staff</a> 棍棒，权杖，拐杖，军事参谋，工作人员，全体职员</span><p>来自古英语 staef,棍棒，木柱，拐杖，来自 Proto-Germanic*stabaz,棍棒，柱子，来自 PIE*stebh, 棍子，柱子，支撑，来自 PIE*sta,站，站立，词源同 stand,staple.引申词义旗杆，并引申比喻 义军事参谋，即司令官的助手，后词义通用化为工作人员，全体职员等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/staff">staff</a></dt><dd><b>staff: </b><span>[OE] <i>Staff </i>is a widespread Germanic word, with relatives in German <i>stab</i>, Dutch and Swedish <i>staf</i>, and Danish <i>stav</i>. These point back to a common Germanic ancestor *<i>stabaz</i>. Its ancestral meaning is ‘stick’, and its use as a collective term for ‘employees’, which dates in English from the 18th century, probably originated as an allusion to the carrying of a <i>staff </i>or ‘stick’ of office by a person in charge of subordinates – who thus became subsumed metaphorically under the notion of his ‘staff’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/staff">staff (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">stæf</span> (plural <span class="foreign">stafas</span>), "walking stick, strong pole used for carrying, rod used as a weapon, pastoral staff," probably originally <span class="foreign">*stæb</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stabaz</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">staf</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">stafr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">stav</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">stef</span>, Middle Low German and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">staf</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">stab</span>, German <span class="foreign">Stab</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">*stafs</span> "element;" Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">stapel</span> "pillar, foundation"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*stebh-</span> "post, stem, to support, place firmly on, fasten" (cognates: Old Lithuanian <span class="foreign">stabas</span> "idol," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">stebas</span> "staff, pillar;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">stoboru</span> "pillar;" Sanskrit <span class="foreign">stabhnati</span> "supports;" Greek <span class="foreign">stephein</span> "to tie around, encircle, wreathe," <span class="foreign">staphyle</span> "grapevine, bunch of grapes;" Old English <span class="foreign">stapol</span> "post, pillar").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As "pole from which a flag is flown," 1610s. In musical notation from 1660s. Sense of "group of military officers that assists a commander" is attested from 1702, apparently from German, from the notion of the "baton" that is a badge of office or authority (a sense attested in English from 1530s); hence <span class="foreign">staff officer</span> (1702), <span class="foreign">staff-sergeant</span> (1811). Meaning "group of employees (as at an office or hospital)" is first found 1837. <span class="foreign">Staff of life</span> "bread" is from the Biblical phrase <span class="foreign">break the staff of bread</span> meaning "cut off the supply of food" (Lev. xxvi:26), translating Hebrew <span class="foreign">matteh lekhem</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Old English word, in plural, was the common one used for "letter of the alphabet, character," hence "writing, literature," and many compounds having to do with writing, such as <span class="foreign">stæfcræft</span> "grammar," <span class="foreign">stæfcræftig</span> "lettered," <span class="foreign">stæflic</span> "literary," <span class="foreign">stæfleahtor</span> "grammatical error," with <span class="foreign">leahtor</span> "vice, sin, offense."&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/staff">staff (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to provide with a staff of assistants," 1859, from <a href="/etym/s/staff" target="_blank">staff</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Staffed</span>; <span class="foreign">staffing</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
