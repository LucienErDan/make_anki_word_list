                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/commit">commit</a> 承诺，保证</span><p>com-, 强调。-mit, 派遣，过去分词-miss, 词源同mission. 该词在使用过程中，扩展了诸多词义，甚至词义演变模糊的词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/commit">commit</a></dt><dd><b>commit: </b><span>[14] Etymologically, <i>commit </i>simply means ‘put together’. It comes from Latin <i>committere</i>, a compound verb formed from the prefix <i>com</i>- ‘together’ and the verb <i>mittere </i>‘put, send’ (whence English <i>missile </i>and <i>mission</i>). It originally meant literally ‘join, connect’, but then branched out along the lines of ‘put for safety, entrust’ (the force of <i>com</i>- here being more intensive than collective) and ‘perpetrate’ (exactly how this sense evolved is not clear).<br><br>The whole range of meanings followed the Latin verb into English, although ‘put together’ was never more than an archaism, and died out in the 17th century. Of derivatives based on the Latin verb’s past participial stem <i>commiss</i>-, <i>commission </i>entered English in the 14th century and <i>commissionaire </i>(via French) in the 18th century. Medieval Latin <i>commissārius </i>produced English <i>commissary </i>[14] and, via French, Russian <i>commissar</i>, borrowed into English in the 20th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/commissar" target="_blank">commissar</a>, <a href="/etym/s/committee" target="_blank">committee</a>, <a href="/etym/s/missile" target="_blank">missile</a>, <a href="/etym/s/mission" target="_blank">mission</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/commit">commit (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 14c., "to give in charge, entrust," from Latin <span class="foreign">committere</span> "to unite, connect, combine; to bring together," from <span class="foreign">com-</span> "together" (see <a href="/etym/s/com-" target="_blank">com-</a>) + <span class="foreign">mittere</span> "to put, send" (see <a href="/etym/s/mission" target="_blank">mission</a>). Evolution into modern range of meanings is not entirely clear. Sense of "perpetrating" was ancient in Latin; in English from mid-15c. The intransitive use (in place of <span class="foreign">commit oneself</span>) first recorded 1982, probably influenced by existentialism use (1948) of <span class="foreign">commitment</span> to translate Sartre's <span class="foreign">engagement</span> "emotional and moral engagement."</dd></dl></div></div>
