                                                                        <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/adventure">adventure</a></dt><dd><b>adventure: </b><span>[13] <i>Adventure </i>derives ultimately from a Latin verb meaning ‘arrive’. It originally meant ‘what comes or happens by chance’, hence ‘luck’, but it took a rather pessimistic downturn via ‘risk, danger’ to (in the 14th century) ‘hazardous undertaking’. Its Latin source was <i>advenīre</i>, formed from the prefix <i>ad</i>and <i>venīre </i>‘come’. Its past participle stem, <i>advent</i>-, produced English <i>advent </i>[12] and <i>adventitious </i>[17], but it was its future participle, <i>adventura </i>‘about to arrive’, which produced <i>adventure</i>.<br><br>In the Romance languages in which it subsequently developed (Italian <i>avventura</i>, Spanish <i>aventura</i>, and French <i>aventure</i>, the source of Middle English <i>aventure</i>) the <i>d </i>disappeared, but it was revived in 15th – 16thcentury French in imitation of Latin. The reduced form <i>venture </i>first appears in the 15th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/adventitious" target="_blank">adventitious</a>, <a href="/etym/s/avent" target="_blank">avent</a>, <a href="/etym/s/venture" target="_blank">venture</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/adventure">adventure (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">auenture</span> "that which happens by chance, fortune, luck," from Old French <span class="foreign">aventure</span> (11c.) "chance, accident, occurrence, event, happening," from Latin <span class="foreign">adventura (res)</span> "(a thing) about to happen," from <span class="foreign">adventurus</span>, future participle of <span class="foreign">advenire</span> "to come to, reach, arrive at," from <span class="foreign">ad-</span> "to" (see <a href="/etym/s/ad-" target="_blank">ad-</a>) + <span class="foreign">venire</span> "to come" (see <a href="/etym/s/venue" target="_blank">venue</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning developed through "risk/danger" (a trial of one's chances), c. 1300, and "perilous undertaking" (late 14c.) and thence to "a novel or exciting incident" (1560s). Earlier it also meant "a wonder, a miracle; accounts of marvelous things" (13c.). The <span class="foreign">-d-</span> was restored 15c.-16c. <a href="/etym/s/Venture" target="_blank">Venture</a> is a 15c. variant.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/adventure">adventure (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "to risk the loss of," from <a href="/etym/s/adventure" target="_blank">adventure</a> (n.). Meaning "to take a chance" is early 14c. Related: <span class="foreign">Adventured</span>; <span class="foreign">adventuring</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
