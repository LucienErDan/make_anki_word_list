                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">Sweep，vt., vi.( 常与up连用)扫，扫除；清扫；掠过。音：“四围泼”。解：四围泼一泼水，扫地不会扬尘士。<br />
2. 谐音“四围扑”-----用扫帚在四周扑打扑打。<br />
3. swoop => sweep: 通过俯冲式的扑打，将灰尘拍走、卷走。</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sweep">sweep</a> 打扫，清扫，横扫，挥舞</span><p>来自古英语 swapan,清扫，打扫，来自 Proto-Germanic*swaipana,清扫，打扫，来自 PIE*swei, 弯，转，摇摆，词源同 swoop,swing.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sweep">sweep</a></dt><dd><b>sweep: </b><span>[13] The Old English word for ‘sweep’ was <i>swāpan</i>, which evolved into Middle English <i>swope</i>. Modern English <i>sweep</i>, which began to emerge in the 13th century, probably came from the old past tense <i>swepe</i>, a descendant of Old English <i>swēop</i>. <i>Swāpan </i>itself came from a prehistoric Germanic base *<i>swei</i>- ‘swing, bend’, which also produced German <i>schweifen </i>‘wander’ and English <i>swift</i>. <i>Swipe </i>[19] probably originated as a dialectal variant of <i>sweep</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/swift" target="_blank">swift</a>, <a href="/etym/s/swipe" target="_blank">swipe</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sweep">sweep (v.)</a></dt><dd>early 14c., "make clean by sweeping with a broom;" mid-14c., "perform the act of sweeping," of uncertain origin, perhaps from a past tense form of Middle English <span class="foreign">swope</span> "sweep," from Old English <span class="foreign">swapan</span> "to sweep" (transitive &amp; intransitive); see <a href="/etym/s/swoop" target="_blank">swoop</a> (v.), or perhaps from a Scandinavian source. Related: <span class="foreign">Swept</span>; <span class="foreign">sweeping</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; From late 14c. as "hasten, rush, move swiftly and strongly;" also "collect by sweeping." From c. 1400 in transitive sense "drive quickly, impel, move or carry forward by force;" mid-15c. as "clear (something) away." Meaning "win all the events" is 1960, American English. Sense of "pass systematically over in search of something" is from 1966. To <span class="foreign">sweep (someone) off (his or her) feet</span> "affect with infatuation" is from 1913.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sweep">sweep (n.)</a></dt><dd>mid-13c., "stroke, force," from <a href="/etym/s/sweep" target="_blank">sweep</a> (v.). Meaning "act of sweeping" is from 1550s. From 1670s as "range, extent of a continued motion." In reference to police or military actions, it is attested from 1837. Sense of "a winning of all the tricks in a card game" is from 1814 (see <a href="/etym/s/sweepstakes" target="_blank">sweepstakes</a>); extended to other sports by 1960. Meaning "rapid survey or inspection" is from 1966. As a shortened form of <span class="foreign">chimney-sweeper</span>, first attested 1796.</dd></dl></div></div>
