                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. post (n.1): "a timber set upright, pillar", from Latin postis "door, post, doorpost," perhaps from por- "forth" (see pro-) + stare "stand". => post (v.1): "to affix (a paper, etc.) to a post" (in a public place), hence, "to make known".<br />
2. post (n.2): post- => post. "place when on duty, place where one is stationed, station for post horses, job, position".<br />
3. post (n.3): "mail system", "riders and horses posted at intervals," from post (n.2) on notion of riders and horses "posted" at intervals along a route to speed mail in relays.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/post">post</a> 柱，桩，张贴，岗位，派驻，邮政，邮件，立刻，马上</span><p>来自拉丁语ponere,放置，-st,过去分词格，词源同position,composite.并引申如下词义：1.柱子，桩，张贴榜文，张贴；2.军事哨所，岗位，派驻；3.古驿站，驿马，邮差，邮政，邮件；4.立刻，马上，来自短语to ride post,骑上驿马，换马，紧急赶路。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post</a></dt><dd><b>post: </b><span>Including the prefix <i>post</i>-, English has four different words <i>post</i>. The oldest, ‘long upright piece of wood, metal, etc’ [OE], was borrowed from Latin <i>postis</i>. From it was derived the verb <i>post </i>‘fix to a post’, which in turn produced <i>poster </i>[19], denoting a placard that can be ‘posted’ up. <i>Post </i>‘mail’ [16] comes via French <i>poste </i>and Italian <i>posta </i>from Vulgar Latin *<i>posta</i>, a contracted version of <i>posita</i>, the feminine form of the past participle of Latin <i>pōnere </i>‘put, place’ (source of English <i>position</i>).<br><br>The notion underlying the sense ‘mail’ is of riders ‘placed’ or stationed at intervals along a road so as to carry letters at speed by a relay system. <i>Post </i>‘job’ [16] reached English via a very similar route, this time from the neuter form of the Latin past participle, <i>positum</i>. This became *<i>postum </i>in Vulgar Latin, which produced Italian <i>posto</i>, French <i>poste</i>, and English <i>post</i>.<br><br>Here again the word’s original meaning, ‘position where a soldier is placed’, reflects that of its Latin source <i>pōnere</i>. The prefix <i>post</i>- comes from the Latin preposition <i>post </i>‘after’. It occurs in a number of English words that go back to Latin ancestors (including <i>posterior </i>[16], <i>posthumous</i>, <i>postpone </i>[16], <i>postscript </i>[16], and the more heavily disguised <i>preposterous</i>), as well as being widely used to create new coinages (such as <i>postgraduate </i>[19] and <i>postwar </i>[20]).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/position" target="_blank">position</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (adv.)</a></dt><dd>1540s, "with post horses," hence, "rapidly;" especially in the phrase <span class="foreign">to ride post</span> "go rapidly," from <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a> (n.3).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"a timber set upright," from Old English <span class="foreign">post</span> "pillar, doorpost," and Old French <span class="foreign">post</span> "post, upright beam," both from Latin <span class="foreign">postis</span> "door, post, doorpost," perhaps from <span class="foreign">por-</span> "forth" (see <a href="/etym/s/pro-" target="_blank">pro-</a>) + <span class="foreign">stare</span> "to stand," from PIE root <span class="foreign">*sta-</span> "to stand, set down, make or be firm" (see <a href="/etym/s/stet" target="_blank">stet</a>). Similar compound in Sanskrit <span class="foreign">prstham</span> "back, roof, peak," Avestan <span class="foreign">parshti</span> "back," Greek <span class="foreign">pastas</span> "porch in front of a house, colonnade," Middle High German <span class="foreign">virst</span> "ridepole," Lithuanian <span class="foreign">pirstas</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pristu</span> "finger" (PIE <span class="foreign">*por-st-i-</span>).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"place when on duty," 1590s, from Middle French <span class="foreign">poste</span> "place where one is stationed," also, "station for post horses" (16c.), from Italian <span class="foreign">posto</span> "post, station," from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*postum</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">positum</span>, neuter past participle of <span class="foreign">ponere</span> "to place, to put" (see <a href="/etym/s/position" target="_blank">position</a> (n.)). Earliest sense in English was military; meaning "job, position" is attested 1690s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"mail system," c. 1500, "riders and horses posted at intervals," from <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a> (n.2) on notion of riders and horses "posted" at intervals along a route to speed mail in relays, probably formed on model of Middle French <span class="foreign">poste</span> in this sense (late 15c.). Meaning "system for carrying mail" is from 1660s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (v.4)</a></dt><dd>"to put up bail money," 1781, from one of the nouns <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a>, but which one is uncertain. Related: <span class="foreign">Posted</span>; <span class="foreign">posting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to affix (a paper, etc.) to a post" (in a public place), hence, "to make known," 1630s, from <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a> (n.1). Related: <span class="foreign">Posted</span>; <span class="foreign">posting</span>.&#13;</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (v.2)</a></dt><dd>in bookkeeping, "to transfer from a day book to a formal account," 1620s, from <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a> (n.2) via a figurative sense of "carrying" by post horses. Related: <a href="/etym/s/Posted" target="_blank">Posted</a>; <span class="foreign">posting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (v.3)</a></dt><dd>"to send through the postal system," 1837, from <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a> (n.3). Earlier, "to travel with relays of horses" (1530s). Related: <span class="foreign">Posted</span>; <span class="foreign">posting</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/post">post (v.5)</a></dt><dd>"to station at a post," from <a href="/etym/s/post" target="_blank">post</a> (n.2). Related: <span class="foreign">Posted</span>; <span class="foreign">posting</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
