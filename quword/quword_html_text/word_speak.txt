                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/speak">speak</a> 说话，交谈，发言，演说</span><p>来自古英语 specan,拼写变体自 sprecan,说话，交谈，来自 Proto-Germanic*sprekana,说话，来 自 PIE*sprek,说话，可能来自 PIE*spreg,播洒，散开，展开，词源同 spark,spread.引申诸相关 词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/speak">speak</a></dt><dd><b>speak: </b><span>[OE] The usual Old English word for ‘speak’ was <i>sprecan</i>, which has close living relatives in German <i>sprechen </i>and Dutch <i>spreken</i>. <i>Specan</i>, the ancestor of modern English <i>speak</i>, did not appear until around the year 1000, but already by the 12th century it had virtually replaced <i>sprecan</i>. It is not known how the <i>r</i>-less form (which has no surviving relatives in other Germanic languages) arose, but it is clearly a secondary development of the <i>r</i>-form.<br><br>This seems to be connected with Danish <i>spage </i>‘crackle’, Lithuanian <i>sprageti </i>‘crackle’, and Sanskrit <i>sphūrj</i>- ‘crackle, rustle’, suggesting that the English word’s use for ‘utter, say’ arose via an earlier ‘crackle, prattle, babble, chatter’ (English ‘crack on about something’, ‘not what it’s cracked up to be’, and ‘crack a joke’ are remnants of an earlier widespread use of English <i>crack </i>for ‘speak’).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/speech" target="_blank">speech</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/speak">speak (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">specan</span>, variant of <span class="foreign">sprecan</span> "to speak, utter words; make a speech; hold discourse (with others)" (class V strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">spræc</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">sprecen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sprek-</span>, <span class="foreign">*spek-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sprecan</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">spreka</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">spreken</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sprehhan</span>, German <span class="foreign">sprechen</span> "to speak," Old Norse <span class="foreign">spraki</span> "rumor, report"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*spreg-</span> (1) "to speak," perhaps identical with PIE root <span class="foreign">*spreg-</span> (2) "to strew," on notion of speech as a "scattering" of words.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The <span class="foreign">-r-</span> began to drop out in Late West Saxon and was gone by mid-12c., perhaps from influence of Danish <span class="foreign">spage</span> "crackle," also used in a slang sense of "speak" (compare <a href="/etym/s/crack" target="_blank">crack</a> (v.) in slang senses having to do with speech, such as <span class="foreign">wisecrack, cracker, all it's cracked up to be</span>). Elsewhere, rare variant forms without <span class="foreign">-r-</span> are found in Middle Dutch (<span class="foreign">speken</span>), Old High German (<span class="foreign">spehhan</span>), dialectal German (<span class="foreign">spächten</span> "speak").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Not the primary word for "to speak" in Old English (the "Beowulf" author prefers <span class="foreign">maþelian</span>, from <span class="foreign">mæþel</span> "assembly, council," from root of <span class="foreign">metan</span> "to meet;" compare Greek <span class="foreign">agoreuo</span> "to speak, explain," originally "speak in the assembly," from <span class="foreign">agora</span> "assembly").</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/speak">speak (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "talk, speech," from <a href="/etym/s/speak" target="_blank">speak</a> (v.). Survived in Scottish English and dialect, but modern use in compounds probably is entirely traceable to Orwell (see <a href="/etym/s/Newspeak" target="_blank">Newspeak</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/speak">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. The Ukrainians <b>speak</b> a Slavonic language similar to Russian. </dt> <dd>乌克兰人所说的那种斯拉夫语系的语言类似于俄语。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>2.
