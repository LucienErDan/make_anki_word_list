                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/sable">sable</a> 黑貂，貂皮</span><p>来自古法语 sable,黑貂，貂皮，最终来自斯拉夫语系某语言，比较俄罗斯语和捷克语 sobol.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sable">sable</a></dt><dd><b>sable: </b><span>[14] The sable, an animal like a large weasel with valuable fur, lives in northern Europe and Asia, and its name reflects where it comes from – for it is of Slavic origin, related to Russian <i>sóbol’</i>. It came west with the fur trade, and was borrowed into medieval Latin as <i>sabellum</i>. From there it made its way into English via Old French <i>sable</i>.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sable">sable (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"fur or pelt of the European sable" (<span class="foreign">Martes zibellina</span>), early 15c., from Middle French <span class="foreign">sable</span> (also <span class="foreign">martre sable</span> "sable martin"), in reference to the mammal or its fur, borrowed in Old French from Germanic (Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sabel</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">sabel</span>, Middle High German <span class="foreign">zobel</span>), ultimately from a Slavic source (compare Russian, Czech <span class="foreign">sobol</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">soból</span>, the name of the animal), "which itself is borrowed from an East-Asiatic language" [Klein], but Russian sources (such as Vasmer) find none of the proposed candidates satisfactory.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/sable">sable (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"black" as a heraldic color, early 14c., commonly identified with <a href="/etym/s/sable" target="_blank">sable</a> (n.1), but the animal's fur is brown and this may be a different word of unknown origin; or it might reflect a medieval custom (unattested) of dyeing sable fur black. As an adjective from late 14c. Emblematic of mourning or grief from c. 1600; c. 1800 as "black" with reference to Africans and their descendants, often with mock dignity.</dd></dl></div></div>
