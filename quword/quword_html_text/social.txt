            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/social">social</a> 社会的，社交的，交际的</span><p>soc-,群体的，社会的，-al,形容词后缀。引申词义社交的，交际的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/social">social</a></dt><dd><b>social: </b><span>[16] Latin <i>socius </i>meant ‘companion, partner’. It came ultimately from the Indo- European base *<i>seq</i>- ‘follow’ (source of English <i>sequel</i>, <i>sequence</i>, etc). From it was derived the adjective <i>sociālis</i>, which has given English <i>social </i>(<i>socialism </i>was coined in the early 19th century). Other Latin derivatives have given English <i>associate</i>, <i>sociable </i>[16] and <i>society </i>[16]. <i>Sociology </i>[19] was borrowed from French <i>sociologie</i>, a term coined in 1830 by the philosopher Auguste Comte.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/associate" target="_blank">associate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sequel" target="_blank">sequel</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sequence" target="_blank">sequence</a>, <a href="/etym/s/society" target="_blank">society</a>, <a href="/etym/s/sue" target="_blank">sue</a>, <a href="/etym/s/suit" target="_blank">suit</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/social">social (adj.)</a></dt><dd>late 15c., "devoted to or relating to home life;" 1560s as "living with others," from Middle French <span class="foreign">social</span> (14c.) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">socialis</span> "of companionship, of allies; united, living with others; of marriage, conjugal," from <span class="foreign">socius</span> "companion, ally," probably originally "follower," from PIE <span class="foreign">*sokw-yo-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*sekw-</span> (1) "to follow," and thus related to <span class="foreign">sequi</span> "to follow" (see <a href="/etym/s/sequel" target="_blank">sequel</a>). Compare Old English <span class="foreign">secg</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">seggr</span> "companion," which seem to have been formed on the same notion). Related: <span class="foreign">Socially</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Sense of "characterized by friendliness or geniality" is from 1660s. Meaning "living or liking to live with others; companionable, disposed to friendly intercourse" is from 1720s. Meaning "of or pertaining to society as a natural condition of human life" first attested 1695, in Locke. Sense of "pertaining to fashionable society" is from 1873.&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Social climber</span> is from 1893; <span class="foreign">social work</span> is 1890; <span class="foreign">social worker</span> 1886. <span class="foreign">Social drinking</span> first attested 1807. <span class="foreign">Social studies</span> as an inclusive term for history, geography, economics, etc., is attested from 1916. <span class="foreign">Social security</span> "system of state support for needy citizens" is attested from 1907 (the Social Security Act was passed by U.S. Congress in 1935). <span class="foreign">Social butterfly</span> is from 1867, in figurative reference to "flitting."&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Social contract</span> (1763) is from translations of Rousseau. <span class="foreign">Social Darwinism</span> attested from 1887. <span class="foreign">Social engineering</span> attested from 1899. <span class="foreign">Social science</span> is from 1785.&#13; In late 19c. newspapers, <span class="foreign">social evil</span> is "prostitution." <span class="foreign">Social justice</span> is attested by 1718; <span class="foreign">social network</span> by 1971; <span class="foreign">social networking</span> by 1984; <span class="foreign">social media</span> by 2008.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/social">social (n.)</a></dt><dd>"friendly gathering," 1870, from <a href="/etym/s/social" target="_blank">social</a> (adj.). In late 17c. it meant "a companion, associate."</dd></dl></div></div>
