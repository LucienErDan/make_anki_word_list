                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/pass">pass</a> 通过，通行，考试及格</span><p>来自法语passer,走过，经过，来自拉丁语*passare,走过，经过，来自passus,脚步，步伐，来自PIE*pete,展开，伸展，词源同 pace,feather,compete.引申诸相关词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pass">pass</a></dt><dd><b>pass: </b><span>[13] Strictly speaking, English has two distinct words <i>pass</i>, although they come from the same ultimate source, and have now virtually merged together again. That source was Latin <i>passus </i>‘step’, which gave English <i>pace</i>. From it was derived the Vulgar Latin verb *<i>passāre</i>, which came to English via Old French <i>passer</i>. The past participle of the English verb has become <i>past</i>; and other related English words include <i>passage </i>and <i>passenger</i>.<br><br>The noun <i>pass </i>‘mountain defile’ originated as a sense of <i>pace</i>, but since the early modern English period has been spelled (and pronounced) <i>pass</i>, partly through reassociation with French <i>pas</i>, partly under the influence of the verb <i>pass</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/pace" target="_blank">pace</a>, <a href="/etym/s/passage" target="_blank">passage</a>, <a href="/etym/s/passenger" target="_blank">passenger</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pass">pass (v.)</a></dt><dd>late 13c. (transitive) "to go by (something)," also "to cross over," from Old French <span class="foreign">passer</span> (11c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*passare</span> "to step, walk, pass" (source also of Spanish <span class="foreign">pasar</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">passare</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">passus</span> "step, pace" (see <a href="/etym/s/pace" target="_blank">pace</a> (n.)). Intransitive sense of "to go on, to move forward, make one's way" is attested from c. 1300. Figurative sense of "to experience, undergo" (as in <span class="foreign">pass the time</span>) is first recorded late 14c. Sense of "to go through an examination successfully" is from early 15c. Meaning "decline to do something" is attested from 1869, originally in cards (euchre). In football, hockey, soccer, etc., the meaning "to transfer the ball or puck to another player" is from c. 1865. Related: <span class="foreign">Passed</span>; <span class="foreign">passing</span>.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The meaning "to be thought to be something one is not" (especially in racial sense) is from 1935, from <span class="foreign">pass oneself off (as)</span>, first found 1809. The general verb sense of "to be accepted as equivalent" is from 1590s. <span class="foreign">Pass up</span> "decline, refuse" is attested from 1896. <span class="foreign">Pass the buck</span> is from 1865, said to be poker slang reference to the buck horn-handled knife that was passed around to signify whose turn it was to deal. <span class="foreign">Pass the hat</span> "seek contributions" is from 1762. <span class="foreign">Pass-fail</span> as a grading method is attested from 1955, American English.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pass">pass (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"mountain defile," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">pas</span> "step, track, passage," from Latin <span class="foreign">passus</span> "step, pace" (see <a href="/etym/s/pace" target="_blank">pace</a> (n.)).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pass">pass (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"written permission to pass into, or through, a place," 1590s, from <a href="/etym/s/pass" target="_blank">pass</a> (v.). Sense of "ticket for a free ride or admission" is first found 1838. Colloquial <span class="foreign">make a pass</span> "offer an amorous advance" first recorded 1928, perhaps from a sporting sense. Phrase <span class="foreign">come to pass</span> (late 15c.) uses the word with a sense of "completion, accomplishment."</dd></dl></div></div>
