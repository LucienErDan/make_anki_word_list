                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/yard">yard</a> 庭院</span><p>来自PIE*gher,围，围场，词源同garden.字母g,y的音变来自旧时书写混淆的产物。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/yard">yard</a> 桁，桅横杆，码</span><p>来自PIE*ghazdh,杆，棍，来自PIE*ghei,刺，击，词源同gad,goad。后该词用以指船上的桅杆以及用做固定的长度单位。比较fathom.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/yard">yard</a></dt><dd><b>yard: </b><span><i>Yard </i>‘enclosed area’ [OE] and <i>yard </i>‘three feet’ [OE] are distinct words, both of ancient ancestry. The former probably goes back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>ghorto</i>-, which also produced Latin <i>cohors </i>‘court’ (source of English <i>cohort </i>and <i>court</i>) and <i>hortus </i>‘garden’ (source of English <i>horticulture</i>) and Russian <i>gorod </i>‘town’ (as in <i>Leningrad</i>).<br><br>Its prehistoric Germanic descendant was *<i>gard</i>-, which, as well as providing English with <i>yard</i>, has produced <i>garden</i>, <i>garth </i>[14] (via Old Norse), and the second syllable of <i>orchard</i>. <i>Yard </i>‘three feet’ originally meant ‘stick, rod’ (a sense preserved nautically, as in <i>yardarm </i>[16]). It goes back ultimately to prehistoric Germanic *<i>gazdaz </i>‘pointed stick’ (source of the <i>gad </i>of <i>gadfly </i>[16], etymologically the fly with the ‘sting’).<br><br>From this was derived West Germanic *<i>gazdjō</i>, which evolved into German <i>gerte </i>‘sapling, riding cane’, Dutch <i>gard </i>‘twig, rod’, and English <i>yard</i>. The Anglo-Saxons used the term as a unit of measurement of land, equal to about five metres (what later became known as a <i>rod, pole</i>, or <i>perch</i>), but its modern use for ‘three feet’ did not emerge until the 14th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/cohort" target="_blank">cohort</a>, <a href="/etym/s/court" target="_blank">court</a>, <a href="/etym/s/garden" target="_blank">garden</a>, <a href="/etym/s/garth" target="_blank">garth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/horticulture" target="_blank">horticulture</a>, <a href="/etym/s/orchard" target="_blank">orchard</a>; <a href="/etym/s/gadfly" target="_blank">gadfly</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/yard">yard (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"patch of ground around a house," Old English <span class="foreign">geard</span> "fenced enclosure, garden, court; residence, house," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gardaz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">garðr</span> "enclosure, garden, yard;" Old Frisian <span class="foreign">garda</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">gaard</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">garto</span>, German <span class="foreign">Garten</span> "garden;" Gothic <span class="foreign">gards</span> "house," <span class="foreign">garda</span> "stall"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ghor-to-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="foreign">*gher-</span> (1) "to grasp, enclose," with derivatives meaning "enclosure" (cognates: Old English <span class="foreign">gyrdan</span> "to gird," Sanskrit <span class="foreign">ghra-</span> "house," Albanian <span class="foreign">garth</span> "hedge," Latin <span class="foreign">hortus</span> "garden," Phrygian <span class="foreign">-gordum</span> "town," Greek <span class="foreign">khortos</span> "pasture," Old Irish <span class="foreign">gort</span> "field," Breton <span class="foreign">garz</span> "enclosure, garden," and second element in Latin <span class="foreign">cohors</span> "enclosure, yard, company of soldiers, multitude").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">gardas</span> "pen, enclosure," Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">gradu</span> "town, city," and Russian <span class="foreign">gorod</span>, <span class="foreign">-grad</span> "town, city" belong to this group, but linguists dispute whether they are independent developments or borrowings from Germanic. As "college campus enclosed by the main buildings," 1630s. In railway usage, "ground adjacent to a train station or terminus, used for switching or coupling trains," 1827. <span class="foreign">Yard sale</span> is attested by 1976.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/yard">yard (n.2)</a></dt><dd>measure of length, Old English <span class="foreign">gerd</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">gierd</span> (West Saxon) "rod, staff, stick; measure of length," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*gazdijo</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gazdjo-</span> "stick, rod" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gerda</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ierde</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">gard</span> "rod;" Old High German <span class="foreign">garta</span>, German <span class="foreign">gerte</span> "switch, twig," Old Norse <span class="foreign">gaddr</span> "spike, sting, nail"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ghazdh-o-</span> "rod, staff, pole" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">hasta</span> "shaft, staff"). The nautical <a href="/etym/s/yard-arm" target="_blank">yard-arm</a> retains the original sense of "stick."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Originally in Anglo-Saxon times a land measure of roughly 5 meters (a length later called <span class="foreign">rod</span>, <span class="foreign">pole</span>, or <span class="foreign">perch</span>). Modern measure of "three feet" is attested from late 14c. (earlier rough equivalent was the <span class="foreign">ell</span> of 45 inches, and the <span class="foreign">verge</span>). In Middle English and after, the word also was a euphemism for "penis" (as in "Love's Labour's Lost," V.ii.676). Slang meaning "one hundred dollars" first attested 1926, American English. Middle English <span class="foreign">yerd</span> (Old English <span class="foreign">gierd</span>) also was "yard-land, yard of land," a varying measure but often about 30 acres or a quarter of a hide.</dd></dl></div></div>
