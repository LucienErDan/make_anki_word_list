                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fashion">fashion</a> 时尚</span><p>来自法语，来自词根fac, 做，词源同face, fact. 即做，准备，形象，外表。后来该词用来指时尚，潮流。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fashion">fashion</a></dt><dd><b>fashion: </b><span>[13] The underlying notion of <i>fashion </i>is of ‘making’, ‘forming’, or ‘shaping’. The main modern sense of the word developed via ‘particular shape or style’, ‘way, manner’, and ‘prevailing or current manner’. English acquired it via Anglo-Norman <i>fasun </i>from Latin <i>factiō</i>, a derivative of <i>facere </i>‘make, do’ (which has contributed an enormous range of vocabulary to English, from <i>fact </i>to <i>difficult</i>, and is distantly related to English <i>do </i>itself).<br><br>Latin <i>factiō </i>was only rarely used in the literal sense ‘making’. In classical times it was generally applied to a ‘group of people acting together’ (hence English <i>faction </i>[16]), and the metaphorical extension to ‘way, manner’ and ‘custom’ taken up by English <i>fashion </i>is a post-classical development.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/difficult" target="_blank">difficult</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fact" target="_blank">fact</a>, <a href="/etym/s/faction" target="_blank">faction</a>, <a href="/etym/s/factory" target="_blank">factory</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fashion">fashion (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">fasoun</span>, "physical make-up or composition; form, shape; appearance," from Old French <span class="foreign">façon</span>, <span class="foreign">fachon</span>, <span class="foreign">fazon</span> "face, appearance; construction, pattern, design; thing done; beauty; manner, characteristic feature" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">factionem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">factio</span>) "a making or doing, a preparing," also "group of people acting together," from <span class="foreign">facere</span> "to make" (see <a href="/etym/s/factitious" target="_blank">factitious</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Especially "style, manner" of make, dress, or embellishment (late 14c.); hence "prevailing custom; mode of dress and adornment prevailing in a place and time" (late 15c.). Meaning "good style, conformity to fashionable society's tastes" is from 1630s.&#13; <blockquote>To call a fashion wearable is the kiss of death. No new fashion worth its salt is wearable. [Eugenia Sheppard, "New York Herald Tribune," Jan. 13, 1960]</blockquote>&#13; In Middle English also spelled <span class="foreign">faschyoun</span>, <span class="foreign">facune</span>, <span class="foreign">faction</span>, etc. <span class="foreign">Fashion plate</span> (1851) originally was "full-page picture in a popular magazine showing the prevailing or latest style of dress," in reference to the typographic plate from which it was printed. Transferred sense of "well-dressed person" had emerged by 1920s. <span class="foreign">After a fashion</span> "to a certain extent" is from 1530s. Shakespeare (c. 1600) has both <span class="foreign">in fashion</span> and <span class="foreign">out of fashion</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fashion">fashion (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to form, give shape to," early 15c.; see <a href="/etym/s/fashion" target="_blank">fashion</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Fashioned</span>; <span class="foreign">fashioning</span>.&#13;</dd></dl></div></div>
