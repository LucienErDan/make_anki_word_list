                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/raven">raven</a> 渡鸦</span><p>来自古英语 hraefn,渡鸦，大乌鸦，来自 PIE*ker,尖叫声，拟声词，词源同 crow,crane.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/raven">raven</a> 掠夺，狼吞虎咽</span><p>来自古法语 raviner,抢劫，来自拉丁语 rapina,抢劫，掠夺，来自 rapere,抓走，夺走，词源同 rape,ravish.后引申词义饥饿，狼吞虎咽。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/raven">raven</a></dt><dd><b>raven: </b><span>English has two separate words <i>raven</i>. The bird-name [OE] is a general Germanic term, related to German <i>rabe</i>, Dutch <i>raaf</i>, and Danish <i>ravn</i>. It goes back to a prehistoric Germanic *<i>khrabnaz </i>or *<i>khraben</i>, which originated in an imitation of the raven’s harsh croaking. The verb <i>raven </i>‘prey, plunder’ [15], nowadays encountered virtually only in its present participle <i>ravening </i>and the derived <i>ravenous</i>, goes back ultimately to Latin <i>rapere </i>‘seize by force’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/rape" target="_blank">rape</a>, <a href="/etym/s/rapture" target="_blank">rapture</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ravenous" target="_blank">ravenous</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/raven">raven (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">hræfn</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">hrefn; hræfn</span> (Northumbrian, West Saxon), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*khrabanaz</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">hrafn</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">ravn</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">raaf</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hraban</span>, German <span class="foreign">Rabe</span> "raven," Old English <span class="foreign">hroc</span> "rook"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*ker-</span> (2), imitative of harsh sounds (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">crepare</span> "to creak, clatter," <span class="foreign">cornix</span> "crow," <span class="foreign">corvus</span> "raven;" Greek <span class="foreign">korax</span> "raven," <span class="foreign">korone</span> "crow;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kruku</span> "raven;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">krauklys</span> "crow"). <blockquote>Raven mythology shows considerable homogeneity throughout the whole area [northern regions of the northern hemisphere] in spite of differences in detail. The Raven peeps forth from the mists of time and the thickets of mythology, as a bird of slaughter, a storm bird, a sun and fire bird, a messenger, an oracular figure and a craftsman or culture hero. [Edward A. Armstrong, "The Folklore of Birds," 1958]</blockquote>Old English also used <span class="foreign">hræmn</span>, <span class="foreign">hremm</span>. The <span class="foreign">raven standard</span> was the flag of the Danish Vikings. The Quran connects the raven with Cain's murder of Abel; but in Christianity the bird plays a positive role in the stories of St. Benedict, St. Paul the Hermit, St. Vincent, etc. It was anciently believed to live to great old age, but the ancients also believed it wanting in parental care. The vikings, like Noah, were said to have used the raven to discover land. "When uncertain of their course they let one loose, and steered the vessel in his track, deeming that the land lay in the direction of his flight; if he returned to the ship, it was supposed to be at a distance" [Charles Swainson, "The Folk Lore and Provincial Names of British Birds," London, 1886].</dd></dl></div></div>
