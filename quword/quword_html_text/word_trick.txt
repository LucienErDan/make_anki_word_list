                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/trick">trick</a> 花招，把戏，戏法，窍门</span><p>来自古法语 trichier,欺骗，诡计，来自拉丁语 tricari,逃避，回避，来自 tricae,琐碎，胡扯，混 乱，可能来自 PIE*ter,转，卷入，词源同 turn,intricate,extricate.引申词义花招，把戏等。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/trick">trick</a></dt><dd><b>trick: </b><span>[15] <i>Trick </i>comes from Old French <i>trique</i>, a variant of <i>triche</i>, which was derived from the verb <i>trichier </i>‘cheat’ (source of English <i>treachery</i>). And this in turn probably came from Latin <i>trīcarī </i>‘make difficulties, play tricks’ (source also of English <i>extricate </i>[17] and <i>intricate </i>[15]), a derivative of <i>trīcae </i>‘difficulties, tricks’, whose origins are unknown. <i>Tricky </i>dates from the 18th century, its use for ‘difficult’ from the end of the 19th century.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/extricate" target="_blank">extricate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/intricate" target="_blank">intricate</a>, <a href="/etym/s/treachery" target="_blank">treachery</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/trick">trick (n.)</a></dt><dd>early 15c., "a cheat, a mean ruse," from Old North French <span class="foreign">trique</span> "trick, deceit, treachery, cheating," from <span class="foreign">trikier</span> "to deceive, to cheat," variant of Old French <span class="foreign">trichier</span> "to cheat, trick, deceive," of uncertain origin, probably from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*triccare</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">tricari</span> "be evasive, shuffle," from <span class="foreign">tricæ</span> "trifles, nonsense, a tangle of difficulties," of unknown origin.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "a roguish prank" is recorded from 1580s; sense of "the art of doing something" is first attested 1610s. Meaning "prostitute's client" is first attested 1915; earlier it was U.S. slang for "a robbery" (1865). To <span class="foreign">do the trick</span> "accomplish one's purpose" is from 1812; to <span class="foreign">miss a trick</span> "fail to take advantage of opportunity" is from 1889; from 1872 in reference to playing the card-game of whist, which might be the original literal sense. <span class="foreign">Trick-or-treat</span> is recorded from 1942. <span class="foreign">Trick question</span> is from 1907.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/trick">trick (v.)</a></dt><dd>"deceive by trickery," 1590s, from <a href="/etym/s/trick" target="_blank">trick</a> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">Tricked</span>; <span class="foreign">tricking</span>. The sense of "to dress, adorn" (c. 1500) is perhaps a different word entirely.</dd></dl></div></div>
