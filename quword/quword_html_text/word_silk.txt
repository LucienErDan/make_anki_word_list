                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/silk">silk</a> 蚕丝，丝绸，丝织品</span><p>来自古英语 sioloc,丝，丝织品，来自拉丁语 sericum,丝，来自 Sericus,丝，来自希腊语 Serikos, 丝织品，来自 Seres 国的物品，来自 Seres,古希腊时期的某东方国家，通常认为是指中国， 可能来自古汉语丝。比较 mare,母马，可能来自古汉语马。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/silk">silk</a></dt><dd><b>silk: </b><span>[OE] Like the substance itself, the word <i>silk </i>originated in the Far East, possibly in Chinese <i>sī </i>‘silk’. Its immediate ancestor is most closely represented by Manchurian <i>sirghe </i>and Mongolian <i>sirkek</i>. Silk-traders brought their term west, and the Greeks used it to coin a name for them: <i>Seres</i>, the ‘silk people’. That is the source of Latin <i>sēricum </i>and Irish <i>sīric </i>‘silk’, and also of English <i>serge</i>.<br><br>But there must have been another oriental form, with an <i>l </i>rather than an <i>r</i>, which made its more northerly way via the Balto-Slavic languages (leaving Russian <i>shelk </i>and Lithuanian <i>shilkai </i>‘silk’) to Germanic, where it has given Swedish and Danish <i>silke </i>and English <i>silk</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/serge" target="_blank">serge</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/silk">silk (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, from Old English <span class="foreign">seoloc</span>, <span class="foreign">sioloc</span> "silk, silken cloth," from Latin <span class="foreign">sericum</span> "silk," plural <span class="foreign">serica</span> "silken garments, silks," literally "Seric stuff," neuter of <span class="foreign">Sericus</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">Serikos</span> "silken; pertaining to the <span class="foreign">Seres</span>," an oriental people of Asia from whom the Greeks got silks. Western cultivation began 552 C.E., when agents from Byzantium impersonating monks smuggled silkworms and mulberry leaves out of China. Chinese <span class="foreign">si</span> "silk," Manchurian <span class="foreign">sirghe</span>, Mongolian <span class="foreign">sirkek</span> have been compared to this and the people name in Greek might be a rendering via Mongolian of the Chinese word for "silk," but this is uncertain.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Also found in Old Norse as <span class="foreign">silki</span> but not elsewhere in Germanic. The more common Germanic form is represented by Middle English <span class="foreign">say</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">seie</span>, with Spanish <span class="foreign">seda</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">seta</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zijde</span>, German <span class="foreign">Seide</span> is from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">seta</span> "silk," perhaps elliptical for <span class="foreign">seta serica</span>, or else a particular use of <span class="foreign">seta</span> "bristle, hair" (see <a href="/etym/s/seta" target="_blank">seta</a> (n.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; According to some sources [Buck, OED], the use of <span class="foreign">-l-</span> instead of <span class="foreign">-r-</span> in the Balto-Slavic form of the word (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">šelku</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šilkai</span>) passed into English via the Baltic trade and may reflect a Chinese dialectal form, or a Slavic alteration of the Greek word. But the Slavic linguist Vasmer dismisses that, based on the initial <span class="foreign">sh-</span> in the Slavic words, and suggests the Slavic words are from Scandinavian rather than the reverse.&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As an adjective from mid-14c. In reference to the "hair" of corn, 1660s, American English. Figurative use of <span class="foreign">silk-stocking</span> (n.) is from 1590s; as an adjective meaning "wealthy" it is attested from 1798, American English (silk stockings, especially worn by men, being regarded as extravagant and reprehensible, indicative of luxurious habits). <span class="foreign">Silk-screen</span> (n.) is first attested 1930; as a verb from 1961. <span class="foreign">Silk road</span> so called in English from 1931.</dd></dl></div></div>
