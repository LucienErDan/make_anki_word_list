            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/evangelist">evangelist</a> 传福音者</span><p>ev-, 好的，同eu-. angel, 天使，福音。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/evangelist">evangelist</a></dt><dd><b>evangelist: </b><span>[12] The original sense of <i>evangelist </i>was ‘writer of a gospel’. English used to have the word <i>evangel </i>‘gospel’. This came via Old French <i>evangile </i>and ecclesiastical Latin <i>evangelium </i>from Greek <i>euaggélion</i>, which in classical times meant ‘reward for bringing good news’ (it was a compound based ultimately on the prefix <i>eu</i>- ‘good, well’ and the noun <i>ággelos </i>‘messenger’ – source of English <i>angel</i>).<br><br>Later on it came to mean simply ‘good news’, and in early Christian texts written in Greek it denoted specifically any of the four books of the New Testament written by Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. (English <i>gospel </i>was originally a literal translation of it.) <i>Evangelist </i>itself comes from the Greek derivative <i>euaggelistés</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/angel" target="_blank">angel</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/evangelist">evangelist (n.)</a></dt><dd>late 12c., "Matthew, Mark, Luke or John," from Old French <span class="foreign">evangelist</span> and directly from Late Latin <span class="foreign">evangelista</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">euangelistes</span> "preacher of the gospel," literally "bringer of good news," from <span class="foreign">euangelizesthai</span> "bring good news," from <span class="foreign">eu-</span> "good" (see <a href="/etym/s/eu-" target="_blank">eu-</a>) + <span class="foreign">angellein</span> "announce," from <span class="foreign">angelos</span> "messenger" (see <a href="/etym/s/angel" target="_blank">angel</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; In early Greek Christian texts, the word was used of the four traditional authors of the narrative gospels. Meaning "itinerant preacher" was another early Church usage, revived in Middle English (late 14c.). Classical Greek <span class="foreign">euangelion</span> meant "the reward of good tidings;" sense transferred in Christian use to the glad tidings themselves. In Late Latin, Greek <span class="foreign">eu-</span> regularly was consonantized to <span class="foreign">ev-</span> before vowels.</dd></dl></div></div>

    document.getElementById('yd-search').onsubmit = function() {

        if (word == '') {
            return false;
        } else if (word.search(/^[\w \-]+$/) == 0) {
            top.location.href = '/w/' + encodeURIComponent(word.replace(/ /g, '_'));
            return false;

                var char_code = match.charCodeAt(0) - 0xfee0;
                return String.fromCharCode(char_code);
            });
        }
