                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bail">bail</a> 保释</span><p>来自拉丁词bajulus, 持有者，责任者。来自旧时的一种法律规定，如须保释某人出去，须自己进监狱代替保释者作为承诺和责任。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bail">bail</a></dt><dd><b>bail: </b><span>There are now three distinct words <i>bail </i>in English, although they may all be related. <i>Bail </i>‘money deposited as a guarantee when released’ [14] comes from Old French <i>bail</i>, a derivative of the verb <i>baillier </i>‘take charge of, carry’, whose source was Latin <i>bājulāre </i>‘carry’, from <i>bājulus </i>‘carrier’. <i>Bail </i>‘remove water’ [13], also spelled <i>bale</i>, probably comes ultimately from the same source; its immediate antecedent was Old French <i>baille </i>‘bucket’, which perhaps went back to a hypothetical Vulgar Latin *<i>bājula</i>, a feminine form of <i>bājulus</i>.<br><br>The <i>bail </i>on top of cricket stumps [18] has been connected with Latin <i>bājulus </i>too – this could have been the source of Old French <i>bail </i>‘cross-beam’ (‘loadcarrying beam’), which could quite plausibly have been applied to cricket bails; on the other hand it may go back to Old French <i>bail</i>, <i>baille </i>‘enclosed court’ (source of English <i>bailey </i>[13]), which originally in English meant the ‘encircling walls of a castle’ but by the 19th century at the latest had developed the sense ‘bar for separating animals in a stable’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bailey" target="_blank">bailey</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bail">bail (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"bond money," late 15c., a sense that apparently developed from that of "temporary release from jail" (into the custody of another, who gives security), recorded from early 15c. That evolved from earlier meaning "captivity, custody" (early 14c.). From Old French <span class="foreign">baillier</span> "to control, to guard, deliver" (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">bajulare</span> "to bear a burden," from <span class="foreign">bajulus</span> "porter," which is of unknown origin. In late 18c. criminal slang, <span class="foreign">to give leg bail</span> meant "to run away."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bail">bail (v.2)</a></dt><dd>"to procure someone's release from prison" (by posting bail), 1580s, from <a href="/etym/s/bail" target="_blank">bail</a> (n.1); usually with <span class="foreign">out</span>. Related: <span class="foreign">Bailed</span>; <span class="foreign">bailing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bail">bail (v.1)</a></dt><dd>"to dip water out of," 1610s, from <span class="foreign">baile</span> (n.) "small wooden bucket" (mid-14c.), from nautical Old French <span class="foreign">baille</span> "bucket, pail," from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">*bajula (aquae)</span>, literally "porter of water," from Latin <span class="foreign">bajulare</span> "to bear a burden" (see <a href="/etym/s/bail" target="_blank">bail</a> (n.1)). To <span class="foreign">bail out</span> "leave suddenly" (intransitive) is recorded from 1930, originally of airplane pilots. Related: <span class="foreign">Bailed</span>; <span class="foreign">bailing</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bail">bail (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"horizontal piece of wood in a cricket wicket," c. 1742, originally "any cross bar" (1570s), probably identical with Middle French <span class="foreign">bail</span> "horizontal piece of wood affixed on two stakes," and with English <span class="foreign">bail</span> "palisade wall, outer wall of a castle" (see <a href="/etym/s/bailey" target="_blank">bailey</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
