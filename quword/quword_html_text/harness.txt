                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/harness">harness</a> 马具，挽具，控制，利用</span><p>来自古法语harnois,武器，装备，可能来自herr,军队，词源同harry,here,拼写受名词后缀-ness影响俗化。后由于战马的重要性引申词义马具，换具，以及动词词义控制马或其它家畜，现多用于指控制和利用自然。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harness">harness</a></dt><dd><b>harness: </b><span>[13] Etymologically, <i>harness </i>is ‘equipment for an army’. It comes via Old French <i>herneis </i>‘military equipment’ from an unrecorded Old Norse *<i>hernest</i>, a compound formed from <i>herr </i>‘army’ (a descendant of prehistoric Germanic *<i>kharjaz </i>‘crowd’ and related to English <i>harangue</i>, <i>harbinger</i>, <i>harbour</i>, and <i>harry</i>) and <i>nest </i>‘provisions’.<br><br>English took it over in the general sense ‘equipment’, and did not apply it specifically to the straps, buckles, etc of a horse until the 14th century (it was originally used for any equestrian equipment, including reins, saddles, etc, but now it denotes exclusively the gear of a draught horse).</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/harangue" target="_blank">harangue</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harbinger" target="_blank">harbinger</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harbour" target="_blank">harbour</a>, <a href="/etym/s/harry" target="_blank">harry</a>, <a href="/etym/s/herald" target="_blank">herald</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harness">harness (n.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "personal fighting equipment, body armor," also "armor or trappings of a war-horse," from Old French <span class="foreign">harnois</span>, a noun of broad meaning: "arms, equipment; harness; male genitalia; tackle; household equipment" (12c.), of uncertain origin, perhaps from Old Norse <span class="foreign">*hernest</span> "provisions for an army," from <span class="foreign">herr</span> "army" (see <a href="/etym/s/harry" target="_blank">harry</a> (v.)) + <span class="foreign">nest</span> "provisions" (see <a href="/etym/s/nostalgia" target="_blank">nostalgia</a>). Non-military sense of "fittings for a beast of burden" is from early 14c. German <span class="foreign">Harnisch</span> "harness, armor" is the French word, borrowed into Middle High German. The Celtic words are believed to be also from French, as are Spanish <span class="foreign">arnes</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">arnez</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">arnese</span>. <span class="foreign">Prive harness</span> (late 14c.) was a Middle English term for "sex organs."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/harness">harness (v.)</a></dt><dd>"to put a harness on a draught animal," c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">harneschier</span> "make ready, equip, arm," from <span class="foreign">harnois</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/harness" target="_blank">harness</a> (n.)); figurative sense "to control for use as power" is from 1690s. Related: <span class="foreign">Harnessed</span>; <span class="foreign">harnessing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
