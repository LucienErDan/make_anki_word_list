                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mare">mare</a> 母马，母驴</span><p>来自古英语mere,来自mearh的阴性格，来自Proto-Germanic*markhjon,来自PIE*mark,马。部分学者认为该词来自古高卢词源，也有部分学者认为该词最终来自汉藏语系，即ma,马。</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mare">mare</a> 月海，月球表面阴暗部</span><p>缩写自lunar mare,月海，来自lunar,月亮的，mare,海，词源同marine.</p><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/mare">mare</a> 夜魔，梦淫妖</span><p>来自古英语mare,魔鬼，夜魔，梦淫妖，来自Proto-Germanic*maron,妖精，来自PIE*mer,伤害，致死，词源同mortal,murder.该词现仅见于nightmare,噩梦。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mare">mare</a></dt><dd><b>mare: </b><span>see <a href="/etym/s/marshal" target="_blank">marshal</a></span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mare">mare (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"female horse," Old English <span class="foreign">mere</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">myre</span> (West Saxon), fem. of <span class="foreign">mearh</span> "horse," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*markhjon-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">meriha</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">merr</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">merrie</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">merrie</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">meriha</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mähre</span> "mare"), said to be of Gaulish origin (compare Irish and Gaelic <span class="foreign">marc</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">march</span>, Breton <span class="foreign">marh</span> "horse"). No known cognates beyond Germanic and Celtic. As the name of a throw in wrestling, it is attested from c. 1600. <span class="foreign">Mare's nest</span> "illusory discovery, excitement over something which does not exist" is from 1610s.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mare">mare (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"broad, dark areas of the moon," 1765, from Latin <span class="foreign">mare</span> "sea" (see <a href="/etym/s/marine" target="_blank">marine</a>), applied to lunar features by Galileo and used thus in 17c. Latin works. They originally were thought to be actual seas.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/mare">mare (n.3)</a></dt><dd>"night-goblin, incubus," Old English <span class="foreign">mare</span> "incubus, nightmare, monster," from <span class="foreign">mera, mære</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*maron</span> "goblin" (cognates: Middle Low German <span class="foreign">mar</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">mare</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">mara</span>, German <span class="foreign">Mahr</span> "incubus," Old Norse <span class="foreign">mara</span> "nightmare, incubus"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*mora-</span> "incubus" (cognates: first element in Old Irish <span class="foreign">Morrigain</span> "demoness of the corpses," literally "queen of the nightmare," also Bulgarian, Serbian <span class="foreign">mora</span>, Czech <span class="foreign">mura</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">zmora</span> "incubus;" French <span class="foreign">cauchemar</span>, with first element from Old French <span class="foreign">caucher</span> "to trample"), from root <span class="foreign">*mer-</span> "to rub away, harm" (see <a href="/etym/s/morbid" target="_blank">morbid</a>).</dd></dl></div></div>
