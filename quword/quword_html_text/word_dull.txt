                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/dull">dull</a> 沉闷的</span><p>PIE*dheu, 烟尘，烟雾，词源同dust, deaf, dumb.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dull">dull</a></dt><dd><b>dull: </b><span>[13] <i>Dull </i>originally meant ‘slow-witted’. It was borrowed from Middle Low German <i>dul</i>, a descendant of the prehistoric Germanic adjective *<i>dulaz</i>, which also produced German <i>toll </i>and Old English <i>dol </i>‘stupid’ (the Old English adjective does not seem to have survived beyond the 10th century). The modern meaning ‘boring’ developed in the 15th century. The now littleused <i>dullard </i>[15] is a derivative (reflecting the adjective’s original sense), as also is probably <i>dolt </i>[16].</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/dolt" target="_blank">dolt</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dull">dull (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "stupid;" early 13c., "blunt, not sharp;" rare before mid-14c., apparently from Old English <span class="foreign">dol</span> "dull-witted, foolish," or an unrecorded parallel word, or from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">dul</span> "slow-witted," both from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*dulaz</span> (cognates: Old Frisian and Old Saxon <span class="foreign">dol</span> "foolish," Old High German <span class="foreign">tol</span>, German <span class="foreign">toll</span> "mad, wild," Gothic <span class="foreign">dwals</span> "foolish"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*dheu-</span> (1) "dust, vapor, smoke" (and related notions of "defective perception or wits"). Of color from early 15c.; of pain or other sensations from 1725. Sense of "boring" first recorded 1580s.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; dull. (8) Not exhilarating; not delightful; as <span class="foreign">to make dictionaries is</span> dull <span class="foreign">work</span>. [Johnson]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; <span class="foreign">Dullsville</span>, slang for "town where nothing happens," attested from 1960.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/dull">dull (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1200, "to grow weary, tire;" of pointed or edged things from c. 1400; of the senses from 1550s; from <a href="/etym/s/dull" target="_blank">dull</a> (adj.). Related: <span class="foreign">Dulled</span>; <span class="foreign">dulling</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
