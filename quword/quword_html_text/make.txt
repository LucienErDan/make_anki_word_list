                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/make">make</a> 做，制造</span><p>来自古英语macian,制造，形成，安排，来自PIE*mag,捏，揉，形成，词源同match,massage.最早可能是来自人类始祖捏泥土以建房，后引申多种词义。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/make">make</a></dt><dd><b>make: </b><span>[OE] <i>Make </i>probably goes back ultimately to an Indo-European base *<i>mag</i>- denoting ‘kneading’ (also the source of Greek <i>mágma </i>‘salve made by kneading’, from which English gets <i>magma </i>[15]). A prehistoric Germanic descendant was *<i>mako</i>- (source of English <i>match </i>‘go together’). From this was derived the West Germanic verb *<i>makōjan</i>, which over the centuries differentiated into German <i>machen</i>, Dutch <i>maken</i>, and English <i>make</i>. <i>Make </i>was not a particularly common verb in Old English (<i>gewyrcan</i>, ancestor of modern English <i>work</i>, was the most usual way of expressing the notion ‘make’), but in the Middle English period its use proliferated.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/magma" target="_blank">magma</a>, <a href="/etym/s/match" target="_blank">match</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/make">make (v.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">macian</span> "to make, form, construct, do; prepare, arrange, cause; behave, fare, transform," from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*makon</span> "to fashion, fit" (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">makon</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">makia</span> "to build, make," Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">maken</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">mahhon</span> "to construct, make," German <span class="foreign">machen</span> "to make"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*mag-</span> "to knead, mix; to fashion, fit" (see <a href="/etym/s/macerate" target="_blank">macerate</a>). If so, sense evolution perhaps is via prehistoric houses built of mud. Gradually replaced the main Old English word, <span class="foreign">gewyrcan</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/work" target="_blank">work</a> (v.)).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "to arrive at" (a place), first attested 1620s, originally was nautical. Formerly used in many places where specific verbs now are used, such as <span class="foreign">to make Latin</span> (c. 1500) "to write Latin compositions." This broader usage survives in some phrases, such as <span class="foreign">to make water</span> "to urinate," <span class="foreign">to make a book</span> "arrange a series of bets" (1828), <span class="foreign">make hay</span> "to turn over mown grass to expose it to sun." <span class="foreign">Make the grade</span> is 1912, perhaps from the notion of railway engines going up an incline.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Read the valuable suggestions in Dr. C.V. Mosby's book -- be prepared to surmount obstacles before you encounter them -- equipped with the power to "make the grade" in life's climb. [advertisement for "Making the Grade," December 1916]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; But the phrase also was in use in a schoolwork context at the time. <span class="foreign">Make do</span> "manage with what is available" is attested from 1867. <span class="foreign">Make time</span> "go fast" is 1849; <span class="foreign">make tracks</span> in this sense is from 1834. To <span class="foreign">make a federal case out of (something)</span> popularized in 1959 movie "Anatomy of a Murder;" to <span class="foreign">make an offer (one) can't refuse</span> is from Mario Puzo's 1969 novel "The Godfather." To <span class="foreign">make (one's) day</span> is from 1909; menacing <span class="foreign">make my day</span> is from 1971, popularized by Clint Eastwood in film "Sudden Impact" (1983). Related: <a href="/etym/s/Made" target="_blank">Made</a>; <span class="foreign">making</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/make">make (n.)</a></dt><dd>"match, mate, companion" (now archaic or dialectal), from Old English <span class="foreign">gemaca</span> "mate, equal; one of a pair, comrade; consort, husband, wife," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gamakon-</span>, related to Old English <span class="foreign">gemæcc</span> "well-matched, suitable," <span class="foreign">macian</span> "to make" (see <a href="/etym/s/make" target="_blank">make</a> (v.)). Meaning "manner in which something is made, design, construction" is from c. 1300. Phrase <span class="foreign">on the make</span> "intent on profit or advancement" is from 1869.</dd></dl></div></div>
                                    <div class="page-header"><h3><a href="/liju/w/make">权威例句</a></h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-liju">
                 <dl><dt>1. For what do we live, but to <b>make</b> sport for our neighbours, and laugh at them in our turn? </dt> <dd>我们活着是为了什么？不就是给邻居当笑柄，再反过来笑他们。</dd> <p>来自《傲慢与偏见》</p> </dl><dl><dt>2. Sometimes things have to fall apart to <b>make</b> way for better things. </dt> <dd>有时候要到达谷底，才会慢慢变好。</dd> <p>来自金山词霸 每日一句</p> </dl><dl><dt>3. Carpe diem. Seize the day, boys. <b>Make</b> your lives extraordinary. </dt> <dd>人生就应该是快乐的，要抓住每一天，孩子们，让你们的生活变得非凡起来。</dd> <p>来自电影《死亡诗社》</p> </dl><dl><dt>4. These large institutions <b>make</b> — and change—the rules to suit themselves. </dt> <dd>这些大机构总是随意制定规定，而且说变就变。</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl><dl><dt>5. Does this dress <b>make</b> my legs look too stumpy? </dt> <dd>这条裙子会使我的腿看上去又短又粗吗？</dd> <p>来自柯林斯例句</p> </dl>                 <dl><p>[ <a href="/liju/w/make">make 造句</a> ]</p></dl>
