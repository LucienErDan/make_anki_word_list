                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/up">up</a> 向上</span><p>来自PIE*upo, 在下面，从下往上，向上，词源同sub-, over.</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/up">up</a></dt><dd><b>up: </b><span>[OE] <i>Up </i>is part of a widespread family of Germanic adverbs which also includes German <i>auf</i>, Dutch and Danish <i>op</i>, and Swedish <i>upp</i>. It goes back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>up</i>-, which also produced English <i>over </i>and the prefixes <i>hyper</i>- and <i>super</i>- and may lie behind English <i>evil</i>. To <i>open </i>something is etymologically to put it ‘up’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/open" target="_blank">open</a>, <a href="/etym/s/over" target="_blank">over</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/up">up (adv.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">up, uppe</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*upp-</span> "up" (cognates: Old Frisian, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">up</span> "up, upward," Old Norse <span class="foreign">upp</span>; Danish, Dutch <span class="foreign">op</span>; Old High German <span class="foreign">uf</span>, German <span class="foreign">auf</span> "up"; Gothic <span class="foreign">iup</span> "up, upward," <span class="foreign">uf</span> "on, upon, under;" Old High German <span class="foreign">oba</span>, German <span class="foreign">ob</span> "over, above, on, upon"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*upo</span> "up from below" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">upa</span> "near, under, up to, on," Greek <span class="foreign">hypo</span> "under, below," Latin <span class="foreign">sub</span> "under;" see <a href="/etym/s/sub-" target="_blank">sub-</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; As a preposition, "to a higher place" from c. 1500; also "along, through" (1510s), "toward" (1590s). Often used elliptically for <span class="foreign">go up</span>, <span class="foreign">come up</span>, <span class="foreign">rise up</span>, etc. <span class="foreign">Up the river</span> "in jail" first recorded 1891, originally in reference to Sing Sing, which is up the Hudson from New York City. To drive someone <span class="foreign">up the wall</span> (1951) is from the notion of the behavior of lunatics or caged animals. Insulting retort <span class="foreign">up yours</span> (scil. <span class="foreign">ass</span>) attested by late 19c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/up">up (v.)</a></dt><dd>1550s, "to drive and catch (swans)," from <a href="/etym/s/up" target="_blank">up</a> (adv.). Intransitive meaning "get up, rise to one's feet" (as in <span class="foreign">up and leave</span>) is recorded from 1640s. Sense of "to move upward" is recorded from 1737. Meaning "increase" (as in <span class="foreign">up the price of oil</span>) is attested from 1915. Compare Old English verb <span class="foreign">uppian</span> "to rise up, swell." Related: <span class="foreign">Upped</span>; <span class="foreign">upping</span>. <span class="foreign">Upping block</span> is attested from 1796.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/up">up (n.)</a></dt><dd>"that which is up," 1530s, from <a href="/etym/s/up" target="_blank">up</a> (adv.). Phrase <span class="foreign">on the up-(and-up)</span> "honest, straightforward" first attested 1863, American English.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/up">up (adj.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1300, "dwelling inland or upland," from <a href="/etym/s/up" target="_blank">up</a> (adv.). Meaning "going up" is from 1784. From 1815 as "excited, exhilarated, happy," hence "enthusiastic, optimistic." <span class="foreign">Up-and-coming</span> "promising" is from 1848. Musical <span class="foreign">up-tempo</span> (adj.) is recorded from 1948.</dd></dl></div></div>
