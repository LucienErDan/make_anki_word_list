                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/bit">bit</a> 一点儿</span><p>词源同bite, 咬，指咬下的一小口。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bit">bit</a></dt><dd><b>bit: </b><span>There are three distinct nouns <i>bit </i>in English, but the two most ancient ones are probably both related ultimately to the verb <i>bite</i>. <i>Bit </i>as in ‘drill bit’ [OE] originally meant simply ‘bite’ or ‘biting’. The Old English word, <i>bite</i>, came from Germanic *<i>bitiz</i>, a derivative of the verb *<i>bītan </i>‘bite’. The ‘drill bit’ sense did not develop until the 16th century.<br><br>The <i>bit </i>placed in a horse’s mouth is probably the same word. <i>Bit </i>meaning ‘small piece’ [OE] also comes from a Germanic derivative of *<i>bītan</i>, in this case *<i>biton</i>; this gave Old English <i>bita </i>‘piece bitten off’. The more general sense, ‘small piece’, developed in the 16th century. The third <i>bit</i>, ‘unit of computer information’ [20], is a blend formed from ‘<i>bi</i>nary dig<i>it</i>’.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/bite" target="_blank">bite</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bit">bit (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"small piece," c. 1200; related Old English <span class="foreign">bite</span> "act of biting," and <span class="foreign">bita</span> "piece bitten off," probably are the source of the modern words meaning "boring-piece of a drill" (1590s), "mouthpiece of a horse's bridle" (mid-14c.), and "a piece bitten off, morsel" (c. 1000). All from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*biton</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">biti</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">bit</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">bite</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bete</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">bizzo</span> "biting," German <span class="foreign">Bissen</span> "a bite, morsel"), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bheid-</span> "to split" (see <a href="/etym/s/fissure" target="_blank">fissure</a>).&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Meaning "small piece, fragment" is from c. 1600. Sense of "short space of time" is 1650s. Theatrical <span class="foreign">bit part</span> is from 1909. Money sense in <span class="foreign">two bits</span>, etc. is originally from Southern U.S. and West Indies, in reference to silver wedges cut or stamped from Spanish dollars (later Mexican <span class="foreign">reals</span>); transferred to "eighth of a dollar."</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bit">bit (n.2)</a></dt><dd>computerese word, 1948 abbreviation coined by U.S. computer pioneer John W. Tukey (1915-2000) of <span class="foreign">binary digit</span>, probably chosen for its identity with <a href="/etym/s/bit" target="_blank">bit</a> (n.1).</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/bit">bit (v.)</a></dt><dd>past tense of <a href="/etym/s/bite" target="_blank">bite</a>.</dd></dl></div></div>
