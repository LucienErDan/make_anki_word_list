            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/fact">fact</a> 事实</span><p>来自PIE*dhe, 做，放置，语源同do, face. 即做出来的事。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fact">fact</a></dt><dd><b>fact: </b><span>[16] A <i>fact </i>is literally ‘something that is done’. It comes from Latin <i>factum </i>‘deed’, a noun based on the past participle of <i>facere </i>‘do’. This verb, a distant relative of English <i>do</i>, has contributed richly to English vocabulary, from obvious derivatives like <i>factitious </i>[17] and <i>factitive </i>[19] to more heavily disguised forms such as <i>difficult</i>, <i>effect</i>, <i>fashion</i>, <i>feasible</i>, <i>feature</i>, and <i>fetish</i>, not to mention the -<i>fic </i>suffix of words like <i>horrific </i>and <i>pacific</i>, and the related verbal suffix -<i>fy</i>.<br><br>To begin with, English adopted the word in its original Latin sense ‘deed’, but this now survives only in legal contexts, such as ‘accessory after the fact’. There is sporadic evidence in classical Latin, however, of its use for ‘something that happens, event’, and this developed in post-classical times to produce ‘what actually is’, the word’s main modern sense in French <i>fait </i>and Italian <i>fatto </i>as well as in their English relative <i>fact</i>. <i>Feat </i>is essentially the same word as <i>fact</i>, filtered through Old French.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/difficult" target="_blank">difficult</a>, <a href="/etym/s/do" target="_blank">do</a>, <a href="/etym/s/effect" target="_blank">effect</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fashion" target="_blank">fashion</a>, <a href="/etym/s/feasible" target="_blank">feasible</a>, <a href="/etym/s/feature" target="_blank">feature</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fetish" target="_blank">fetish</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/fact">fact (n.)</a></dt><dd>1530s, "action, anything done," especially "evil deed," from Latin <span class="foreign">factum</span> "an event, occurrence, deed, achievement," in Medieval Latin also "state, condition, circumstance," literally "thing done" (source also of Old French <span class="foreign">fait</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">hecho</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">fatto</span>), noun use of neuter of <span class="foreign">factus</span>, past participle of <span class="foreign">facere</span> "to do" (see <a href="/etym/s/factitious" target="_blank">factitious</a>). Main modern sense of "thing known to be true" is from 1630s, from notion of "something that has actually occurred."&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Compare <a href="/etym/s/feat" target="_blank">feat</a>, which is an earlier adoption of the same word via French. <span class="foreign">Facts</span> "real state of things (as distinguished from a statement of belief)" is from 1630s. <span class="foreign">In fact</span> "in reality" is from 1707. <span class="foreign">Facts of life</span> "harsh realities" is from 1854; euphemistic sense of "human sexual functions" first recorded 1913. Alliterative pairing of <span class="foreign">facts and figures</span> is from 1727.&#13; <blockquote>&#13; Facts and Figures are the most stubborn Evidences; they neither yield to the most persuasive Eloquence, nor bend to the most imperious Authority. [Abel Boyer, "The Political State of Great Britain," 1727]&#13; </blockquote></dd></dl></div></div>
