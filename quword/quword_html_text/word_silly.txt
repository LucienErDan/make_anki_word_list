                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/silly">silly</a> 愚蠢的，傻里傻气的</span><p>来自古英语 saelig,高兴的，愉悦的，无忧无虑的，来自 Proto-Germanic*seligaz,高兴的，来自 PIE*sel,高兴的，词源同 solace,console.后词义由高兴的，无忧无虑的引申为傻里傻气的，愚 蠢的。词义演变比较 nice,好的，原义为无知的。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/silly">silly</a></dt><dd><b>silly: </b><span>[OE] In one of the more celebrated semantic volte-faces in the history of the English lexicon, <i>silly </i>has been transformed over the past millennium from ‘blessed, happy’ to ‘stupid’. The word goes back ultimately to a prehistoric West Germanic *<i>sǣliga</i>, a derivative of *<i>sǣli </i>‘luck, happiness’. It reached Old English as <i>gesǣlig</i>, still meaning ‘happy’, but as it evolved formally in Middle English through <i>seely </i>to <i>silly</i>, its meaning developed via ‘blessed’, ‘pious’, ‘innocent, harmless’, ‘pitiable’, and ‘feeble’ to ‘feeble in mind, foolish’. The related German <i>selig </i>retains its original meaning ‘happy, blessed’.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/silly">silly (adj.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">gesælig</span> "happy, fortuitous, prosperous" (related to <span class="foreign">sæl</span> "happiness"), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sæligas</span> (cognates: Old Norse <span class="foreign">sæll</span> "happy," Old Saxon <span class="foreign">salig</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">salich</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">salig</span>, German <span class="foreign">selig</span> "blessed, happy, blissful," Gothic <span class="foreign">sels</span> "good, kindhearted"), from PIE <span class="foreign">*sele-</span> "of good mood; to favor," from root <span class="foreign">*sel-</span> (2) "happy, of good mood; to favor" (cognates: Latin <span class="foreign">solari</span> "to comfort," Greek <span class="foreign">hilaros</span> "cheerful, gay, merry, joyous").&#13; <blockquote>&#13; This is one of the few instances in which an original long <span class="foreign">e</span> (<span class="foreign">ee</span>) has become shortened to <span class="foreign">i</span>. The same change occurs in <span class="foreign">breeches</span>, and in the American pronunciation of <span class="foreign">been</span>, with no change in spelling. [Century Dictionary]&#13; </blockquote>&#13; The word's considerable sense development moved from "happy" to "blessed" to "pious," to "innocent" (c. 1200), to "harmless," to "pitiable" (late 13c.), "weak" (c. 1300), to "feeble in mind, lacking in reason, foolish" (1570s). Further tendency toward "stunned, dazed as by a blow" (1886) in <span class="foreign">knocked silly,</span> etc. <span class="foreign">Silly season</span> in journalism slang is from 1861 (August and September, when newspapers compensate for a lack of hard news by filling up with trivial stories). <span class="foreign">Silly Putty</span> trademark claims use from July 1949.</dd></dl></div></div>
