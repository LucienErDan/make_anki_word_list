                        <div class="page-header"><h3>记忆方法</h3></div>
            <div class="row" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;">1. "graft in, implant," from in- "in" (see in- (2)) + oculus "bud," originally "eye".<br />
2. => "implant a bud into a plant".<br />
3. ogle => oculus "bud," originally "eye".<br />
4. because bud have the likeness of eye.</div>

                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/inoculate">inoculate</a> 接种，打预防针</span><p>in-,进入，使，-oc,眼，词源同eye,ocular.比喻用法，由眼引申词义针眼，打针。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/inoculate">inoculate</a></dt><dd><b>inoculate: </b><span>[15] Far-fetched as the connection may seem, <i>inoculate </i>actually comes ultimately from Latin <i>oculus </i>‘eye’ (source of English <i>ocular </i>[16] and <i>oculist </i>[17]). By metaphorical extension <i>oculus </i>was applied to the ‘bud’ of a plant (much like the <i>eye </i>of a potato in English), and the verb <i>inoculāre </i>was coined to denote the grafting on of a bud or other plan part.<br><br>That was how it was used when originally adopted into English (‘Peaches have their Season at May Kalends them to inoculate’, <i>Palladius on Husbandry </i>1440), and the modern sense ‘introduce antigens into the body’ did not emerge before the early 18th century, based on the notion of ‘engrafting’ or ‘implanting’ an immunising virus into a person. It was originally used with reference to smallpox.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/eye" target="_blank">eye</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ferocious" target="_blank">ferocious</a>, <a href="/etym/s/ocular" target="_blank">ocular</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/inoculate">inoculate (v.)</a></dt><dd>mid-15c., "implant a bud into a plant," from Latin <span class="foreign">inoculatus</span>, past participle of <span class="foreign">inoculare</span> "graft in, implant," from <span class="foreign">in-</span> "in" (see <a href="/etym/s/in-" target="_blank">in-</a> (2)) + <span class="foreign">oculus</span> "bud," originally "eye" (see <a href="/etym/s/eye" target="_blank">eye</a> (n.)). Meaning "implant germs of a disease to produce immunity" first recorded (in <span class="foreign">inoculation</span>) 1714, originally in reference to smallpox. After 1799, often used in sense of "to vaccine inoculate." Related: <span class="foreign">Inoculated</span>; <span class="foreign">inoculating</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>

        if (word == '') {
            return false;
