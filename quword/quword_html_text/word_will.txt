                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>中文词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/will">will</a> 将要，意志，遗嘱</span><p>来自PIE*wel,希望，意愿，词源同volunteer,voluptuous.用于法律名词遗嘱。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/will">will</a></dt><dd><b>will: </b><span><i>Will </i>the noun [OE] and the two verbs <i>will </i>[OE] all go back ultimately to the Indo- European base *<i>wel</i>-, *<i>wol</i>- ‘be pleasing’, which also produced English <i>voluntary</i>, <i>voluptuous</i>, <i>wealth</i>, <i>well </i>‘satisfactorily’, etc. From it was derived a noun, *<i>weljon</i>, which evolved into English <i>will</i>, and also German <i>wille</i>, Dutch <i>wil</i>, Swedish <i>vilja</i>, and Danish <i>vilje</i>.<br><br>The verb <i>will </i>‘decide on or resolve by force of the will’ was formed in the prehistoric Germanic period from the noun. The auxiliary verb <i>will</i>, expressing intention or future time, comes from a prehistoric Germanic *<i>weljan</i>. <i>Would </i>evolved from its original Old English past form <i>wolde</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/voluntary" target="_blank">voluntary</a>, <a href="/etym/s/voluptuous" target="_blank">voluptuous</a>, <a href="/etym/s/wealth" target="_blank">wealth</a>, <a href="/etym/s/will" target="_blank">will</a>, <a href="/etym/s/would" target="_blank">would</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/will">will (v.1)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">*willan</span>, <span class="foreign">wyllan</span> "to wish, desire; be willing; be used to; be about to" (past tense <span class="foreign">wolde</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*willjan</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">willian</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vilja</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">willa</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">willen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">wellan</span>, German <span class="foreign">wollen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">wiljan</span> "to will, wish, desire," Gothic <span class="foreign">waljan</span> "to choose").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; The Germanic words are from PIE root <span class="foreign">*wel-</span> (2) "to wish, will" (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vrnoti</span> "chooses, prefers," <span class="foreign">varyah</span> "to be chosen, eligible, excellent," <span class="foreign">varanam</span> "choosing;" Avestan <span class="foreign">verenav-</span> "to wish, will, choose;" Greek <span class="foreign">elpis</span> "hope;" Latin <span class="foreign">volo</span>, <span class="foreign">velle</span> "to wish, will, desire;" Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">voljo</span>, <span class="foreign">voliti</span> "to will," <span class="foreign">veljo</span>, <span class="foreign">veleti</span> "to command;" Lithuanian <span class="foreign">velyti</span> "to wish, favor," <span class="foreign">pa-velmi</span> "I will," <span class="foreign">viliuos</span> "I hope;" Welsh <span class="foreign">gwell</span> "better").&#13; <br /><br />&#13; Compare also Old English <span class="foreign">wel</span> "well," literally "according to one's wish;" <span class="foreign">wela</span> "well-being, riches." The use as a future auxiliary was already developing in Old English. The implication of intention or volition distinguishes it from <a href="/etym/s/shall" target="_blank">shall</a>, which expresses or implies obligation or necessity. Contracted forms, especially after pronouns, began to appear 16c., as in <span class="foreign">sheele</span> for "she will." The form with an apostrophe is from 17c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/will">will (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">will</span>, <span class="foreign">willa</span> "mind, determination, purpose; desire, wish, request; joy, delight," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wiljon-</span> (cognates: Old Saxon <span class="foreign">willio</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vili</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">willa</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">wil</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">willio</span>, German <span class="foreign">Wille</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">wilja</span> "will"), related to <span class="foreign">*willan</span> "to wish" (see <a href="/etym/s/will" target="_blank">will</a> (v.1)). The meaning "written document expressing a person's wishes about disposition of property after death" is first recorded late 14c.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/will">will (v.2)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">willian</span> "to determine by act of choice," from <a href="/etym/s/will" target="_blank">will</a> (n.). From mid-15c. as "dispose of by will or testament." Often difficult to distinguish from <a href="/etym/s/will" target="_blank">will</a> (v.1).</dd></dl></div></div>
