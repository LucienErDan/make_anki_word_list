                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pump">pump</a></dt><dd><b>pump: </b><span>[15] The precise origins of <i>pump </i>have never been established. It is now widespread throughout the European languages, by dint of assiduous borrowing (French <i>pompe</i>, for instance), but its epicentre appears to have been northwestern Europe, with Middle Low German <i>pumpe </i>or Middle Dutch <i>pompe</i>. It started out, no doubt, as a vocal imitation of the sound of pumping.</span><br></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pump">pump (n.1)</a></dt><dd>"apparatus for forcing liquid or air," early 15c., of uncertain origin, possibly from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">pompe</span> "water conduit, pipe," or Middle Low German <span class="foreign">pumpe</span> "pump" (Modern German <span class="foreign">Pumpe</span>), both from some North Sea sailors' word, possibly of imitative origin.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pump">pump (n.2)</a></dt><dd>"low shoe without fasteners," 1550s, of unknown origin, perhaps echoic of the sound made when walking in them, or perhaps from Dutch <span class="foreign">pampoesje</span>, from Javanese <span class="foreign">pampoes</span>, of Arabic origin. Klein's sources propose a connection with <span class="foreign">pomp</span> (n.). Related: <span class="foreign">pumps</span>.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/pump">pump (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1500, from <a href="/etym/s/pump" target="_blank">pump</a> (n.1). Metaphoric extension in <span class="foreign">pump (someone) for information</span> is from 1630s. To <span class="foreign">pump iron</span> "lift weights for fitness" is from 1972. Related: <span class="foreign">Pumped</span>; <span class="foreign">pumping</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
