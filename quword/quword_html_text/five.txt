                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/five">five</a></dt><dd><b>five: </b><span>[OE] <i>Five </i>is one of a general Indo-European family of words signifying ‘five’. It goes back ultimately to Indo-European *<i>pengke</i>, which also produced Greek <i>pénte </i>(source of English <i>pentagon </i>[16], <i>pentecost </i>[OE] – literally ‘fiftieth day’ – <i>pentagram </i>[19], etc), Sanskrit <i>panca </i>(source of English <i>punch </i>‘spiced drink’), and Latin <i>quīnque</i>. In due course this under-went a phonetic transformation to *<i>pempe</i>, which was the direct ancestor of prehistoric Germanic *<i>fimfi</i>. This led on in its turn to German <i>fünf</i>, Dutch <i>vijf</i>. Swedish and Danish <i>fem</i>, and English <i>five</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/finger" target="_blank">finger</a>, <a href="/etym/s/fist" target="_blank">fist</a>, <a href="/etym/s/pentagon" target="_blank">pentagon</a>, <a href="/etym/s/punch" target="_blank">punch</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/five">five (n.)</a></dt><dd>Old English <span class="foreign">fif</span> "five," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fimfe</span> (cognates: Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fif</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fif</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vijf</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fimm</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">funf</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fimf</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*penkwe-</span> (cognates: Sanskrit <span class="foreign">panca</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">pente</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">quinque</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">peti</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">penke</span>, Old Welsh <span class="foreign">pimp</span>). The sound shift that removed the <span class="foreign">*-m-</span> is a regular development involving Old English, Old Frisian, and Old Saxon (as in <span class="foreign">thought</span>, from stem of <span class="foreign">think</span>; <span class="foreign">couth</span> from <span class="foreign">*kunthaz</span>; <span class="foreign">us</span> from <span class="foreign">*uns</span>).&#13; <br /><br /><span class="foreign">Five-and-ten</span> (Cent Store) is from 1880, American English, with reference to prices of goods for sale. <span class="foreign">Five-star</span> (adj.) is from 1913 of hotels, 1945 of generals.
