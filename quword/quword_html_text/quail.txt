            <div class="row" id="yd-ciyuan" style="font-family:SimSun,serif;"><span class="ciyuan-title"><a target="_blank" href="/ciyuan/s/quail">quail</a> 鹌鹑</span><p>拟声词，模仿这种鸟叫的声音。</p></div>
                                                <div class="page-header"><h3>英语词源</h3></div>
            <div class="row"><div class="thumbnail" id="yd-etym"><dl><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quail">quail</a></dt><dd><b>quail: </b><span><i>Quail </i>the bird [14] and <i>quail </i>‘cower’ [15] are not related. The former comes via Old French <i>quaille </i>from medieval Latin <i>coacula</i>, which probably originated in imitation of the bird’s grating cry. It is not known for certain where the verb (which originally meant ‘decline, wither, give way’) came from, although some have linked it with another verb <i>quail</i>, now obsolete, which meant ‘curdle’. This came via Old French <i>quailler </i>from Latin <i>coāgulāre</i>, source of English <i>coagulate</i>.</span><br><span class="cognate">=&gt; <a href="/etym/s/coagulate" target="_blank">coagulate</a></span></dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quail">quail (n.)</a></dt><dd>migratory game bird, late 14c. (early 14c. as a surname (<span class="foreign">Quayle</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">quaille</span> (Modern French <span class="foreign">caille</span>), perhaps via Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">quaccula</span> (source also of Provençal <span class="foreign">calha</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">quaglia</span>, Old Spanish <span class="foreign">coalla</span>), or directly from a Germanic source (compare Dutch <span class="foreign">kwakkel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">quahtala</span> "quail," German <span class="foreign">Wachtel</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">wihtel</span>), imitative of the bird's cry. Or the English word might be directly from Proto-Germanic. Slang meaning "young attractive woman" first recorded 1859.</dd><dt><a target="_blank" href="/etym/s/quail">quail (v.)</a></dt><dd>c. 1400, "have a morbid craving;" early 15c., "grow feeble or sick;" mid-15c., "to fade, fail, give way," of unknown origin, perhaps from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">quelen</span> "to suffer, be ill," from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kwaljan</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*gwele-</span> (1) "to throw, to pierce" (see <a href="/etym/s/quell" target="_blank">quell</a>). Or from obsolete <span class="foreign">quail</span> "to curdle" (late 14c.), from Old French <span class="foreign">coailler</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">coagulare</span> (see <a href="/etym/s/coagulate" target="_blank">coagulate</a>). Sense of "lose heart, shrink, cower" is attested from 1550s. According to OED, common 1520-1650, then rare until 19c., when apparently it was revived by Scott. Related: <span class="foreign">Quailed</span>; <span class="foreign">quailing</span>.</dd></dl></div></div>
