and meaning of fiend" href="/word/fiend#etymonline_v_5916"><em>fiend</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">feond</span> &quot;enemy, foe, adversary,&quot; originally present participle of <span class="foreign">feogan</span> &quot;to hate,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fijand-</span> &quot;hating, hostile&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fiand</span> &quot;enemy,&quot; Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fiond</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">viant</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vijand</span> &quot;enemy,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">fjandi</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fiant</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">fijands</span>), from suffixed form of PIE root <span class="foreign">*pe(i)-</span> &quot;to hurt&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pijati</span> &quot;reviles, scorns;&quot; Avestan <span <p>As spelling suggests, the word originally was the opposite of <span class="crossreference">friend</span> (n.). Both are from the active participles of the Germanic verbs for &quot;to love&quot; and &quot;to hate.&quot; Boutkan says the &quot;fiend&quot; word was a Germanic analogical formation from the &quot;friend&quot; word. According to Bammesberger [&quot;English Etymology&quot;], &quot;The long vowel in FIEND is regular. In FRIEND the vowel has been shortened; perhaps the shortening is due to compounds like FRIENDSHIP, in which the consonant group (<span class="foreign">-nds-</span>) regularly caused shortening of the preceeding long vowel.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Fiend</span> at first described any hostile enemy (male and female, with abstract noun form <span class="foreign">feondscipe</span> &quot;fiendship&quot;), but it began to be used in late Old English for &quot;the Devil, <span class="crossreference">Satan</span>&quot; (literally &quot;adversary&quot;) as the &quot;enemy of mankind,&quot; which shifted its sense to &quot;diabolical person&quot; (early 13c.). The old sense of the word devolved to <span class="foreign">foe</span>, then to the imported word <span class="crossreference">enemy</span>. For spelling with <span class="foreign">-ie-</span> see <span class="crossreference">field</span>. Meaning &quot;devotee (of whatever is indicated),&quot; as in <span class="foreign">dope fiend</span>, is from 1865.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fiend#etymonline_v_5916"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of arch-fiend" href="/word/arch-fiend#etymonline_v_26493">arch-<em>fiend</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1667, from <span class="crossreference">arch</span> (adj.) + <span class="crossreference">fiend</span> (n.).
