<p>The figurative <span class="foreign"><strong>brass tacks</strong></span> &quot;essentials of a matter&quot; that you get <span class="foreign">down to</span> (1897, popular from c. 1910) perhaps are the ones said to have been nailed to the counters of a dry goods stores and used to measure cloth, suggesting precision, but the metaphor was unclear from the start, and brass tacks or nails in late 19c. were commonly noted as being used in upholstering. A 1911 advertisement begins &quot; &#x27;Getting down to brass tacks&#x27; is a characteristic American slang phrase, full of suggestion but of obscure origin.&quot;</p>

<p>The figurative <span class="foreign"><strong>brass monkey</strong></span> that suffers anatomical loss in freezing weather is attested by 1843:</p>

<blockquote>Old Knites was as cool as a cucumber, and would have been so, independent of the weather, which, as he expressed it, was cold enough to freeze the nose off a brass monkey. [&quot;An Incident of the Canadian Rebellion,&quot; in The Worcester Magazine, June, 1843]</blockquote>

<p>As a noun from late 13c., &quot;one of a pair of characters, propositions, terms, etc., the most different possible within the same class.&quot; The phrase <span class="foreign">on the contrary</span> &quot;in precise or extreme opposition to what has been said&quot; is attested from c. 1400 as <span class="foreign">in the contrary</span>.</p>
<blockquote>If we take the statement <span class="foreign">All men are mortal</span>, its contrary is <span class="foreign">Not all men are mortal</span>, its converse is <span class="foreign">All mortal beings are men</span>, &amp; its opposite is <span class="foreign">No men are mortal</span>. The contrary, however, does not exclude the opposite, but includes it as its most extreme form. Thus <span class="foreign">This is white</span> has only one opposite, <span class="foreign">This is black</span>, but many contraries, as <span class="foreign">This is not white, This is coloured, This is dirty, This is black</span>; &amp; whether the last form is called the <span class="foreign">contrary</span>, or more emphatically the <span class="foreign">opposite</span>, is usually indifferent. But to apply <span class="foreign">the opposite</span> to a mere contrary (e.g. to <span class="foreign">I did not hit him</span> in relation to <span class="foreign">I hit him</span>, which has no opposite), or to the converse (e.g. to <span class="foreign">He hit me</span> in relation to <span class="foreign">I hit him</span>, to which it is neither contrary nor opposite), is a looseness that may easily result in misunderstanding; the temptation to go wrong is intelligible when it is remembered that with certain types of sentence (<span class="foreign">A exceeds B</span>) the converse &amp; the opposite are identical (<span class="foreign">B exceeds A</span>).
