<p>The sense evolution from &quot;to love&quot; to &quot;free&quot; is perhaps from the terms &quot;beloved&quot; or &quot;friend&quot; being applied to the free members of one&#x27;s clan (as opposed to slaves; compare Latin <span class="foreign">liberi</span>, meaning both &quot;free persons&quot; and &quot;children of a family&quot;). <p>Meaning &quot;clear of obstruction&quot; is from mid-13c.; sense of &quot;unrestrained in movement&quot; is from c. 1300; of animals, &quot;loose, at liberty, wild,&quot; late 14c. Sense of &quot;characterized by liberty of action or expression&quot; is from 1630s; of art, etc., &quot;not holding strictly to rule or form,&quot; from 1813. Of nations, &quot;not subject to foreign rule or to despotism,&quot; recorded in English from late 14c. (<span class="foreign"><strong>Free world</strong></span> &quot;non-communist nations&quot; attested from 1950 on notion of &quot;based on principles of civil liberty.&quot;) Sense of &quot;given without cost&quot; is 1580s, from notion of &quot;free of cost.&quot;</p>

<blockquote>In verbs the prefix denotes (a) intensive or completive action or process, or (b) action that miscarries, turns out for the worse, results in failure, or produces adverse or opposite results. In many verbs the prefix exhibits both meanings, and the verbs frequently have secondary and figurative meanings or are synonymous with the simplex. [<a href="http://quod.lib.umich.edu/m/med/">Middle English Compendium</a>]</blockquote>
<p>Probably originally in Germanic with a sense of &quot;forward, forth,&quot; but it spun out complex sense developments in the historical languages. Disused in Modern English. Ultimately from the same root as <span class="crossreference">fore</span> (adv.). From its use in participles it came to be an intensive prefix of adjectives in Middle English (for example Chaucer&#x27;s <span class="foreign">forblak</span> &quot;exceedingly black&quot;), but all these now seem to be obsolete.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/for-#etymonline_v_40739"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of for" href="/word/for#etymonline_v_11770"><em>for</em> (prep.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Old English <span class="foreign">for</span> &quot;before, in the sight of, in the presence of; as far as; during, before; on account of, for the sake of; in place of, instead of,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fur</span> &quot;before; in&quot; (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">furi</span> &quot;before,&quot; Old Frisian <span class="foreign">for</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vore</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">voor</span> &quot;for, before;&quot; German <span class="foreign">für</span> &quot;for;&quot; Danish <span class="foreign">for</span> &quot;for,&quot; <span class="foreign">før</span> &quot;before;&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">faur</span> &quot;for,&quot; <span class="foreign">faura</span> &quot;before&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*per-</span> (1) &quot;forward,&quot; hence &quot;in front of, before,&quot; etc.
