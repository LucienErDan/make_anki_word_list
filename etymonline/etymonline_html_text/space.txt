and meaning of space" href="/word/space#etymonline_v_23940"><em>space</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;extent or area; room&quot; (to do something), a shortening of Old French <span class="foreign">espace</span> &quot;period of time, distance, interval&quot; (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">spatium</span> &quot;room, area, distance, stretch of time,&quot; a word of unknown origin (also source of Spanish <span class="foreign">espacio</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">spazio</span>).</p>

<p>From early 14c. as &quot;a place,&quot; also &quot;amount or extent of time.&quot; From mid-14c. as &quot;distance, interval of space;&quot; from late 14c. as &quot;ground, land, territory; extension in three dimensions; distance between two or more points.&quot; From early 15c. as &quot;size, bulk,&quot; also &quot;an assigned position.&quot; Typographical sense is attested from 1670s (typewriter <span class="foreign"><strong>space-bar</strong></span> is from 1876, earlier <span class="foreign">space-key</span>, 1860).</p>

<p>Astronomical sense of &quot;stellar depths, immense emptiness between the worlds&quot; is by 1723, perhaps as early as &quot;Paradise Lost&quot; (1667), common from 1890s. <span class="foreign">Space age</span> is attested from 1946. Many compounds first appeared in science fiction and speculative writing, such as <span class="foreign"><strong>spaceship</strong></span> (1894, &quot;A Journey in Other Worlds,&quot; John Jacob Astor); <span class="foreign"><strong>spacecraft</strong></span> (1928, &quot;Popular Science&quot;); <span class="foreign"><strong>space travel</strong></span> (1931); <span class="foreign"><strong>space station</strong></span> (1936, &quot;Rockets Through Space&quot;); <span class="crossreference">spaceman</span> (1942, &quot;Thrilling Wonder Stories&quot;). <span class="foreign"><strong>Space race</strong></span> attested from 1959. <span class="foreign"><strong>Space shuttle</strong></span> attested by 1970.</p>
