<p>Old English <span class="foreign">dream</span> meant &quot;joy, mirth, noisy merriment,&quot; also &quot;music.&quot; Much study has failed to prove that Old English <span class="foreign">dream</span> is the source of the modern word for &quot;sleeping vision,&quot; despite being identical in form. Perhaps the meaning of the word changed dramatically, or &quot;vision&quot; was an unrecorded secondary Old English meaning of <span class="foreign">dream</span>, or there are two words here.</p>
<p>OED offers this theory for the absence of <span class="foreign">dream</span> in the modern sense in the record of Old English: &quot;It seems as if the presence of <span class="foreign">dream</span> &#x27;joy, mirth, music,&#x27; had caused <span class="foreign">dream</span> &#x27;dream&#x27; to be avoided, at least in literature, and <span class="foreign">swefn</span>, lit. &#x27;sleep,&#x27; to be substituted ....&quot;</p>
<p>The <span class="foreign">dream</span> that meant &quot;joy, mirth, music&quot; faded out of use after early Middle English. According to Middle English Compendium, the replacement of <span class="foreign">swefn</span> (Middle English <span class="foreign">swevn</span>) by <span class="foreign">dream</span> in the sense &quot;sleeping vision&quot; occurs earliest and is most frequent in the East Midlands and the North of England, where Scandinavian influence was strongest.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Dream</span> in the sense of &quot;that which is presented to the mind by the imaginative faculty, though not in sleep&quot; is from 1580s. The meaning &quot;ideal or aspiration&quot; is from 1931, from the earlier sense of &quot;something of dream-like beauty or charm&quot; (1888). The notion of &quot;ideal&quot; is behind <span class="foreign">dream girl</span> (1850), etc.</p>
<p>Before it meant &quot;sleeping vision&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">swefn</span> meant &quot;sleep,&quot; as did a great many Indo-European &quot;dream&quot; nouns originally, such as Lithuanian <span class="foreign">sapnas</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">sunu</span>, and the Romanic words (French <span class="foreign">songe</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">sueño</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">sogno</span> all from Latin <span class="foreign">somnium</span>.
