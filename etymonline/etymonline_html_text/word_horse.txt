and meaning of horse" href="/word/horse#etymonline_v_14467"><em>horse</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;solidungulate perissodactyl mammal of the family Equidæ and genus Equus&quot; [Century Dictionary], Old English <span class="foreign">hors</span> &quot;horse,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*harss-</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">hross</span>, Old Frisian, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">hors</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">ors</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">ros</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hros</span>, German <span class="foreign">Roß</span> &quot;horse&quot;), of unknown origin. By some, connected to PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kers-</span> &quot;to run,&quot; source of Latin <span class="foreign">currere</span> &quot;to run.&quot; Boutkan prefers the theory that it is a loan-word from an Iranian language (Sarmatian) also borrowed into Uralic (compare Finnish <span class="foreign">varsa</span> &quot;stallion&quot;),</p>
<p>The usual Indo-European word is represented by Old English <span class="foreign">eoh</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">hippos</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">equus</span>, from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*ekwo-</span>. Another Germanic &quot;horse&quot; word is Old English <span class="foreign">vicg</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wegja-</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">wegk-</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">wigg</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">vigg</span>), which is of uncertain origin. In many other languages, as in English, this root has been lost in favor of synonyms, probably via superstitious taboo on uttering the name of an animal so important in Indo-European religion. For the Romanic words (French <span class="foreign">cheval</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">caballo</span>) see <span class="crossreference">cavalier</span> (n.); for Dutch <span class="foreign">paard</span>, German <span class="foreign">Pferd</span>, see <span class="crossreference">palfrey</span>; for Swedish <span class="foreign">häst</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">hest</span> see <span class="crossreference">henchman</span>. As plural Old English had collective singular <span class="foreign">horse</span> as well as <span class="foreign">horses</span>, in Middle English also sometimes <span class="foreign">horsen</span>, but <span class="foreign">horses</span> has been the usual plural since 17c.</p>
<p>Used at least since late 14c. of various devices or appliances which suggest a horse (as in <span class="foreign">sawhorse</span>), typically in reference to being &quot;that upon which something is mounted.&quot; For sense of &quot;large, coarse,&quot; see <span class="crossreference">horseradish</span>. Slang use for &quot;heroin&quot; is attested by 1950. To <span class="foreign">ride a horse that was foaled of an acorn</span> (1670s) was through early 19c. a way to say &quot;be hanged from the gallows.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>Horse latitudes</strong></span> first attested 1777, the name of unknown origin, despite much speculation. A <span class="foreign"><strong>dead horse</strong></span> as a figure for something that has ceased to be useful is from 1630s; to <span class="foreign">flog a dead horse</span> &quot;attempt to revive interest in a worn-out topic&quot; is from 1864.</p>
<blockquote>HORSEGODMOTHER, a large masculine wench; one whom it is difficult to rank among the purest and gentlest portion of the community. [John Trotter Brockett, &quot;A Glossary of North Country Words,&quot; 1829]</blockquote>
<p>The term itself is attested from 1560s. <span class="foreign"><strong>The horse&#x27;s mouth</strong></span> as a source of reliable information is from 1921, perhaps originally of racetrack tips, from the fact that a horse&#x27;s age can be determined accurately by looking at its teeth. To <span class="foreign"><strong>swap horses while crossing the river</strong></span> (a bad idea) is from the American Civil War and appears to have been originally one of Abe Lincoln&#x27;s stories. <span class="foreign"><strong>Horse-and-buggy</strong></span> meaning &quot;old-fashioned&quot; is recorded from 1926 slang, originally in reference to a &quot;young lady out of date, with long hair.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>hold (one&#x27;s) horses</strong></span> &quot;restrain one&#x27;s enthusiasm, be patient&quot; is from 1842, American English; the notion is of keeping a tight grip on the reins.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/horse#etymonline_v_14467"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of horse" href="/word/horse#etymonline_v_42085"><em>horse</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late Old English <span class="foreign">horsian</span> &quot;to provide with a horse or horses,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">horse</span> (n.).
