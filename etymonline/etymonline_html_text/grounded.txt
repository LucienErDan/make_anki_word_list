But asking to be relieved from duty indicates sanity and thus he must keep flying missions.</p>
<blockquote>There was only one catch and that was Catch-22, which specified that a concern for one&#x27;s safety in the face of dangers that were real and immediate was the process of a rational mind. Orr was crazy and could be grounded. All he had to do was ask; and as soon as he did, he would no longer be crazy and would have to fly more missions. Orr would be crazy to fly more missions and sane if he didn&#x27;t, but if he was sane he had to fly them. If he flew them he was crazy and didn&#x27;t have to; but if he didn&#x27;t want to he was sane and had to. Yossarian was moved very deeply by the absolute simplicity of this clause of Catch-22 and let out a respectful whistle.<br/>
<br/>
&quot;That&#x27;s some catch, that Catch-22,&quot; he observed.<br/>
<br/>
&quot;It&#x27;s the best there is,&quot; Doc Daneeka agreed.</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Catch-22#etymonline_v_5483"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Valentine" href="/word/Valentine#etymonline_v_4608">Valentine (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-15c., &quot;sweetheart chosen on St. Valentine&#x27;s Day,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">Valentinus</span>, the name of two early Italian saints (from Latin <span class="foreign">valentia</span> &quot;strength, capacity;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">valence</span>). Choosing a sweetheart on this day originated 14c. as a custom in English and French court circles. Meaning &quot;letter or card sent to a sweetheart&quot; first recorded 1824. The romantic association of the day is said to be from it being around the time when birds choose their mates.</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">For this was on seynt Volantynys day</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Whan euery bryd cometh there to chese his make.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[Chaucer, &quot;Parlement of Foules,&quot; c. 1381]</blockquote>

<p>Probably the date was the informal first day of spring in whatever French region invented the custom (many surviving medieval calendars reckon the start of spring on the 7th or 22nd of February). No evidence connects it with the Roman <span class="crossreference">Lupercalia</span> (an 18c. theory) or to any romantic or avian quality in either of the saints. The custom of sending special cards or letters on this date flourished in England c. 1840-1870, declined around the turn of the 20th century, and revived 1920s.</p>
<blockquote>To speak of the particular Customs of the English Britons, I shall begin with Valentine&#x27;s Day, Feb. 14. when young Men and Maidens get their several Names writ down upon Scrolls of Paper rolled up, and lay &#x27;em asunder, the Men drawing the Maidens Names, and these the Mens; upon which, the Men salute their chosen Valentines and present them with Gloves, &amp;c. This Custom (which sometimes introduces a Match) is grounded upon the Instinct of Animals, which about this Time of the Year, feeling a new Heat by the approach of the Sun, begin to couple. Coined in American English to name a movement among Protestants c. 1920-25 based on scriptural inerrancy, etc., and associated with William Jennings Bryan, among others. The original notion might have been of &quot;fundamental truths.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>Fundamentalism is a protest against that rationalistic interpretation of Christianity which seeks to discredit supernaturalism. This rationalism, when full grown, scorns the miracles of the Old Testament, sets aside the virgin birth of our Lord as a thing unbelievable, laughs at the credulity of those who accept many of the New Testament miracles, reduces the resurrection of our Lord to the fact that death did not end his existence, and sweeps away the promises of his second coming as an idle dream. It matters not by what name these modernists are known. The simple fact is that, in robbing Christianity of its supernatural content, they are undermining the very foundations of our holy religion. They boast that they are strengthening the foundations and making Christianity more rational and more acceptable to thoughtful people. Christianity is rooted and grounded in supernaturalism, and when robbed of supernaturalism it ceases to be a religion and becomes an exalted system of ethics. [Curtis Lee Laws, &quot;Herald &amp; Presbyter,&quot; July 19, 1922]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Fundamentalist</span> is said (by George McCready Price) to have been first used in print by Curtis Lee Laws (1868-1946), editor of &quot;The Watchman Examiner,&quot; a Baptist newspaper. The movement may have roots in the Presbyterian General Assembly of 1910, which drew up a list of five defining qualities of &quot;true believers&quot; which other evangelicals published in a mass-circulation series of books called &quot;The Fundamentals.&quot; A World&#x27;s Christian Fundamentals Association was founded in 1918. The words reached widespread use in the wake of the contentious Northern Baptist Convention of 1922 in Indianapolis. In denominational use, <span class="foreign">fundamentalist</span> was opposed to <span class="foreign">modernist</span>. Applied to other religions since 1956 (earliest extension is to the Muslim Brotherhood).</p>
<blockquote>A new word has been coined into our vocabulary -- two new words -- &#x27;Fundamentalist&#x27; and &#x27;Fundamentalism.&#x27; They are not in the dictionaries as yet -- unless in the very latest editions. But they are on everyone&#x27;s tongue. [Address Delivered at the Opening of the Seminary, Sept. 20, 1922, by Professor Harry Lathrop Reed, &quot;Auburn Seminary Record&quot;]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true"
