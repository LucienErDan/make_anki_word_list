<p>1520s, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">dictionarium</span> &quot;collection of words and phrases,&quot; probably a shortening of <span class="foreign">dictionarius (liber)</span> &quot;(book) of words,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">dictionarius</span> &quot;of words,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dictio</span> &quot;a saying, expression,&quot; in Late Latin &quot;a word,&quot; noun of action from past-participle stem of <span class="foreign">dicere</span> &quot;speak, tell, say,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*deik-</span> &quot;to show,&quot; also &quot;pronounce solemnly.&quot;</p>
<p>The Medieval Latin word is said to have been first used by Johannes de Garlandia (John of Garland) as the title of a Latin vocabulary published c. 1220. Probably first English use in title of a book was in Sir Thomas Elyot&#x27;s &quot;Latin Dictionary&quot; (1538).</p>
<p>As an adjective, &quot;of or pertaining to a dictionary,&quot; from 1630s.  <span class="foreign">Dictionarist</span> &quot;compiler of a dictionary&quot; (1610s) is older than <span class="foreign">dictionarian</span> (1806 as a noun, 1785 as an adjective).
