title="Origin and meaning of sibling" href="/word/sibling#etymonline_v_23479"><em>sibling</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;brother or sister,&quot; 1903, modern revival (in anthropology) of Old English <span class="foreign">sibling</span> &quot;relative, kinsman,&quot; from <span class="foreign">sibb</span> &quot;kinship, relationship; love, friendship, peace, happiness,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sibja-</span> &quot;blood relation, relative,&quot; properly &quot;one&#x27;s own&quot; (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">sibba</span>, Old Frisian, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sibbe</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">sippa</span>, German <span class="foreign">Sippe</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">sibja</span> &quot;kin, kindred&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*s(w)e-bh(o)-</span> (source also of Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">sobistvo</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">sob</span> &quot;character, individuality&quot;), an enlargement of the root <span class="foreign">*swe-</span> &quot;self&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">idiom</span>). Related to the second element in <span class="crossreference">gossip</span>.</p>
<blockquote>The word &#x27;sib&#x27; or &#x27;sibling&#x27; is coming into use in genetics in the English-speaking world, as an equivalent of the convenient German term &#x27;Geschwister&#x27; [E.&amp;C. Paul, &quot;Human Heredity,&quot; 1930]</blockquote>
<p>In Old English, <span class="foreign">sibb</span> and its compounds covered grounds of &quot;brotherly love, familial affection&quot; which tended later to lump into <span class="crossreference">love</span> (n.), as in <span class="foreign">sibsumnes</span> &quot;peace, concord, brotherly love,&quot; <blockquote><span class="foreign">Frank</span>, literally, <span class="foreign">free</span>; the freedom may be in regard to one&#x27;s own opinions, which is the same as <span class="foreign">openness</span>, or in regard to things belonging to others, where the freedom may go so far as to be unpleasant, or it may disregard conventional ideas as to reticence. Hence, while <span class="foreign">openness</span> is consistent with timidity, <span class="foreign">frankness</span> implies some degree of boldness. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>A generalization of the tribal name; the connection is that Franks, as the conquering class, alone had the status of freemen in a world that knew only free, captive, or slave. For sense connection of &quot;being one of the nation&quot; and &quot;free,&quot; compare Latin <span class="foreign">liber</span> &quot;free,&quot; from the same root as German <span class="foreign">Leute</span> &quot;nation, people&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">liberal</span> (adj.)) and Slavic &quot;free&quot; words (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">svobodi</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">swobodny</span>, Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">slobodan</span>) which are cognates of the first element in English <span class="crossreference">sibling</span> &quot;brother, sister&quot; (in Old English used more generally: &quot;relative, kinsman&quot;).
