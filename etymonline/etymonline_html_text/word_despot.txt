form <span class="foreign">dispotto</span> (1580s as <span class="foreign">despot)</span>; from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">despota</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">despotes</span> &quot;master of a household, lord, absolute ruler,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*dems-pota-</span> &quot;house-master,&quot; from the genitive of the root <span <p>Originally in English in reference to Byzantine rulers or Christian rulers in Ottoman provinces and often neutral. It became fully negative with the French Revolution, where it was applied to Louis XVI. In English the sense of &quot;one who governs according to his own will, under a recognized right but uncontrolled by constitutional restrictions or the wishes of his subjects&quot; is by 1610s; by c. 1800 it was used generally for &quot;a tyrant, an oppressor.&quot;</p>
