<br/><br/>
The name was also changed by Anglo-French loss or metathesis of liquids in words containing <span class="foreign">-l-</span>, <span class="foreign">-n-</span>, or <span class="foreign">-r-</span> (also evident in the derivatives of Old French <span class="foreign">Berengier</span> &quot;bear-spear&quot; -- Old High German <span class="foreign">Beringar</span> -- name of one of the paladins in the Charlemagne romances and a common given name in England 12c. and 13c., which has come down in surnames as <span class="foreign">Berringer, Bellanger, Benger</span>, etc.). Thus <span class="foreign">Sarop-</span> became <span class="foreign">Salop-</span> and in the 12c. and 13c. the overwhelming spelling in government records was <span class="foreign">Salopesberie</span>, which accounts for the abbreviation <span class="foreign">Salop</span> for the modern county.
<br/><br/>
During all this, the Anglo-Saxon inhabitants (as opposed to the French scribes) still pronounced it properly, and regular sound evolutions probably produced a pronunciation something like <span class="foreign">Shrobesbury</span> (which turns up on a 1327 patent roll). After a predictable <span class="foreign">-b-</span> to <span class="foreign">-v-</span> (a vowel in the Middle Ages) to <span class="foreign">-u-</span> shift, the modern spelling begins to emerge 14c. and is fully established 15c.
