class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-12c., &quot;a wondrous work of God,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">miracle</span> (11c.) &quot;miracle, story of a miracle, miracle <p>From mid-13c. as &quot;something that excites wonder or astonishment, extraordinary or remarkable feat,&quot; without regard to divinity or supernatural power. It replaced Old English <span class="foreign">wundortacen</span>, <span class="foreign">wundorweorc</span>. The Greek words rendered as <span class="foreign">miracle</span> in the English bibles were <span class="foreign">semeion</span> &quot;sign,&quot; <span class="foreign">teras</span> &quot;wonder,&quot; and <span class="foreign">dynamis</span> &quot;power,&quot; which in the Vulgate were translated respectively as <span class="foreign">signum</span>, <span class="foreign">prodigium</span>, and <span class="foreign">virtus</span>.</p>
