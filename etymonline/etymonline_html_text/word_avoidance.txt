<br/><br/>
The neuter genitive pronoun in Middle English was <span class="foreign">his</span>, but the clash between grammatical gender and sexual gender, or else the application of the word to both human and non-human subjects, evidently made users uncomfortable. Restriction of <span class="foreign">his</span> to the masculine and avoidance of it as a neuter pronoun is evidenced in Middle English, and <span class="foreign">of it</span> and <span class="foreign">thereof</span> (as in KJV) were used for the neuter possessive. Originally &quot;young pig&quot; (the word for adults was <span class="foreign">swine</span>). Apparently related to Low German <span class="foreign">bigge</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">big</span> (&quot;but the phonology is difficult&quot; -- OED). The meaning &quot;oblong piece of metal&quot; is first attested 1580s, on the notion of &quot;large mass.&quot; Applied to persons, usually in contempt, since 1540s; the derogatory slang meaning &quot;police officer&quot; has been in underworld slang at least since 1811.</p>
<blockquote>The pigs frisked my panney, and nailed my screws; the officers searched my house, and seized my picklock keys. [&quot;Dictionary of Buckish Slang, University Wit and Pickpocket Eloquence,&quot; London, 1811]</blockquote>
<p>Another Old English word for &quot;pig&quot; was <span class="foreign">fearh</span>, related to <span class="foreign">furh</span> &quot;furrow,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*perk-</span> &quot;dig, furrow&quot; (source also of Latin <span class="foreign">porc-us</span> &quot;pig,&quot; see <span class="crossreference">pork</span>). &quot;This reflects a widespread IE tendency to name animals from typical attributes or activities&quot; [Lass]. Synonyms <span class="foreign">grunter</span>, <span class="foreign">porker</span> are from sailors&#x27; and fishermen&#x27;s euphemistic avoidance of uttering the word <span class="foreign">pig</span> at sea, a superstition perhaps based on the fate of the Gadarene swine, who drowned. The image of a <span class="foreign">pig in a poke</span> is attested from 1520s (see <span class="crossreference">poke</span> (n.1)). Flying pigs as a type of something unreal is from 1610s.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/pig#etymonline_v_14967"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of belly" href="/word/belly#etymonline_v_8289">belly (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>a general Germanic word for &quot;leather bag, pouch, pod&quot; that in English has evolved to mean a part of the body; from Old English <span class="foreign">belg</span>, <span class="foreign">bylig</span> (West Saxon), <span class="foreign">bælg</span> (Anglian) &quot;leather bag, purse, pouch, pod, husk, bellows,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*balgiz</span> &quot;bag&quot; (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">belgr</span> &quot;bag, bellows,&quot; <span class="foreign">bylgja</span> &quot;billow,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">balgs</span> &quot;wine-skin&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhelgh-</span> &quot;to swell,&quot; extended form of root <span class="crossreference">*bhel-</span> (2) &quot;to blow, swell.&quot;</p>
<p>By c. 1200 it was being used for &quot;the stomach,&quot; especially as a symbol of gluttony, and by late 14c. to mean &quot;abdomen of a human or animal, front part of the body between the breast and the groin or the diaphragm and the pelvis.&quot;</p>
<p>The Old English word for &quot;belly, stomach&quot; was <span class="foreign">buc</span> (cognate with German <span class="foreign">Bauch</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">buik</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">buk</span>, from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*būkaz</span>, a word indicative of swelling, with no known connections). The plural of Old English <span class="foreign">belg</span> emerged in Middle English as a separate word, <span class="crossreference">bellows</span>. Meaning &quot;bulging part or convex surface of anything&quot; is 1590s. The West Germanic root had a figurative or extended sense of &quot;anger, arrogance&quot; (as in Old English <span class="foreign">bolgenmod</span> &quot;enraged;&quot; <span class="foreign">belgan</span> (v.) &quot;to become angry&quot;), probably from the notion of &quot;swelling.&quot;</p>
<p>Indo-European languages commonly use the same word for both the external belly and the internal (stomach, womb, etc.), but the distinction of external and internal is somewhat present in English <span class="foreign">belly/stomach</span>; Greek <span class="foreign">gastr-</span> (see <span class="crossreference">gastric</span>) in classical language denoted the paunch or belly, while modern science uses it only in reference to the stomach as an organ.</p>
<p>As a personal name from 12c. <span class="foreign">Belly-naked</span> in Middle English was &quot;stripped to the belly, completely naked.&quot; Fastidious avoidance of <span class="foreign">belly</span> in speech and writing (compensated for by stretching the senses of imported <span class="crossreference">stomach</span> and <span class="crossreference">abdomen</span>, baby-talk <span class="crossreference">tummy</span> and misappropriated <span class="crossreference">midriff</span>) began late 18c. and the word was banished from Bibles in many early 19c. editions.</p>
