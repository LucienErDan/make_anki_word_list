<p>Century Dictionary and OED compare Dutch <span class="foreign">dutten</span> &quot;take a nap; mope;&quot; Icelandic <span class="foreign">dotta</span> &quot;to nod, sleep;&quot; Middle High German <span class="foreign">totzen</span> &quot;take a nap.&quot; Wedgwood writes, &quot;The radical sense seems to be to nod the head, thence to become sleepy, to doze, to become confused in the understanding.&quot;</p>
<p>From late 15c. as &quot;be infatuated, bestow excessive love.&quot; Also in Middle English &quot;to decay, deteriorate,&quot; in reference to rotten timber, etc. (mid-15c.). There was a noun <span class="foreign">dote</span> &quot;fool, simpleton, senile man&quot; (mid-12c.), but Middle English Compendium considers this to be from the verb. Related: <span class="foreign">Doted</span>; <span class="foreign">dotes</span>; <span class="foreign">doting</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/dote#etymonline_v_13968"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of crank" href="/word/crank#etymonline_v_298">crank (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;bent or vertical handle for turning a revolving axis,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">*cranc</span>, implied in <span class="foreign">crancstæf</span> &quot;a weaver&#x27;s instrument,&quot; <span class="foreign">crencestre</span> &quot;female weaver, spinster,&quot; which is related to <span class="foreign">crincan</span> &quot;to bend, yield,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*krank-</span> &quot;bend, curl up&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cringe</span>).</p>
<p>English retains the literal sense of the ancient word (&quot;something bent or crooked&quot;), while in other Germanic languages it tends to have only a figurative sense (German and Dutch <span class="foreign">krank</span> &quot;sick,&quot; formerly &quot;weak, small&quot;). The Continental definition entered into English <span class="foreign">crank</span> via slang <span class="foreign">counterfeit crank</span> &quot;one who shams sickness to get charity&quot; (1560s). OED notes that &quot;the 16th c. vagabonds&#x27; cant contains words taken directly from Continental languages.&quot; It apparently lingered in the north (the 1825 supplement to Jamieson&#x27;s Scottish dictionary has <span class="foreign">crank</span> &quot;infirm, weak, etc.&quot;) and might have influenced the development of the English word.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;twist or turn of speech, grotesquery in words&quot; is from 1590s; that of &quot;absurd or unreasonable act&quot; (perhaps caused by &quot;twisted judgment&quot;) is from 1848. The sense of &quot;eccentric person,&quot; especially one who is irrationally fixated, is first recorded 1833; this sometimes is said to be from the crank of a barrel organ, which makes it play the same tune over and over; but more likely it is a back-formation from <span class="crossreference">cranky</span> (q.v.) and thus from the notion of one having a mental &quot;twist.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The person who adopts &quot;any presentiment, any extravagance as most in nature,&quot; is not commonly called a Transcendentalist, but is known colloquially as a &quot;crank.&quot; [Oliver W. Holmes, &quot;Ralph Waldo Emerson&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>There also was a <span class="foreign">crank</span> (adj.) in Middle English meaning &quot;lively, brisk, merry,&quot; but it is of uncertain origin and connection. <span class="foreign">Cranky</span> for &quot;merry, lively&quot; lingered into 19c. in northern England dialects and American English. Meaning &quot;methamphetamine&quot; attested by 1989, from the verb.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/crank#etymonline_v_298"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fetish" href="/word/fetish#etymonline_v_5884">fetish (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;material object regarded with awe as having mysterious powers or being the representative of a deity that may be worshipped through it,&quot; 1610s, <span class="foreign">fatisso</span>, from Portuguese <span class="foreign">feitiço</span> &quot;charm, sorcery, allurement,&quot; noun use of an adjective meaning &quot;artificial.&quot;</p>
<p>The Portuguese adjective is from Latin <span class="foreign">facticius</span> &quot;made by art, artificial,&quot; from <span class="foreign">facere</span> &quot;to make, do, produce&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*dhe-</span> &quot;to set, put;&quot; compare French <span class="foreign">factice</span> &quot;artificial,&quot; restored from Old French <span class="foreign">faitise</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">facticius</span>). Via the French word, Middle English had <span class="foreign">fetis</span>, <span class="foreign">fetice</span> (adj.) &quot;cleverly made, neat, elegant&quot; (of things), &quot;handsome, pretty, neat&quot; (of persons). But in the Middle Ages the Romanic derivatives of the word took on magical senses; compare Portuguese <span class="foreign">feiticeria</span> &quot;sorcery, witchcraft,&quot; <span class="foreign">feiticeiro</span> &quot;sorcerer, wizard.&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">facticius</span> in Spanish has become <span class="foreign">hechizo</span> &quot;artificial, imitated,&quot; also &quot;bewitchment, fascination.&quot;</p>
<p>The specific Portuguese use of the word that brought it to English probably began among Portuguese sailors and traders who used the word as a name for charms and talismans worshipped by the inhabitants of the Guinea coast of Africa. It was picked up and popularized in anthropology by Charles de Brosses&#x27; <span class="foreign">&quot;Du culte des dieux fétiches&quot;</span> (1760), which influenced the word&#x27;s spelling in English (French <span class="foreign">fétiche</span> also is borrowed 18c. from the Portuguese word).</p>
<blockquote>Any material image of a religious idea is an idol; a material object in which force is supposed to be concentrated is a Fetish; a material object, or a class of material objects, plants, or animals, which is regarded by man with superstitious respect, and between whom and man there is supposed to exist an invisible but effective force, is a Totem. [J. Fitzgerald Lee, &quot;The Greater Exodus,&quot; London, 1903]</blockquote>
<p>Figurative sense of &quot;something irrationally revered, object of blind devotion&quot; appears to be an extension made by the New England Transcendentalists (1837). For sexual sense (1897), see <span class="crossreference">fetishism</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fetish#etymonline_v_5884"><span>Relate
