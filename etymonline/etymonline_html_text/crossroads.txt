<blockquote>The Celtic and Gm. forms are often reconstructed as <span class="foreign">*kr(e)u-k-</span>, but we find vacillating vocalism within Gm.; also, the meanings &#x27;backbone&#x27; and &#x27;heap&#x27; are not necessarily connected. Even if the words in <span class="foreign">*kruk-</span> from Latin and Italo-Celtic belong together, the root structure does not look PIE (and a root enlargement <span class="foreign">k</span> is unknown), and might be interpreted as a non-IE substratum word borrowed into Italo-Celtic. <p>The figurative use for &quot;a central difficulty&quot; (1718) is older in English than the literal sense; perhaps it is from Latin <span class="foreign">crux interpretum</span> &quot;a point in a text that is impossible to interpret,&quot; the literal meaning of which is something like &quot;crossroads of interpreters.&quot; But Century Dictionary ascribes it to &quot;the cross as an instrument of torture; hence anything that puzzles or vexes in a high degree ....&quot; Extended sense of &quot;central point&quot; is attested by 1888.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/crux#etymonline_v_29117"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of trivia" href="/word/trivia#etymonline_v_17849">trivia (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;trivialities, bits of information of little consequence,&quot; by 1932, from the title of a popular book by U.S.-born British aphorist Logan Pearsall Smith (1865-1946) first published in 1902 but popularized in 1918 (with &quot;More Trivia&quot; following in 1921 and a collected edition including both in 1933), containing short essays often tied to observation of small things and commonplace moments. <span class="foreign">Trivia</span> is Latin, plural of <span class="foreign">trivium</span> &quot;place where three roads meet;&quot; in transferred use, &quot;an open place, a public place.&quot; The adjectival form of this, <span class="foreign">trivialis</span>, meant &quot;public,&quot; hence &quot;common, commonplace&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">trivial</span>).</p>
<p>The Romans also had <span class="foreign">trivius dea</span>, the &quot;goddess of three ways,&quot; another name for Hecate, perhaps originally in her triple aspect (Selene/Diana/Proserpine), but also as the especial divinity of crossroads (Virgil has &quot;Nocturnisque hecate triviis ululata per urbes&quot;). John Gay took this arbitrarily as the name of a goddess of streets and roads for his mock Georgic &quot;Trivia: Or, the Art of Walking the Streets of London&quot; (1716); Smith writes in his autobiography that he got the title from Gay.</p>
<blockquote>I KNOW too much; I have stuffed too many of the facts of History and Science into my intellectuals. My eyes have grown dim over books; believing in geological periods, cave dwellers, Chinese Dynasties, and the fixed stars has prematurely aged me. [&quot;Trivia,&quot; 1918 edition]</blockquote>
<p>Then noted c. 1965 as an informal fad game among college students wherein one asked questions about useless bits of information from popular culture (&quot;What was Donald Duck&#x27;s address?&quot;) and others vied to answer first.</p>
<blockquote>Nobody really wins in this game which concentrates on sports, comics and television. Everyone knows that Amos&#x27;s wife on the &quot;Amos &#x27;n&#x27; Andy Show&quot; is Ruby, but who knows that she is from Marietta, Georgia? Trivia players do. They also know the fourth man in the infield of Tinker-to-Evers-to-Chance, the Canadian who shot down Baron Von Richtofen, and can name ten Hardy Boy books. [&quot;Princeton Alumni Weekly,&quot; Nov. 9, 1965]</blockquote>
<p>The board game <span class="foreign">Trivial Pursuit</span> was released 1982 and was a craze in U.S. for several years thereafter.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/trivia#etymonline_v_17849"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Hobbit" href="/word/Hobbit#etymonline_v_12058">Hobbit (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1937, coined in the fantasy tales of J.R.R. lanthorns, silkies, cauld-lads, death-hearses, goblins, hob-headlesses, bugaboos, kows, or cowes, nickies, nacks necks, waiths, miffies, buckies, ghouls, sylphs, guests, swarths, freiths, freits, gy-carlins Gyre-carling, pigmies, chittifaces, nixies, Jinny-burnt-tails, dudmen, hell-hounds, dopple-gangers, boggleboes, bogies, redmen, portunes, grants, <strong>hobbits</strong>, hobgoblins, brown-men, cowies, dunnies, wirrikows, alholdes, mannikins, follets, korreds, lubberkins, cluricauns, kobolds, leprechauns, kors, mares, korreds, puckles korigans, sylvans, succubuses, blackmen, shadows, banshees, lian-hanshees, clabbernappers, Gabriel-hounds, mawkins, doubles, corpse lights or candles, scrats, mahounds, trows, gnomes, sprites, fates, fiends, sibyls, nicknevins, whitewomen, fairies, thrummy-caps, cutties, and nisses, and apparitions of every shape, make, form, fashion, kind and description, that there was not a village in England that had not its own peculiar ghost.
