<blockquote>In part the loanword from French is used mainly with reference to fashionable dancing while the older native word persists in use with reference to folk-dancing, as definitively Russ. <span class="foreign">pljasat&#x27;</span> vs. <span class="foreign">tancovat&#x27;</span> [Carl Darling Buck, &quot;A Dictionary of Selected Synonyms in the Principal Indo-European Languages,&quot; 1949].</blockquote>
<p>In English it replaced Old English <span class="foreign">sealtian</span>, itself a borrowing from Latin <span class="foreign">saltare</span> &quot;to dance,&quot; frequentative of <span class="foreign">salire</span> &quot;to leap&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">salient</span> (adj.); &quot;dance&quot; words frequently are derived from words meaning &quot;jump, leap&quot;). Related: <span class="foreign">Danced</span>; <span class="foreign">dancing</span>.</p>

[Table Talk, September 1889]</blockquote>

<blockquote>The number of women [in the audience] was ludicrously disproportionate, and the number of American women was noticeable. Some of them seemed slightly pensive, but all were interested.
