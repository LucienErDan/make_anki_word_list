title="Origin and meaning of front" href="/word/front#etymonline_v_14206"><em>front</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">late 13c., &quot;forehead,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">front</span> &quot;forehead, brow&quot; (12c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">frontem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">frons</span>) &quot;forehead, brow, front; countenance, expression (especially as an indicator of truthfulness or shame); facade of a building, forepart; external appearance; vanguard, front rank,&quot; a word of &quot;no plausible etymology&quot; (de Vaan). Perhaps literally &quot;that which projects,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhront-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*bhren-</span> &quot;to project, stand out&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">brink</span>). Or from PIE <span class="foreign">*ser-</span> (4), &quot;base of prepositions and preverbs with the basic meaning &#x27;above, over, up, upper&#x27;&quot; [Watkins, not in Pokorny].
<br/><br/>
Sense &quot;foremost part of anything&quot; emerged in the English word mid-14c.; sense of &quot;the face as expressive of temper or character&quot; is from late 14c. (hence <span class="foreign">frontless</span> &quot;shameless,&quot; c. 1600). The military sense of &quot;foremost part of an army&quot; (mid-14c.) led to the meaning &quot;field of operations in contact with the enemy&quot; (1660s); <span class="foreign">home front</span> is from 1919. Meaning &quot;organized body of political forces&quot; is from 1926.


Sense of &quot;public facade&quot; is from 1891; that of &quot;something serving as a cover for illegal activities&quot; is from 1905. Adverbial phrase <span class="foreign">in front</span> is from 1610s. Meteorological sense first recorded 1921.
