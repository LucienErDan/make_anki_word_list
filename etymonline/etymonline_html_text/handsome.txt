title="Origin and meaning of handsome" href="/word/handsome#etymonline_v_1436"><em>handsome</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1400, <span class="foreign">handsom</span> &quot;easy to handle, ready at hand,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">hand</span> (n.) + <span class="crossreference">-some</span> (1). Sense extended to &quot;fit, appropriate&quot; (1550s, implied in <span class="foreign">handsomely</span>), then &quot;having fine form, good-looking, agreeable to the eye&quot; (1580s). Meaning &quot;generous, on a liberal scale&quot; (of rewards, etc.) first recorded 1680s.</p>
<blockquote>[Americans] use the word &quot;handsome&quot; much more extensively than we do: saying that Webster made a handsome speech in the Senate: that a lady talks handsomely, (eloquently:) that a book sells handsomely. A gentleman asked me on the Catskill Mountain, whether I thought the sun handsomer there than at New York. [Harriet Martineau, &quot;Society in America,&quot; 1837]</blockquote>
<p>Related: <span class="foreign">Handsomeness</span>. For sense development, compare <span class="crossreference">pretty</span> (adj.). Similar formation in Dutch <span class="foreign">handzaam</span> &quot;tractable, serviceable.&quot;</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Handsome</span> is founded upon the notion of proportion, symmetry, as the result of cultivation or work; a <span class="foreign">handsome</span> figure is strictly one that has been developed by attention to physical laws into the right proportions. It is less spiritual than <span class="foreign">beautiful</span>; a <span class="foreign">handsome</span> face is not necessarily a <span class="foreign">beautiful</span> face.
