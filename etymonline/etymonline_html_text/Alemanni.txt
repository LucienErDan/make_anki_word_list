title="Origin and meaning of Alemanni" href="/word/Alemanni#etymonline_v_8128"><em>Alemanni</em> </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">name of a Germanic tribe or confederation from the Elbe River region that in late Roman times settled along the upper Rhine in Alsace and part of Switzerland, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*Alamanniz</span>, probably meaning &quot;all-man&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">all</span> + <span class="crossreference">man</span> (n.)) and likely denoting a coalition or alliance of tribes rather than a single group.
<br/><br/>
But on another theory perhaps meaning rather &quot;foreign men&quot; (compare <span class="foreign">Allobroges</span>, name of a Celtic tribe in what is now Savoy, in Latin literally &quot;the aliens,&quot; in reference to their having driven out the original inhabitants), in which case the <span class="foreign">al-</span> is cognate with the first element in Latin <span class="foreign">alius</span> &quot;the other&quot; and English <span class="crossreference">else</span>.
<br/><br/>
The defeat of the Alemanni by a Frank-led army at Strasburg in 496 C.E. led to the conversion of Clovis and the rise of Frankish political power. The Alemanni were absorbed into the Frankish Kingdom in 796. Not historically important, but through proximity and frequent conflict with the Franks their name became the source of French <span class="foreign">Allemand</span>, the usual word for &quot;German, a German,&quot; and <span class="foreign">Allemagne</span> &quot;Germany.&quot; In modern use, <span class="foreign">Alemannish</span>, <span class="foreign">Alemannic</span> refers to the dialects of modern southwestern Germany; <span class="foreign">Alamannic</span> refers to the ancient tribes and their language.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Alemanni#etymonline_v_8128"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of allemande" href="/word/allemande#etymonline_v_8172">allemande (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">name for a German dance in 3/4 time, 1775, from French <span class="foreign">Allemande</span>, fem. of <span class="foreign">allemand</span> &quot;German&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">Alemanni</span>). Perhaps originally the name of an individual tribe, but Gaulish (Celtic) origins have been proposed, from words perhaps originally meaning &quot;noisy&quot; (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">garim</span> &quot;to shout&quot;) or &quot;neighbor&quot; (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">gair</span> &quot;neighbor&quot;). <blockquote>Þe empere passede from þe Grees to þe Frenschemen and to þe Germans, þat beeþ Almayns. [John of Trevisa, translation of Higdon&#x27;s <span class="foreign">&quot;Polychronicon,&quot;</span> 1387]</blockquote>
<p>Their name for themselves, <span class="foreign">die Deutschen</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Dutch</span>), dates from 12c.
