<p>It might be ultimately from PIE <span class="foreign">*ner-</span> (1) &quot;left,&quot; also &quot;below&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">narakah</span> &quot;hell,&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">neretos</span> &quot;deeper, lower down,&quot; <span class="foreign">enerthen</span> &quot;from beneath,&quot; Oscan-Umbrian <span class="foreign">nertrak</span> &quot;left&quot;), as north is to the left when one faces the rising sun. <p>The usual word for &quot;north&quot; in the Romance languages ultimately is from English, for example Old French <span class="foreign">north</span> (Modern French <span class="foreign">nord</span>), borrowed from Old English <span class="foreign">norð</span>; Italian <span class="foreign">nord</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">norte</span> are borrowed from French.</p>
