title="Origin and meaning of soft" href="/word/soft#etymonline_v_23832"><em>soft</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Old English <span class="foreign">softe</span>, earlier <span class="foreign">sefte</span>, &quot;gentle, mild-natured; easeful, comfortable, calm, undisturbed; luxurious,&quot; from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*samfti</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*samftijaz</span> &quot;level, even, smooth, gentle, soft&quot; (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">safti</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">semfti</span>, German <span class="foreign">sanft</span>; and from a variant form with <span class="foreign">-ch-</span> for <span class="foreign">-f-</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">sachte</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zacht</span>, German <span class="foreign">sacht</span>), from root <span class="foreign">*som-</span> &quot;fitting, agreeable.&quot;
<br/><br/>
From c. 1200 of material things, &quot;not stiff, not coarse, fine; yielding to weight.&quot; From late 14c. of wind, rain, etc. Of sounds, &quot;quiet, not loud,&quot; from early 13c. Of words, &quot;mild, restrained; courteous&quot; mid-14c. From late 14c. as &quot;indulgent,&quot; also &quot;physically feeble; easily overcome, lacking manly courage.&quot; From 1755 of water (&quot;relatively free from mineral salts&quot;), from 1789 of coal. Meaning &quot;foolish, simple, silly&quot; is attested from 1620s; earlier &quot;easily moved or swayed; soft-hearted, sympathetic; docile&quot; (early 13c.). In reference to drinks, &quot;non-alcoholic&quot; from 1880. As an adverb, Old English <span class="foreign">softe</span> &quot;gently;&quot; late 13c. as &quot;quietly.&quot; As an interjection from 1540s.
