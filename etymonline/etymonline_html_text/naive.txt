<p>The word&#x27;s considerable sense development moved from &quot;happy&quot; to &quot;blessed&quot; to &quot;pious,&quot; to &quot;innocent&quot; (c. 1200), to &quot;harmless,&quot; to &quot;pitiable&quot; (late 13c.), &quot;weak&quot; (c. 1300), to &quot;feeble in mind, lacking in reason, foolish&quot; (1570s). Further tendency toward &quot;stunned, dazed as by a blow&quot; (1886) in <span class="foreign">knocked silly,</span> etc. <span class="foreign">Silly season</span> in journalism slang is from 1861 (August and September, when newspapers compensate for a lack of hard news by filling up with trivial stories). <span class="foreign">Silly Putty</span> trademark claims use from July 1949.</p>
<blockquote>It is a widespread phenomenon that the words for &#x27;innocent&#x27;, apart from their legal use, develop, through &#x27;harmless, guileless&#x27;, a disparaging sense &#x27;credulous, naive, simple, foolish.&#x27; [Buck]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/silly#etymonline_v_23518"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of jabroni" href="/word/jabroni#etymonline_v_35179">jabroni (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 2000, professional wrestling slang for one whose main purpose is to make the better-known wrestlers of the organization look good; he or she does this by losing to them. More commonly known as a <span class="foreign">jobber</span>, in a specialized sense of that word (though some enthusiasts claim there is a difference), and perhaps a mock-Italianized form of that word (but compare <span class="foreign">jaboney</span> &quot;naive person; immigrant; hoodlum,&quot; a word of unknown origin, in American English use c. 1990).</p>
<blockquote><strong>Jobber</strong> -- A performer who regularly loses on television and doesn&#x27;t receive much if any push. A comparable term for such a performer is <span class="foreign">jabroni</span>, which is a favorite catch-phrase of Dwayne &quot;The Rock&quot; Johnson. To soften the blow of such labels, some wrestling promotions refer to jobbers as <span class="foreign">enhancement talent</span>. <span class="foreign">Carpenter</span> was the phrase used by earlier generations.
