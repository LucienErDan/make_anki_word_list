title="Origin and meaning of deplorable" href="/word/deplorable#etymonline_v_29595"><em>deplorable</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1610s, &quot;that may or must be deplored, lamentable, grievous, miserable;&quot; from 1640s as &quot;pitiable, wretched, contemptible,&quot; 1610s, from <span class="crossreference">-able</span> + <span class="crossreference">deplore</span> (v.) &quot;lament, bewail, give up as hopeless,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">déplorer</span> (13c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">deplorare</span> &quot;bewail, lament, give up for lost,&quot; from <span class="foreign">de-</span> &quot;entirely&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">de-</span>) + <span class="foreign">plorare</span> &quot;weep, cry out,&quot; which is of unknown origin.</p>
<p>Perhaps from or inspired by French <span class="foreign">déplorable</span> or directly from Late Latin <span class="foreign">deplorabilis</span>. &quot;It is sometimes, in a more lax and jocular sense, used for contemptible; despicable: as <span class="foreign">deplorable</span> nonsense; <span class="foreign">deplorable</span> stupidity&quot; [Johnson, 1755]. Related: <span class="foreign">Deplorably</span>; <span class="foreign">deplorableness</span>; <span class="foreign">deplorability</span>.</p>
<p>As a noun it is attested from 1830 as &quot;deplorable ills.&quot; <span class="foreign">Deplorables</span> was used politically in reference to the ministry of Charles X of France in the 1820s (<span class="foreign">le ministère déplorable</span>).
