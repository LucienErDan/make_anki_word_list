a sense now obsolete, shortened from <span class="foreign">imene</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">gemæne</span> &quot;common, public, general, universal, shared by all,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ga-mainiz</span> &quot;possessed jointly&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mene</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gimeni</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">gemeine</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gemene</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">gemeen</span>, German <span class="foreign">gemein</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">gamains</span> &quot;common&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ko-moin-i-</span> &quot;held in common,&quot; <p>Meaning &quot;of common or low origin, inferior in rank or status&quot; (of persons) is attested from early 14c. Sense of &quot;ordinary, inferior in attainment or skill&quot; is from late 14c. Also from late 14c. as &quot;poor in quality, of little value,&quot; though this sense survived longer in American than in England. James Stirling, in &quot;Letters from the Slave States&quot; [London, 1857], mentioning <span class="foreign">mean whites</span> (poor whites in the South who do manual labor and are looked down on by the slaves) notes, &quot; &#x27;Mean&#x27; is an Americanism for &#x27;poor,&#x27; &#x27;shabby.&#x27; They speak here of a &#x27;mean&#x27; hotel, a &#x27;mean&#x27; dinner, &amp;c.&quot;</p>

<p>The pejorative sense of &quot;without dignity of mind, destitute of honor, low-minded&quot; is from 1660s; the specific sense of &quot;stingy, niggardly&quot; is recorded by 1755; the weaker sense of &quot;disobliging, pettily offensive&quot; is from 1839, originally American English slang. All these developments of the English word were furthered by its coincidence in form with <span class="crossreference">mean</span> (adj.2) &quot;middle, middling,&quot; which also was used in disparaging senses, and OED notes that some usages of <span class="foreign">mean</span> it cites &quot;might be referred almost equally to the native and to the foreign adj.; the truth is probably that they are of mixed ancestry.&quot;</p>

<p>The inverted sense of &quot;remarkably good&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">plays a mean Rhythm Master</span>) first recorded c. 1900, perhaps from phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>no mean _______</strong></span> &quot;not inferior&quot; (1590s, also, &quot;not average,&quot; reflecting further confusion with <span class="foreign"><strong>mean</strong></span> (adj.2.)).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mean#etymonline_v_12495"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of mean" href="/word/mean#etymonline_v_12496"><em>mean</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;that point, place, or state which is halfway between extremes;&quot; c. 1300, originally in music, &quot;a tone intermediate between two other tones,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">meien</span> &quot;middle, means, intermediary,&quot; noun use of adjective from Late Latin <span class="foreign">medianus</span> &quot;of or that is in the middle,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">medius</span> &quot;in the middle,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*medhyo-</span> &quot;middle.&quot;</p>
