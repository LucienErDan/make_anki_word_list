<blockquote>The toga as the Roman national dress was allowed to be worn by free citizens only. A stranger not in full possession of the rights of a Roman citizen could not venture to appear in it. Even banished Romans were in imperial times precluded from wearing it. The appearance in public in a foreign dress was considered as contempt of the majesty of the Roman people. Even boys appeared in the toga, called, owing to the purple edge attached to it (a custom adopted from the Etruscans) <span class="foreign">toga praetexta</span>. On completing his sixteenth, afterward his fifteenth, year (<span class="foreign">tirocinium fori</span>), the boy exchanged the toga praetexta for the <span class="foreign">toga virilis</span>, <span class="foreign">pura</span>, or <span class="foreign">libera</span>--a white cloak without the purple edge. <p>Breeches, like the word for them (Latin <span class="foreign">bracae</span>) were alien to the Romans, being the dress of Persians, Germans, and Gauls, so that <span class="foreign">bracatus</span> &quot;wearing breeches&quot; was a term in Roman geography meaning &quot;north of the Alps.&quot; College fraternity <span class="foreign">toga party</span> was re-popularized by movie &quot;Animal House&quot; (1978), but this is set in 1962 and the custom seems to date from at least the mid-1950s.</p>

<p>The Romans also had <span class="foreign">trivius dea</span>, the &quot;goddess of three ways,&quot; another name for Hecate, perhaps originally in her triple aspect (Selene/Diana/Proserpine), but also as the especial divinity of crossroads (Virgil has &quot;Nocturnisque hecate triviis ululata per urbes&quot;). John Gay took this arbitrarily as the name of a goddess of streets and roads for his mock Georgic &quot;Trivia: Or, the Art of Walking the Streets of London&quot; (1716); Smith writes in his autobiography that he got the title from Gay.</p>
<blockquote>I KNOW too much; I have stuffed too many of the facts of History and Science into my intellectuals. My eyes have grown dim over books; believing in geological periods, cave dwellers, Chinese Dynasties, and the fixed stars has prematurely aged me. [&quot;Trivia,&quot; 1918 edition]</blockquote>
<p>Then noted c. 1965 as an informal fad game among college students wherein one asked questions about useless bits of information from popular culture (&quot;What was Donald Duck&#x27;s address?&quot;) and others vied to answer first.</p>
<blockquote>Nobody really wins in this game which concentrates on sports, comics and television. Everyone knows that Amos&#x27;s wife on the &quot;Amos &#x27;n&#x27; Andy Show&quot; is Ruby, but who knows that she is from Marietta, Georgia? Trivia players do. They also know the fourth man in the infield of Tinker-to-Evers-to-Chance, the Canadian who shot down Baron Von Richtofen, and can name ten Hardy Boy books. <p>Abstract sense of &quot;time as an indefinite continuous duration&quot; is recorded from late 14c. <blockquote><span class="foreign">to have a good time</span> ( = a time of enjoyment) was common in Eng. from c 1520 to c 1688; it was app. retained in America, whence readopted in Britain in 19th c. [OED]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Time of day</span> (now mainly preserved in negation, i.e. what someone won&#x27;t give you if he doesn&#x27;t like you) was a popular 17c. salutation (as in &quot;Good time of day vnto your Royall Grace,&quot; &quot;Richard III,&quot; I.iii.18), hence <span class="foreign">to give (one) the time of day</span> &quot;greet socially&quot; (1590s); earlier was <span class="foreign">give good day</span> (mid-14c.). <span class="foreign">The times</span> &quot;the current age&quot; is from 1590s. <span class="foreign">Behind the times</span> &quot;old-fashioned&quot; is recorded from 1831. <span class="foreign">Times</span> as the name of a newspaper dates from 1788.</p>

<blockquote>Jones [archaeologist of A.D. 5139] potters about for a while in the region which we have come to regard as New York, finds countless ruins, but little of interest to the historian except a calcified direction sheet to something called a &quot;Time Capsule.&quot; Jones finds the capsule but cannot open it, and decides, after considerable prying at the lid, that it is merely evidence of an archaic tribal ceremony called a &quot;publicity gag&quot; of which he has already found many examples.
