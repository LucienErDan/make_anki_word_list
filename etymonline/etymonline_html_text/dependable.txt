Pronunciation development is that of <span class="crossreference">sugar</span> (n.).
<br/><br/>
As an affirmative meaning &quot;yes, certainly&quot; it dates from 1803, from Middle English meanings &quot;firmly established; having no doubt,&quot; and phrases like <span class="foreign">to be sure</span> (1650s), <span class="foreign">sure enough</span> (1540s), and <span class="foreign">for sure</span> (1580s). The use as an adverb meaning &quot;assuredly&quot; goes back to early 14c. <span class="foreign">Sure-footed</span> is from 1630s, literal and figurative; <span class="foreign">sure thing</span> dates from 1836. In 16c.-17c., <span class="foreign">Suresby</span> was an appellation for a person to be depended upon (see <span class="crossreference">rudesby</span>).</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/sure#etymonline_v_22407"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of maverick" href="/word/maverick#etymonline_v_12455">maverick (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1867, &quot;calf or yearling found without an owner&#x27;s brand,&quot; a word from the great cattle ranches of the American West, so called for Samuel A. <span class="foreign">Maverick</span> (1803-1870), Texas cattle owner who was notoriously negligent in branding his calves.</p>
<blockquote>All neat stock found running at large in this State, without a mother, and upon which there is neither mark nor brand, shall be deemed a maverick, and shall be sold to the highest bidder for cash, at such time and place, and under such rules and regulations, as the round-up commissioners of the district shall prescribe. [act to amend the General Statutes of the State of Colorado, approved April 8, 1885]</blockquote>
<p>The family name is an old one in Boston, and a different Samuel Maverick was killed in the Boston Massacre. The sense of &quot;individualist, unconventional person&quot; is said to be attested by 1886, via the notion of &quot;masterless,&quot; but its modern popularity  seems to date to the late 1930s and the career of Maury <span class="foreign">Maverick</span> (1895-1954) of Texas, grandson of Samuel the rancher and a Democratic congressman 1935-1939 famous for his liberal independent streak, who also coined <span class="crossreference">gobbledygook</span>.</p>
<p>&quot;The Crisis&quot; (April 1939) wrote that &quot;During his stormy career in Washington Maverick became known as the one dependable liberal among the southerners. He recognized the broad problems of our nation, refusing to allow his vision to be limited by sectional prejudices, or racial or economic bugaboos. He was the only southern congressman to vote for the Gavagan federal anti-lynching bill. Not only did he vote for it, but he made a speech on the floor of the House in support of it.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/maverick#etymonline_v_12455"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of -able" href="/word/-able#etymonline_v_49">-able </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>common termination and word-forming element of English adjectives (typically based on verbs) and generally adding a notion of &quot;capable of; allowed; worthy of; requiring; to be ______ed,&quot; sometimes &quot;full of, causing,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">-able</span> and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">-abilis</span>. It is properly <span class="foreign">-ble</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">-bilis</span> (the vowel being generally from the stem ending of the verb being suffixed), and it represents PIE <span class="foreign">*-tro-</span>, a suffix used to form nouns of instrument, cognate with the second syllables of English <span class="crossreference">rudder</span> and <span class="crossreference">saddle</span> (n.).</p>
<p>A living element in English, used in new formations from either Latin or native words (<span class="foreign">readable</span>, <span class="foreign">bearable</span>) and also with nouns (<span class="foreign">objectionable</span>, <span class="foreign">peaceable</span>). Sometimes with an active signification (<span class="foreign">suitable</span>, <span class="foreign">capable</span>), sometimes of neutral signification (<span class="foreign">durable</span>, <span class="foreign">conformable</span>). It has become very elastic in meaning, as in a <span class="foreign">reliable witness</span>, a <span class="foreign">playable foul ball</span>, <span class="foreign">perishable goods</span>. A 17c. writer has <span class="foreign">cadaverable</span> &quot;mortal.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>To take a single example in detail, no-one but a competent philologist can tell whether <span class="foreign">reasonable</span> comes from the verb or the noun <span class="foreign">reason</span>, nor whether its original sense was that can be reasoned out, or that can reason, or that can be reasoned with, or that has reason, or that listens to reason, or that is consistent with reason; the ordinary man knows only that it can now mean any of these, &amp; justifiably bases on these &amp; similar facts a generous view of the termination&#x27;s capabilities; <span class="foreign">credible</span> meaning for him worthy of credence, why should not <span class="foreign">reliable</span> &amp; <span class="foreign">dependable</span> mean worthy of reliance &amp; dependence? [Fowler]</blockquote>
<p>In Latin, <span class="foreign">-abilis</span> and <span class="foreign">-ibilis</span> depended on the inflectional vowel of the verb. Hence the variant form <span class="foreign">-ible</span> in Old French, Spanish, English. In English, <span class="foreign">-able</span> tends to be used with native (and other non-Latin) words, <span class="foreign">-ible</span> with words of obvious Latin origin (but there are exceptions). The Latin suffix is not etymologically connected with <span class="crossreference">able</span>, but it long has been popularly associated with it, and this probably has contributed to its vigor as a living suffix.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/-able#etymonline_v_49"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right"
