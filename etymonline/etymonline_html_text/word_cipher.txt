title="Origin and meaning of cipher" href="/word/cipher#etymonline_v_13714"><em>cipher</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;arithmetical symbol for zero,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">cifre</span> &quot;nought, zero,&quot; Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">cifra</span>, which, with Spanish and Italian <span class="foreign">cifra</span>, ultimately is from Arabic <span class="foreign">sifr</span> &quot;zero,&quot; literally &quot;empty, nothing,&quot; from <span class="foreign">safara</span> &quot;to be empty;&quot; a loan-translation of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">sunya-s</span> &quot;empty.&quot; Klein says Modern French <span class="foreign">chiffre</span> is from Italian <span class="foreign">cifra</span>. </p>
<p>The word came to Europe with Arabic numerals. From &quot;zero,&quot; it came to mean &quot;any numeral&quot; (early 15c.), then (first in French and Italian) &quot;secret way of writing; coded message&quot; (a sense first attested in English 1520s), because early codes often substituted numbers for letters. Meaning &quot;the key to a cipher or secret writing&quot; is by 1885, short for <span class="foreign">cipher key</span> (by 1835).</p>
<p>Figurative sense of &quot;something or someone of no value, consequence, or power&quot; is from 1570s.</p>

De Vaan traces this through Proto-Italic <span class="foreign">*kaud-ek-</span> to PIE <span class="foreign">*kehu-d- &quot;</span>cleaved, separate,&quot; which he also sees as the root of <span class="foreign">cauda</span> &quot;tail&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">coda</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;cipher, system of signals and the rules which govern their use&quot; (the sense in <span class="foreign">secret code</span>) is from 1808. <span class="foreign">Code-name</span> is from 1879 (in telegraphy). Meaning &quot;system of expressing information and instructions in a form usable by a computer&quot; is from 1946.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/code#etymonline_v_15772"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of slang" href="/word/slang#etymonline_v_23642">slang (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1756, &quot;special vocabulary of tramps or thieves,&quot; later &quot;jargon of a particular profession&quot; (1801). The sense of &quot;very informal language characterized by vividness and novelty&quot; is by 1818.</p>
<p><a href="https://blog.oup.com/2016/09/slang-word-origin/">Anatoly Liberman</a> writes here an extensive account of the established origin of the word from the Northern England noun <span class="foreign">slang</span> &quot;a narrow piece of land running up between other and larger divisions of ground&quot; and the verb <span class="foreign">slanger</span> &quot;linger, go slowly,&quot; which is of Scandinavian origin (compare Norwegian <span class="foreign">slenge</span> &quot;hang loose, sling, sway, dangle,&quot; Danish <span class="foreign">slænge</span> &quot;to throw, sling&quot;). &quot;Their common denominator seems to be &#x27;to move freely in any direction&#x27; &quot; [Liberman]. Noun derivatives of these (Danish <span class="foreign">slænget</span>, Norwegian <span class="foreign">slenget</span>) mean &quot;a gang, a band,&quot; and Liberman compares Old Norse <span class="foreign">slangi</span> &quot;tramp&quot; and <span class="foreign">slangr</span> &quot;going astray&quot; (used of sheep). He writes:</p>
<blockquote>It is not uncommon to associate the place designated for a certain group and those who live there with that group’s language. John Fielding and the early writers who knew the noun <span class="foreign">slang</span> used the phrase <span class="foreign">slang patter</span>, as though that patter were a kind of talk belonging to some territory.</blockquote>
<p>So the sense evolution would be from slang &quot;a piece of delimited territory&quot; to &quot;the territory used by tramps for their wandering,&quot; to &quot;their camping ground,&quot; and finally to &quot;the language used there.&quot; The sense shift then passes through itinerant merchants:</p>
<blockquote>Hawkers use a special vocabulary and a special intonation when advertising their wares (think of modern auctioneers), and many disparaging, derisive names characterize their speech; <span class="foreign">charlatan</span> and <span class="foreign">quack</span> are among them.</blockquote>
<p>Liberman concludes: </p>
<blockquote>[Slang] is a dialectal word that reached London from the north and for a long time retained the traces of its low origin. The route was from &quot;territory; turf&quot; to &quot;those who advertise and sell their wares on such a territory,&quot; to &quot;the patter used in advertising the wares,&quot; and to &quot;vulgar language&quot; (later to “any colorful, informal way of expression”).</blockquote>
<p>-----</p>
<blockquote>[S]lang is a conscious offence against some conventional standard of propriety. A mere vulgarism is not slang, except when it is purposely adopted, and acquires an artificial currency, among some class of persons to whom it is not native. The other distinctive feature of slang is that it is neither part of the ordinary language, nor an attempt to supply its deficiencies. The slang word is a deliberate substitute for a word of the vernacular, just as the characters of a cipher are substitutes for the letters of the alphabet, or as a nickname is a substitute for a personal name. [Henry Bradley, from &quot;Slang,&quot; in &quot;Encyclopedia Britannica,&quot; 11th ed.]</blockquote>
<p>A word that ought to have survived is <span class="foreign">slangwhanger</span> (1807, American English) &quot;noisy or abusive talker or writer.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/slang#etymonline_v_23642"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fuck" href="/word/fuck#etymonline_v_14228">fuck (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;to have sexual intercourse with&quot; (transitive), until recently a difficult word to trace in usage, in part because it was omitted as taboo by the editors of the original OED when the &quot;F&quot; entries were compiled (1893-97). Johnson also had excluded the word, and <span class="foreign">fuck</span> wasn&#x27;t in a single English language dictionary from 1795 to 1965. &quot;The Penguin Dictionary&quot; broke the taboo in the latter year. Houghton Mifflin followed, in 1969, with &quot;The American Heritage Dictionary,&quot; but it also published a &quot;Clean Green&quot; edition without the word, to assure itself access to the public high school market.</p>
<p>Written form attested from at least early 16c.; OED 2nd edition cites 1503, in the form <span class="foreign">fukkit</span>, and the earliest attested appearance of current spelling is 1535 (&quot;Bischops ... may fuck thair fill and be vnmaryit&quot; [Sir David Lyndesay, <span class="foreign">&quot;Ane Satyre of the Thrie Estaits&quot;</span>]). Presumably it is a more ancient word, but one not written in the kind of texts that have survived from Old English and Middle English [September 2015: the verb appears to have been found recently in an English court manuscript from 1310]. Buck cites proper name <span class="foreign">John le Fucker</span> from 1278, but that surname could have other explanations. The word apparently is hinted at in a scurrilous 15c. poem, titled <span class="foreign">&quot;Flen flyys,&quot;</span> written in bastard Latin and Middle English. The relevant line reads:</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">Non sunt in celi</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">quia fuccant uuiuys of heli</blockquote>
<p>&quot;They [the monks] are not in heaven because they fuck the wives of [the town of] Ely.&quot; <span class="foreign">Fuccant</span> is pseudo-Latin, and in the original it is written in cipher. The earliest examples of the word otherwise are from Scottish, which suggests a Scandinavian origin, perhaps from a word akin to Norwegian dialectal <span class="foreign">fukka</span> &quot;copulate,&quot; or Swedish dialectal <span class="foreign">focka</span> &quot;copulate, strike, push,&quot; and <span class="foreign">fock</span> &quot;penis.&quot;</p>
<p>Another theory traces the Modern English verb to Middle English <span class="foreign">fyke</span>, <span class="foreign">fike</span> &quot;move restlessly, fidget&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">fike</span>) which also meant &quot;dally, flirt,&quot; and probably is from a general North Sea Germanic word (compare Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">fokken</span>, German <span class="foreign">ficken</span> &quot;fuck,&quot; earlier &quot;make quick movements to and fro, flick,&quot; still earlier &quot;itch, scratch;&quot; the vulgar sense attested from 16c.). This would parallel in sense the vulgar Middle English term for &quot;have sexual intercourse,&quot; <span class="foreign">swive</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">swifan</span> &quot;to move lightly over, sweep&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">swivel</span>). But OED remarks that these &quot;cannot be shown to be related&quot; to the English word. Liberman has this to say:</p>
<blockquote>Germanic words of similar form (f + vowel + consonant) and meaning &#x27;copulate&#x27; are numerous. One of them is G. <span class="foreign">ficken</span>. They often have additional senses, especially &#x27;cheat,&#x27; but their basic meaning is &#x27;move back and forth.&#x27; ... Most probably, <span class="foreign">fuck</span> is a borrowing from Low German and has no cognates outside Germanic.</blockquote>
<p>Chronology and phonology rule out Shipley&#x27;s attempt to derive it from Middle English <span class="foreign">firk</span> &quot;to press hard, beat.&quot; The unkillable urban legend that this word is an acronym of some sort (a fiction traceable on the internet to 1995 but probably predating that), and the &quot;pluck yew&quot; fable, are results of <a href="http://www.etymonline.com/baloney.php"><strong>ingenious trifling</strong></a> (also see <a href="http://www.snopes.com/language/acronyms/fuck.htm"><strong>here</strong></a>). The Old English verb for &quot;have sexual intercourse with&quot; was <span class="foreign">hæman</span>, from <span class="foreign">ham</span> &quot;dwelling, home,&quot; with a sense of &quot;take home, co-habit.&quot; French <span class="foreign">foutre</span> and Italian <span class="foreign">fottere</span> seem to resemble the English word but are unrelated, descending rather from Latin <span class="foreign">futuere</span>, which perhaps is from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bhau-</span> &quot;to strike,&quot; extended via a figurative use &quot;from the sexual application of violent action&quot; [Shipley; compare the sexual slang use of <span class="foreign">bang</span>, etc.].</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Fuck</span> was outlawed in print in England (by the Obscene Publications Act, 1857) and the U.S. (by the Comstock Act, 1873). The word continued in common speech, however. During World War I:</p>
<blockquote>It became so common that an effective way for the soldier to express this emotion was to omit this word. Thus if a sergeant said, &#x27;Get your ----ing rifles!&#x27; it was understood as a matter of routine. But if he said &#x27;Get your rifles!&#x27; there was an immediate implication of urgency and danger. [John Brophy, &quot;Songs and Slang of the British Soldier: 1914-1918,&quot; pub. 1930]</blockquote>
<p>The legal barriers against use in print broke down in mid-20c. with the &quot;Ulysses&quot; decision (U.S., 1933) and &quot;Lady Chatterley&#x27;s Lover&quot; (U.S., 1959; U.K., 1960). The major breakthrough in publication was James Jones&#x27; &quot;From Here to Eternity&quot; (1950), with 50 <span class="foreign">fucks</span> (down from 258 in the original manuscript).</p>
<p>The abbreviation <span class="foreign">F</span> (or <span class="foreign">eff</span>) probably began as euphemistic, but by 1943 it was regarded as a cuss word in its own right. In 1948, the publishers of &quot;The Naked and the Dead&quot; persuaded Norman Mailer to use the euphemism <span class="foreign">fug</span>. When Mailer later was introduced to Dorothy Parker, she greeted him with, &quot;So you&#x27;re the man who can&#x27;t spell &#x27;fuck&#x27; &quot; [The quip sometimes is attributed to Tallulah Bankhead]. As a written euphemism, <span class="foreign">muck</span> is attested by 1929 (Hemingway used it in &quot;For Whom the Bell Tolls,&quot; 1940). Related: <span class="foreign">Fucked</span>; <span class="foreign">fucking</span>.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Fuck-all</strong></span> &quot;nothing&quot; first recorded 1960. Verbal phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>fuck up</strong></span> &quot;to ruin, spoil, destroy&quot; is attested from c. 1916. A widespread group of Slavic words (such as Polish <span class="foreign">pierdolić</span>) can mean both &quot;fornicate&quot; and &quot;make a mistake.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>Fuck off</strong></span> is attested from 1929; as a command to depart, by 1944. Egyptian legal agreements from the 23rd Dynasty (749-21 B.C.E.) frequently include the phrase, &quot;If you do not obey this decree, may a donkey copulate with you!&quot; [Reinhold Aman, &quot;Maledicta,&quot; Summer 1977].</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fuck#etymonline_v_14228"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa
