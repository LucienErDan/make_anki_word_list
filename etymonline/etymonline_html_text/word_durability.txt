A mystery word, with no known cognates beyond English. Perhaps akin to French <span class="foreign">brasser</span> &quot;to brew,&quot; because it is an alloy. It also has been compared to Old Swedish <span class="foreign">brasa</span> &quot;fire,&quot; but no sure connection can be made. Yet another theory connects it with Latin <span class="foreign">ferrum</span> &quot;iron,&quot; itself of obscure origin.</p>
<p>Words for &quot;brass&quot; in other languages (such as German <span class="foreign">Messing</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">mæsling</span>, French <span class="foreign">laiton</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">ottone</span>) also tend to be difficult to explain. As brass was unknown in early antiquity (it was well-known to Strabo, 1c., but not mentioned by Homer), the use of the English word in Bible translations, etc., likely means &quot;bronze.&quot; The Romans were the first to deliberately make it.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">When works of Greek and Roman antiquity in &#x27;brass&#x27; began to be critically examined, and their material discriminated, the Italian word for &#x27;brass&#x27; (<span class="foreign">bronzo</span>, <span class="foreign">bronze</span>) came into use to distinguish this &#x27;ancient brass&#x27; from the current allot.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[OED]</blockquote>
