and meaning of pork" href="/word/pork#etymonline_v_18479"><em>pork</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300 (early 13c. in surname <span class="foreign">Porkuiller</span>), &quot;flesh of a pig as food,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">porc</span> &quot;pig, swine, boar,&quot; and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">porcus</span> &quot;pig, tame swine,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*porko-</span> &quot;young pig.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Pork barrel</span> in the literal sense is from 1801, American English; meaning &quot;state&#x27;s financial resources (available for distribution)&quot; is attested from 1907 (in full, <span class="foreign">national pork barrel</span>); it was noted as an expression of U.S. President President William Howard Taft:</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Now there is a proposition that we issue $500,000,000 or $1,000,000,000 of bonds for a waterway, and then that we just apportion part to the Mississippi and part to the Atlantic, a part to the Missouri and a part to the Ohio. I am opposed to it. I am opposed to it because it not only smells of the pork barrel, but it will be the pork barrel itself. Let every project stand on its bottom.&quot; [&quot;The Outlook,&quot; Nov. 6, 1909, quoting Taft]</blockquote>
<p>The magazine article that includes the quote opens with:</p>
<blockquote>We doubt whether any one knows how or when, or from what application of what story, the phrase &quot;the National pork barrel&quot; has come into use. If not a very elegant simile, it is at least an expressive one, and suggests a graphic picture of Congressmen eager for local advantage going, one after another, to the National pork barrel to take away their slices for home consumption.</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Pork</span> in this sense is attested from 1862 (compare figurative use of <span class="crossreference">bacon</span>). <span class="foreign">Pork chop</span> is attested from 1858. <span class="foreign">Pork pie</span> is from 1732; <span class="foreign">pork-pie hat</span> (1855) originally described a woman&#x27;s style popular c. 1855-65, so called for its shape.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/pork#etymonline_v_18479"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of porker" href="/word/porker#etymonline_v_46650">porker (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">1650s, &quot;young hog fattened for food,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">pork</span> (n.).
