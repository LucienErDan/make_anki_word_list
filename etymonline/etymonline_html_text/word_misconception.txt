<blockquote>A word here as to the misconception labored under by our English neighbor; he evidently does not understand the American manner of doing things. <p>Modern use is mostly from computer gaming (2001). To <span class="foreign">level off</span> &quot;cease rising or falling&quot; is from 1920, originally in aviation. Related: <span class="foreign">Levelled</span>; <span class="foreign">levelling</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/level#etymonline_v_43196"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of -ment" href="/word/-ment#etymonline_v_32036">-ment </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>common suffix of Latin origin forming nouns, originally from French and representing Latin <span class="foreign">-mentum</span>, which was added to verb stems to make nouns indicating the result or product of the action of the verb or the means or instrument of the action. In Vulgar Latin and Old French it came to be used as a formative in nouns of action. French inserts an <span class="foreign">-e-</span> between the verbal root and the suffix (as in <span class="foreign">commenc-e-ment</span> from <span class="foreign">commenc-er</span>; with verbs in <span class="foreign">ir</span>, <span class="foreign">-i-</span> is inserted instead (as in <span class="foreign">sent-i-ment</span> from <span class="foreign">sentir</span>).</p>

<p>Used with English verb stems from 16c. (for example <span class="crossreference">amazement</span>, <span class="crossreference">betterment</span>, <span class="crossreference">merriment</span>, the last of which also illustrates the habit of turning <span class="foreign">-y</span> to <span class="foreign">-i-</span> before this suffix).</p>

<blockquote>The stems to which <span class="foreign">-ment</span> is normally appended are those of verbs; freaks like <span class="foreign">oddment</span> &amp; <span class="foreign">funniment</span> should not be made a precedent of; they are themselves due to misconception of <span class="foreign">merriment</span>, which is not from the adjective, but from an obsolete verb <span class="foreign">merry</span> to rejoice. [Fowler]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/-ment#etymonline_v_32036"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of turkey" href="/word/turkey#etymonline_v_18833">turkey (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1540s, originally &quot;guinea fowl&quot; (<span class="foreign">Numida meleagris</span>), a bird imported from Madagascar via Turkey, and called <span class="foreign">guinea fowl</span> when brought by Portuguese traders from West Africa. The larger North American bird (<span class="foreign">Meleagris gallopavo</span>) was domesticated by the Aztecs, introduced to Spain by conquistadors (1523) and thence to wider Europe. The word <span class="foreign">turkey</span> first was applied to it in English 1550s because it was identified with or treated as a species of the guinea fowl, and/or because it got to the rest of Europe from Spain by way of North Africa, then under Ottoman (Turkish) rule. Indian corn was originally <span class="foreign">turkey corn</span> or <span class="foreign">turkey wheat</span> in English for the same reason.</p>
<p>The Turkish name for it is <span class="foreign">hindi</span>, literally &quot;Indian,&quot; probably influenced by Middle French <span class="foreign">dinde</span> (c. 1600, contracted from <span class="foreign">poulet d&#x27;inde</span>, literally &quot;chicken from India,&quot; Modern French <span class="foreign">dindon</span>), based on the then-common misconception that the New World was eastern Asia.</p>
<blockquote>After the two birds were distinguished and the names differentiated, <span class="foreign">turkey</span> was erroneously retained for the American bird, instead of the African. From the same imperfect knowledge and confusion <span class="foreign">Melagris</span>, the ancient name of the African fowl, was unfortunately adopted by Linnæus as the generic name of the American bird. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>The New World bird itself reputedly reached England by 1524 at the earliest estimate, though a date in the 1530s seems more likely. The wild turkey, the North American form of the bird, was so called from 1610s. By 1575, turkey was becoming the usual main course at an English Christmas. Meaning &quot;inferior show, failure,&quot; is 1927 in show business slang, probably from the bird&#x27;s reputation for stupidity. Meaning &quot;stupid, ineffectual person&quot; is recorded from 1951. <span class="foreign">Turkey shoot</span> &quot;something easy&quot; is World War II-era, in reference to marksmanship contests where turkeys were tied behind a log with their heads showing as targets. To <span class="foreign">talk turkey</span> (1824) supposedly comes from an old tale of a Yankee attempting to swindle an Indian in dividing up a turkey and a buzzard as food.</p></section></object><a
