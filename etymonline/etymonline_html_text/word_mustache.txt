Sense extended 1680s to other than religious opinions.
<br/><br/>
Earliest French use of the word is as the name of a people apparently in southern Gaul, which led to the theory, now considered doubtful on phonetic grounds, that the word comes from <span class="foreign">Visigothus</span>. The typical use in Old French seems to have been as a derogatory nickname for Normans, leading to another theory (not universally accepted) that traces it to the Normans&#x27; (alleged) frequent use of the Germanic oath <span class="foreign">bi God</span>. OED dismisses in a three-exclamation-mark fury one fanciful version of the &quot;by god&quot; theory as &quot;absurdly incongruous with facts.&quot; At the end, not much is left standing except Spanish <span class="foreign">bigote</span> &quot;mustache,&quot; which also has been proposed as the origin of the word, but not explained, so the chief virtue of that theory is the lack of evidence for or against it.
<br/><br/>
In support of the &quot;by God&quot; theory the surnames <span class="foreign">Bigott</span>, <span class="foreign">Bygott</span> are attested in Normandy and in England from the 11c., and French name-etymology sources (such as Dauzat) explain it as a derogatory name applied by the French to the Normans and representing &quot;by god.&quot; The English were known as <span class="foreign">goddamns</span> 200 years later in Joan of Arc&#x27;s France, and during World War I Americans serving in France were said to be known as <span class="foreign">les sommobiches</span> (see <span class="crossreference">son of a bitch</span>) for their characteristic oaths. But the sense development in <span class="foreign">bigot</span> would be difficult to explain. According to Donkin, the modern use first appears in French in 16c.
