<blockquote>A <span class="foreign">verbal concordance</span> consists of an alphabetical list of the principal words used in the work, under each of which references to the passages in which it is found are arranged in order, generally with citation of the essential part of each. A <span class="foreign">real concordance</span> is an alphabetical index of subjects. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>

<blockquote>In the precomputing era, search technology was unavailable, and a concordance offered readers of long works such as the Bible something comparable to search results for every word that they would have been likely to search for. Today, the ability to combine the result of queries concerning multiple terms (such as searching for words near other words) has reduced interest in concordance publishing. [Wikipedia]</blockquote>
<p>Originally a citation of parallel passages in the books of the Bible. It refers to the kind of reduced alphabetical character which arose 7c. and was from about 9c. substituted in writing for the large uncial. From it the small or lower-case letters of the modern Latin alphabet were derived.</p>

<p>As an adjective, from 1727 in printing, &quot;not capital, of reduced form, small&quot; (of letters); the general sense of &quot;extremely small&quot; is attested by 1893. Related: <span class="foreign">Minuscular</span>.</p>

<br/><br/>
Obsolete in English in its original sense (<span class="foreign">index finger</span> is recorded from 1768). Meaning &quot;alphabetical list of a book&#x27;s contents with directions where in the text to find them&quot; is from late 16c., from Latin phrases such as <span class="foreign">Index Nominum</span> &quot;Index of Names.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Meaning &quot;object serving as a pointer on an instrument, hand of a clock or watch&quot; is from 1590s. Scientific sense (<span class="foreign">refractive index</span>, etc.) is from 1829, from notion of &quot;an indicator.&quot; Economic sense (<span class="foreign">cost-of-living index</span>, etc.) is from 1870, from the scientific usage. 
<br/><br/>

[Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>1520s, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">dictionarium</span> &quot;collection of words and phrases,&quot; probably a shortening of <span class="foreign">dictionarius (liber)</span> &quot;(book) of words,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">dictionarius</span> &quot;of words,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dictio</span> &quot;a saying, expression,&quot; in Late Latin &quot;a word,&quot; noun of action from past-participle stem of <span class="foreign">dicere</span> &quot;speak, tell, say,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*deik-</span> &quot;to show,&quot; also &quot;pronounce solemnly.&quot;</p>
<p>The Medieval Latin word is said to have been first used by Johannes de Garlandia (John of Garland) as the title of a Latin vocabulary published c. 1220. Probably first English use in title of a book was in Sir Thomas Elyot&#x27;s &quot;Latin Dictionary&quot; (1538).</p>
<p>As an adjective, &quot;of or pertaining to a dictionary,&quot; from 1630s.  <span class="foreign">Dictionarist</span> &quot;compiler of a dictionary&quot; (1610s) is older than <span class="foreign">dictionarian</span> (1806 as a noun, 1785 as an adjective). Grose&#x27;s 1788 &quot;Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue&quot; has &quot;RICHARD SNARY. A dictionary.&quot;</p>
