Christianity; the Germanic word is of unknown origin. The Old English (Anglian) cognate <span class="foreign">giuli</span> was the Anglo-Saxons&#x27; name for a two-month midwinter season corresponding to Roman December and January, a time of important feasts but not itself a festival.</p>

<p>After conversion to Christianity the word narrowed to mean &quot;the 12-day feast of the Nativity&quot; (which began Dec. 25), but was replaced by <span class="foreign">Christmas</span> by 11c., except in the northeast (areas of Danish settlement), where it remained the usual word.</p>
<p>Revived 19c. by writers to mean &quot;the Christmas of &#x27;Merrie England.&#x27; &quot; First direct reference to the Yule log is 17c. According to some sources, Old Norse <span class="foreign">jol</span> was borrowed into Old French as <span class="foreign">jolif</span>, hence Modern French <span class="foreign">joli</span> &quot;pretty, nice,&quot; <blockquote>concerning which Touffon ye are to vnderstand, that in the East Indies often times, there are not stormes as in other countreys; but euery 10. or 12. yeeres there are such tempests and stormes, that it is a thing incredible, but to those that haue seene it, neither do they know certainly what yeere they wil come. <p>This sense of the word, in reference to titanic storms in the East Indies, first appears in Europe in Portuguese in the mid-16th century. Chinese (Cantonese) <span class="foreign">tai fung</span> &quot;a great wind&quot; also might have influenced the form or sense of the word in English, and that term and the Indian one may have had some mutual influence; <span class="foreign">toofan</span> still means &quot;big storm&quot; in India.</p>
