Modern English combinations with <span class="foreign">full</span> tend to have it at the end of the word (as <span class="crossreference">-ful</span>), but this is a recent development and in Old English it was more common at the start, but this word and <span class="crossreference">fulsome</span> appear to be the only survivors.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fulfill#etymonline_v_14239"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of agree" href="/word/agree#etymonline_v_8053">agree (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;to give consent, assent,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">agreer</span> &quot;to please, satisfy; to receive with favor, take pleasure in&quot; (12c.), a contraction of phrase <span class="foreign">a gré</span> &quot;favorably, of good will,&quot; literally &quot;to (one&#x27;s) liking&quot; (or a like contraction in Medieval Latin) from <span class="foreign">a</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">ad</span> &quot;to&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ad-</span>) + Old French <span class="foreign">gre</span>, <span class="foreign">gret</span> &quot;that which pleases,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">gratum</span>, neuter of <span class="foreign">gratus</span> &quot;pleasing, welcome, agreeable&quot; (from suffixed form of PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwere-</span> (2) &quot;to favor&quot;).<br/>
<br/>
In Middle English also &quot;to please, gratify, satisfy,&quot; a sense preserved in <span class="crossreference">agreeable</span>. Of parties, &quot;come to agreement; make a settlement,&quot; mid-15c.; meaning &quot;to be in harmony in opinions&quot; is from late 15c. Of things, &quot;to coincide,&quot; from 1520s. To <span class="foreign">agree to differ</span> is from 1785 (also <span class="foreign">agree to disagree</span>, 1792).
