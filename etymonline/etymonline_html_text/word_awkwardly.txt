<p>Meaning &quot;anything superlative or fine&quot; is from 1786. As an adjective, &quot;characteristic of a dandy, affectedly neat and trim,&quot; by 1813; earlier in the sense of &quot;fine, splendid, first-rate&quot; (1792) and in this sense it was very popular c. 1880-1900.</p>

<p>The popular guess, since at least 1827, is that it is from French <span class="foreign">Dandin</span>, a mock surname for a foolish person used in 16c. by Rabelais (<span class="foreign">Perrin Dandin</span>), also by Racine, La Fontaine, and Molière, from <span class="foreign">dandiner</span> &quot;to walk awkwardly, waddle.&quot; Farmer rejects this and derives it from <span class="foreign">dandyprat</span>, an Elizabethan word for &quot;a dwarf; a page; a young or insignificant person,&quot; originally (early 16c.) the name of a small silver coin. Both words are of unknown origin, and OED finds the connection of both to <span class="foreign">dandy</span> to be &quot;without any apparent ground.&quot; English <span class="foreign">dandy</span> was itself borrowed into French c. 1830.</p>
