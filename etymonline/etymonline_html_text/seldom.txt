class="foreign">seldum</span>, alteration of <span class="foreign">seldan</span> &quot;seldom, rarely,&quot;  from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*selda-</span> &quot;strange, rare&quot; (source German <span class="foreign">selten</span>), perhaps ultimately from the base of <span class="crossreference">self</span> (q.v.).
<br/><br/>
Form shifted on analogy of adverbial dative plurals in <span class="foreign">-um</span> (such as <span class="crossreference">whilom</span> &quot;at one time,&quot; from <span class="foreign">while</span>). The same development also created <span class="foreign">litlum</span> from <span class="foreign">little</span>, <span class="foreign">miclum</span> from <span class="foreign">mickle</span>. German <span class="foreign">seltsam</span> &quot;strange, odd,&quot; Dutch <span class="foreign">zeldzaam</span> are related, but with the second element conformed to their versions of <span class="crossreference">-some</span>.
<br/><br/>
<span class="foreign">Seldom-times</span> is from mid-15c. (Old English had <span class="foreign">seldhwanne</span> &quot;seldwhen&quot;). <span class="foreign">Seldom-seen</span> is from mid-15c. (Old English had <span class="foreign">seldsiene</span>, &quot;seld-seen&quot;).
<br/><br/>
Some compounds using the old form survived through Middle English, such as <span class="foreign">selcouth</span>&quot;rarely or little-known, unusual, strange, wonderful,&quot; from Old English <span class="foreign">selcuð</span>, <span class="foreign">seld-cuð</span>, from <span class="foreign">seldan</span> + <span class="foreign">cuð</span>  (see <span class="crossreference">couth</span>). Old English <span class="foreign">seldan</span> had comparative <span class="foreign">seldor</span>, superlative <span class="foreign">seldost</span>; in early Middle English, as <span class="foreign">seldan</span> changed form and lost its connection with these, <span class="foreign">selde</span> was formed as a positive.
