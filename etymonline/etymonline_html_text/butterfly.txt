title="Origin and meaning of butterfly" href="/word/butterfly#etymonline_v_18153"><em>butterfly</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>common name of any lepidopterous insect active in daylight, Old English <span class="foreign">buttorfleoge</span>, evidently <span class="crossreference">butter</span> (n.) + <span class="crossreference">fly</span> (n.), but the name is of obscure signification. Perhaps based on the old notion that the insects (or, according to Grimm, witches disguised as butterflies) consume butter or milk that is left uncovered. Or, less creatively, simply because the pale yellow color of many species&#x27; wings suggests the color of butter. Another theory connects it to the color of the insect&#x27;s excrement, based on Dutch cognate <span class="foreign">boterschijte</span>. Also see <span class="crossreference">papillon</span>.</p>
<p>Applied to persons from c. 1600, originally in reference to vain and gaudy attire; by 1806 in reference to transformation from early lowly state; in reference to flitting tendencies by 1873. The swimming stroke so called from 1935. As a type of mechanical nut, 1869. <span class="foreign">Butterflies</span> &quot;light stomach spasms caused by anxiety&quot; is from 1908. &quot;Who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel?&quot; is from Pope.</p>
<blockquote>The butterfly effect is a deceptively simple insight extracted from a complex modern field. As a low-profile assistant professor in MIT&#x27;s department of meteorology in 1961, [Edward] Lorenz created an early computer program to simulate weather. One day he changed one of a dozen numbers representing atmospheric conditions, from .506127 to .506. That tiny alteration utterly transformed his long-term forecast, a point Lorenz amplified in his 1972 paper, &quot;Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly&#x27;s Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?&quot; [Peter Dizikes, &quot;The Meaning of the Butterfly,&quot; The Boston Globe, June 8, 2008]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/butterfly#etymonline_v_18153"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fly" href="/word/fly#etymonline_v_8955">fly (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">fleoge</span> &quot;a fly, winged insect,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fleugon</span> &quot;flying insect&quot; (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fleiga</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fluga</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">vlieghe</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vlieg</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">flioga</span>, German <span class="foreign">Fliege</span> &quot;fly&quot;); literally &quot;the flying (insect)&quot; (compare Old English <span class="foreign">fleogende</span> &quot;flying&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*pleu-</span> &quot;to flow,&quot; which is also the source of <span class="crossreference">fly</span> (v.1).</p>
<p>Originally any winged insect (moths, gnats, beetles, locusts, hence <span class="crossreference">butterfly</span>, etc.) and long used by farmers and gardeners for any insect parasite. <span class="foreign">Flies</span> figuratively for &quot;large numbers&quot; of anything is from 1590s. Plural <span class="foreign">flien</span> (as in <span class="foreign">oxen</span>, etc.) gradually normalized 13c.-15c. to <span class="foreign">-s</span>. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fly in the ointment</strong></span> is from Eccles. x:1. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fly on the wall</strong></span> &quot;unseen observer&quot; first recorded 1881. <span class="foreign"><strong>No flies on _____</strong></span> &quot;no lack of activity or alertness on the part of,&quot; is attested by 1866. Meaning &quot;fish-hook dressed to resemble an insect&quot; is from 1580s; <span class="foreign"><strong>Fly-fishing</strong></span> is from 1650s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fly-catcher</strong></span> &quot;bird which eats insects on the wing&quot; is from 1670s. The <span class="foreign"><strong>fly agaric</strong></span> mushroom (1788) so called because it was used as a poison for flies.</p>
<p>The sense of &quot;a flight, flying&quot; is from mid-15c. From the verb and the notion of &quot;flapping as a wing does&quot; comes the noun sense of &quot;tent flap&quot; (1810), which was extended to &quot;strip of material sewn into a garment as a covering for buttons&quot; or some other purpose (1844). Baseball <span class="foreign"><strong>fly ball</strong></span> attested by 1866. To do something <span class="foreign"><strong>on the fly</strong></span> is 1856, apparently from baseball.</p>
<blockquote>When the catcher sees several fielders running to catch a ball, he should name the one he thinks surest to take it, when the others should not strive to catch the ball on the fly, but only, in case of its being missed, take it on the bound.
