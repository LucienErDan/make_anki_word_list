The first element probably is cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">haga</span> &quot;enclosure, portion of woodland marked off for cutting&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">hedge</span> (n.)). <br/><br/>
One of the magic words for which there is no male form, suggesting its original meaning was close to &quot;diviner, soothsayer,&quot; which were always female in northern European paganism, and <span class="foreign">hægtesse</span> seem at one time to have meant &quot;woman of prophetic and oracular powers&quot; (Ælfric uses it to render the Greek &quot;pythoness,&quot; the voice of the Delphic oracle), a figure greatly feared and respected. Later, the word was used of village wise women.
<br/><br/>
<span class="foreign">Haga</span> is also the <span class="foreign">haw-</span> in <span class="crossreference">hawthorn</span>, which is an important tree in northern European pagan religion. There may be several layers of folk etymology here. Confusion or blending with <span class="foreign">heathenish</span> is suggested by Middle English <span class="foreign">hæhtis</span>, <span class="foreign">hægtis</span> &quot;hag, witch, fury, etc.,&quot; and <span class="foreign">haetnesse</span> &quot;goddess,&quot; used of Minerva and Diana.
<br/><br/>
If the <span class="foreign">hægtesse</span> once was a powerful supernatural woman (in Norse it is an alternative word for <span class="crossreference">Norn</span>, any of the three weird sisters, the equivalent of the Fates), it might originally have carried the hawthorn sense. Later, when the pagan magic was reduced to local scatterings, it might have had the sense of &quot;hedge-rider,&quot; or &quot;she who straddles the hedge,&quot; because the hedge was the boundary between the civilized world of the village and the wild world beyond. The <span class="foreign">hægtesse</span> would have a foot in each reality. Even later, when it meant the local healer and root collector, living in the open and moving from village to village, it may have had the mildly pejorative Middle English sense of <span class="foreign">hedge-</span> (<span class="foreign">hedge-priest</span>, etc.), suggesting an itinerant sleeping under bushes.
