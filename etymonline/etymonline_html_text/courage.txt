title="Origin and meaning of courage" href="/word/courage#etymonline_v_19178"><em>courage</em> (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">corage</span>, &quot;heart (as the seat of emotions),&quot; hence &quot;spirit, temperament, state or frame of mind,&quot;from Old French <span class="foreign">corage</span> &quot;heart, innermost feelings; temper&quot; (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">courage</span>), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*coraticum</span> (source of Italian <span class="foreign">coraggio</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">coraje</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">cor</span> &quot;heart&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kerd-</span> &quot;heart&quot;).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;valor, quality of mind which enables one to meet danger and trouble without fear&quot; is from late 14c. In this sense Old English had <span class="foreign">ellen</span>, which also meant &quot;zeal, strength.&quot; Words for &quot;heart&quot; also commonly are metaphors for inner strength.</p>
<p>In Middle English, the word was used broadly for &quot;what is in one&#x27;s mind or thoughts,&quot; hence &quot;bravery,&quot; but also &quot;wrath, pride, confidence, lustiness,&quot; or any sort of inclination, and it was used in various phrases, such as <span class="foreign">bold corage</span> &quot;brave heart,&quot; <span class="foreign">careful corage</span> &quot;sad heart,&quot; <span class="foreign">fre corage</span> &quot;free will,&quot; <span class="foreign">wikked corage</span> &quot;evil heart.&quot; </p>
