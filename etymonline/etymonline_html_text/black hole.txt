<p>The same root produced Old English <span class="foreign">blac</span> &quot;bright, shining, glittering, pale;&quot; the connecting notions being, perhaps, &quot;fire&quot; (bright) and &quot;burned&quot; (dark), or perhaps &quot;absence of color.&quot; &quot;There is nothing more variable than the signification of words designating colour&quot; [Wedgwood].</p>
<p>The usual Old English word for &quot;black&quot; was <span class="foreign">sweart</span> (see <span class="crossreference">swart</span>). According to OED: &quot;In ME. it is often doubtful whether <span class="foreign">blac</span>, <span class="foreign">blak</span>, <span class="foreign">blake</span>, means &#x27;black, dark,&#x27; or &#x27;pale, colourless, wan, livid.&#x27; &quot; Used of dark-skinned people in Old English.</p>
<p>Of coffee with nothing added, attested by 1796. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black drop</strong></span> (1823) was a liquid preparation of opium, used medicinally. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black-fly</strong></span> (c. 1600) was used of various insects, especially an annoying pest of the northern American woods. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black Prince</strong></span> as a nickname of the eldest son of Edward III is attested by 1560s; the exact signification is uncertain.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;fierce, terrible, wicked&quot; is from late 14c. Figurative senses often come from the notion of &quot;without light,&quot; moral or spiritual. Latin <span class="foreign">niger</span> had many of the same figurative senses (&quot;gloomy; unlucky; bad, wicked, malicious&quot;). The metaphoric use of the Greek word, <span class="foreign">melas</span>, however, tended to reflect the notion of &quot;shrouded in darkness, overcast.&quot; In English it has been the color of sin and sorrow at least since c. 1300; the sense of &quot;with dark purposes, malignant&quot; emerged 1580s (in <span class="foreign">black art</span> &quot;necromancy;&quot; it is also the sense in <span class="foreign">black magic</span>). <span class="foreign"><strong>Black flag</strong></span>, flown (especially by pirates) as a signal of &quot;no mercy,&quot; is from 1590s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black dog</strong></span> &quot;melancholy&quot; attested from 1826.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Black belt</strong></span> is from 1870 in reference to district extending across the U.S. South with heaviest African population (also sometimes in reference to the fertility of the soil); it is attested from 1913 in the judo sense, worn by one who has attained a certain high degree of proficiency. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black power</strong></span> is from 1966, associated with Stokely Carmichael. <span class="foreign"><strong>Black English</strong></span> &quot;English as spoken by African-Americans,&quot; is by 1969.
