city&quot; (10c., Modern French <span class="foreign">cité</span>), from earlier <span class="foreign">citet</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">civitatem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">civitas</span>; in Late Latin sometimes <span class="foreign">citatem</span>) originally &quot;citizenship, condition or rights of a citizen, membership in the community,&quot; later &quot;community of citizens, state, commonwealth&quot; (used, for instance of the Gaulish tribes), from <span class="foreign">civis</span> &quot;townsman,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kei- </span>(1) &quot;to lie,&quot; also forming words for &quot;bed, couch,&quot; and with a secondary sense of &quot;beloved, dear.&quot;</p>
<p>Now &quot;a large and important town,&quot; but originally in early Middle English a walled town, a capital or cathedral town. Distinction from <span class="crossreference">town</span> is early 14c. OED calls it &quot;Not a native designation, but app[arently] at first a somewhat grandiose title, used instead of the OE. <span class="foreign">burh&quot; </span>(see <span class="crossreference">borough</span>).</p>
<p>Between Latin and English the sense was transferred from the inhabitants to the place. The Latin word for &quot;city&quot; was <span class="foreign">urbs</span>, but a resident was <span class="foreign">civis</span>. <span class="foreign">Civitas</span> seems to have replaced <span class="foreign">urbs</span> as Rome (the ultimate <span class="foreign">urbs</span>) lost its prestige. Loss of Latin <span class="foreign">-v-</span> is regular in French in some situations (compare <span class="foreign">alleger</span> from <span class="foreign">alleviare</span>; <span class="foreign">neige</span> from <span class="foreign">nivea</span>; <span class="foreign">jeune</span> from <span class="foreign">juvenis</span>. A different sound evolution from the Latin word yielded Italian <span class="foreign">citta</span>, Catalan <span class="foreign">ciutat</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">ciudad</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">cidade</span>.</p>
<p>London is <span class="foreign">the city</span> from 1550s. As an adjective, &quot;pertaining to a city, urban,&quot; from c. 1300. <span class="foreign"><strong>City hall</strong></span> &quot;chief municipal offices&quot; is first recorded 1670s; to <span class="foreign"><strong>fight city hall</strong></span> is 1913, American English. <span class="foreign"><strong>City slicker</strong></span> &quot;a smart and plausible rogue, of a kind usu. found in cities&quot; [OED] is first recorded 1916 (see <span class="crossreference">slick</span> (adj.)). <span class="foreign"><strong>City limits</strong></span> is from 1825.</p>
