His cousins and their descendants constitute the royal line of Holland.</p>
<p>The name is from the town of <span class="foreign">Orange</span> on the Rhone in France, which became part of the Nassau principality in 1530. Its Roman name was <span class="foreign">Arausio</span>, which is said in 19c. sources to be from <span class="foreign">aura</span> &quot;a breeze&quot; and a reference to the north winds which rush down the valley, but perhaps this is folk etymology of a Celtic word. The name subsequently was corrupted to <span class="foreign">Auranche</span>, then <span class="foreign">Orange</span>.</p>
<p>The town has no obvious association with the fruit other than being on the road from Marseilles to Paris, along which masses of oranges were transported to northern France and beyond. In this roundabout way the political/religious movement of Northern Irish Protestantism acquired an association with the color orange, the Irish national flag acquired its orange band, and Syracuse University in New York state acquired its &quot;Otto the Orange&quot; mascot.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Orangemen#etymonline_v_7107"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of rune" href="/word/rune#etymonline_v_16645">rune (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Old English <span class="foreign">run, rune</span> &quot;secret, mystery, dark mysterious statement, (secret) council,&quot; also &quot;a runic letter&quot; (<span class="foreign">runstæf</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*runo</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">run</span> &quot;a secret, magic sign, runic character,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">runa</span> &quot;a secret conversation, whisper,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">runa</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ru-no-</span>, source of technical terms of magic in Germanic and Celtic (source also of Gaelic <span class="foreign">run</span> &quot;a secret, mystery, craft, deceit, purpose, intention, desire,&quot; Welsh <span class="foreign">rhin</span> &quot;a secret, charm, virtue&quot;). Also see <span class="crossreference">Runnymede</span>.
<br/><br/>
The presumption often is that the magical sense was the original one of the word, and the use of runes as letters was secondary. However, this derivation is questioned by some linguists: &quot;[T]he obsession with magic of many runologists can be explained more from the psychology of the scholars than from the intrinsic contents of the inscriptions. ... [F]or almost all [of these scholars] the aura of mystery which they ascribe to the fuþark was a supplementary attraction in an otherwise austere field of labor&quot; [French scholar Lucien Musset, quoted in Elmer H. Antonsen, &quot;The Runes: The Earliest Germanic Writing System,&quot; in &quot;The Origins of Writing,&quot;University ogf Nebraska, 1989] .
<br/><br/>
The word entered Middle English as <span class="foreign">roun</span> and by normal evolution would have become Modern English <span class="foreign">*rown</span>, but it died out mid-15c. when the use of runes did. The modern usage is from late 17c., from German philologists who had reintroduced the word in their writings from a Scandinavian source (such as Danish <span class="foreign">rune</span>, from Old Norse <span class="foreign">run</span>).
