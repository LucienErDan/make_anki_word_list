also &quot;supernatural being of subhuman size,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*dweraz</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">dwerch</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">dwerg</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">twerg</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zwerg</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">dvergr</span>), perhaps from PIE <span class="foreign">*dhwergwhos</span> &quot;something tiny,&quot; but with no established cognates outside Germanic.</p>
<p>Also used by c. 1200 of an animal or plant much below the ordinary size of its species.&quot; The use of <span class="foreign">dwarf</span> in the Germanic mythological sense, &quot;a diminished and generally deformed being, dwelling in rocks and hills and skilled in working metals,&quot; seems to have faded after Middle English and been revived after c. 1770 from German.</p>
<blockquote>Whilst in this and other ways the dwarfs do at times have dealings with mankind, yet on the whole they seem to shrink from man; they give the impression of a downtrodden afflicted race, which is on the point of abandoning its ancient home to new and more powerful invaders. There is stamped on their character something <span class="foreign">shy</span> and something <span class="foreign">heathenish</span>, which estranges them from intercourse with christians. They chafe at human faithlessness, which no doubt would primarily mean the apostacy from heathenism. In the poems of the Mid. Ages, <span class="foreign">Laurin</span> is expressly set before us as a <span class="foreign">heathen</span>. It goes sorely against the dwarfs to see churches built, <span class="foreign">bell-ringing</span> ... disturbs their ancient privacy; they also hate the clearing of forests, agriculture, new fangled pounding-machinery for ore. [&quot;Teutonic Mythology,&quot; Jakob Grimm, transl. Stallybrass, 1883]</blockquote>
<p>The shift of the Old English guttural at the end of the word to modern <span class="foreign">-f</span> is typical (compare <span class="crossreference">enough</span>, <span class="crossreference">draft</span>) and begins to appear early 14c. In Middle English it also was <span class="foreign">dwerþ</span>, <span class="foreign">dwerke</span>. Old English plural <span class="foreign">dweorgas</span> became Middle English <span class="foreign">dwarrows</span>, later leveled down to <span class="foreign">dwarfs</span>. The use of <span class="foreign">dwarves</span> for the legendary race was popularized by J.R.R. Tolkien. As an adjective, from 1590s.</p>

<p>The use of <span class="foreign">giant</span> and <span class="foreign">dwarf</span> in reference to stars of the highest and lowest luminosity is attested by 1914, said to have been suggested by Danish astronomer Ejnar Hertzsprung, (1873-1967); hence <span class="foreign">red dwarf</span> (attested by 1922), <span class="foreign">white dwarf</span>, <span class="foreign">black dwarf</span>
