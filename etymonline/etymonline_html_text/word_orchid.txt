&quot;School Botanty,&quot; from Modern Latin <span class="foreign">Orchideæ</span> (Linnaeus), the plant&#x27;s family name, from Latin <span class="foreign">orchis</span>, a kind of orchid, from Greek <span class="foreign">orkhis</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">orkheos</span>) &quot;orchid,&quot; literally &quot;testicle,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*h(o)rghi-</span>, the <p>The plant so called because of the shape of its root; Greek <span class="foreign">orkhis</span> also was the name of a kind of olive, also so called for its shape. Earlier in English in Latin form, <span class="foreign">orchis</span> (1560s), and in Middle English it was <span class="foreign">ballockwort</span> (c. 1300; see <span class="crossreference">ballocks</span>). It was the typical name in the North and the Northern armies for a Confederate soldier during the American Civil War, and the Southern soldiers were, collectively <span class="foreign">Johnnies</span>, generically <span class="foreign">Johnny Reb</span>. Century Dictionary finds this etymology &quot;improbable,&quot; and OED rates it &quot;not very plausible with regard to sense,&quot; but no one seems to have a better explanation.</p>
<p>The medial <span class="foreign">-f-</span> disappeared 14c. The word is not found outside English except where borrowed from it. Sense of &quot;woman of superior position in society&quot; is c. 1200; that of &quot;woman whose manners and sensibilities befit her for high rank in society&quot; is from 1861 (<span class="crossreference">ladylike</span> suggesting this sense is attested from 1580s, and <span class="foreign">ladily</span> from c. 1400). Meaning &quot;woman chosen as an object of chivalrous love&quot; is from early 14c. Used commonly as an address to any woman since 1890s.</p>
<p>Applied since Old English to the Holy Virgin, hence many extended usages in plant names, place names, etc., from genitive singular <span class="foreign">hlæfdigan</span>, which in Middle English merged with the nominative, so that <span class="foreign">lady-</span> often represents <span class="foreign">(Our) Lady&#x27;s</span>, as in <span class="crossreference">ladybug</span>. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lady Day</strong></span> (late 13c.) was the festival of the Annunciation of the Virgin Mary (March 25). <span class="foreign"><strong>Ladies&#x27; from Old French <span class="foreign">fol</span> &quot;madman, insane person; idiot; rogue; jester,&quot; also &quot;blacksmith&#x27;s bellows,&quot; also an adjective meaning &quot;mad, insane&quot; (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">fou</span>), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">follus</span> (adj.) &quot;foolish,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">follis</span> &quot;bellows, leather bag,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*bhel-</span> (2) &quot;to blow, swell.&quot;</p>
<p>The sense evolution probably is from Vulgar Latin use of <span class="foreign">follis</span> in a sense of &quot;windbag, empty-headed person.&quot; Compare also Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vatula-</span> &quot;insane,&quot; literally &quot;windy, inflated with wind.&quot; But some sources suggest evolution from Latin <span class="foreign">folles</span> &quot;puffed cheeks&quot; (of a buffoon), a secondary sense from plural of <span class="foreign">follis</span>. One makes the &quot;idiot&quot; sense original, the other the &quot;jester&quot; sense.</p>
<blockquote>The word has in mod.Eng. a much stronger sense than it had at an earlier period; it has now an implication of insulting contempt which does not in the same degree belong to any of its synonyms, or to the derivative <span class="foreign">foolish</span>. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Also used in Middle English for &quot;sinner, rascal, impious person&quot; (late 13c.). Meaning &quot;jester, court clown&quot; in English is attested c. 1300, though it is not always possible to tell whether the reference is to a professional entertainer counterfeiting mental weakness or an amusing lunatic, and the notion of the <span class="foreign">fool sage</span> whose sayings are ironically wise is also in English from c. 1300. The French word probably also got into English via its borrowing in the Scandinavian languages of the vikings (Old Norse <span class="foreign">fol</span>, Old Danish <span class="foreign">fool</span>, <span class="foreign">fol</span>).</p>
<blockquote>There is no foole to the olde foole [&quot;Proverbs of John Heywood,&quot; 1546]</blockquote>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>make a fool of</strong></span> (someone) &quot;cause to appear ridiculous&quot; is from 1620s (<span class="foreign">make fool</span> &quot;to deceive, make (someone) appear a fool&quot; is from early 15c.). <span class="foreign"><strong>Feast of Fools</strong></span> (early 14c., from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">festum stultorum</span>) was the burlesque festival celebrated in some churches on New Year&#x27;s Day in medieval times. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fool&#x27;s gold</strong></span> &quot;iron pyrite&quot; is from 1829. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fool&#x27;s paradise</strong></span> &quot;illusory state of happiness&quot; is from mid-15c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fool-trap</strong></span> is from 1690s. <span class="foreign">Foolosopher</span>, a useful insult, is in a 1549 translation of Erasmus. <span class="foreign">Fool&#x27;s ballocks</span> is described in OED as &quot;an old name&quot; for the green-winged orchid. <span class="foreign">Fool-killer</span> &quot;imaginary personage invested with authority to put to death anybody notoriously guilty of great folly&quot; is from 1851, American English.</p>
<blockquote><strong>Fool killer</strong>, a great American myth imagined by editors, who feign that his or its services are greatly needed, and frequently alluded to as being &quot;around&quot; or &quot;in town&quot; when some special act of folly calls for castigation. Whether the fool-killer be an individual or an instrument cannot always be gathered from the dark phraseology in which he or it is alluded to; but the weight of authority would sanction the impersonal interpretation. [Walsh, &quot;Handy-Book of Literary Curiosities,&quot; 1892]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fool#etymonline_v_11755"><span>Relate
