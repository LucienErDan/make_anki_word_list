title="Origin and meaning of mustard" href="/word/mustard#etymonline_v_19316"><em>mustard</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 13c. (late 12c. as a surname), &quot;seed of the mustard plant crushed and used as a condiment paste or for medicinal purposes,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">mostarde</span> &quot;mustard; mustard plant&quot; (Modern French <span class="foreign">moutarde</span>), from <span class="foreign">moust</span> &quot;must,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">mustum</span> &quot;new wine&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">must</span> (n.1)); so called because it was originally prepared by adding must to the ground seeds of the plant to make a paste. As the name of the plant itself, by mid-14c. in English. As a color name, it is attested from 1848.</p>

<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Mustard-pot</strong></span> is attested from early 15c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Mustard gas</strong></span><span class="foreign">,</span> World War I poison (first used by the Germans at Ypres, 1917), so called for its color and smell and burning effect on eyes and lungs; chemical name is dichlordiethyl sulfide; it contains no mustard and is an atomized liquid, not a gas. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut the mustard</strong></span> (1907, usually in negative) is probably from slang <span class="foreign">mustard</span> &quot;genuine article, best thing&quot; (1903) on notion of &quot;that which enhances flavor.&quot;</p>
