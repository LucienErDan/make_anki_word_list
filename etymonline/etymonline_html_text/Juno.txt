A doublet of <span class="crossreference">mint</span> (n.2)).</p>
<blockquote>It had been justly stated by a British writer that the power to make a small piece of paper, not worth one cent, by the inscribing of a few names, to be worth a thousand dollars, was a power too high to be entrusted to the hands of mortal man. [John C. Calhoun, speech, U.S. Senate, Dec. 29, 1841] </blockquote>
<p>Extended by early 19c. to include paper recognized and accepted as a substitute for coin. The highwayman&#x27;s threat <span class="foreign"><strong>your money or your life</strong></span> is attested by 1774. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>in the money</strong></span> (1902) originally referred to &quot;one who finishes among the prize-winners&quot; (in a horse race, etc.). The challenge to <span class="foreign"><strong>put (one&#x27;s) money where (one&#x27;s) mouth is</strong></span> is recorded by 1942 in African-American vernacular. <span class="foreign"><strong>Money-grub</strong></span> for &quot;avaricious person, one who is sordidly intent on amassing money&quot; is from 1768; <span class="foreign"><strong>money-grubber</strong></span> is by 1835. The image of money burning a hole in someone&#x27;s pocket is attested from 1520s (<span class="foreign">brennyd out the botom of hys purs</span>).</p>
