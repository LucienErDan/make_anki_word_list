title="Origin and meaning of by" href="/word/by#etymonline_v_18164"><em>by</em> (prep., adv.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">be-</span> (unstressed) or <span class="foreign">bi</span> (stressed) &quot;near, in, by, during, about,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bi</span> &quot;around, about,&quot; in compounds often merely intensive (source also of Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">bi</span> &quot;by, near,&quot; Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bie</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">bij</span>, German <span class="foreign">bei</span> &quot;by, at, near,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">bi</span> &quot;about&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhi</span>, reduced form of root <span class="crossreference">*ambhi-</span> &quot;around.&quot;</p>
<p>As an adverb by c. 1300, &quot;near, close at hand.&quot; OED (2nd ed. print) has 38 distinct definitions of it as a preposition. Originally an adverbial particle of place, which sense survives in place names (<span class="foreign">Whitby</span>, <span class="foreign">Grimsby</span>, etc., also compare <span class="crossreference">rudesby</span>). Elliptical use for &quot;secondary course&quot; was in Old English (opposed to <span class="foreign">main</span>, as in <span class="crossreference">byway</span>, also compare <span class="foreign">by-blow</span> &quot;illegitimate child,&quot; 1590s, Middle English <span class="foreign">loteby</span> &quot;a concubine,&quot; from obsolete <span class="foreign">lote</span> &quot;to lurk, lie hidden&quot;). This also is the sense of the second <span class="foreign">by</span> in the phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>by the by</strong></span> (1610s). <span class="foreign">By the way</span> literally means &quot;along the way&quot; (c. 1200), hence &quot;in passing by,&quot; used figuratively to introduce a tangential observation (&quot;incidentally&quot;) by 1540s.</p>
<p>To swear <span class="foreign">by</span> something or someone is in Old English, perhaps originally &quot;in the presence of.&quot; Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>by and by</strong></span> (early 14c.) originally meant &quot;one by one,&quot; with <span class="foreign">by</span> apparently denoting succession; modern sense of &quot;before long&quot; is from 1520s. <span class="foreign"><strong>By and large</strong></span> &quot;in all its length and breadth&quot;
