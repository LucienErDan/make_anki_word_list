<p>The variation of <span class="foreign">-ize</span> and <span class="foreign">-ise</span> began in Old French and Middle English, perhaps aided by a few words (such as <span class="foreign">surprise</span>, see below) where the ending is French or Latin, not Greek. With the classical revival, English partially reverted to the correct Greek <span class="foreign">-z-</span> spelling from late 16c. But the 1694 edition of the authoritative French Academy dictionary standardized the spellings as <span class="foreign">-s-</span>, which influenced English.</p>

<p>In Britain, despite the opposition to it (at least formerly) of OED, <span class="foreign">Encyclopaedia Britannica</span>, the &quot;Times of London,&quot; and Fowler, <span class="foreign">-ise</span> remains dominant. Fowler thinks this is to avoid the difficulty of remembering the short list of common words not from Greek which must be spelled with an <span class="foreign">-s-</span> (such as <span class="crossreference">advertise</span>, <span class="crossreference">devise</span>, <span class="crossreference">surprise</span>). American English has always favored <span class="foreign">-ize</span>.
