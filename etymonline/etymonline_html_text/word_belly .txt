<p>By c. 1200 it was being used for &quot;the stomach,&quot; especially as a symbol of gluttony, and by late 14c. to mean &quot;abdomen of a human or animal, front part of the body between the breast and the groin or the diaphragm and the pelvis.&quot;</p>
<p>The Old English word for &quot;belly, stomach&quot; was <span class="foreign">buc</span> (cognate with German <span class="foreign">Bauch</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">buik</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">buk</span>, from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*būkaz</span>, a word indicative of swelling, with no known connections). The plural of Old English <span class="foreign">belg</span> emerged in Middle English as a separate word, <span class="crossreference">bellows</span>. Meaning &quot;bulging part or convex surface of anything&quot; is 1590s. The West Germanic root had a figurative or extended sense of &quot;anger, arrogance&quot; (as in Old English <span class="foreign">bolgenmod</span> &quot;enraged;&quot; <span class="foreign">belgan</span> (v.) &quot;to become angry&quot;), probably from the notion of &quot;swelling.&quot;</p>
<p>Indo-European languages commonly use the same word for both the external belly and the internal (stomach, womb, etc.), but the distinction of external and internal is somewhat present in English <span class="foreign">belly/stomach</span>; Greek <span class="foreign">gastr-</span> (see <span class="crossreference">gastric</span>) in classical language denoted the paunch or belly, while modern science uses it only in reference to the stomach as an organ.</p>
<p>As a personal name from 12c. <span class="foreign">Belly-naked</span> in Middle English was &quot;stripped to the belly, completely naked.&quot; Fastidious avoidance of <span class="foreign">belly</span> in speech and writing (compensated for by stretching the senses of imported <span class="crossreference">stomach</span> and <span class="crossreference">abdomen</span>, baby-talk <span class="crossreference">tummy</span> and misappropriated <span class="crossreference">midriff</span>) began late 18c. and the word was banished from Bibles in many early 19c. editions.</p>
