title="Origin and meaning of business" href="/word/business#etymonline_v_18132"><em>business</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">bisignes</span> (Northumbrian) &quot;care, anxiety, occupation,&quot; from <span class="foreign">bisig</span> &quot;careful, anxious, busy, occupied, diligent&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">busy</span> (adj.)) + <span class="crossreference">-ness</span>. The original sense is obsolete, as is the Middle English sense of &quot;state of being much occupied or engaged&quot; (mid-14c.), the latter replaced by <span class="crossreference">busyness</span>. Johnson&#x27;s dictionary also has <span class="foreign">busiless</span> &quot;At leisure; without business; unemployed.&quot; Modern two-syllable pronunciation is 17c.</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;a person&#x27;s work, occupation, that which one does for a livelihood&quot; is first recorded late 14c. (in late Old English <span class="foreign">bisig</span> (adj.) appears as a noun with the sense &quot;occupation, state of employment&quot;). Sense of &quot;that which is undertaken as a duty&quot; is from late 14c. Meaning &quot;what one is about at the moment&quot; is from 1590s. Sense of &quot;trade, commercial engagements, mercantile pursuits collectively&quot; is first attested 1727, on the notion of &quot;matters which occupy one&#x27;s time and attention.&quot; In 17c. <span class="foreign">business</span> also could mean &quot;sexual intercourse.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Business card</strong></span> first attested 1840; <span class="foreign"><strong>business letter</strong></span> from 1766. <span class="foreign"><strong>Business end</strong></span> &quot;the practical or effective part&quot; (of something) is American English, by 1874. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>business as usual</strong></span> attested from 1865. To <span class="foreign"><strong>mean business</strong></span> &quot;be intent on serious action&quot; is from 1856.
