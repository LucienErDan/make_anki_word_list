<br/><br/>
Pope Gregory the Great intended to make London, as the largest southern Anglo-Saxon city, the metropolitan see of southern England, but Christianity got a foothold first in the minor kingdom of Kent, whose heathen ruler Ethelbert had married a Frankish Christian princess. London was in the Kingdom of Essex and out of reach of the missionaries at first. Therefore, in part perhaps to flatter Ethelbert, his capital was made the cathedral city. Related: <span class="foreign">Canterburian</span>. The earliest use is as a slang word for &quot;the whining speech of beggars asking for alms&quot; (1640s), from the verb in this sense (1560s), from Old North French <span class="foreign">canter</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">chanter</span>) &quot;to sing, chant,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">cantare</span>, frequentative of <span class="foreign">canere</span> &quot;to sing&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kan-</span> &quot;to sing&quot;).</p>
<p>Century Dictionary notes the ecclesiastical use of <span class="foreign">cantus</span> in Medieval Latin, and writes, &quot;The word <span class="foreign">cant</span> may thus have become associated with beggars; but there may have been also an allusion to a perfunctory performance of divine service and hence a hypocritical use of religious phrases.&quot; The sense in English expanded after 1680 to mean &quot;the jargon of criminals and vagabonds,&quot; and from thence the word was applied contemptuously by any sect or school to the phraseology of its rival.</p>
