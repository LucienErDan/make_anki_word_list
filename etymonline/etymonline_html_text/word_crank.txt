and meaning of crank" href="/word/crank#etymonline_v_298"><em>crank</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;bent or vertical handle for turning a revolving axis,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">*cranc</span>, implied in <span class="foreign">crancstæf</span> &quot;a weaver&#x27;s instrument,&quot; <span class="foreign">crencestre</span> &quot;female weaver, spinster,&quot; which is related to <span class="foreign">crincan</span> &quot;to bend, yield,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*krank-</span> &quot;bend, curl up&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cringe</span>).</p>
<p>English retains the literal sense of the ancient word (&quot;something bent or crooked&quot;), while in other Germanic languages it tends to have only a figurative sense (German and Dutch <span class="foreign">krank</span> &quot;sick,&quot; formerly &quot;weak, small&quot;). The Continental definition entered into English <span class="foreign">crank</span> via slang <span class="foreign">counterfeit crank</span> &quot;one who shams sickness to get charity&quot; (1560s). OED notes that &quot;the 16th c. vagabonds&#x27; cant contains words taken directly from Continental languages.&quot; It apparently lingered in the north (the 1825 supplement to Jamieson&#x27;s Scottish dictionary has <span class="foreign">crank</span> &quot;infirm, weak, etc.&quot;) and might have influenced the development of the English word.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;twist or turn of speech, grotesquery in words&quot; is from 1590s; that of &quot;absurd or unreasonable act&quot; (perhaps caused by &quot;twisted judgment&quot;) is from 1848. The sense of &quot;eccentric person,&quot; especially one who is irrationally fixated, is first recorded 1833; this sometimes is said to be from the crank of a barrel organ, which makes it play the same tune over and over; but more likely it is a back-formation from <span class="crossreference">cranky</span> (q.v.) and thus from the notion of one having a mental &quot;twist.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The person who adopts &quot;any presentiment, any extravagance as most in nature,&quot; is not commonly called a Transcendentalist, but is known colloquially as a &quot;crank.&quot; [Oliver W. Holmes, &quot;Ralph Waldo Emerson&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>There also was a <span class="foreign">crank</span> (adj.) in Middle English meaning &quot;lively, brisk, merry,&quot; but it is of uncertain origin and connection. <span class="foreign">Cranky</span> for &quot;merry, lively&quot; lingered into 19c. in northern England dialects and American English.
