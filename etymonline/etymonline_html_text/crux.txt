<p>The modern English form is comparatively late, and even the earlier <span class="foreign">croisade</span> is post-Middle English (French <span class="foreign">croisade</span> replaced earlier <span class="foreign">croisée</span>). Middle English nouns were <span class="foreign">croiserie</span> (c. 1300), <span class="foreign">creiserie</span>.</p>
<p>Especially in reference to the medieval expeditions undertaken by European Christians for recovery of the Holy Land from Muslims. Generally they are counted as seven between 1095 and 1271, but some smaller efforts (e.g. the &quot;Children&#x27;s Crusade&quot;) are omitted and the word sometimes is extended to other religiously motivated expeditions (e.g. against the Albigenses or the Prussians). Also from Latin crux are Italian <span class="foreign">croce</span>, French <span class="foreign">croix</span>, Spanish and Portuguese <span class="foreign">cruz</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kruis</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kreuz</span>. </p>
<p>The modern word is the northern England form and has predominated. Middle English also had two other forms of the same word, arriving from the continent by different paths: <span class="foreign">cruche, crouche</span> (c. 1200) was from Medieval Latin, with pronunciation as in Italian <span class="foreign">croce</span> (compare <span class="foreign">Crouchmas</span> &quot;festival of the Invention of the Cross,&quot; late 14c.)<span class="foreign">.</span> Later, especially in southern England, the form <span class="foreign">crois, croice</span>, from Old French, was the common one (compare <span class="foreign">croisade</span>, the older form of <span class="crossreference">crusade</span>). The Old English word was <span class="crossreference">rood</span>.</p>
<p>By c. 1200 as &quot;ornamental likeness of the cross, something resembling or in the form of a cross; sign of the cross made with the right hand or with fingers.&quot; From mid-14c. as &quot;small cross with a human figure attached; a crucifix;&quot; late 14c. as &quot;outdoor structure or monument in the form of a cross.&quot; Also late 14c. as &quot;a cross formed by two lines drawn or cut on a surface; two lines intersecting at right angles; the shape of a cross without regard to religious signification.&quot; From late 12c. as a surname.</p>
<p>From c. 1200 in English in the figurative sense &quot;the burden of a Christian; any suffering voluntarily borne for Christ&#x27;s sake; a trial or affliction; penance in Christ&#x27;s name,&quot; from Matthew x.38, xvi.24, etc. Theological sense &quot;crucifixion and death of Christ as a necessary part of his mission&quot; is from late 14c.</p>
<p>As &quot;a mixing of breeds in the production of animals&quot; from 1760, hence broadly &quot;a mixture of the characteristics of two different things&quot; (1796). In pugilism, 1906, from the motion of the blow, crossing over the opponent&#x27;s lead (1880s as a verb; <span class="foreign">cross-counter</span> (n.) is from 1883).
