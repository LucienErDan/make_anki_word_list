the vowel indicating that the modern word derives from oblique cases (genitive <span class="foreign">nihte</span>, dative <span class="foreign">niht</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*nahts</span> (source also of Old Saxon and Old High German <span class="foreign">naht</span>, Old Frisian and Dutch <span class="foreign">nacht</span>, German <span class="foreign">Nacht</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">natt</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">nahts</span>).</p>
<p>The Germanic words are from PIE <span class="foreign">*nekwt-</span> &quot;night&quot; (source also of Greek <span class="foreign">nuks</span> &quot;a night,&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">nox</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">nochd</span>, Sanskrit <span class="foreign">naktam</span> &quot;at night,&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">naktis</span> &quot;night,&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">nosti</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">noch&#x27;</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">henoid</span> &quot;tonight&quot;), according to Watkins, probably from a verbal root <span class="foreign">*neg-</span> &quot;to be dark, be night.&quot; For spelling with <span class="foreign">-gh-</span> see <span class="crossreference">fight</span>.</p>
<blockquote>The fact that the Aryans have a common name for night, but not for day (q.v.), is due to the fact that they reckoned by nights. [Weekley]</blockquote>
<p>Compare German <span class="foreign">Weihnachten</span> &quot;Christmas.&quot; In early times, the day was held to begin at sunset, so Old English <span class="foreign">monanniht</span> &quot;Monday night&quot; was the night before Monday, or what we would call <span class="foreign">Sunday night</span>. The Greeks, by contrast, counted their days by mornings.</p>
