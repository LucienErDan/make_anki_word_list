elder, experienced,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*althaz</span> &quot;grown up, adult&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">ald</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">alþeis</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">oud</span>, German <span class="foreign">alt</span>), originally a past participle stem of a verb meaning &quot;grow, nourish&quot; (compare Gothic <span class="foreign">alan</span> &quot;to grow up,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">ala</span> &quot;to nourish&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*al-</span> (2) &quot;to grow, nourish.&quot;</p>
<p>The usual PIE root is <span class="foreign">*sen-</span> (see <span class="crossreference">senior</span> (adj.)). A few Indo-European languages distinguish words for &quot;old&quot; (vs. young) from words for &quot;old&quot; (vs. new), and some have separate words for aged persons as opposed to old things. Latin <span class="foreign">senex</span> was used of aged living things, mostly persons, while <span class="foreign">vetus</span> (literally &quot;having many years&quot;) was used of inanimate things. Greek <span class="foreign">geraios</span> was used mostly of humans; Greek <span class="foreign">palaios</span> was used mostly of things, of persons only in a derogatory sense. Greek also had <span class="foreign">arkhaios</span>, literally &quot;belonging to the beginning,&quot; which parallels French <span class="foreign">ancien</span>, used mostly with reference to things &quot;of former times.&quot;</p>

<p>Old English also had <span class="foreign">fyrn</span> &quot;ancient,&quot; related to Old English <span class="foreign">feor</span> &quot;far, distant&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">far</span>, and compare Gothic <span class="foreign">fairneis</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">forn</span> &quot;old, of old, of former times,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">firni</span> &quot;old, experienced&quot;). The original Old English vowel is preserved in Scots <span class="foreign">auld</span>, also in <span class="crossreference">alderman</span>. The original comparative and superlative (<span class="foreign">elder</span>, <span class="foreign">eldest</span>) are retained in particular uses.</p>

<p>First record of <span class="foreign"><strong>old-timer</strong></span> is from 1860. Expression <span class="foreign"><strong>old as the hills</strong></span> is recorded by 1819. The <span class="foreign"><strong>good old days</strong></span> dates from 1828. <span class="foreign"><strong>Of old</strong></span> &quot;of old times&quot; is from late 14c.  <span class="foreign"><strong>Old Testament</strong></span> is attested from mid-14c.</p>
