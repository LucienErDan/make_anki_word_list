<p>This is apparently from a root <span class="foreign">*nauti-</span> &quot;death, to be exhausted,&quot; source also of Old English <span class="foreign">ne, neo</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">na</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">naus</span> &quot;corpse;&quot; Old Irish <span class="foreign">naunae</span> &quot;famine, shortage,&quot; Old Cornish <span class="foreign">naun</span> &quot;corpse;&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">navi</span> &quot;corpse,&quot; <span class="foreign">nazda</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">nuzda</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">nędza</span> &quot;misery, distress;&quot; Old Prussian <span class="foreign">nowis</span> &quot;corpse,&quot; <span class="foreign">nautin</span> &quot;need, distress,&quot; <span class="foreign">nawe</span> &quot;death;&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">novyti</span> &quot;to torture, kill,&quot; <span class="foreign">nove</span> &quot;death.&quot; As it is attested only in Germanic, Celtic, and Balto-Slavic, it might be non-PIE, from a regional substrate language.</p>

<p>From 12c. as &quot;lack of something that is necessary or important; state or condition of needing something;&quot; also &quot;a necessary act, required work or duty.&quot; Meaning &quot;extreme poverty, destitution, want of means of subsistence&quot; is from early 14c. </p>

<p>The more common Old English word for &quot;need, necessity, want&quot; was <span class="foreign">ðearf</span>, but they were connected via a notion of &quot;trouble, pain,&quot; and the two formed a compound, <span class="foreign">niedðearf</span> &quot;need, necessity, compulsion, thing needed.&quot; <span class="foreign">Nied</span> also might have been influenced by Old English <span class="foreign">neod</span> &quot;desire, longing,&quot; which often was spelled the same. <span class="foreign">Nied</span> was common in Old English compounds, such as <span class="foreign">niedfaru</span> &quot;compulsory journey,&quot; a euphemism for &quot;death;&quot; <span class="foreign">niedhæmed</span> &quot;rape&quot; (the second element being an Old English word meaning &quot;sexual intercourse&quot;);  <span class="foreign">niedling</span> &quot;slave.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/need#etymonline_v_2366"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of need" href="/word/need#etymonline_v_43179"><em>need</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">neodian</span> &quot;be necessary, be required (for some purpose),&quot; intransitive; also transitive, &quot;require, have need of,&quot; from the same root as <span class="crossreference">need</span> (n.).
