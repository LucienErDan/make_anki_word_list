<p>The spelling with <span class="foreign">-d-</span> probably reflects influence of Anglo-French <span class="foreign">murdre</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">mordre</span>, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">murdrum</span>, which is from the Germanic word. <p>In Old Norse, custom distinguished <span class="foreign">morð</span> &quot;secret slaughter&quot; from <span class="foreign">vig</span> &quot;slaying.&quot; The former involved concealment, or slaying a man by night or when asleep, and was a heinous crime. The latter was not a disgrace, if the killer acknowledged his deed, but he was subject to vengeance or demand for compensation.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;product of an untimely birth&quot; is from 1630s; earlier in this sense was <span class="foreign">abortive</span> (early 14c.). Another earlier noun in English for &quot;miscarriage&quot; was <span class="foreign">abort</span> (early 15c.). In the Middle English translation of Guy de Chauliac&#x27;s &quot;Grande Chirurgie&quot; (early 15c.) Compare <span class="crossreference">miscarriage</span>.</p>
<p>In 19c. some effort was made to distinguish <span class="foreign">abortion</span> &quot;expulsion of the fetus between 6 weeks and 6 months&quot; from <span class="foreign">miscarriage</span> (the same within 6 weeks of conception) and <span class="foreign">premature labor</span> (delivery after 6 months but before due time). The deliberate miscarriage was <span class="foreign">criminal abortion</span>. This broke down late 19c. as <span class="foreign">abortion</span> came to be used principally for intentional miscarriages, probably via phrases such as <span class="foreign">procure an abortion</span>.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Criminal abortion</span> is premeditated or intentional abortion procured, at any of pregnancy, by artificial means, and solely for the purpose of preventing the birth of a living child : feticide. At common law the criminality depended on the abortion being caused after quickening. [Century Dictionary, 1899]</blockquote>
