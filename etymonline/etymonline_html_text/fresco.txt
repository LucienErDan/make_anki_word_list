Sense of &quot;new, recent&quot; is from c. 1300; that of &quot;not stale or worn&quot; is from early 14c.; of memories from mid-14c. The metathesis, and the expanded Middle English senses of &quot;new,&quot; &quot;pure,&quot; &quot;eager&quot; probably are by influence of (or from) Old French <span class="foreign">fres</span> (fem. <span class="foreign">fresche</span>; Modern French <span class="foreign">frais</span> &quot;fresh, cool&quot;), which is from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*frisko-</span>, and thus related to the English word. The Germanic root also is the source of Italian and Spanish <span class="foreign">fresco</span>. <blockquote>This word has been connected with Greek forms such as [<span class="foreign">kotylē</span>] &quot;bowl, dish.&quot; Yet the Greek word is no perfect formal match, and words for types of vessels are very often loanwords. It seems best to assume this for <span class="foreign">catinus</span> too. [de Vaan]</blockquote>
<p>One of the few Latin loan-words in Proto-Germanic, along with <span class="foreign">*punda-</span> &quot;measure of weight or money&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">pound</span> (n.1)) and a word relating to &quot;merchant&quot; that yielded <span class="crossreference">cheap</span> (adj.). &quot;[I]t is striking that all have something to do with trade&quot; [Don Ringe, &quot;From Proto-Indo-European to Proto-Germanic,&quot; Oxford 2006]. Perhaps the Latin word was confused with a native Germanic one.</p>

<p>Spelling with a <span class="foreign">-k-</span> (c. 1300) probably is from influence of Old Norse cognate <span class="foreign">ketill</span>. The smaller sense of &quot;tea-kettle&quot; is attested by 1769.</p>

Perhaps it is rather a variant of <span class="foreign">kittle</span>/<span class="foreign">kiddle</span> &quot;weir or fence with nets set in rivers or along seacoasts for catching fish&quot; (c. 1200, in the Magna Charta as Anglo-Latin <span class="foreign">kidellus</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">quidel</span>, probably from Breton <span class="foreign">kidel</span> &quot;a
