from Middle French <span class="foreign">constructif</span> or directly from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">constructivus</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">construct-</span>, past-participle stem of <span class="foreign">construere</span> &quot;to heap up,&quot; from assimilated form of <span class="foreign">com</span> &quot;with, together&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">con-</span>) + <span class="foreign">struere</span> &quot;to pile up&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*stere-</span> &quot;to spread&quot;).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;pertaining to construction&quot; is from 1817; sense of &quot;having the quality of constructing&quot; is from 1841, especially &quot;contributing helpfully.&quot; Related: <span class="foreign">Constructively; constructiveness</span>. <span class="foreign">Constructive criticism</span> is attested by 1841, originally in theology and philosophy.</p>
<blockquote>Constructive criticism has frequently secured, in various departments of scientific inquiry, positive results, the value of which cannot be over-estimated; but there are not wanting instances in which a destructively critical method has performed services equally as valuable. Groundless hypotheses, unwarrantable theories, and baseless prejudices, required to be swept away, so that a constructive criticism might operate freely and successfully. [&quot;The Christian Ambassador,&quot; vol. ix, 1871]</blockquote>
<p>It later was extended to education and became personal:</p>
<blockquote>Constructive criticism points out a specific deficiency, and suggests a specific remedy. It is destructive in tearing down the wrong, but constructive in replacing value. Such criticism will afford the teacher the satisfaction of having a definite basis on which to work. <p>As a type of what is trifling or unimportant, attested from late 13c. Meaning &quot;hollow tube through which a drink is sucked&quot; is recorded from 1851. To <span class="foreign">draw straws</span> as a means of deciding something is recorded from 1779 (the custom probably is older). As an adjective, &quot;made of straw,&quot; mid-15c.; hence &quot;false, sham.&quot; <span class="foreign">Straw poll</span> is from 1932; earlier <span class="foreign">straw vote</span> (1866). <span class="foreign">Straw hat</span> first attested mid-15c. To <span class="foreign">clutch</span> (or <span class="foreign">grasp</span> or <span class="foreign">catch</span>) <span class="foreign">at straws</span> (1748) is what a drowning man proverbially would do. The <span class="foreign">last straw</span> (1836 apart from the full phrase) is from the proverbial image: &quot;it is the last straw that breaks the camel&#x27;s back&quot; (or, less often, the mare&#x27;s, the horse&#x27;s, or the elephant&#x27;s), an image in use in English by 1755.</p>
<blockquote>Let it not, however, be inferred that taxation cannot be pushed too far : it is, as the Oriental proverb says, the last straw that overloads the camel ; a small addition, if ill-timed, may overturn the whole.
