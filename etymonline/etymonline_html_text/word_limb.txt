<p>The unetymological <span class="foreign">-b</span> began to appear late 1500s for no etymological reason (perhaps by influence of <span class="crossreference">limb</span> (n.2)). The Old English plural was often <span class="foreign">limu</span>; <span class="foreign">limen</span> and other plural forms in <span class="foreign">-n</span> lasted into Middle English. Since c. 1400 especially of a leg; in Victorian English this usage was somewhat euphemistic, &quot;out of affected or prudish unwillingness to use the word <span class="foreign">leg</span>&quot; [Century Dictionary]. However in Old and Middle English, and until lately in dialects, it could mean &quot;any visible body part&quot;:</p>
<blockquote>The lymmes of generacion were shewed manyfestly. [Caxton, &quot;The subtyl historyes and fables of Esope, Auyan, Alfonce, and Poge,&quot; 1484]</blockquote>
<p>Hence, <span class="foreign">limb-lifter</span> &quot;fornicator&quot; (1570s). <span class="foreign">Limb of the law</span> was 18c. derisive slang for a lawyer or police officer. To <span class="foreign"><strong>go out on a limb</strong></span> in figurative sense &quot;enter a risky situation&quot; is from 1897. Alliterative <span class="foreign"><strong>life and limb</strong></span> in reference to the body inclusively is from c. 1200.
