<p>Especially used for burning heretics (emblematic of this from 1550s), so that phrase <span class="foreign">fire and faggot</span> was used to indicate &quot;punishment of a heretic.&quot; Heretics who recanted were required to wear an embroidered figure of a faggot on the sleeve as an emblem and reminder of what they deserved.</p>

<p>The near-universal European word now, it appeared in Europe as Latin <span class="foreign">catta</span> (Martial, c. 75 C.E.), Byzantine Greek <span class="foreign">katta</span> (c. 350) and was in general use on the continent by c. 700, replacing Latin <span class="foreign">feles</span>. Probably ultimately Afro-Asiatic (compare Nubian <span class="foreign">kadis</span>, Berber <span class="foreign">kadiska</span>, both meaning &quot;cat&quot;). Arabic <span class="foreign">qitt</span> &quot;tomcat&quot; may be from the same source. Cats were domestic in Egypt from c. 2000 B.C.E., but not a familiar household animal to classical Greeks and Romans. The nine lives have been proverbial at least since 1560s.</p>
<p>The Late Latin word also is the source of Old Irish and Gaelic <span class="foreign">cat</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">kath</span>, Breton <span class="foreign">kaz</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">gatto</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">gato</span>, French <span class="foreign">chat</span> (12c.). Independent, but ultimately from the same source are words in the Slavic group: Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kotuka</span>, <span class="foreign">kotel&#x27;a</span>, Bulgarian <span class="foreign">kotka</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">koška</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">kot</span>, along with Lithuanian <span class="foreign">katė</span> and non-Indo-European Finnish <span class="foreign">katti</span>, which is from Lithuanian.</p>
<p>Extended to lions, tigers, etc. c. 1600. As a term of contempt for a woman, from early 13c. Slang sense of &quot;prostitute&quot; is from at least c. 1400. Slang sense of &quot;fellow, guy,&quot; is from 1920, originally in African-American vernacular; narrower sense of &quot;jazz enthusiast&quot; is recorded from 1931.</p>
