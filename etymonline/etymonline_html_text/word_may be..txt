<p>The modern verb <span class="foreign">to be</span> in its entirety represents the merger of two once-distinct verbs, the &quot;b-root&quot; represented by <span class="foreign">be</span> and the <span class="foreign">am/was</span> verb, which was itself a conglomerate. Roger Lass (&quot;Old English&quot;) describes the verb as &quot;a collection of semantically related paradigm fragments,&quot; while Weekley calls it &quot;an accidental conglomeration from the different Old English dial[ect]s.&quot; It is the most irregular verb in Modern English and the most common. Collective in all Germanic languages, it has eight different forms in Modern English:</p>
<p><strong>BE</strong> (infinitive, subjunctive, imperative); <strong>AM</strong> (present 1st person singular); <strong>ARE</strong> (present 2nd person singular and all plural); <strong>IS</strong> (present 3rd person singular); <strong>WAS</strong> (past 1st and 3rd persons singular); <strong>WERE</strong> (past 2nd person singular, all plural; subjunctive); <strong>BEING</strong> (progressive &amp; present participle; gerund); <strong>BEEN</strong> (perfect participle).</p>
<p>The paradigm in Old English was: <span class="foreign">eom, beo</span> (present 1st person singular); <span class="foreign">eart, bist</span> (present 2nd person singular); <span class="foreign">is, bið</span> (present 3rd person singular);  <span class="foreign">sind, sindon, beoð</span> (present plural in all persons); <span class="foreign">wæs</span> (past 1st and 3rd person singular); <span class="foreign">wære</span> (past 2nd person singular); <span class="foreign">wæron</span> (past plural in all persons); <span class="foreign">wære</span> (singular subjunctive preterit); <span class="foreign">wæren</span> (plural subjunctive preterit).</p>
<p>The &quot;b-root&quot; had no past tense in Old English, but often served as future tense of <span class="foreign">am/was</span>. In 13c. it took the place of the infinitive, participle and imperative forms of <span class="foreign">am/was</span>. Later its plural forms (<span class="foreign">we beth, ye ben, they be</span>) became standard in Middle English and it made inroads into the singular (<span class="foreign">I be, thou beest, he beth</span>), but forms of <span class="foreign">are</span> claimed this turf in the 1500s and replaced <span class="foreign">be</span> in the plural. For the origin and evolution of the <span class="foreign">am/was</span> branches of this tangle, see <span class="crossreference">am</span> and <span class="crossreference">was</span>.</p>

<br/><br/>
The Old English prefix also was used to make transitive verbs and as a privative prefix (as in <span class="foreign">behead</span>). The sense &quot;on all sides, all about&quot; naturally grew to include intensive uses (as in <span class="foreign">bespatter</span> &quot;spatter about,&quot; therefore &quot;spatter very much,&quot; <span class="foreign">besprinkle</span>, etc.). <span class="foreign">Be-</span> also can be causative, or have just about any sense required.
