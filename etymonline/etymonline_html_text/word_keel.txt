<p>OED and Middle English Compendium say this word is separate from the <span class="foreign">keel</span> that means &quot;a strong, clumsy boat, barge&quot; (c. 1200), which might be instead from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">kiel</span> &quot;ship&quot; (cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">ceol</span> &quot;ship&#x27;s prow,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">kiel</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kiel</span> &quot;ship&quot;). But the two words have influenced each other or partly merged, and Barnhart calls them cognates. <span class="foreign">Keel</span> still is used locally for &quot;flat-bottomed boat&quot; in the U.S. and England, especially on the Tyne.</p>
