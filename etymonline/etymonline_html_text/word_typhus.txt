title="Origin and meaning of typhus" href="/word/typhus#etymonline_v_18891"><em>typhus</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>acute infectious fever, usually accompanied by prostration, delirium, and small reddish spots, 1785, from medical Latin, from Greek <span class="foreign">typhos</span> &quot;stupor caused by fever,&quot; literally &quot;smoke,&quot; from <span class="foreign">typhein</span> &quot;to smoke,&quot; related to <span class="foreign">typhos</span> &quot;blind,&quot; <span class="foreign">typhon</span> &quot;whirlwind,&quot; from PIE <span <blockquote>The Greek term [typhos] <span class="foreign">(smoke, mist, fog)</span> was employed by Hippocrates to define a confused state of the intellect, with a tendency to stupor <span class="foreign">(stupor attonitus)</span>; and in this sense it is aptly applied to typhus fever with its slow cerebration and drowsy stupor. Boissier de Sauvages first (in 1760) called this fever &quot;typhus,&quot; and the name was adopted by Cullen of Edinburgh in 1769. Previous to the time of de Sauvages typhus was known as &quot;Pestilential&quot; or &quot;Putrid Fever,&quot; or by some name suggested by the eruption, or expressive of the locality in which it appeared, as &quot;Camp,&quot; &quot;Jail,&quot; &quot;Hospital,&quot; or &quot;Ship Fever&quot; (Murchison). [Thomas Clifford, ed., &quot;A System of Medicine,&quot; New York, 1897]</blockquote>

<blockquote>concerning which Touffon ye are to vnderstand, that in the East Indies often times, there are not stormes as in other countreys; but euery 10. or 12. yeeres there are such tempests and stormes, that it is a thing incredible, but to those that haue seene it, neither do they know certainly what yeere they wil come. [&quot;The voyage and trauell of M. Caesar Fredericke, Marchant of Venice, into the East India, and beyond the Indies&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>This sense of the word, in reference to titanic storms in the East Indies, first appears in Europe in Portuguese in the mid-16th century. It apparently is from <span class="foreign">tufan</span>, a word in Arabic, Persian, and Hindi meaning &quot;big cyclonic storm.&quot; Yule [&quot;Hobson-Jobson,&quot; London, 1903] writes that &quot;the probability is that Vasco [da Gama] and his followers got the <span class="foreign">tufao</span> ... direct from the Arab pilots.&quot;</p>
<p>The Arabic word sometimes is said to be from Greek <span class="foreign">typhon</span>, but other sources consider it purely Semitic, though the Greek word might have influenced the form of the word in English. <span class="foreign">Al-tufan</span> occurs several times in the Koran for &quot;a flood or storm&quot; and also for Noah&#x27;s Flood. Chinese (Cantonese) <span class="foreign">tai fung</span> &quot;a great wind&quot; also might have influenced the form or sense of the word in English, and that term and the Indian one may have had some mutual influence; <span class="foreign">toofan</span> still means &quot;big storm&quot; in India.</p>
