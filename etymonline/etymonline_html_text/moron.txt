and meaning of moron" href="/word/moron#etymonline_v_18371"><em>moron</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1910, medical Latin, &quot;one of the highest class of feeble-minded persons,&quot; from Greek (Attic) <span class="foreign">mōron</span>, neuter of <span class="foreign">mōros</span> &quot;foolish, dull, sluggish, stupid,&quot; a word of uncertain origin. The former connection with Sanskrit <span class="foreign">murah</span> &quot;idiotic&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">moratorium</span>) is in doubt. Latin <span class="foreign">morus</span> &quot;foolish&quot; is a loan-word from Greek.</p>
<p>Adopted by the American Association for the Study of the Feeble-minded with a technical definition &quot;adult with a mental age between 8 and 12;&quot; used as an insult since 1922 and subsequently dropped from technical use. Linnæus had introduced <span class="foreign">morisis</span> &quot;idiocy.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>The feeble-minded may be divided into: (1) Those who are totally arrested before the age of three so that they show the attainment of a two-year-old child or less; these are the idiots. (2) Those so retarded that they become permanently arrested between the ages of three and seven; these are imbeciles. (3) Those so retarded that they become arrested between the ages of seven and twelve; these were formerly called feeble-minded, the same term that is applied to the whole group. We are now proposing to call them morons, this word being the Greek for &quot;fool.&quot; The English word &quot;fool&quot; as formerly used describes exactly this grade of child--one who is deficient in judgment or sense. [Henry H. A Biblical word, originally used for a wide-spreading shade tree with fig-like fruit (<span class="foreign">Ficus sycomorus</span>) common in Egypt, Palestine, Syria, etc., whose leaves somewhat resemble those of the mulberry; applied in English from 1580s to a large species of European maple (also <span class="foreign">plane-tree</span>), perhaps because both it and the Biblical tree were notable for their shadiness (the Holy Family took refuge under a sycamore on the flight to Egypt), and from 1814 to the North American shade tree that also is called a <span class="crossreference">buttonwood</span>, which was introduced to Europe from Virginia 1637 by John Tradescant the Younger).
<br/><br/>
Spelling apparently influenced by <span class="foreign">sycamine</span> &quot;black mulberry tree,&quot; which is from Greek <span class="foreign">sykcaminos</span>, which also is mentioned in the Bible (Luke xvii.6).
