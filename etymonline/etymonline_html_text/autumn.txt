class="foreign">autumnus</span> (also <span class="foreign">auctumnus</span>, perhaps influenced by <span class="foreign">auctus</span> &quot;increase&quot;), which is of unknown origin. Perhaps from Etruscan, but Tucker suggests a meaning &quot;drying-up season&quot; and a root in <span class="foreign">*auq-</span> (which would suggest the form in <span class="foreign">-c-</span> was the original) and compares archaic English <span class="foreign">sere-month</span> &quot;August.&quot; De Vaan writes, &quot;Although &#x27;summer&#x27;, &#x27;winter&#x27; and &#x27;spring&#x27; are inherited IE words in Latin, a foreign origin of autumnus is conceivable, since we cannot reconstruct a PIE word for &#x27;autumn&#x27;&quot;.</p>

<p><span class="crossreference">Harvest</span> (n.) was the English name for the season until <span class="foreign">autumn</span> began to displace it 16c. Astronomically, from the descending equinox to the winter solstice; in Britain, the season is popularly August through October; in U.S., September through November. Compare Italian <span class="foreign">autunno</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">otoño</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">outono</span>, all from the Latin word.</p>

<p>As de Vaan notes, autumn&#x27;s names across the Indo-European languages leave no evidence that there ever was a common word for it.
