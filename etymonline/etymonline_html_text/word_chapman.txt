title="Origin and meaning of chapman" href="/word/chapman#etymonline_v_47006"><em>chapman</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;peddler, itinerant tradesman,&quot; Middle English form of Old English <span class="foreign">ceapman</span> &quot;tradesman,&quot; from West Germanic compound <span class="foreign">*kaupman-</span> (source also of Old High German <span class="foreign">choufman</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kauffman</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">koopman</span>), formed with equivalents of <span class="crossreference">man</span> (n.) + West Germanic <span class="foreign">*kaup-</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">cop</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">kap</span> &quot;trade, purchase,&quot; Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">coop</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">koop</span> &quot;trade, market, bargain,&quot; <span class="foreign">kauf</span> &quot;trader,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">ceap</span> &quot;barter, business; a purchase&quot;).</p>
<p>This is from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kaupōn-</span> (source also of Danish <span class="foreign">kjøb</span> &quot;purchase, bargain,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">kaup</span> &quot;bargain, pay;&quot; compare also Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">kupiti</span> &quot;to buy,&quot; a Germanic loan-word), probably an early Germanic borrowing (Boutkan says 1c. C.E.) from Latin <span class="foreign">caupo</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">cauponis</span>) &quot;petty tradesman, huckster, peddler,&quot; which is of unknown origin.</p>
<p>Compare <span class="crossreference">cheap</span> (adj.). In Middle English and later, <span class="foreign">chapman</span> also could mean &quot;a customer, purchaser.&quot; In a c. 1200 work the Devil is <span class="foreign">þe chapmon of helle</span> as &quot;the purchaser of souls.&quot; </p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/chapman#etymonline_v_47006"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of chap" href="/word/chap#etymonline_v_8423">chap (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1570s, &quot;customer,&quot; short for obsolete <span class="crossreference">chapman</span> in its secondary sense &quot;purchaser, trader&quot; (also see <span class="crossreference">cheap</span>). Compare, from the same borrowing, German <span class="foreign">kaufen</span> &quot;to buy,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">kaupa</span> &quot;to bargain, barter,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">kaupon</span> &quot;to traffic, trade.&quot;</p>
<p>The sense evolution is from the noun meaning &quot;a barter, a purchase&quot; to &quot;a purchase as rated by the buyer,&quot; hence the adjectival meaning &quot;inexpensive,&quot; the main modern sense, via Middle English phrases such as <span class="foreign">god chep</span> &quot;favorable bargain&quot; (12c., a translation of French <span class="foreign">a bon marché</span>).</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;lightly esteemed, common&quot; is from 1590s (compare similar evolution of Latin <span class="foreign">vilis</span>). The meaning &quot;low in price&quot; was represented in Old English by <span class="foreign">undeor</span>, literally &quot;un-dear&quot; (but <span class="foreign">deop ceap</span>, literally &quot;deep cheap,&quot; meant &quot;high price&quot;).</p>
<p>The word also was used in Old English for &quot;market&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">ceapdæg</span> &quot;market day&quot;), a sense surviving in place names <span class="foreign">Cheapside</span>, <span class="foreign">East Cheap</span>, etc. To do something <span class="foreign">on the cheap</span> &quot;with very little expense&quot; is from 1859. <span class="foreign">Cheap shot</span> originally was U.S. football jargon for a head-on tackle; extended sense &quot;unfair hit&quot; in politics, etc. is by 1970.</p>
<p>German <span class="foreign">billig</span> &quot;cheap&quot; is from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">billik</span>, originally &quot;fair, just,&quot; with a sense evolution via <span class="foreign">billiger preis</span> &quot;fair price,&quot;
