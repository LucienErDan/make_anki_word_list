title="Origin and meaning of mulberry" href="/word/mulberry#etymonline_v_19233"><em>mulberry</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;tree of the genus <span class="foreign">Morus</span>;&quot; mid-14c. in reference to a berry from the tree; an alteration of <span class="foreign">morberie</span> (13c.) from or cognate with Middle High German <span <p>The Latin word probably is from Greek <span class="foreign">moron</span> &quot;mulberry,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*moro-</span> &quot;blackberry, mulberry&quot; (source also of Armenian <span class="foreign">mor</span> &quot;blackberry,&quot; Middle Irish <span class="foreign">merenn</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">merwydden</span> &quot;mulberry&quot;). As a color-name by 1837. The children&#x27;s singing game with a chorus beginning &quot;Here we go round the mulberry bush&quot; is attested by 1820s, first in Scotland.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mulberry#etymonline_v_19233"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of sycamore" href="/word/sycamore#etymonline_v_22506">sycamore (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">mid-14c., <span class="foreign">sicamour</span> &quot;mulberry-leaved fig tree,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">sicamor</span>, <span class="foreign">sagremore</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">sycomorus</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">sykomoros</span> &quot;African fig-tree,&quot; literally &quot;fig-mulberry,&quot; from <span class="foreign">sykon</span> &quot;fig&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">fig</span>) + <span class="foreign">moron</span>  (see <span class="crossreference">mulberry</span>). But according to many sources this is more likely a folk-etymology of Hebrew <span class="foreign">shiqmah</span> &quot;mulberry.&quot;
<br/><br/>
A Biblical word, originally used for a wide-spreading shade tree with fig-like fruit (<span class="foreign">Ficus sycomorus</span>) common in Egypt, Palestine, Syria, etc., whose leaves somewhat resemble those of the mulberry; applied in English from 1580s to a large species of European maple (also <span class="foreign">plane-tree</span>), perhaps because both it and the Biblical tree were notable for their shadiness (the Holy Family took refuge under a sycamore on the flight to Egypt), and from 1814 to the North American shade tree that also is called a <span class="crossreference">buttonwood</span>, which was introduced to Europe from Virginia 1637 by John Tradescant the Younger).
<br/><br/>
Spelling apparently influenced by <span class="foreign">sycamine</span> &quot;black mulberry tree,&quot; which is from Greek <span class="foreign">sykcaminos</span>, which also is mentioned in the Bible (Luke xvii.6). For the sake of clarity, some writers have used the more Hellenic <span class="foreign">sycomore</span> in reference to the Biblical tree.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/sycamore#etymonline_v_22506"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Morus" href="/word/Morus#etymonline_v_45097">Morus (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">genus of mulberry trees, from Latin <span class="foreign">morus</span> &quot;mulberry tree.&quot;</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Morus#etymonline_v_45097"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of arboricide" href="/word/arboricide#etymonline_v_52393">arboricide (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;wanton destruction of trees,&quot; 1853, from Latin <span class="foreign">arbor</span> &quot;tree&quot; + ending from <span class="crossreference">suicide</span>, etc. Meaning &quot;one who wantonly cuts down trees&quot; is from 1873. <p>Extension of the word to the shrub itself is from 1520s. The leaves or sprigs were woven as wreaths for conquerors or poets, hence &quot;honorary crown or garland bestowed as a prize for victory or excellence&quot; (1560s). <span class="foreign"><strong>Bay-leaf</strong></span> is from 1630s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Bay-berry</strong></span> (1570s) was coined after the sense of the original word had shifted to the tree.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/bay#etymonline_v_5346"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of silk" href="/word/silk#etymonline_v_23515">silk (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, from Old English <span class="foreign">seoloc</span>, <span class="foreign">sioloc</span> &quot;silk, silken cloth,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">sericum</span> &quot;silk,&quot; plural <span class="foreign">serica</span> &quot;silken garments, silks,&quot; literally &quot;Seric stuff,&quot; neuter of <span class="foreign">Sericus</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">Serikos</span> &quot;silken; pertaining to the <span class="foreign">Seres</span>,&quot; an oriental people of Asia from whom the Greeks got silks. Western cultivation began 552 C.E., when agents from Byzantium impersonating monks smuggled silkworms and mulberry leaves out of China. Chinese <span class="foreign">si</span> &quot;silk,&quot; Manchurian <span class="foreign">sirghe</span>, Mongolian <span class="foreign">sirkek</span> have been compared to this and the people name in Greek might be a rendering via Mongolian of the Chinese word for &quot;silk,&quot; but this is uncertain.</p>
<p>Also found in Old Norse as <span class="foreign">silki</span> but not elsewhere in Germanic. The more common Germanic form is represented by Middle English <span class="foreign">say</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">seie</span>, with Spanish <span class="foreign">seda</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">seta</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">zijde</span>, German <span class="foreign">Seide</span> is from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">seta</span> &quot;silk,&quot; perhaps elliptical for <span class="foreign">seta serica</span>, or else a particular use of <span class="foreign">seta</span> &quot;bristle, hair&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">seta</span> (n.)).</p>
<p>According to some sources [Buck, OED], the use of <span class="foreign">-l-</span> instead of <span class="foreign">-r-</span> in the Balto-Slavic form of the word (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">šelku</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šilkai</span>) passed into English via the Baltic trade and may reflect a Chinese dialectal form, or a Slavic alteration of the Greek word. But the Slavic linguist Vasmer dismisses that, based on the initial <span class="foreign">sh-</span> in the Slavic words, and suggests the Slavic words are from Scandinavian rather than the reverse.</p>
<p>As an adjective from mid-14c. In reference to the &quot;hair&quot; of corn, 1660s, American English (<span class="foreign">corn-silk</span> is from 1861). Figurative use of <span class="foreign">silk-stocking</span> (n.) is from 1590s; as an adjective meaning &quot;wealthy&quot; it is attested from 1798, American English (silk stockings, especially worn
