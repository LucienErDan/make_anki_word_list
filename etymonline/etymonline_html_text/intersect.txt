<p>It has largely displaced Old English <span class="foreign">ceorfan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">carve</span> (v.)), <span class="foreign">snian</span>, and <span class="foreign">scieran</span> (see <span class="crossreference">shear</span>). <p>From early 14c. as &quot;to make or fashion by cutting or carving.&quot; From c. 1400 as &quot;to intersect or cross.&quot; From early 15c. as &quot;abridge or shorten by omitting a part.&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to wound the sensibilities of&quot; is from 1580s (to <span class="foreign">cut the heart</span> in the same sense is attested from early 14c.). Sense of &quot;sever connection or relations with&quot; is from 1630s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to be absent without excuse&quot; is British university slang from 1794. Colloquial or slang sense of &quot;move off with directness and rapidity&quot; is from 1580s. <p>Meaning &quot;to dilute, adulterate&quot; (liquor, etc.) is by 1930. Colloquial sense of &quot;to divide or share&quot; is by 1928, perhaps an image from meat-carving at table. As a director&#x27;s call to halt recording or performing, by 1931 (in an article about Pete, the bulldog with the black-ringed eye in the Hal Roach studios shorts, who was said to know the word). <p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut down</strong></span> is from late 14c. as &quot;to fell;&quot; by 1821 as &quot;to slay&quot; (as with a sword); 1857 as &quot;to curtail.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (someone or something) down to size</strong></span> is from 1821 as &quot;reduce to suitable dimensions;&quot; the figurative sense, &quot;reduce to the proper level of importance,&quot; is by 1927.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut in</strong></span> &quot;enter suddenly and unceremoniously&quot; is from 1610s; sense of &quot;suddenly join in conversation, interrupt&quot; is by 1830. <p>In nautical use to <span class="foreign"><strong>cut a feather</strong></span> (1620s) is to move so fast as to make water foam under the bow. <p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut the teeth</strong></span> &quot;have the teeth grow through the gums&quot; as an infant is from 1670s.
