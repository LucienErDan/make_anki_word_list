originally a contracted past participle of <span class="foreign">fættian</span> &quot;to cram, stuff,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*faitida</span> &quot;fatted,&quot; from verb <span class="foreign">*faitjan</span> &quot;to fatten,&quot; from <span class="foreign">*faita-</span> &quot;plump, fat&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fatt</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">feitr</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vet</span>, German <span class="foreign">feist</span> &quot;fat&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*poid-</span> &quot;to abound in water, milk, fat, etc.&quot; <p>Meaning &quot;abounding in comforts, prosperous&quot; is late 14c. Teen slang meaning &quot;attractive, up to date&quot; (also later <span class="crossreference">phat</span>) is attested from 1951. <span class="foreign">Fat cat</span> &quot;privileged and rich person&quot; is from 1928; <span class="foreign"><strong>fat chance</strong></span> &quot;no chance at all&quot; attested from 1905, perhaps ironic (the expression is found earlier in the sense &quot;good opportunity&quot;). <span class="foreign"><strong>Fathead</strong></span> is from 1842; <span class="foreign">fat-witted</span> is from 1590s; <span class="foreign"><strong>fatso</strong></span> is first recorded 1943. Expression <span class="foreign"><strong>the fat is in the fire</strong></span> originally meant &quot;the plan has failed&quot; (1560s).</p>

<p>Transferred sense, in reference to persons, &quot;full of confidence in one&#x27;s knowledge or judgment, made certain in reference to a matter or thing,&quot; from mid-14c. (also a sense in Latin). Meaning &quot;established as true beyond doubt&quot; in English is from c. 1400. Meaning &quot;indefinite, not specifically named, known but not described&quot; is from late 14c.</p>
<blockquote>Different as this seems to be from sense I, it is hardly separable from it in a large number of examples: thus, in [<span class="foreign">a certain hour</span>], the <span class="foreign">hour</span> was quite &#x27;certain&#x27; or &#x27;fixed&#x27;, but it is not communicated to the reader; to him it remains, so far as his knowledge is concerned, quite indefinite; it may have been, <span class="foreign">as far as he knows</span>, at any hour; though, <span class="foreign">as a fact</span>, it was at a particular hour. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Lewis &amp; Short write that Latin <span class="foreign">certus</span> also was sometimes indefinite, &quot;of things, the certainty of whose existence is given, but whose nature is not more definitely designated, or comes not into consideration ....&quot;</p>
<p>Hence the euphemistic use, attested from mid-18c., as in <span class="foreign">woman of a certain age</span> &quot;an old maid;&quot; <span class="foreign">woman of a certain description</span> &quot;disreputable woman;&quot; <span class="foreign">in a certain condition</span> &quot;pregnant;&quot; <span class="foreign">a certain disease</span> &quot;venereal disease;&quot; <span class="foreign">of a certain weight</span> &quot;obese.&quot; Used with proper names from 1785, &quot;often conveying a slight shade of disdain&quot; [OED]. <span class="foreign">Certainer</span>, <span class="foreign">certainest</span> were common to c. 1750, but have fallen from proper use for some reason.
