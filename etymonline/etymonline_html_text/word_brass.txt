and meaning of brass" href="/word/brass#etymonline_v_17060"><em>brass</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;yellow malleable alloy metal, harder than copper,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">bræs</span> &quot;brass, bronze,&quot; originally any alloy of copper, in England usually with tin (this is now called <span class="crossreference">bronze</span>), later and in modern use an alloy of roughly two parts copper to one part zinc. A mystery word, with no known cognates beyond English. Perhaps akin to French <span class="foreign">brasser</span> &quot;to brew,&quot; because it is an alloy. It also has been compared to Old Swedish <span class="foreign">brasa</span> &quot;fire,&quot; but no sure connection can be made. Yet another theory connects it with Latin <span class="foreign">ferrum</span> &quot;iron,&quot; itself of obscure origin.</p>
<p>Words for &quot;brass&quot; in other languages (such as German <span class="foreign">Messing</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">mæsling</span>, French <span class="foreign">laiton</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">ottone</span>) also tend to be difficult to explain. As brass was unknown in early antiquity (it was well-known to Strabo, 1c., but not mentioned by Homer), the use of the English word in Bible translations, etc., likely means &quot;bronze.&quot; The Romans were the first to deliberately make it.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">When works of Greek and Roman antiquity in &#x27;brass&#x27; began to be critically examined, and their material discriminated, the Italian word for &#x27;brass&#x27; (<span class="foreign">bronzo</span>, <span class="foreign">bronze</span>) came into use to distinguish this &#x27;ancient brass&#x27; from the current allot.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[OED]</blockquote>
<p>Rhetorically or figuratively it was the common type of hardness, durability, or obduracy since late 14c. The meaning &quot;effrontery, impudence, excessive assurance&quot; is from 1620s. Slang sense of &quot;high officials&quot; is first recorded 1899, from their insignia. Meaning &quot;brass musical instruments of a band&quot; is from 1832.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/brass#etymonline_v_17060"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of brass" href="/word/brass#etymonline_v_53024"><em>brass</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;made of brass,&quot; c. 1400, from <span class="crossreference">brass</span> (n.). Compare <span class="crossreference">brazen</span> (adj.). Slang <span class="foreign">brass balls</span> &quot;toughness, courage&quot; (emphatically combining two words that serve as metaphors for the same thing) is attested by 1960s. <span class="foreign">Brass-band</span> is from 1827.</p>

<p>The figurative <span class="foreign"><strong>brass tacks</strong></span> &quot;essentials of a matter&quot; that you get <span class="foreign">down to</span> (1897, popular from c. 1910) perhaps are the ones said to have been nailed to the counters of a dry goods stores and used to measure cloth, suggesting precision, but the metaphor was unclear from the start, and brass tacks or nails in late 19c. were commonly noted as being used in upholstering. A 1911 advertisement begins &quot; &#x27;Getting down to brass tacks&#x27; is a characteristic American slang phrase, full of suggestion but of obscure origin.&quot;</p>

<p>The figurative <span class="foreign"><strong>brass monkey</strong></span> that suffers anatomical loss in freezing weather is attested by 1843:</p>

<blockquote>Old Knites was as cool as a cucumber, and would have been so, independent of the weather, which, as he expressed it, was cold enough to freeze the nose off a brass monkey. [&quot;An Incident of the Canadian Rebellion,&quot; in The Worcester Magazine, June, 1843]</blockquote>

Also from French are Dutch <span class="foreign">brons</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bronze</span>, etc., and ultimately from the Medieval Latin word are Spanish <span class="foreign">bronce</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">bronza</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">bronc</span>, Albanian <span class="foreign">brunze</span>, etc.</p>
