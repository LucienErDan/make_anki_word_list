<blockquote>Zoologically speaking, there are no native rabbits in the United States; they are all hares. When it appears it is almost always applied to the so-called Belgian hare, which, curiously enough, is not a hare at all, but a true rabbit. [Mencken, &quot;The American Language&quot;]</blockquote>

<p><span class="foreign">Rabbit punch</span> &quot;chop on the back of the neck&quot; so called from resemblance to a gamekeeper&#x27;s method of dispatching an injured rabbit. Pulling rabbits from a hat as a conjurer&#x27;s trick recorded by 1843. <span class="foreign">Rabbit&#x27;s foot</span> &quot;good luck charm&quot; first attested 1879, in U.S. Southern black culture. The word perhaps is from Iberian Celtic (classical writers say it is Hispanic).</p>
<p>Middle English had two forms: <span class="foreign">cony, conny</span>, also <span class="foreign">coning, cunin, conyng</span>; Old French had <span class="foreign">conil</span> alongside <span class="foreign">conin</span>. Apparently the plural form <span class="foreign">conis</span> (from <span class="foreign">conil</span>, with the <span class="foreign">-l-</span> elided) was taken into English and regularly single-ized as <span class="foreign">cony</span>. The Old French form in <span class="foreign">-n</span> was borrowed in Dutch (<span class="foreign">konijn</span>) and German (<span class="foreign">Kaninchen</span>, a diminutive), and is preserved in the surname <span class="foreign">Cunningham</span> (from a place-name in Ayrshire). Rabbits not being native to northern Europe, there was no Germanic word for them.</p>
<p><span class="crossreference">Rabbit</span> arose 14c. to mean the young of the species, but gradually pushed out the older word 19c., after British slang picked up <span class="foreign">coney</span> as a punning synonym for <span class="foreign">cunny</span> &quot;cunt&quot; (compare <span class="foreign">connyfogle</span> &quot;to deceive (a woman) in order to win sexual favors&quot;). The word was in the King James Bible (Proverbs xxx.26, etc.), however, so it couldn&#x27;t be entirely dropped, and the solution was to change the pronunciation of the original short vowel (rhyming with <span class="foreign">honey, money</span>) to rhyme with <span class="foreign">bony, stony</span>. In the Old Testament, the word translates Hebrew <span class="foreign">shaphan</span> &quot;rock-badger.&quot;</p>
<p>Association with &quot;cheating&quot; is from <span class="foreign">coney-catcher</span>, &quot;A term made famous by [Robert] Greene in 1591, and in great vogue for 60 years after&quot; [OED]</p>
<blockquote>CONY-CATCHER. A sharper, or cheat. Minshew has well expressed the origin of the term: A <span class="foreign">conie-catcher</span>, a name given to deceivers, by a metaphor, taken from those that rob warrens, and conie-grounds, using all means, sleights, and cunning to deceive them, as pitching of haies before their holes, fetching them in by tumblers, &amp;c. [Nares, &quot;Glossary&quot;]</blockquote>
