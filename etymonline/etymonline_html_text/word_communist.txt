title="Origin and meaning of communist" href="/word/communist#etymonline_v_28437"><em>communist</em> </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1841, as both a noun and adjective, from French <span class="foreign">communiste</span>, from <span class="foreign">commun</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">comun</span> &quot;common, general, free, open, public;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">common</span> (adj.)) + <span class="foreign">-iste</span> (see <span class="crossreference">-ist</span>). First attested in writing by John Goodwin Barmby (1820-1881), British Owenite and utopian socialist who founded the London Communist Propaganda Society in 1841. Main modern sense, &quot;an opponent of capitalism or supporter of revolutionary leftism,&quot; emerged after publication of Communist Manifesto (<span class="foreign">&quot;Manifest der Kommunistischen Partei&quot;</span>) in 1848.</p>
<blockquote>All communists without exception propose that the people as a whole, or some particular division of the people, as a village or commune, should own all the means of production--land, houses, factories, railroads, canals, etc.; that production should be carried on in common; and that officers, selected in one way or another, should distribute among the inhabitants the fruits of their labor. [Richard T. Ely, &quot;French and German Socialism in Modern Times,&quot; New York, 1883] </blockquote>
<p> Shortened form <span class="foreign">Commie</span> is attested from 1939. <p>Originally a theory of society. As the name of a political or economic  theory which rests upon the abolition of the right of private property, especially the means of production and distribution, and seeks the overthrow of capitalism by revolutions, it is attested from 1850, a translation of German <span class="foreign">Kommunismus</span> (itself from French), in Marx and Engels&#x27; &quot;Manifesto of the Communist Party.&quot; Compare <span class="crossreference">communist</span>. </p>
<p>By 1919 and through and mid-20c. it was a general a term of abuse for revolutionaries, implying anti-social criminality without regard to political theory.</p>
<blockquote>Each [i.e. <span class="foreign">socialism, communism, anarchism</span>] stands for a state of things, or a striving after it, that differs much from that which we know; &amp; for many of us, especially those who are comfortably at home in the world as it is, they have consequently come to be the positive, comparative, &amp; superlative, distinguished not in kind but in degree only, of the terms of abuse applicable to those who would disturb our peace.
