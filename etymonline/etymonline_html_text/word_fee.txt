words, one from Old English, one from an Old French form of the same Germanic word, and both ultimately from a PIE root meaning &quot;cattle.&quot;</p>
<p>The Old English word is <span class="foreign">feoh</span> &quot;livestock, cattle; movable property; possessions in livestock, goods, or money; riches, treasure, wealth; money as a medium of exchange or payment,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fehu</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fehu</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fihu</span>, German <span class="foreign">Vieh</span> &quot;cattle,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">faihu</span> &quot;money, fortune&quot;). This is from PIE <span class="foreign">*peku-</span> &quot;cattle&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pasu</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">pekus</span> &quot;cattle;&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">pecu</span> &quot;cattle,&quot; <span class="foreign">pecunia</span> &quot;money, property&quot;).</p>
<p>The other word is Anglo-French <span class="foreign">fee</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">fieu</span>, a variant of <span class="foreign">fief</span> &quot;possession, holding, domain; feudal duties, payment&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">fief</span>), which apparently is a Germanic compound in which the first element is cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">feoh</span>.</p>
<p>Via Anglo-French come the legal senses &quot;estate in land or tenements held on condition of feudal homage; land, property, possession&quot; (c. 1300). Hence <span class="foreign">fee-simple</span> (late 14c.) &quot;absolute ownership,&quot; as opposed to <span class="foreign">fee-tail</span> (early 15c.) &quot;entailed ownership,&quot; inheritance limited to some particular class of heirs (second element from Old French <span class="foreign">taillir</span> &quot;to cut, to limit&quot;).</p>
<p>The feudal sense was extended from landholdings to inheritable offices of service to a feudal lord (late 14c.; in Anglo-French late 13c.), for example <span class="foreign">forester of fe</span> &quot;a forester by heritable right.&quot; As these often were offices of profit, the word came to be used for &quot;remuneration for service in office&quot; (late 14c.), hence, &quot;payment for (any kind of) work or services&quot; (late 14c.).
