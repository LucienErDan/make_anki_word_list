and meaning of face" href="/word/face#etymonline_v_1056"><em>face</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;the human face, a face; facial appearance or expression; likeness, image,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">face</span> &quot;face, countenance, look, appearance&quot; (12c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*facia</span> (source also of Italian <span class="foreign">faccia</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">facies</span> &quot;appearance, form, figure,&quot; and secondarily &quot;visage, countenance,&quot; which probably is literally &quot;form imposed on something&quot; and related to <span class="foreign">facere</span> &quot;to make&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*dhe-</span> &quot;to set, put&quot;).</p>
<p>Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">andwlita</span> &quot;face, countenance&quot; (from root of <span class="foreign">wlitan</span> &quot;to see, look&quot;) and <span class="foreign">ansyn</span>, <span class="foreign">ansien</span>, the usual word (from the root of <span class="foreign">seon</span> &quot;see&quot;). Words for &quot;face&quot; in Indo-European commonly are based on the notion of &quot;appearance, look,&quot; and are mostly derivatives from verbs for &quot;to see, look&quot; (as with the Old English words, Greek <span class="foreign">prosopon</span>, literally &quot;toward-look,&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">veidas</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*weid-</span> &quot;to see,&quot; etc.). But in some cases, as here, the word for &quot;face&quot; means &quot;form, shape.&quot; In French, the use of <span class="foreign">face</span> for &quot;front of the head&quot; was given up 17c. and replaced by <span class="foreign">visage</span> (older <span class="foreign">vis</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">visus</span> &quot;sight.&quot;</p>
<p>From late 14c. as &quot;outward appearance (as contrasted to some other reality);&quot; also from late 14c. as &quot;forward part or front of anything;&quot; also &quot;surface (of the earth or sea), extent (of a city).&quot; Typographical sense of &quot;part of the type which forms the letter&quot; is from 1680s.</p>
<blockquote>Whan she cometh hoom, she raumpeth in my face And crieth &#x27;false coward.&#x27; [Chaucer, &quot;Monk&#x27;s Tale&quot;]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Face to face</strong></span> is from mid-14c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Face time</strong></span> is attested from 1990. To <span class="foreign"><strong>lose face</strong></span> &quot;lose prestige&quot; (1835), is from Chinese <span class="foreign">tu lien</span>; hence also <span class="foreign"><strong>save face</strong></span> (1915). To <span class="foreign"><strong>show (one&#x27;s) face</strong></span> &quot;make or put in an appearance&quot; is from mid-14c. (<span class="foreign">shewen the face</span>). To <span class="foreign"><strong>make a face</strong></span> &quot;change the appearance of the face in disgust, mockery, etc.&quot; is from 1560s. <span class="foreign">Two faces under one hood</span> as a figure of duplicity is attested from mid-15c.</p>
