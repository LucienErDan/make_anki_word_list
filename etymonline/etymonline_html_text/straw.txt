&quot;stems or stalks of certain species of grains,&quot; apparently literally &quot;that which is scattered or strewn,&quot; related to <span class="foreign">streowian</span> (see <span class="crossreference">strew</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*straw-</span> &quot;that which is scattered&quot; (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">stra</span>, Danish <p>As a type of what is trifling or unimportant, attested from late 13c. Meaning &quot;hollow tube through which a drink is sucked&quot; is recorded from 1851. To <span class="foreign">draw straws</span> as a means of deciding something is recorded from 1779 (the custom probably is older). As an adjective, &quot;made of straw,&quot; mid-15c.; hence &quot;false, sham.&quot; <span class="foreign">Straw poll</span> is from 1932; earlier <span class="foreign">straw vote</span> (1866). <span class="foreign">Straw hat</span> first attested mid-15c. To <span class="foreign">clutch</span> (or <span class="foreign">grasp</span> or <span class="foreign">catch</span>) <span class="foreign">at straws</span> (1748) is what a drowning man proverbially would do. The <span class="foreign">last straw</span> (1836 apart from the full phrase) is from the proverbial image: &quot;it is the last straw that breaks the camel&#x27;s back&quot; (or, less often, the mare&#x27;s, the horse&#x27;s, or the elephant&#x27;s), an image in use in English by 1755.</p>
<blockquote>Let it not, however, be inferred that taxation cannot be pushed too far : it is, as the Oriental proverb says, the last straw that overloads the camel ; a small addition, if ill-timed, may overturn the whole.
