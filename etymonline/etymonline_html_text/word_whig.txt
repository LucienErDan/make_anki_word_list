and meaning of Whig" href="/word/Whig#etymonline_v_7952"><em>Whig</em> </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>British political party, 1657, in part perhaps a disparaging use of <span class="foreign">whigg</span> &quot;a country bumpkin&quot; (1640s); but mainly a shortened form of <span class="foreign">Whiggamore</span> (1649) &quot;one of the adherents of the Presbyterian cause in western Scotland who marched on Edinburgh in 1648 to oppose Charles I.&quot; Perhaps originally &quot;a horse drover,&quot; from dialectal verb <span class="foreign">whig</span> &quot;to urge forward&quot; + <span class="crossreference">mare</span>. In 1689 the name was first used in reference to members of the British political party that opposed the Tories. American Revolution sense of &quot;colonist who opposes Crown policies&quot; is from 1768. Later it was applied to opponents of Andrew Jackson (as early as 1825), and taken as the name of a political party (1834) that merged into the Republican Party in 1854-56.</p>
<blockquote>[I]n the spring of 1834 Jackson&#x27;s opponents adopted the name Whig, traditional term for critics of executive usurpations. James Watson Webb, editor of the New York <span class="foreign">Courier and Enquirer</span>, encouraged use of the name. [Henry] Clay gave it national currency in a speech on April 14, 1834, likening &quot;the whigs of the present day&quot; to those who had resisted George III, and by summer it was official. [Daniel Walker Howe, &quot;What Hath God Wrought,&quot; 2007, p.390]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Whig historian</span> is recorded from 1924.
