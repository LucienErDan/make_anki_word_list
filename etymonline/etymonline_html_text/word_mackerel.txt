title="Origin and meaning of mackerel" href="/word/mackerel#etymonline_v_2142"><em>mackerel</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>edible fish of the North Atlantic (<span class="foreign">Scomber scombrus</span>), c. 1300, from Old French <span class="foreign">maquerel</span> &quot;mackerel&quot; (Modern French <span class="foreign">maquereau</span>), of unknown origin; perhaps so called from the dark blotches with which the fish is marked, from Latin <span class="foreign">macula</span> &quot;spot, stain&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">macula</span>). But the word is apparently identical with Old French <span class="foreign">maquerel</span> &quot;pimp, procurer, broker, agent, intermediary&quot; (itself attested in English in this sense by early 15c.), a word from a Germanic source (compare Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">makelaer</span> &quot;broker,&quot; from Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mek</span> &quot;marriage,&quot; from <span class="foreign">maken</span> &quot;to make&quot;).</p>

<p>The connection would be obscure, but medieval people had imaginative notions about the erotic habits of beasts. The fish approach the shore in shoals in summertime to spawn. Exclamation <span class="foreign">holy mackerel</span> is attested from 1876.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mackerel#etymonline_v_2142"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of tunny" href="/word/tunny#etymonline_v_18818">tunny (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">large sea-fish of the mackerel order, 1520s, probably from Middle French <span class="foreign">thon</span> (14c.), from Old Provençal <span class="foreign">ton</span> and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">thunnus</span> &quot;a tuna, tunny,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">thynnos</span> &quot;a tuna, tunny,&quot; possibly with a literal sense of &quot;darter,&quot; from <span class="foreign">thynein</span> &quot;dart along.&quot;</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/tunny#etymonline_v_18818"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of scad" href="/word/scad#etymonline_v_37533">scad (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1600, Cornish name for a type of fish (also known as <span class="foreign">horse mackerel</span>) abundant on the British coast; of uncertain origin, perhaps a variant of <span class="crossreference">shad</span>. Adopted at conversion for Latin <span class="foreign">sanctus</span>.</p>
<p>Primary (pre-Christian) meaning is not possible to determine, but probably it was &quot;that must be preserved whole or intact, that cannot be transgressed or violated,&quot; and connected with Old English <span class="foreign">hal</span> (see <span class="crossreference">health</span>) and Old High German <span class="foreign">heil</span> &quot;health, happiness, good luck&quot; (source of the German salutation <span class="foreign">Heil</span>). <span class="foreign"><strong>Holy water</strong></span> was in Old English.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Holy</span> has been used as an intensifying word from 1837; in expletives since 1880s (such as <span class="foreign"><strong>holy smoke</strong></span>, 1883, <span class="foreign"><strong>holy mackerel</strong></span>, 1876, <span class="foreign"><strong>holy cow</strong></span>, 1914, <span class="foreign"><strong>holy moly</strong></span> etc.), most of them euphemisms for <span class="foreign">holy Christ</span> or <span class="foreign">holy Moses</span>. <span class="foreign"><strong>Holy Ghost</strong></span> was in Old English (in Middle English often written as one word). <span class="foreign"><strong>Holy League</strong></span> is used of various European alliances; the <span class="foreign"><strong>Holy Alliance</strong></span> was that formed personally by the sovereigns of Russia, Austria, and Prussia in 1815; it ended in 1830.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/holy#etymonline_v_12110"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of lobster" href="/word/lobster#etymonline_v_12363">lobster (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>large, long-tailed, stalk-eyed, 10-legged marine shellfish (Homarus vulgaris), early Middle English <span class="foreign">lopster</span>, <span class="foreign">lopister</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">loppestre</span> &quot;lobster,&quot; also &quot;locust,&quot; a corruption of Latin <span class="foreign">locusta</span>, <span class="foreign">lucusta</span> &quot;locust, grasshopper,&quot; which is of unknown origin. De Vaan writes that &quot;The only word similar in form and meaning is <span class="foreign">lacerta</span> &#x27;lizard; mackerel&#x27;, but there is no common preform in sight. ... [T]hey could be cognate words in the language from which Latin borrowed these forms.&quot;</p>
<p>The change of Latin <span class="foreign">-c-</span> to English <span class="foreign">-p-</span> (and, from late 14c., to <span class="foreign">-b-</span>) is unexplained; perhaps it is by influence of Old English <span class="foreign">loppe</span>, <span class="foreign">lobbe</span> &quot;spider.&quot; The ending seems to have been altered by the old fem. agent noun suffix (preserved in <span class="foreign">Baxter</span>, <span class="foreign">Webster</span>, etc.; see <span class="crossreference">-ster</span>), which approximated the sound of Latin <span class="foreign">-sta</span>.</p>
<p>OED says the Latin word originally meant &quot;lobster or some similar crustacean, the application to the locust being suggested by the resemblance in shape.&quot; Trilobite fossils in Worcestershire limestone quarries were known colloquially as <span class="foreign">locusts</span>, which seems to have been the generic word for &quot;unidentified arthropod&quot; (as <span class="foreign">apple</span> was for &quot;foreign fruit&quot;). <span class="foreign">Locusta</span> in the sense &quot;lobster&quot; also appears in Old Cornish <span class="foreign">legast</span> and French <span class="foreign">langouste</span> (12c.), now &quot;crawfish, crayfish,&quot; but in Old French both &quot;lobster&quot; and &quot;locust&quot; (a 13c. psalter has God giving over the crops of Egypt to the <span class="foreign">langoustes</span>).</p>
<p>As slang for &quot;a British soldier&quot; since 1640s, originally in reference to the jointed armor of the Roundhead cuirassiers, later (1660) to the red coat, the color of a boiled lobster.</p>
