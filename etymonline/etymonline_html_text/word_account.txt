title="Origin and meaning of account" href="/word/account#etymonline_v_125"><em>account</em> (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;counting,&quot; especially &quot;reckoning of money received and paid, detailed statement of funds owed or spent or property held,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">acont</span> &quot;(financial) account, reckoning, terminal payment,&quot; from <span class="foreign">a</span> &quot;to&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ad-</span>) + <span class="foreign">cont</span> &quot;counting, reckoning of money to be paid,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">computus</span> &quot;a calculation,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">computare</span> &quot;to count, sum up, reckon together,&quot; from <span class="foreign">com</span> &quot;with, together&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">com-</span>) + <span class="foreign">putare</span> &quot;to reckon,&quot; originally &quot;to prune,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*pau-</span> (2) &quot;to cut, strike, stamp.&quot;</p>
<p>From the first often in plural form; sometimes in late Middle English <span class="foreign">accompt</span> (see <span class="crossreference">account</span> (v.)). Meaning &quot;course of business dealings requiring records&quot; is from 1640s; hence &quot;arrangement to keep money in a business, bank, etc.&quot; (1833), also &quot;customer or client having an account&quot; (1937). <span class="foreign"><strong>Money of account</strong></span> (1690s), that used in reckoning but not circulating as coin or paper, preserves the &quot;counting&quot; sense of the word.</p>
<p>From the notion of &quot;rendering an account&quot; comes the sense &quot;statement answering for conduct&quot; (mid-14c.) and the general sense &quot;narration, recital of facts,&quot; attested by 1610s. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>by all accounts</strong></span> is attested from 1798. From the notion of &quot;statement of reasons&quot; comes <span class="foreign"><strong>on no account</strong></span> &quot;under no circumstances&quot; (1704). Also from c. 1300 in reference to answering for one&#x27;s conduct, especially at the Last Judgment. Meaning &quot;estimation, consideration,&quot; especially in the eyes of others, is from late 14c.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>On account</strong></span> in the financial sense &quot;as an item to be accounted for at the final settlement&quot; is from 1610s, hence <span class="foreign"><strong>on account of</strong></span> in the general sense &quot;for the sake of, in regard to, in consideration of&quot; (1640s, originally <span class="foreign">upon account of</span>). Also <span class="foreign">on (my, your, etc.) account</span> &quot;on (one&#x27;s) behalf.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>give accounts</strong></span> &quot;prepare or present a statement of funds and property&quot; is from mid-15c; the older term was <span class="foreign">cast accounts</span> (mid-14c.); to <span class="foreign"><strong>take account of</strong></span> originally was to make an inventory; <span class="foreign"><strong>take into account</strong></span> &quot;take account of&quot; is from 1680s.</p>
<blockquote>The spellings <span class="foreign">accompt</span>, <span class="foreign">accomptable</span>, etc. are artificial forms used, not prevailingly, in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. They are now obsolete, or nearly so, though <span class="foreign">accompt</span> and <span class="foreign">accomptant</span> may still be used in the formal or legal style. The pronunciation has always conformed to the regular spelling, <span class="foreign">account</span>, <span class="foreign">accountable</span>, etc. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/account#etymonline_v_125"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of account" href="/word/account#etymonline_v_40250"><em>account</em> (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1300, &quot;to count, enumerate,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">aconter</span> &quot;to enumerate; reckon up, render account&quot; (Modern French <span class="foreign">conter</span>), from <span class="foreign">a</span> &quot;to&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ad-</span>) + <span class="foreign">conter</span> &quot;to count, tell&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">count</span> (v.)).
