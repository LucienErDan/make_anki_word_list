<p>The Sanskrit compound is <span class="foreign">krmih</span> &quot;worm&quot; (from PIE root <span class="foreign">*kwrmi-</span> &quot;worm,&quot; source also of Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kirmis</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">cruim</span>, Albanian <span class="foreign">krimp</span> &quot;worm&quot;) + <span class="foreign">-ja-</span> &quot;produced&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gene-</span> &quot;give birth, beget&quot;). The insect lives in the Levant and southern Europe on a species of small evergreen oak (kermes oak) and in ancient Europe were the main source of red and scarlet dye. The dye is prepared from the dried bodies of pregnant females, which alive resemble small roundish grains about the size of peas and cling immobile to the tree on which they live. From this fact kermes dye was, for a long time, mistaken as being from a seed or excrescence of the tree, and the word for it in Greek was <span class="foreign">kokkos</span>, literally &quot;a grain, seed&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cocco-</span>). This was passed to Latin as <span class="foreign">coccum</span>, <span class="foreign">coccus</span> &quot;berry [sic] yielding scarlet dye,&quot; in late use &quot;scarlet color, scarlet garment.&quot;</p>
<p>So important was kermes (<span class="foreign">coccus</span>) as a commercial source of scarlet dye that derivatives of the name for it have displaced the original word for &quot;red&quot; in many languages, such as Welsh <span class="foreign">coch</span> (from Latin), Modern Greek <span class="foreign">kokkinos</span>. Also compare Russian <span class="foreign">čcermnyj</span> &quot;purple-red,&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">čruminu</span>. Compare also <span class="crossreference">crimson</span> (n.).</p>
<p>Kermes dyes have been found in burial wrappings in Anglo-Scandinavian York, but the use of kermes dyes seems to have been lost in Europe from the Dark Ages until early 15c. It fell out of use again with the introduction of <span class="crossreference">cochineal</span> (the word for which itself might be from <span class="foreign">coccus</span>) from the New World.</p>
<blockquote>Cloths dyed with kermes are of a deep red colour; and though much inferior in brilliancy to the scarlet cloths dyed with real Mexican cochineal, they retain the colour better and are less liable to stain. The tapestries of Brussels and other parts of Flanders, which have scarcely lost any thing of their original brilliancy, even after a lapse of 200 years, were all dyed with kermes. [W.T. <p>The insect (<span class="foreign">Coccus Cacti</span>) was so called from 1590s. It lives on the prickly pear cactus in Mexico and Central America and is a relative of the kermes and has similar, but more intense, dying qualities. Aztecs and other Mexican Indians used it as a dyestuff. It first is mentioned in Europe in 1523 in Spanish correspondence to Hernán Cortés in Mexico. Specimens were brought to Spain in the 1520s, and cloth merchants in Antwerp were buying cochineal in insect and powdered form in Spain by the 1540s. It soon superseded the use of kermes as a tinctorial substance.
