<blockquote>In Middle English, both the possessive singular and the common plural forms were regularly spelled <span class="foreign">es</span>, and when the <span class="foreign">e</span> was dropped in pronunciation and from the written word, the habit grew up of writing an apostrophe in place of the lost <span class="foreign">e</span> in the possessive singular to distinguish it from the plural. Later the apostrophe, which had come to be looked upon as the sign of the possessive, was carried over into the plural, but was written after the <span class="foreign">s</span> to differentiate that form from the possessive singular. By a process of popular interpretation, the <span class="foreign">&#x27;s</span> was supposed to be a contraction for <span class="foreign">his</span>, and in some cases the <span class="foreign">his</span> was actually &quot;restored.&quot; [Samuel C. <blockquote>And this is the nature of the equitable, a correction of law where it is defective owing to its universality. ... [Aristotle, &quot;Nicomachean Ethics&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>It also was used in English from 1775 in reference to wines from Lesbos. shamelessness&quot; among women (especially fellatio), but not necessarily female homosexuality, and they did not differentiate such things the way we have.</p></section></object><a data-more="true"
