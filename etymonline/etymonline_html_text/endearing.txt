<p>Possibly from Old French <span class="foreign">embuie</span> &quot;one fettered,&quot; from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*imboiare</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">boia</span> &quot;leg iron, yoke, leather collar,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">boeiai dorai</span> &quot;ox hides.&quot; (Words for &quot;boy&quot; double as &quot;servant, attendant&quot; across the Indo-European map -- compare Italian <span class="foreign">ragazzo</span>, French <span class="foreign">garçon</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">pais</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">knave</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">otroku</span> -- and often it is difficult to say which meaning came first.)</p>
<p>But it also appears to be identical with East Frisian <span class="foreign">boi</span> &quot;young gentleman,&quot; and perhaps with Dutch <span class="foreign">boef</span> &quot;knave,&quot; from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">boeve</span>, perhaps from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">buobe</span>. For a different conjecture: Used slightingly of young men in Middle English, also in familiar or contemptuous use of criminal toughs or men in the armed services. In some local uses &quot;a man,&quot; without reference to age (OED lists &quot;in Cornwall, in Ireland, in the far West of the U.S.&quot;). <blockquote>In Old English, only the proper name <span class="foreign">Boia</span> has been recorded.
