title="Origin and meaning of cucumber" href="/word/cucumber#etymonline_v_432"><em>cucumber</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;common running garden plant,&quot; cultivated from earliest times in many Old World countries, also the long, fleshy fruit of the plant, late 14c., <span class="foreign">cucomer</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">cocombre</span> (13c., Modern French <span class="foreign">concombre</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">cucumerem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">cucumis</span>), perhaps from a pre-Italic Mediterranean language. The Latin word also is the source of Italian <span class="foreign">cocomero</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">cohombro</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">cogombro</span>. Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">eorþæppla</span> (plural), literally &quot;earth-apples.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Cowcumber</span> was the common form of the word in 17c.-18c., in good literary use and representing the modern evolution of the Middle English form. <span class="foreign">Cucumber</span> is an attempted reversion to Latin. In 1790s the pronunciation &quot;cowcumber&quot; was standard except in western England dialects and &quot;coocumber&quot; was considered pedantic, but 30 years later, with the spread of literacy and education &quot;cowcumber&quot; was limited to the ignorant and old-fashioned.</p>
<p>It was planted as a garden vegetable by 1609 by Jamestown colonists. Phrase <span class="foreign">cool as a cucumber</span> (c. 1732) embodies ancient folk knowledge confirmed by science in 1970: inside of a field cucumber on a warm day is 20 degrees cooler than the air temperature.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/cucumber#etymonline_v_432"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of gherkin" href="/word/gherkin#etymonline_v_6059">gherkin (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">small cucumber used for pickling (either a small, prickly type of cucumber produced by a certain plant (<span class="foreign">Cucumis anguria</span>), or a green or immature common cucumber), 1660s, from early modern Dutch <span class="foreign">gurken</span>, <span class="foreign">augurken</span> (late 16c.) &quot;small pickled cucumber,&quot; from East Frisian <span class="foreign">augurk</span> &quot;cucumber,&quot; probably from a Balto-Slavic source (compare Polish <span class="foreign">ogórek</span> &quot;cucumber,&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">agurkas</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">oguretsŭ</span>), possibly ultimately from Medieval Greek <span class="foreign">angourion</span> &quot;a kind of cucumber,&quot; which is said to be from Persian <span class="foreign">angarah</span> [Klein, etc.], but OED seems to regard this as unlikely. A Dutch source says the Greek is from a word for &quot;immature&quot; and that the vegetable originated in northern India and came to Eastern Europe via the Byzantine Empire.
<br/><br/>
The Dutch suffix is perhaps the diminutive <span class="foreign">-kin</span>, though some regard it as a plural affix, with the Dutch word mistaken for a singular in English. The <span class="foreign">-h-</span> was added 1800s to preserve the hard &quot;g&quot; pronunciation.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/gherkin#etymonline_v_6059"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of gourd" href="/word/gourd#etymonline_v_9084">gourd (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1300, from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">gourde</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">coorde</span>, ultimately from Latin <span class="foreign">cucurbita</span> &quot;gourd,&quot; which is of uncertain origin, perhaps related to <span class="foreign">cucumis</span> &quot;cucumber.&quot; Dried and excavated, the shell was used as a scoop or dipper.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/gourd#etymonline_v_9084"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of pickle" href="/word/pickle#etymonline_v_14941">pickle (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1400, probably from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">pekel</span> &quot;pickle, brine,&quot; or related words in Low German and East Frisian (Dutch <span class="foreign">pekel</span>, East Frisian <span class="foreign">päkel</span>, German <span class="foreign">pökel</span>), of uncertain origin or original meaning. Klein suggests the name of a medieval Dutch fisherman who developed the process. Originally a sauce served with meat or fowl; meaning &quot;cucumber preserved in pickle&quot; first recorded 1707, via use of the word for the salty liquid in which meat, etc. was preserved (c. 1500). Figurative sense of &quot;sorry plight&quot; first recorded 1560s, from the time when the word still meant a sauce served on meat about to be eaten. Meaning &quot;troublesome boy&quot; is from 1788, perhaps from the notion of being &quot;imbued&quot; with roguery.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/pickle#etymonline_v_14941"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of brass" href="/word/brass#etymonline_v_53024">brass (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;made of brass,&quot; c. 1400, from <span class="crossreference">brass</span> (n.). Compare <span class="crossreference">brazen</span> (adj.). Slang <span class="foreign">brass balls</span> &quot;toughness, courage&quot; (emphatically combining two words that serve as metaphors for the same thing) is attested by 1960s. <span class="foreign">Brass-band</span> is from 1827.</p>

<p>The figurative <span class="foreign"><strong>brass tacks</strong></span> &quot;essentials of a matter&quot; that you get <span class="foreign">down to</span> (1897, popular from c. 1910) perhaps are the ones said to have been nailed to the counters of a dry goods stores and used to measure cloth, suggesting precision, but the metaphor was unclear from the start, and brass tacks or nails in late 19c. were commonly noted as being used in upholstering. A 1911 advertisement begins &quot; &#x27;Getting down to brass tacks&#x27; is a characteristic American slang phrase, full of suggestion but of obscure origin.&quot;</p>

<p>The figurative <span class="foreign"><strong>brass monkey</strong></span> that suffers anatomical loss in freezing weather is attested by 1843:</p>

<blockquote>Old Knites was as cool as a cucumber, and would have been so, independent of the weather, which, as he expressed it, was cold enough to freeze the nose off a brass monkey. [&quot;An Incident of the Canadian Rebellion,&quot; in The Worcester Magazine, June, 1843]</blockquote>

It might have come from Chinese <span class="foreign">koechiap</span> &quot;brine of fish,&quot; which, if authentic, perhaps is from the Chinese community in northern Vietnam [Terrien de Lacouperie, in &quot;Babylonian and Oriental Record,&quot; 1889, 1890]. <span class="foreign">Catsup</span> (earlier <span class="foreign">catchup</span>, 1680s) is a failed attempt at Englishing, still in use in U.S., influenced by <span class="foreign">cat</span> and <span class="foreign">sup</span>.
<br/><br/>
Originally a fish sauce made from various plant juices, the word came to be used in English for a wide variety of spiced gravies and sauces; &quot;Apicius Redivivus; or, the Cook&#x27;s Oracle,&quot; by William Kitchiner, London, 1817, devotes 7 pages to recipes for different types of catsup (his book has 1 spelling of <span class="foreign">ketchup</span>, 72 of <span class="foreign">catsup</span>), including walnut, mushroom, oyster, cockle and mussel, <span class="foreign">tomata</span>, white (vinegar and anchovies figure in it), cucumber, and pudding catsup.
