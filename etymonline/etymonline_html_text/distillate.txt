<br/><br/>
Meaning &quot;supernatural immaterial creature; angel, demon; an apparition, invisible corporeal being of an airy nature&quot; is attested from mid-14c.; from late 14c. as &quot;a ghost&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">ghost</span> (n.)). <br/><br/>
From late 14c. in alchemy as &quot;volatile substance; distillate;&quot; from c. 1500 as &quot;substance capable of uniting the fixed and the volatile elements of the philosopher&#x27;s stone.&quot; Hence <span class="foreign">spirits</span> &quot;volatile substance;&quot; sense narrowed to &quot;strong alcoholic liquor&quot; by 1670s. This also is the sense in <span class="foreign">spirit level</span> (1768). Also from mid-14c. as &quot;character, disposition; way of thinking and feeling, state of mind; source of a human desire;&quot; in Middle English <span class="foreign">freedom of spirit</span> meant &quot;freedom of choice.&quot; From late 14c. as &quot;divine substance, divine mind, God;&quot; also &quot;Christ&quot; or His divine nature; &quot;the Holy Ghost; divine power;&quot; also, &quot;extension of divine power to man; inspiration, a charismatic state; charismatic power, especially of prophecy.&quot; Also &quot;essential nature, essential quality.&quot; From 1580s in metaphoric sense &quot;animation, vitality.&quot;
<br/><br/>
According to Barnhart and OED, originally in English mainly from passages in Vulgate, where the Latin word translates Greek <span class="foreign">pneuma</span> and Hebrew <span class="foreign">ruah</span>. Distinction between &quot;soul&quot; and &quot;spirit&quot; (as &quot;seat of emotions&quot;) became current in Christian terminology (such as Greek <span class="foreign">psykhe</span> vs. <span class="foreign">pneuma</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">anima</span> vs. <span class="foreign">spiritus</span>) but &quot;is without significance for earlier periods&quot; [Buck]. Latin <span class="foreign">spiritus</span>, usually in classical Latin &quot;breath,&quot; replaces <span class="foreign">animus</span> in the sense &quot;spirit&quot; in the imperial period and
