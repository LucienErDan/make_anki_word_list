<p>Philip Cowen, first editor of &quot;The American Hebrew,&quot; suggests a source in Yiddish <span class="foreign">kikel</span> &quot;circle.&quot; According to him, Jewish immigrants, ignorant of writing with the Latin alphabet, signed their entry forms with a circle, eschewing the customary &quot;X&quot; as a sign of Christianity. <p>It corresponds to the Old English adjective <span class="foreign">þeodisc</span> &quot;belonging to the people,&quot; which was used especially of the common language of Germanic people (as opposed to Latin), a derivative of the Old English noun <span class="foreign">þeod</span> &quot;people, race, nation.&quot; The language name is first attested in Latin as <span class="foreign">theodice</span> (786 C.E.) in correspondence between Charlemagne&#x27;s court and the Pope, in reference to a synodical conference in Mercia; thus it refers to Old English. Its first use in reference to a German language (as opposed to a Germanic one) is two years later. The sense was extended from the language to the people who spoke it (in German, <span class="foreign">Diutisklant</span>, ancestor of <span class="foreign">Deutschland</span>, was in use by 13c.).</p>
<p>The sense in of the adjective in English narrowed to &quot;of the Netherlands&quot; in 17c., after they became a united, independent state and the focus of English attention and rivalry. In Holland, <span class="foreign">Duits</span> (formerly <span class="foreign">duitsch</span>) is used of the people of Germany. The old use of <span class="foreign">Dutch</span> for &quot;German&quot; continued in America (Irving and Cooper still distinguish <span class="foreign">High Dutch</span> &quot;German&quot; and <span class="foreign">Low Dutch</span> &quot;Dutch&quot;) and survives in <span class="foreign"><strong>Pennsylvania Dutch</strong></span> for the descendants of religious sects that immigrated from the Rhineland and Switzerland and their language.</p>
<p>Since c. 1600, <span class="foreign">Dutch</span> (adj.) has been a &quot;pejorative label pinned by English speakers on almost anything they regard as inferior, irregular, or contrary to &#x27;normal&#x27; (i.e., their own) practice&quot; [Rawson]. E.g. ironical <span class="foreign"><strong>Dutch treat</strong></span><span class="foreign">,</span> of each person paying for himself (1887), <span class="foreign"><strong>Dutch courage</strong></span> &quot;boldness inspired by intoxicating spirits&quot; (1809), nautical <span class="foreign"><strong>Dutch talent</strong></span> &quot;any piece of work not done in shipshape style (1867), etc. -- probably exceeded in such usage only by <span class="crossreference">Indian</span> and <span class="crossreference">Irish</span> -- reflecting first British commercial and military rivalry and later heavy German immigration to U.S.</p>
<blockquote><strong>Dutch concert</strong>, a concert in which each one sings his own song at the same time that his neighbor sings his; or a concert in which each one sings a verse of any song he pleases, some well-known chorus being sung after each verse. [Century Dictionary, 1897]</blockquote>
<blockquote>The Dutch themselves spoke English well enough to understand the unsavory connotations of the label and in 1934 Dutch officials were ordered by their government to stop using the term <span class="foreign">Dutch</span>. Instead, they were to rewrite their sentences so as to employ the official <span class="foreign">The Netherlands</span>. [Rawson]</blockquote>
