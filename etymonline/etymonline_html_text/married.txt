<p>Perhaps ultimately &quot;provided with a <span class="foreign">*mari</span>,&quot; a young woman, from PIE <span class="foreign">*mari-, *mori-</span> &quot;young wife, young woman&quot; (source also of Welsh <span class="foreign">morwyn</span> &quot;girl, maiden,&quot; Middle Welsh <span class="foreign">merch</span> &quot;daughter&quot;), akin to <span class="foreign">*meryo-</span> &quot;young man&quot; (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">marya-</span> &quot;young man, suitor&quot;).</p>
<p>By early 14c. as &quot;to take (someone) in marriage, take for a husband or wife;&quot; by late 14c. as &quot;become husband and wife according to law or custom; get married (to one another).&quot; Transitive sense, of a priest, etc., who performs the rite of marriage, &quot;to unite in wedlock or matrimony,&quot; by 1520s.</p>
<p>Figurative meaning &quot;unite intimately or by some close bond of connection&quot; is from early 15c. Related: <span class="crossreference">Married</span>; <span class="foreign">marrying</span>. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>the marrying kind</strong></span>, describing one inclined toward marriage and almost always used with a negative, is attested by 1824, probably short for <span class="foreign">marrying kind of men</span>, which is from a popular 1756 essay by Chesterfield.</p>
