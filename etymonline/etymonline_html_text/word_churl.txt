<p>It had various meaning in early Middle English, including &quot;man of the common people,&quot; &quot;a country man,&quot; &quot;husbandman,&quot; &quot;free peasant;&quot; by 1300, it meant &quot;bondman, villain,&quot; also &quot;fellow of low birth or rude manners.&quot;</p>

A word of unknown origin.</p>
<p>Possibly from Old French <span class="foreign">embuie</span> &quot;one fettered,&quot; from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*imboiare</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">boia</span> &quot;leg iron, yoke, leather collar,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">boeiai dorai</span> &quot;ox hides.&quot; (Words for &quot;boy&quot; double as &quot;servant, attendant&quot; across the Indo-European map -- compare Italian <span class="foreign">ragazzo</span>, French <span class="foreign">garçon</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">pais</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">knave</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">otroku</span> -- and often it is difficult to say which meaning came first.)</p>
<p>But it also appears to be identical with East Frisian <span class="foreign">boi</span> &quot;young gentleman,&quot; and perhaps with Dutch <span class="foreign">boef</span> &quot;knave,&quot; from Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">boeve</span>, perhaps from Middle Low German <span class="foreign">buobe</span>. This suggests a gradational relationship to <span class="crossreference">babe</span>. For a different conjecture: Used slightingly of young men in Middle English, also in familiar or contemptuous use of criminal toughs or men in the armed services. In some local uses &quot;a man,&quot; without reference to age (OED lists &quot;in Cornwall, in Ireland, in the far West of the U.S.&quot;). Meaning &quot;male negro slave or Asian personal servant of any age&quot; attested from c. 1600. Extended form <span class="foreign"><strong>boyo</strong></span> is attested from 1870. Emphatic exclamation <span class="foreign"><strong>oh, boy</strong></span> attested from 1892. <span class="foreign"><strong>Boy-meets-girl</strong></span> &quot;typical of a conventional romance&quot; is from 1945; the phrase itself is from 1934 as a dramatic formula. <span class="foreign"><strong>Boy-crazy</strong></span> &quot;eager to associate with males&quot; is from 1923.</p>
<blockquote>In Old English, only the proper name <span class="foreign">Boia</span> has been recorded.
