and meaning of lady" href="/word/lady#etymonline_v_1995"><em>lady</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, <span class="foreign">lafdi</span>, <span class="foreign">lavede</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">hlæfdige</span> (Northumbrian <span class="foreign">hlafdia</span>, Mercian <span class="foreign">hlafdie</span>), &quot;mistress of a household, wife of a lord,&quot; apparently literally &quot;one who kneads bread,&quot; from <span class="foreign">hlaf</span> &quot;bread&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">loaf</span> (n.)) + <span class="foreign">-dige</span> &quot;maid,&quot; which is related to <span class="foreign">dæge</span> &quot;maker of dough&quot; (which is the first element in <span class="foreign">dairy</span>; see <span class="crossreference">dey</span> (n.1)). Also compare <span class="crossreference">lord</span> (n.)). Century Dictionary finds this etymology &quot;improbable,&quot; and OED rates it &quot;not very plausible with regard to sense,&quot; but no one seems to have a better explanation.</p>
<p>The medial <span class="foreign">-f-</span> disappeared 14c. The word is not found outside English except where borrowed from it. Sense of &quot;woman of superior position in society&quot; is c. 1200; that of &quot;woman whose manners and sensibilities befit her for high rank in society&quot; is from 1861 (<span class="crossreference">ladylike</span> suggesting this sense is attested from 1580s, and <span class="foreign">ladily</span> from c. 1400). Meaning &quot;woman chosen as an object of chivalrous love&quot; is from early 14c. Used commonly as an address to any woman since 1890s.</p>
<p>Applied since Old English to the Holy Virgin, hence many extended usages in plant names, place names, etc., from genitive singular <span class="foreign">hlæfdigan</span>, which in Middle English merged with the nominative, so that <span class="foreign">lady-</span> often represents <span class="foreign">(Our) Lady&#x27;s</span>, as in <span class="crossreference">ladybug</span>. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lady Day</strong></span> (late 13c.) was the festival of the Annunciation of the Virgin Mary (March 25). <span class="foreign"><strong>Ladies&#x27; man</strong></span> first recorded 1784; <span class="foreign"><strong>lady-killer</strong></span> &quot;man supposed to be dangerously fascinating to women&quot; is from 1811. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lady of pleasure</strong></span> recorded from 1640s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lady&#x27;s slipper</strong></span> as a type of orchid is from 1590s.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/lady#etymonline_v_1995"><span>Relate entries &amp;
