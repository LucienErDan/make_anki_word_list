title="Origin and meaning of bronze" href="/word/bronze#etymonline_v_17157"><em>bronze</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1721, &quot;alloy of copper and (usually) a smaller amount of tin,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">bronze</span>, from Italian <span class="foreign">bronzo</span>, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">bronzium</span>, which is of uncertain origin. Perhaps cognate (via notion of color) with Venetian <span class="foreign">bronza</span> &quot;glowing coals,&quot; or German <span class="foreign">brunst</span> &quot;fire.&quot; Perhaps influenced by Latin <span class="foreign">Brundisium</span> the Italian town of Brindisi (Pliny writes of <span class="foreign">aes Brundusinum</span>). Perhaps ultimately from Persian <span class="foreign">birinj</span> &quot;copper.&quot;</p>
<p>In Middle English, the distinction between bronze (copper-tin alloy) and brass (copper-zinc alloy) was not clear, and both were called <span class="foreign">bras</span> (see <span class="crossreference">brass</span> (n.)). Used historically for bells, cannons, statuary, and fine mechanical works. Also from French are Dutch <span class="foreign">brons</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bronze</span>, etc., and ultimately from the Medieval Latin word are Spanish <span class="foreign">bronce</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">bronza</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">bronc</span>, Albanian <span class="foreign">brunze</span>, etc.</p>
<p>A <span class="foreign"><strong>bronze medal</strong></span> has been given to a third-place finisher at least since 1852. The archaeological <span class="foreign"><strong>Bronze Age</strong></span> (1850) falls between the Stone and Iron ages, and is a reference to the principal material for making weapons and ornaments.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/bronze#etymonline_v_17157"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of bronze" href="/word/bronze#etymonline_v_44272"><em>bronze</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">1640s, &quot;give the color or appearance of bronze to,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">bronzer</span> (16c.) or else from <span class="crossreference">bronze</span> (n.). Figuratively, of feelings, hearts, etc., &quot;to harden like bronze,&quot; 1726. Meaning &quot;to make to be brown or bronze in color&quot; (by exposure to the sun, etc.) is from 1792. Related: <span class="foreign">Bronzed</span>; <span class="foreign">bronzing</span>. </section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/bronze#etymonline_v_44272"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of brass" href="/word/brass#etymonline_v_17060">brass (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;yellow malleable alloy metal, harder than copper,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">bræs</span> &quot;brass, bronze,&quot; originally any alloy of copper, in England usually with tin (this is now called <span class="crossreference">bronze</span>), later and in modern use an alloy of roughly two parts copper to one part zinc. A mystery word, with no known cognates beyond English. Perhaps akin to French <span class="foreign">brasser</span> &quot;to brew,&quot; because it is an alloy. It also has been compared to Old Swedish <span class="foreign">brasa</span> &quot;fire,&quot; but no sure connection can be made. Yet another theory connects it with Latin <span class="foreign">ferrum</span> &quot;iron,&quot; itself of obscure origin.</p>
<p>Words for &quot;brass&quot; in other languages (such as German <span class="foreign">Messing</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">mæsling</span>, French <span class="foreign">laiton</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">ottone</span>) also tend to be difficult to explain. As brass was unknown in early antiquity (it was well-known to Strabo, 1c., but not mentioned by Homer), the use of the English word in Bible translations, etc., likely means &quot;bronze.&quot; The Romans were the first to deliberately make it.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">When works of Greek and Roman antiquity in &#x27;brass&#x27; began to be critically examined, and their material discriminated, the Italian word for &#x27;brass&#x27; (<span class="foreign">bronzo</span>, <span class="foreign">bronze</span>) came into use to distinguish this &#x27;ancient brass&#x27; from the current allot.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[OED]</blockquote>
<p>Rhetorically or figuratively it was the common type of hardness, durability, or obduracy since late 14c. The meaning &quot;effrontery, impudence, excessive assurance&quot; is from 1620s. Slang sense of &quot;high officials&quot; is first recorded 1899, from their insignia. Meaning &quot;brass musical instruments of a band&quot; is from 1832.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/brass#etymonline_v_17060"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of copper" href="/word/copper#etymonline_v_19058">copper (n.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>malleable metallic element, noted for its peculiar red color, tenacity, malleability, and electric conductivity, late Old English <span class="foreign">coper</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kupar</span> (source also of Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">koper</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">koparr</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kupfar</span>), from Late Latin <span class="foreign">cuprum</span>, contraction of Latin <span class="foreign">Cyprium (aes)</span> &quot;Cyprian (metal),&quot; after Greek <span class="foreign">Kyprios</span> &quot;Cyprus&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">Cyprus</span>).</p>
<p>Ancient Greek had <span class="foreign">khalkos</span> &quot;ore, copper, bronze;&quot; an old IE word for &quot;ore, copper, bronze&quot; is retained in Sanskrit <span class="foreign">ayah</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">aes</span>. Latin <span class="foreign">aes</span> originally was &quot;copper,&quot; but this was extended to its alloy with tin (see <span class="crossreference">bronze</span>), and as this was far more extensively used than pure copper, the word&#x27;s primary sense shifted to the alloy and a new word evolved for &quot;copper,&quot; from the Latin form of the name of the island of Cyprus, where copper was mined (the alchemists associated copper with Venus).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Aes</span> passed into Germanic (which originally did not distinguish copper from its alloys) and became English <span class="crossreference">ore</span>. In Latin, <span class="foreign">aes</span> was the common word for &quot;cash, coin, debt, wages&quot; in many figurative expressions. Chemical symbol <span class="foreign">Cu</span> is from <span class="foreign">cuprum</span>.</p>
<p>As &quot;a copper coin,&quot; from 1580s; as &quot;a vessel made of copper,&quot; 1660s.
