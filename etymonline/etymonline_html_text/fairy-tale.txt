<blockquote>In ordinary use an <span class="foreign">elf</span> differs from a <span class="foreign">fairy</span> only in generally seeming young, and being more often mischievous. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>But that was before Tolkien. As a type of supernatural being from late 14c. [contra Tolkien; for example &quot;This maketh that ther been no fairyes&quot; in &quot;Wife of Bath&#x27;s Tale&quot;], perhaps via intermediate forms such as <span class="foreign">fairie knight</span> &quot;supernatural or legendary knight&quot; (c. 1300), as in Spenser, where faeries are heroic and human-sized. As a name for the diminutive winged beings in children&#x27;s stories from early 17c.</p>
<blockquote>Yet I suspect that this flower-and-butterfly minuteness was also a product of &quot;rationalization,&quot; which transformed the glamour of Elfland into mere finesse, and invisibility into a fragility that could hide in a cowslip or shrink behind a blade of grass. It seems to become fashionable soon after the great voyages had begun to make the world seem too narrow to hold both men and elves; when the magic land of Hy Breasail in the West had become the mere Brazils, the land of red-dye-wood. [J.R.R. Tolkien, &quot;On Fairy-Stories,&quot; 1947]</blockquote>
<p>Hence, figurative adjective use in reference to lightness, fineness, delicacy. Slang meaning &quot;effeminate male homosexual&quot; is recorded by 1895. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fairy ring</strong></span>, of certain fungi in grass fields (as we would explain it now), is from 1590s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fairy godmother</strong></span> attested from 1820.
