and meaning of clue" href="/word/clue#etymonline_v_13857"><em>clue</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;anything that guides or directs in an intricate case,&quot; 1590s, a special use of a revised spelling of <span class="crossreference">clew</span> &quot;a ball of thread or yarn&quot; (q.v.). The word, which is native Germanic, in Middle English was <span class="foreign">clewe</span>, also <span class="foreign">cleue</span>; some words borrowed from Old French in -<span class="foreign">ue</span>, -<span class="foreign">eu</span> also were spelled <span class="foreign">-ew</span> in Middle English, such as <span class="foreign">blew, imbew,</span> but these later were reformed to <span class="foreign">-ue</span>, and this process was extended to native words (<span class="foreign">hue, true, clue</span>) which had ended in a vowel and <span class="foreign">-w</span>. The spelling <span class="foreign">clue</span> is first attested mid-15c.</p>
<p>The sense shift is originally in reference to the clew of thread given by Ariadne to Theseus to use as a guide out of the Labyrinth in Greek mythology. The purely figurative sense of &quot;that which points the way,&quot; without regard to labyrinths, is from 1620s. As something which a bewildered person does not have, by 1948.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">Thus hardy Theseus, with intrepid Feet,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Travers&#x27;d the dang&#x27;rous Labyrinth of Crete;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">But still the wandring Passes forc&#x27;d his Stay,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Till Ariadne&#x27;s Clue unwinds the Way</blockquote>
