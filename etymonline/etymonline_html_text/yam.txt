<p>One of the most stable English letters (it has almost always the same sound), it is a latecomer to the alphabet and originally had no sound value. The letter itself began as a scribal modification of Roman <span class="foreign">-i-</span> in continental Medieval Latin. The scribes added a &quot;hook&quot; to small <span class="foreign">-i-</span>, especially in the final position in a word or roman numeral, to distinguish it from the strokes of other letters. The dot on the <span class="foreign">-i-</span> (and thus the <span class="foreign">-j-</span>) and the capitalization of the pronoun <span class="crossreference">I</span> are other solutions to the same problems.</p>
<p>In English, <span class="foreign">-j-</span> was used as a roman numeral throughout Middle English, but the letter <span class="foreign">-y-</span> was used to spell words ending an &quot;i&quot; sound, so <span class="foreign">-j-</span> was not needed to represent a sound. Instead, it was introduced into English c. 1600-1640 to take up the consonantal sound that had evolved from the Roman <span class="foreign">i-</span> since Late Latin times. In Italian, <span class="foreign">g-</span> was used to represent this, but in other languages <span class="foreign">j-</span> took the job. This usage is attested earliest in Spanish, where it was in place before 1600.</p>
<blockquote>No word beginning with J is of Old English derivation. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>English dictionaries did not distinguish words beginning in <span class="foreign">-i-</span> and <span class="foreign">-j-</span> until 19c., and <span class="foreign">-j-</span> formerly was skipped when letters were used to express serial order.</p>
<p>In Latin texts printed in modern times, <span class="foreign">-j-</span> often is used to represent Latin <span class="foreign">-i-</span> before <span class="foreign">-a-</span>, <span class="foreign">-e-</span>, <span class="foreign">-o-</span>, <span class="foreign">-u-</span> in the same syllable, which in Latin was sounded as the consonant in Modern English <span class="foreign">you</span>, <span class="foreign">yam</span>, etc., but the custom has been controversial among Latinists:</p>
<blockquote>The character <span class="foreign">J</span>, <span class="foreign">j</span>, which represents the letter sound in some school-books, is an invention of the seventeenth century, and is not found in MSS., nor in the best texts of the Latin authors. [Lewis]</blockquote>
<p>In English words from Hebrew, <span class="foreign">-j-</span> represents <span class="foreign">yodh</span>, which was equivalent to English consonantal <span class="foreign">y</span> (hence <span class="foreign">hallelujah</span>) but many of the Hebrew names later were conformed in sound to the modern <span class="foreign">-j-</span> (compare <span
