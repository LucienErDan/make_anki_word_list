<blockquote>Raven mythology shows considerable homogeneity throughout the whole area [northern regions of the northern hemisphere] in spite of differences in detail. <p>Old English also used <span class="foreign">hræmn</span>, <span class="foreign">hremm</span>. The <span class="foreign">raven standard</span> was the flag of the Danish Vikings. The vikings, like Noah, were said to have used the raven to discover land. &quot;When uncertain of their course they let one loose, and steered the vessel in his track, deeming that the land lay in the direction of his flight; if he returned to the ship, it was supposed to be at a distance&quot; [Charles Swainson, &quot;The Folk Lore and Provincial Names of British Birds,&quot; London, 1886].</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/raven#etymonline_v_3411"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fret" href="/word/fret#etymonline_v_14170">fret (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">fretan</span> &quot;devour, feed upon, consume,&quot; from Proto-Germanic compound <span class="foreign">*fra-etan</span> &quot;to eat up,&quot; from <span class="foreign">*fra-</span> &quot;completely&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*per-</span> (1) &quot;forward,&quot; hence &quot;through&quot;) + <span class="foreign">*etan</span> &quot;to eat&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*ed-</span> &quot;to eat&quot;). <p>The sense evolution probably is from Vulgar Latin use of <span class="foreign">follis</span> in a sense of &quot;windbag, empty-headed person.&quot; Compare also Sanskrit <span class="foreign">vatula-</span> &quot;insane,&quot; literally &quot;windy, inflated with wind.&quot; But some sources suggest evolution from Latin <span class="foreign">folles</span> &quot;puffed cheeks&quot; (of a buffoon), a secondary sense from plural of <span class="foreign">follis</span>. <blockquote>The word has in mod.Eng. a much stronger sense than it had at an earlier period; it has now an implication of insulting contempt which does not in the same degree belong to any of its synonyms, or to the derivative <span class="foreign">foolish</span>. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>Also used in Middle English for &quot;sinner, rascal, impious person&quot; (late 13c.). The French word probably also got into English via its borrowing in the Scandinavian languages of the vikings (Old Norse <span class="foreign">fol</span>, Old Danish <span class="foreign">fool</span>, <span class="foreign">fol</span>).</p>
<blockquote>There is no foole to the olde foole [&quot;Proverbs of John Heywood,&quot; 1546]</blockquote>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>make a fool of</strong></span> (someone) &quot;cause to appear ridiculous&quot; is from 1620s (<span class="foreign">make fool</span> &quot;to deceive, make (someone) appear a fool&quot; is from early 15c.). <span class="foreign"><strong>Feast of Fools</strong></span> (early 14c., from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">festum stultorum</span>) was the burlesque festival celebrated in some churches on New Year&#x27;s Day in medieval times. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fool&#x27;s gold</strong></span> &quot;iron pyrite&quot; is from 1829. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fool&#x27;s paradise</strong></span> &quot;illusory state of happiness&quot; is from mid-15c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fool-trap</strong></span> is from 1690s. <span class="foreign">Foolosopher</span>, a useful insult, is in a 1549 translation of Erasmus. <span class="foreign">Fool&#x27;s ballocks</span> is described in OED as &quot;an old name&quot; for the green-winged orchid. <span class="foreign">Fool-killer</span> &quot;imaginary personage invested with authority to put to death anybody notoriously guilty of great folly&quot; is from 1851, American English.</p>
<blockquote><strong>Fool killer</strong>, a great American myth imagined by editors, who feign that his or its services are greatly needed, and frequently alluded to as being &quot;around&quot; or &quot;in town&quot; when some special act of folly calls for castigation. Whether the fool-killer be an individual or an instrument cannot always be gathered from the dark phraseology in which he or it is alluded to; but the weight of authority would sanction the impersonal interpretation. [Walsh, &quot;Handy-Book of Literary Curiosities,&quot; 1892]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fool#etymonline_v_11755"><span>Relate
