<p>The English word may be in part from Old Norse mythological <span class="foreign">Hel</span> (from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*halija</span> &quot;one who covers up or hides something&quot;), in Norse mythology the name of Loki&#x27;s daughter who rules over the evil dead in <span class="foreign">Niflheim</span>, the lowest of all worlds (<span class="foreign">nifl</span> &quot;mist&quot;). A pagan concept and word fitted to a Christian idiom. In Middle English, also of the <span class="foreign">Limbus Patrum</span>, place where the Patriarchs, Prophets, etc. awaited the Atonement. Used in the KJV for Old Testament Hebrew <span class="foreign">Sheol</span> and New Testament Greek <span class="foreign">Hades</span>, <span class="foreign">Gehenna</span>. Used figuratively for &quot;state of misery, any bad experience&quot; at least since late 14c. As an expression of disgust, etc., first recorded 1670s.</p>
