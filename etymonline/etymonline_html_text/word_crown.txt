and meaning of crown" href="/word/crown#etymonline_v_396"><em>crown</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 12c., <span class="foreign">coroune, croune</span>, &quot;royal crown, ornament for the head as a symbol of sovereignty,&quot; from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">coroune</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">corone</span> (13c., Modern French <span class="foreign">couronne</span>) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">corona</span> &quot;crown,&quot; originally &quot;wreath, garland,&quot; related to Greek <span class="foreign">korōnē</span> &quot;anything curved, a kind of crown,&quot; according to Watkins from a suffixed form of PIE root <span class="crossreference">*sker-</span> (2) &quot;to turn, bend.&quot;</p>

<p>But Beekes considers the &quot;crown&quot; sense as derived from the formally identical Greek word <span class="foreign">korōnē</span> &quot;crow&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">raven</span>), which, he says, was used metaphorically &quot;of all kinds of curved or hook-formed objects.&quot; &quot;Moreover,&quot; he writes, &quot;the metaphorical use of <span class="foreign">[korōnē</span>] &#x27;crow&#x27; is nothing remarkable given the use of its cognates ...; the metaphors may have originated from the shape of the beak or the claws of the bird.&quot; Compare Latin <span class="foreign">corax</span> &quot;crow,&quot; also &quot;a hooked engine of war,&quot; French <span class="foreign">corbeau</span> &quot;raven,&quot; also &quot;cantilever;&quot; English <span class="crossreference">crowbar</span>, etc. </p>

<p>Old English used <span class="foreign">corona</span>, directly from Latin. Figuratively, &quot;regal power,&quot; from c. 1200. From late 14c. as &quot;a crowning honor or distinction.&quot; From c. 1300 as &quot;top part of the skull or head;&quot; from 1670s as &quot;top of a hat.&quot; From 1804 as &quot;part of a tooth which appears above the gum.&quot;</p>

<p>Extended late 14c. to &quot;coin bearing the imprint of a crown or a crowned head,&quot; especially the British silver 5-shilling piece.
