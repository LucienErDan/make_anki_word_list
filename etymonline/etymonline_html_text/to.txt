and meaning of to" href="/word/to#etymonline_v_15316">to (prep.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">to</span> &quot;in the direction of, for the purpose of, furthermore,&quot; from West Germanic <span class="foreign">*to</span> (source also of Old Saxon and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">to</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">toe</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">zuo</span>, German <span class="foreign">zu</span> &quot;to&quot;), from PIE pronominal base <span class="foreign">*do-</span> &quot;to, toward, upward&quot; (source also of Latin <span class="foreign">donec</span> &quot;as long as,&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">do</span> &quot;as far as, to,&quot; Greek suffix <span Not found in Scandinavian, where the equivalent of <span class="crossreference">till</span> (prep.) is used.</p>
<p>The nearly universal use of <span class="foreign">to</span> with infinitives (<span class="foreign">to sleep, to dream</span>, etc.) arose in Middle English out of the Old English dative use of <span class="foreign">to</span>, and it helped drive out the Old English inflectional endings (though in this use <span class="foreign">to</span> itself is a mere sign, without meaning).</p>
<p>Commonly used as a prefix in Middle English (<span class="foreign">to-hear</span> &quot;listen to,&quot; etc.), but few of these survive (<span class="foreign">to-do</span>, <span class="foreign">together</span>, and time references such as <span class="foreign">today</span>, <span class="foreign">tonight</span>, <span class="foreign">tomorrow</span> -- Chaucer also has <span class="foreign">to-yeere</span>). <span class="foreign">To and fro</span> &quot;side to side&quot; is attested from mid-14c. Phrase <span class="foreign">what&#x27;s it to you</span> &quot;how does that concern you?&quot; (1819) is a modern form of an old question:</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">Huæd is ðec ðæs?</blockquote>
