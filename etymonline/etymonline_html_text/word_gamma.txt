Alphabetic writing came to Rome via the southern Etruscan &quot;Caeretan&quot; script, in which <span class="foreign">gamma</span> was written as a crescent. Early Romans made little use of Greek <span class="foreign">kappa</span> and used <span class="foreign">gamma</span> for both the &quot;g&quot; and &quot;k&quot; sounds, the latter more frequently, so that the &quot;k&quot; sound came to be seen as the proper one for <span class="foreign">gamma</span>. Classical Latin <span class="foreign">-c-</span>, with only the value &quot;k,&quot; passed to Celtic and, via missionary Irish monks, to the Anglo-Saxons.</p>

<p>In some Old English words, before some vowels and in certain positions, <span class="foreign">-c-</span> had a &quot;ts&quot; sound that was respelled <span class="foreign">ch-</span> in Middle English by French scribes (<span class="crossreference">chest</span>, <span class="crossreference">cheese</span>, <span class="crossreference">church</span>; see <span class="crossreference">ch</span>). In Old English <span class="foreign">-k-</span> was known but little used.</p>

<p>Meanwhile, in Old French, many &quot;k&quot; sounds drifted to &quot;ts&quot; and by 13c., &quot;s,&quot; but still were written <span class="foreign">-c-</span>. Thus the 1066 invasion brought to the English language a flood of French and Latin words in which <span class="foreign">-c-</span> represented &quot;s&quot; (as in <span class="crossreference">cease</span>, <span class="crossreference">ceiling</span>, <span class="crossreference">circle</span>) and a more vigorous use of <span class="foreign">-k-</span> to distinguish that sound. By 15c. even native English words with <span class="foreign">-s-</span> were being respelled with <span class="foreign">-c-</span> for &quot;s&quot; (<span class="crossreference">ice</span>, <span class="foreign">mice</span>, <span class="foreign">lice</span>).</p>

<p>In some English words from Italian, the <span class="foreign">-c-</span> has a &quot;ch&quot; sound (via a sound evolution somewhat like the Old French one). In German, <span class="foreign">-c-</span> in loanwords was regularized to <span class="foreign">-k-</span> or <span class="foreign">-z-</span> (depending on pronunciation) in the international spelling reform of 1901, which was based on the Duden guide of 1880.</p>

<p>As a symbol in the Roman numeral system, &quot;one hundred;&quot; the symbol originally was a Greek theta, but was later reduced in form and understood to stand for <span class="foreign">centum</span>. The distinction is to avoid confusion with &amp; in such formations as <span class="foreign">&amp;c.</span>, a once common way of writing <span class="foreign">etc.</span> (the <span class="foreign">et</span> in <span class="foreign">et cetera</span> is Latin for &quot;and&quot;). <br/><br/>
The symbol is based on the Latin word <span class="foreign">et</span> &quot;and,&quot; and comes from an old Roman system of shorthand signs (<span class="foreign">ligatures</span>) attested in Pompeiian graffiti, and not (as sometimes stated) from the <span class="foreign">Tironian Notes</span>, which was a different form of shorthand, probably invented by Cicero&#x27;s companion Marcus Tullius Tiro, which used a different symbol, something like a reversed capital gamma, to indicate <span class="foreign">et</span>. This Tironian symbol was maintained by some medieval scribes, including Anglo-Saxon chroniclers, who sprinkled their works with a symbol like a numeral <span class="foreign">7</span> to indicate the word <span class="foreign">and</span>.
<br/><br/>

<br/><br/>
It is similar to the Hyades but more distant, about 600 light-years away, consists of about 1,000 stars, mostly older, the brightest of them around magnitude 6.5, thus not discernible to the naked eye even on the clearest nights, but their collective light makes a visible fuzz of glow that the ancients likened to a cloud (the original <span class="crossreference">nebula</span>); Galileo was the first to resolve it into stars (1609). <br/><br/>
&quot;Manger&quot; to the Romans perhaps by influence of two nearby stars, Gamma and Delta Cancri, dim and unspectacular but both for some reason figuring largely in ancient astrology and weather forecasting, and known as &quot;the Asses&quot; (Latin <span class="foreign">Aselli</span>), supposedly those of Silenus.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Praesepe#etymonline_v_47332"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of K" href="/word/K#etymonline_v_1757">K </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>eleventh Roman letter, from Greek <span class="foreign">kappa</span>, from Phoenician <span class="foreign">kaph</span> or a similar Semitic source, said to mean literally &quot;hollow of the hand&quot; and to be so called for its shape.</p>
<p>Little used in classical Latin, which at an early age conformed most of its words (the exceptions had ritual importance) to a spelling using <span class="foreign">-c-</span> (a character derived from Greek gamma). In Late Latin, pronunciation of <span class="foreign">-c-</span> shifted (in the direction of &quot;s&quot;). Greek names brought into Latin also were regularized with a <span class="foreign">-c-</span> spelling, and then underwent the Late Latin sound-shift; hence the modern pronunciation of <span class="foreign">Cyrus</span>, <span class="foreign">Circe</span>. To keep their pronunciation clear, the many Greek words (often Church words) that entered Latin after this shift tended to take Latin <span class="foreign">-k-</span> for Greek kappa.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">K-</span> thus became a supplementary letter to <span class="foreign">-c-</span> in Medieval Latin, used with Greek and foreign words. But most of the languages descended from Latin had little need of it, having evolved other solutions to the sound shifts.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">K-</span> also was scarce in Old English. After the Norman conquest, new scribal habits restricted <span class="foreign">-c-</span> and expanded the use of <span class="foreign">-k-</span>, which began to be common in English spelling from 13c. This probably was done because the sound value of <span class="foreign">-c-</span> was evolving in French and the other letter was available to clearly mark the &quot;k&quot; sound for scribes working in English. For more, see <span class="crossreference">C</span>.</p>
<p>In words transliterated from Arabic, Hebrew, Turkish, Japanese, Hawaiian, etc., it represents several different sounds lumped. In modern use some of them are now with <span class="crossreference">kh-</span>; in older borrowings they often followed traditional English spelling and were written with a <span class="foreign">C-</span> (<span class="foreign">Corea</span>, <span class="foreign">Caaba</span>, etc.).</p>
<p>As a symbol for <span class="crossreference">potassium</span>, it represents Latin <span class="foreign">kalium</span> &quot;potash.&quot; In <span class="foreign">CMYK</span> as a color system for commercial printing it means &quot;black&quot; but seems to stand for <span class="foreign">key</span> in a specialized printing sense. Slang meaning &quot;one thousand dollars&quot; is 1970s, from <span class="crossreference">kilo-</span>. <span class="foreign">K</span> as a measure of capacity (especially in computer memory) meaning &quot;one thousand&quot; also is an abbreviation of <span class="crossreference">kilo-</span>.</p>
<p>As an indication of &quot;strikeout&quot; in baseball score-keeping it dates from 1874 and is said to represent the last letter of <span class="foreign">struck</span>. The invention of the scorecard symbols is attributed to English-born U.S. newspaperman Henry Chadwick (1824-1908) principally of the old New York &quot;Clipper,&quot; who had been writing baseball since 1858, and who explained it thus:</p>
