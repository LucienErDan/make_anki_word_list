<p>In early use often in reference to the borderlands beside Wales, sometimes rendering Old English <span class="foreign">Mercia</span>; later especially of the English border with Scotland. There was a verb <span class="foreign">marchen</span> in Middle English (c. 1300), &quot;to have a common boundary,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">marchier</span> &quot;border upon, lie alongside,&quot; which survived in dialect.</p>
<p>This is the old Germanic word for &quot;border, boundary,&quot; but as it came to mean &quot;borderland&quot; in many languages, other words were shifted or borrowed to indicate the original sense (compare <span class="crossreference">border</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">bound</span> (n.)&quot;border, boundary&quot;). <p>If not etymologically related to <span class="foreign">board</span> (n.1), the two forms represented in English by these words were nonetheless confused at an early date in most Germanic languages, a situation made worse in English because this Germanic word also was adopted in Medieval Latin as <span class="foreign">bordus</span> (source of Italian and Spanish <span class="foreign">bordo</span>) and entered Old French as <span class="foreign">bort</span> &quot;beam, board, plank; side of a ship&quot; (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">bord</span>), via either Medieval Latin or Frankish, and from thence it came over with the Normans to mingle with its native cousins. By now the senses are inextricably tangled. Some etymology dictionaries treat them as having been the same word all along.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>go by the board</strong></span> originally was &quot;fall overboard&quot; (1757), of a mast, etc., hence, generally, &quot;be completely lost or destroyed&quot; (1835).
