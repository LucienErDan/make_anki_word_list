class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Old English <span class="foreign">understandan</span> &quot;comprehend, grasp the idea of,&quot; probably literally &quot;stand in the midst of,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">under</span> + <span class="foreign">standan</span> &quot;to stand&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">stand</span> (v.)). If this is the meaning, the <span class="foreign">under</span> is not the usual word meaning &quot;beneath,&quot; but from Old English <span class="foreign">under</span>, from PIE <span class="foreign">*nter-</span> &quot;between, among&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">antar</span> &quot;among, between,&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">inter</span> &quot;between, among,&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">entera</span> &quot;intestines;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">inter-</span>). Related: <span class="foreign">Understood</span>; <span class="crossreference">understanding</span>.
<br/><br/>
That is the suggestion in Barnhart, but other sources regard the &quot;among, between, before, in the presence of&quot; sense of Old English prefix and preposition <span class="foreign">under</span> as other meanings of the same word. &quot;Among&quot; seems to be the sense in many Old English compounds that resemble <span class="foreign">understand</span>, such as <span class="foreign">underniman</span> &quot;to receive,&quot; <span class="foreign">undersecan</span> &quot;examine, investigate, scrutinize&quot; (literally &quot;underseek&quot;), <span class="foreign">underðencan</span> &quot;consider, change one&#x27;s mind,&quot; <span class="foreign">underginnan</span> &quot;to begin.&quot; It also seems to be the sense still in expressions such as <span class="foreign">under such circumstances</span>.
<br/><br/>
Perhaps the ultimate sense is &quot;be close to;&quot; compare Greek <span class="foreign">epistamai</span> &quot;I know how, I know,&quot; literally &quot;I stand upon.&quot; Similar formations are found in Old Frisian (<span class="foreign">understonda</span>), Middle Danish (<span class="foreign">understande</span>), while other Germanic languages use compounds meaning &quot;stand before&quot; (German <span class="foreign">verstehen</span>, represented in Old English by <span class="foreign">forstanden</span> &quot;understand,&quot; also &quot;oppose, withstand&quot;). For this concept, most Indo-European languages use figurative extensions of compounds that literally mean &quot;put together,&quot; or &quot;separate,&quot; or &quot;take, grasp&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">comprehend</span>). Old English <span class="foreign">oferstandan</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">overstonden</span>, literally &quot;over-stand&quot; seem to have been used only in literal senses.
