<p>Sense of &quot;commemoration&quot; (of someone or something) is from c. 1300. Meaning &quot;faculty of remembering; the mental capacity of retaining unconscious traces of conscious impressions or states, and of recalling these to consciousness in relation to the past,&quot; is late 14c. in English. Meaning &quot;length of time included in the consciousness or observation of an individual&quot; is from 1520s. </p>
<blockquote>I am grown old and my memory is not as active as it used to be. When I was younger I could remember anything, whether it had happened or not; but my faculties are decaying now and soon I shall be so I cannot remember any but the things that never happened. It is sad to go to pieces like this, but we all have to do it. [Mark Twain, &quot;Autobiography&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;that which is remembered; anything fixed in or recalled to the mind&quot; is by 1817, though the correctness of this use was disputed in 19c. The word was extended, with more or less of figurativeness, in 19c. to analogous physical processes. Computer sense, &quot;device which stores information,&quot; is from 1946. <p>The Romans also had <span class="foreign">trivius dea</span>, the &quot;goddess of three ways,&quot; another name for Hecate, perhaps originally in her triple aspect (Selene/Diana/Proserpine), but also as the especial divinity of crossroads (Virgil has &quot;Nocturnisque hecate triviis ululata per urbes&quot;). John Gay took this arbitrarily as the name of a goddess of streets and roads for his mock Georgic &quot;Trivia: Or, the Art of Walking the Streets of London&quot; (1716); Smith writes in his autobiography that he got the title from Gay.</p>
<blockquote>I KNOW too much; I have stuffed too many of the facts of History and Science into my intellectuals. My eyes have grown dim over books; believing in geological periods, cave dwellers, Chinese Dynasties, and the fixed stars has prematurely aged me. [&quot;Trivia,&quot; 1918 edition]</blockquote>
<p>Then noted c. 1965 as an informal fad game among college students wherein one asked questions about useless bits of information from popular culture (&quot;What was Donald Duck&#x27;s address?&quot;) and others vied to answer first.</p>
<blockquote>Nobody really wins in this game which concentrates on sports, comics and television. Everyone knows that Amos&#x27;s wife on the &quot;Amos &#x27;n&#x27; Andy Show&quot; is Ruby, but who knows that she is from Marietta, Georgia? Trivia players do. They also know the fourth man in the infield of Tinker-to-Evers-to-Chance, the Canadian who shot down Baron Von Richtofen, and can name ten Hardy Boy books. [&quot;Princeton Alumni Weekly,&quot; Nov. 9, 1965]</blockquote>
<p>The board game <span class="foreign">Trivial Pursuit</span> was released 1982 and was a craze in U.S. for several years thereafter.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/trivia#etymonline_v_17849"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of family" href="/word/family#etymonline_v_1107">family (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 15c., &quot;servants of a household,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">familia</span> &quot;family servants, domestics collectively, the servants in a household,&quot; thus also &quot;members of a household, the estate, property; the household, including relatives and servants,&quot; abstract noun formed from <span class="foreign">famulus</span> &quot;servant, slave,&quot; which is of unknown origin.</p>
<p>The Latin word rarely appears in the sense &quot;parents with their children,&quot; for which <span class="foreign">domus</span> (see <span class="crossreference">domestic</span> (adj.)) was used. Derivatives of <span class="foreign">famulus</span> include <span class="foreign">famula</span> &quot;serving woman, maid,&quot; <span class="foreign">famulanter</span> &quot;in the manner of a servant,&quot; <span class="foreign">famulitas</span> &quot;servitude,&quot; <span class="foreign">familiaris</span> &quot;of one&#x27;s household, private,&quot; <span class="foreign">familiaricus</span> &quot;of household slaves,&quot; <span class="foreign">familiaritas</span> &quot;close friendship.&quot;</p>
<p>In English, sense of &quot;collective body of persons who form one household under one head and one domestic government, including parents, children, and servants, and as sometimes used even lodgers or boarders&quot; [Century Dictionary] is from 1540s. From 1660s as &quot;parents with their children, whether they dwell together or not,&quot; also in a more general sense, &quot;persons closely related by blood, including aunts, uncles, cousins;&quot; earlier &quot;those who descend from a common progenitor, a house, a lineage&quot; (1580s). Hence, &quot;any group of things classed as kindred based on common distinguishing characteristics&quot; (1620s); as a scientific classification, between genus and order, from 1753.</p>
<p>It replaced Old English <span class="foreign">hiwscipe</span>, <span class="foreign">hiwan</span> &quot;family,&quot; cognate with Old Norse <span class="foreign">hjon</span> &quot;one of the household; married couple, man and wife; domestic servant,&quot; and with Old High German <span class="foreign">hiwo</span> &quot;husband,&quot; <span class="foreign">hiwa</span> &quot;wife,&quot; also with Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šeimyna</span> &quot;family,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">haims</span> &quot;village,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">ham</span> &quot;village, home&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">home</span> (n.)).</p>
<p>As an adjective from c. 1600; with the meaning &quot;suitable for a family,&quot; by 1807. <span class="foreign"><strong>Family values</strong></span> is recorded by 1966. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>in a family way</strong></span> &quot;pregnant&quot; is from 1796. <span class="foreign"><strong>Family circle</strong></span> is 1809; <span class="foreign"><strong>family man</strong></span> &quot;man devoted to wife and children, man inclined to lead a domestic life&quot; is 1856 (earlier it meant &quot;thief,&quot; 1788, from <span class="foreign">family</span> in a slang sense of &quot;the fraternity of thieves&quot;). <span class="foreign"><strong>Family tree</strong></span> &quot;graph of ancestral relations&quot; attested from 1752: The phrase is attested from 1844.</p>
<blockquote>I have certainly known more men destroyed by the desire to have wife and child and to keep them in comfort than I have seen destroyed by drink and harlots. [William Butler Yeats, &quot;Autobiography&quot;]</blockquote>
