<p>The name was used by Dutch cartographers (for example Mercator, 1595). OED says it is perhaps originally from Færoic <span class="foreign">mal(u)streymur</span>. The hawk so called either for its size or because it looks speckled when in flight.</p>

<p>Early firearms often were given names of beasts (compare <span class="crossreference">dragoon</span>, also <span class="foreign">falcon</span>, a kind of cannon mentioned by Hakluyt), and the equivalent word in Italian was used to mean &quot;an arrow for a crossbow.&quot; The French word had been borrowed earlier into Middle English (late 14c.; c. 1200 as a surname) in its literal sense of &quot;sparrow-hawk.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/musket#etymonline_v_19303"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of shark" href="/word/shark#etymonline_v_23352">shark (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1560s, of uncertain origin; apparently the word and the first specimen were brought to London by Capt. John Hawkins&#x27;s second expedition (landed 1565; see Hakluyt).</p>
<blockquote>There is no proper name for it that I knowe, but that sertayne men of Captayne Haukinses doth call it a &#x27;sharke&#x27; [handbill advertising an exhibition of the specimen, 1569]</blockquote>
<p>The meaning &quot;dishonest person who preys on others,&quot; though attested only from 1599 (<span class="foreign">sharker</span> &quot;artful swindler&quot; in this sense is from 1594), may be the original sense, later transferred to the large, voracious marine fish. If so, it is possibly from German <span class="foreign">Schorck</span>, a variant of <span class="foreign">Schurke</span> &quot;scoundrel, villain,&quot; agent noun of Middle High German <span class="foreign">schürgen</span> (German <span class="foreign">schüren</span>) &quot;to poke, stir.&quot;</p>
<p>But on another theory, the English word is from a Mayan word, <span class="foreign">xoc</span>, which might have meant &quot;shark.&quot; Northern Europeans seem not to have been familiar with the larger sort of sharks before voyages to the tropics began.</p>
<p>A slightly earlier name for it in English was <span class="foreign">tiburon</span>, from Spanish <span class="foreign">tiburón</span> (1520s), which probably is from a native word from South America, such as Tupi <span class="foreign">uperu</span> &quot;shark&quot; (source also of Portuguese <span class="foreign">tubarão</span>, Catalan <span class="foreign">tauró</span>). Middle English had <span class="foreign">hound-fish</span> (early 14c.), which probably was used of dogfish and other small sharks. The general Germanic word seems to be represented by Old Norse <span class="foreign">har</span> (Norwegian <span class="foreign">hai</span>, Danis <span class="foreign">haj</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">haai</span>, German <span class="foreign">Hai</span>, also borrowed in Finnish, Latvian), which is of unknown origin. French <span class="foreign">requin</span> is literally &quot;grimacer,&quot; from Norman <span class="foreign">requin</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">reschignier</span> &quot;to bare the teeth, grimace.&quot; An ancient Greek word for a shark was <span class="foreign">karkharias</span>, from <span class="foreign">karkharos</span> &quot;sharp, jagged, biting.&quot; Latin used <span class="foreign">squalus</span>, from the root of English <span class="crossreference">whale</span> (n.); Lithuanian <span class="foreign">ryklys</span> is literally &quot;swallower.&quot;</p>
<p>The English word was applied (or re-applied) to voracious or predatory persons, on the image of the fish, from 1707 (originally of pick-pockets); <span class="foreign">loan shark</span> is attested from 1905. <span class="foreign">Sharkskin</span> (1851) was used for binding books, etc. As the name of a type of fabric held to resemble it, it is recorded from 1932.</p>
