and meaning of Spenserian" href="/word/Spenserian#etymonline_v_24002">Spenserian (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">1817, from Edmund <span class="foreign">Spenser</span> (c. 1552-1599), Elizabethan poet (for the origin of the surname, see <span class="crossreference">Spencer</span>). <span class="foreign">Spenserian stanza</span>, which he employed in the &quot;Faerie Queen,&quot; consists of eight decasyllabic lines and a final Alexandrine, with rhyme scheme <span class="foreign">ab ab bc bcc</span>.
<br/><br/>
&quot;The measure soon ceases to be Spenser&#x27;s except in its mere anatomy of rhyme-arrangement&quot; [Elton, &quot;Survey of English Literature 1770-1880,&quot; 1920]; it is the meter in Butler&#x27;s &quot;Hudibras,&quot; Scott&#x27;s &quot;Lady of the Lake,&quot; and notably the &quot;Childe Harold&quot; of Byron, who found (quoting Beattie) that it allowed him to be &quot;either droll or pathetic, descriptive or sentimental, tender or satirical, as the humour strikes me; for, if I mistake not, the measure which I have adopted admits equally of all these kinds of composition.&quot;</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Spenserian#etymonline_v_24002"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of creationism" href="/word/creationism#etymonline_v_29033">creationism (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1847, originally a Christian theological position that God immediately created out of nothing a soul for each person born; from <span class="crossreference">creation</span> + <span class="crossreference">-ism</span>.</p>
<p>As &quot;science teaching based on a fundamentalist interpretation of the Book of Genesis, the scientific theory attributing the origin of matter and life to immediate acts of God,&quot; opposed to <span class="foreign">evolutionism</span>, it is attested from 1880. Century Dictionary (1897) defines <span class="foreign">creationism</span> in this sense as &quot;The doctrine that matter and all things were created, substantially as they now exist, by the fiat of an omnipotent Creator, and not gradually evolved or developed.&quot;</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Creation science</span> is attested by 1970 as an alternative name for a theory of science not inconsistent with Christian fundamentalism. <span class="foreign">Creationist</span> (n.) in an &quot;anti-Darwin&quot; sense is attested by 1859 in a letter of Darwin&#x27;s, and it is said to be used in Darwin&#x27;s unpublished writings as far back as 1842.</p>
<blockquote>James Ussher (1581-1656), Archbishop of Armagh, Primate of All Ireland, and Vice-Chancellor of Trinity College in Dublin was highly regarded in his day as a churchman and as a scholar. Of his many works, his treatise on chronology has proved the most durable. Based on an intricate correlation of Middle Eastern and Mediterranean histories and Holy writ, it was incorporated into an authorized version of the Bible printed in 1701, and thus came to be regarded with almost as much unquestioning reverence as the Bible itself. Having established the first day of creation as Sunday 23 October 4004 B.C. ... Ussher calculated the dates of other biblical events, concluding, for example, that Adam and Eve were driven from Paradise on Monday 10 November 4004 BC, and that the ark touched down on Mt Ararat on 5 May 1491 BC &quot;on a Wednesday&quot;. [Craig, G.Y., and E.J. Jones, &quot;A Geological Miscellany,&quot; Princeton University Press, 1982.]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/creationism#etymonline_v_29033"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fagus" href="/word/fagus#etymonline_v_50457">fagus (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>botanical genus of beech trees, from Latin <span class="foreign">fagus</span> &quot;beech,&quot; from PIE root <span class="foreign">*bhago-</span> &quot;beech tree&quot; (source also of Greek <span class="foreign">phegos</span> &quot;oak,&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">fagus</span> &quot;beech,&quot; Russian <span class="foreign">buzina</span> &quot;elder,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">bece</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">bok</span>, German <span class="foreign">Buche</span> &quot;beech&quot;), perhaps with a ground sense of &quot;edible&quot; (and connected with the root of Greek <span class="foreign">phagein</span> &quot;to eat,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*bhag-</span> &quot;to share out, apportion; to get a share&quot;). Beech mast was an ancient food source for agricultural animals across a wide stretch of Europe.</p>
<blockquote>The restriction to western IE languages and the reference to different trees have suggested to some scholars that this word was not PIE, but a later loanword. In the Balkans, from which the beech started to spread after 6000 BC, the [Greek] word means &#x27;oak,&#x27; not &#x27;beech.&#x27; Yet &#x27;oak&#x27; and &#x27;beech&#x27; are both &#x27;fruit-bearing trees,&#x27; so that a semantic shift from &#x27;oak&#x27; to &#x27;beech&#x27; appears quite conceivable. The word itself may then have been PIE after all. [de Vaan]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/fagus#etymonline_v_50457"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of serve" href="/word/serve#etymonline_v_23279">serve (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 12c., &quot;to render habitual obedience to,&quot; also &quot;minister, give aid, give help,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">servir</span> &quot;to do duty toward, show devotion to; set table, serve at table; offer, provide with,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">servire</span> &quot;be a servant, be in service, be enslaved;&quot; figuratively &quot;be devoted; be governed by; comply with; conform; flatter,&quot; originally &quot;be a slave,&quot; related to <span class="foreign">servus</span> &quot;slave,&quot; which is of uncertain origin.</p>
<p>Perhaps from Etruscan (compare Etruscan proper names <span class="foreign">Servi</span>, <span class="foreign">Serve</span>, Latinized as <span class="foreign">Servius</span>), but de Vaan says it is from Proto-Italic <span class="foreign">*serwo-</span> &quot;shepherd,&quot; <span class="foreign">*serwā-</span> &quot;observation,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*seruo-</span> &quot;guardian&quot; (source also of Avestan <span class="foreign">haraiti</span> &quot;heeds, protects&quot;):</p>
<blockquote>Rix 1994a argues that the original meaning of <span class="foreign">*serwo-</span> probably was &#x27;guard, shepherd&#x27;, which underwent a pejorative development to &#x27;slave&#x27; in Italy between 700 and 450 BC. <span class="foreign">Servire</span> would be the direct derivative of <span class="foreign">servus</span>, hence &#x27;be a slave&#x27;; <span class="foreign">servare</span> would in his view be derived from an older noun <span class="foreign">*serwa-</span> or <span class="foreign">*serwom</span> &#x27;observation, heedance&#x27;.</blockquote>
<p>By c. 1200 also as &quot;to be in the service of, perform a service for; attend upon, be personal servant to; be a slave; owe allegiance to; officiate at Mass or other religious rites;&quot; from early 13c. as &quot;set food at table;&quot; mid-14c. as &quot;to wait on (customers).&quot; From late 14c. as &quot;treat (someone or something) in some fashion.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign">serve (someone) right</span> &quot;to treat as he deserves&quot; is recorded from 1580s. Sense of &quot;be useful, be beneficial, be suitable for a purpose or function&quot; is from early 14c.; that of &quot;take the place or meet the needs of, be equal to the task&quot; is from late 14c.; that of &quot;suffice&quot; is from mid-15c. Meaning &quot;render active military service&quot; is from 1510s. Sporting sense, in tennis, badminton, etc., first recorded 1580s. Legal sense &quot;present&quot; (a writ, warrant, etc.), &quot;give legal notice of&quot; is from early 15c.</p>
