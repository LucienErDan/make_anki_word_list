Evidence from Dutch (cited in Grimm, &quot;Teutonic Mythology&quot;) suggests that it might originally have been Woden&#x27;s wagon. More recent names for it are <span class="foreign">the Plough</span> (by 15c., chiefly British) and <span class="foreign">the Dipper</span> (1833, chiefly American).<br/>
<br/>
It is called &quot;the Wagon&quot; in a Mesopotamian text from 1700 B.C.E., and it is mentioned in the Biblical Book of Job. The seven bright stars in the modern constellation Ursa Major have borne a dual identity in Western history at least since Homer&#x27;s time, being seen as both a wagon and a bear: as in Latin <span class="foreign">plaustrum</span> &quot;freight-wagon, ox cart&quot; and <span class="foreign">arctos</span> &quot;bear,&quot; both used of the seven-star pattern, as were equivalent Greek <span class="foreign">amaxa</span> (Attic <span class="foreign">hamaxa</span>) and <span class="foreign">arktos</span>.<br/>
<br/>
The identification with a wagon is easy to see, with four stars as the body and three as the pole. The identification with a bear is more difficult, as the figure has a tail longer than its body. As Allen writes, &quot;The conformation of the seven stars in no way resembles the animal,--indeed the contrary ....&quot; But he suggests the identification &quot;may have arisen from Aristotle&#x27;s idea that its prototype was the only creature that dared invade the frozen north.&quot; The seven stars never were below the horizon in the latitude of the Mediterranean in Homeric and classical times (though not today, due to precession of the equinoxes). See also <span class="crossreference">arctic</span> for the identification of the bear and the north in classical times.<br/>
<br/>
A variety of French and English sources from the early colonial period independently note that many native North American tribes in the northeast had long seen the seven-star group as a bear tracked by three hunters (or a hunter and his two dogs).<br/>
<br/>
Among the Teutonic peoples, it seems to have been only a wagon, not a bear. A 10c. Anglo-Saxon astronomy manual uses the Greek-derived <span class="foreign">Aretos</span>, but mentions that &quot;unlearned men&quot; call it &quot;Charles&#x27;s Wain&quot;:</p>
<blockquote>Arheton hatte an tungol on norð dæle, se haefð seofon steorran, &amp; is for ði oþrum naman ge-hatan septemtrio, þone hatað læwede meon carles-wæn. [&quot;Anglo-Saxon Manual of Astronomy&quot;] </blockquote>
<p>[<span class="foreign">Septemtrio</span>, the seven oxen, was yet another Roman name.] The star picture was not surely identified as a bear in English before late 14c.<br/>
<br/>
The unlearned of today are corrected that the seven stars are not the Great Bear but form only a part of that large constellation. But those who applied the name &quot;Bear&quot; apparently did so originally only to these seven stars, and from Homer&#x27;s time down to Thales, &quot;the Bear&quot; meant just the seven stars. From Rome to Anglo-Saxon England to Arabia to India, ancient astronomy texts mention a supposed duplicate constellation to the northern bear in the Southern Hemisphere, never visible from the north. This perhaps is based on sailors&#x27; tales of the Southern Cross.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
