<p>The Latin word was taken in ecclesiastical use for &quot;decree of the Church.&quot; General sense of &quot;a rule or principle&quot; is from late 14c.; that of &quot;standard of judging&quot; is from c. 1600. <blockquote>The secular canon, with the word meaning a catalog of approved authors, does not actually begin until the middle of the eighteenth century .... <blockquote>I am a suthern man, I can not geste &#x27;rum, ram, ruf&#x27; by letter. [Chaucer, &quot;Parson&#x27;s Prologue and Tale&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>After the Norman-French invasion of 1066, the peoples of the island were distinguished as <span class="foreign">English</span> and <span class="foreign">French</span>, but after a few generations all were <span class="foreign">English</span>, and Latin-speaking scribes, who knew and cared little about Germanic history, began to use <span class="foreign">Anglo-Saxones</span> to refer to the pre-1066 inhabitants and their descendants. When interest in Old English writing revived late 16c., the word was extended to the language we now call <span class="foreign">Old English</span>.</p>

<blockquote>In the last years of the reign of Elizabeth, Camden revived the use of the old name <span class="foreign">Anglosaxones,</span> and, probably for the first time, used <span class="foreign">lingua Anglosaxonica</span> for the language of England before the Norman conquest. He explains that <span class="foreign">Anglosaxones</span> means the Saxons of England, in contradistinction to those of the continent; and, in his English <span class="foreign">Remains,</span> he, accordingly, renders it by &quot;English Saxons.&quot; Throughout the seventeenth century, and even later, &quot;English Saxon&quot; continued to be the name ordinarily applied by philologists to the language of king Alfred, but, in the eighteenth century, this gave place to &quot;Anglo-Saxon.&quot; [Henry Bradley, in &quot;Cambridge History of English Literature,&quot; 1907]</blockquote>

<p>It has been used rhetorically for &quot;English&quot; in an ethnological sense from 1832, and revisioned as <span class="crossreference">Angle</span> + <span class="crossreference">Saxon</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Anglo-Saxon#etymonline_v_13440"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of son of a bitch" href="/word/son%20of%20a%20bitch#etymonline_v_25707">son of a bitch </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1707 as a direct phrase, but implied much earlier, and Old Norse had <span class="foreign">bikkju-sonr</span>. <p>&quot;Probably the most common American vulgarity from about the middle of the eighteenth century to the middle of the twentieth&quot; [Rawson].</p>
<blockquote>Our maid-of-all-work in that department [indecency] is <span class="foreign">son-of-a-bitch</span>, which seems as pale and ineffectual to a Slav or a Latin as <span class="foreign">fudge</span> does to us. <blockquote>It was in 1934 also that the New York <span class="foreign">Daily News</span>, with commendable frankness, in reporting a hearing in Washington at which Senator Huey P. Perhaps from a PIE root meaning &quot;bitter&quot; (source also of Latin <span class="foreign">alumen</span> &quot;alum&quot;), or from PIE <span class="foreign">*alu-t</span> &quot;ale,&quot; from root <span class="foreign">*alu-</span>, which has connotations of &quot;sorcery, magic, possession, and intoxication&quot; [Watkins]. <blockquote>In the fifteenth century, and until the seventeenth, ale stood for the unhopped fermented malt liquor which had long been the native drink of these islands. Beer was the hopped malt liquor introduced from the Low Countries in the fifteenth century and popular first of all in the towns. By the eighteenth century, however, all malt liquor was hopped and there had been a silent mutation in the meaning of the two terms. For a time the terms became synonymous, in fact, but local habits of nomenclature still continued to perpetuate what had been a real difference: &#x27;beer&#x27; was the malt liquor which tended to be found in towns, &#x27;ale&#x27; was the term in general use in the country districts. <p>Always used in English for the main meal of the day, but the time of that has gradually shifted later.</p>
<blockquote>In medieval and modern Europe the common practice, down to the middle of the eighteenth century, was to take this meal about midday, or in more primitive times even as early as 9 or 10 A.M. In France, under the old régime, the dinner-hour was at 2 or 3 in the afternoon; but when the Constituent Assembly moved to Paris, since it sat until 4 or 5 o&#x27;clock, the hour for dining was postponed. The custom of dining at 6 o&#x27;clock or later has since become common, except in the country, where early dinner is still the general practice. [Century Dictionary, 1897]</blockquote>
<p>The change from midday to evening began with the fashionable classes. Compare <span class="crossreference">dinette</span>.</p>
