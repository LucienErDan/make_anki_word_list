This is conjectured to be from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*gheis-</span>, used in forming words involving the notions of excitement, amazement, or fear (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">hedah</span> &quot;wrath;&quot; Avestan <span class="foreign">zaesha-</span> &quot;horrible, frightful;&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">usgaisjan</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">gæstan</span> &quot;to frighten&quot;).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Ghost</span> is the English representative of the usual West Germanic word for &quot;supernatural being.&quot; In Christian writing in Old English it is used to render Latin <span class="foreign">spiritus</span> (see <span class="crossreference">spirit</span> (n.)), a sense preserved in <span class="foreign">Holy Ghost</span>. Sense of &quot;disembodied spirit of a dead person,&quot; especially imagined as wandering among the living or haunting them, is attested from late 14c. and returns the word toward its likely prehistoric sense.</p>
<p>Most Indo-European words for &quot;soul, spirit&quot; also double with reference to supernatural spirits. Many have a base sense of &quot;appearance&quot; (such as Greek <span class="foreign">phantasma</span>; French <span class="foreign">spectre</span>; Polish <span class="foreign">widmo</span>, from Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">videti</span> &quot;to see;&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">scin</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">giskin</span>, originally &quot;appearance, apparition,&quot; related to Old English <span class="foreign">scinan</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">skinan</span> &quot;to shine&quot;). Other concepts are in French <span class="foreign">revenant</span>, literally &quot;returning&quot; (from the other world), Old Norse <span class="foreign">aptr-ganga</span>, literally &quot;back-comer.&quot; Breton <span class="foreign">bugelnoz</span> is literally &quot;night-child.&quot; Latin <span class="crossreference">manes</span> probably is a euphemism.</p>
<p>The <span class="foreign">gh-</span> spelling appeared early 15c. in Caxton, influenced by Flemish and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gheest</span>, but was rare in English before mid-16c. Sense of &quot;slight suggestion, mere shadow or semblance&quot; (in <span class="foreign"><strong>ghost image</strong></span><strong>, </strong><span class="foreign"><strong>ghost of a chance</strong></span>, etc.) is first recorded 1610s; sense of &quot;one who secretly does work for another&quot; is from 1884. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost town</strong></span> is from 1908. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost story</strong></span> is by 1811. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost-word</strong></span> &quot;apparent word or false form in a manuscript due to a blunder&quot; is from 1886 (Skeat). <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost in the machine</strong></span> was British philosopher Gilbert Ryle&#x27;s term (1949) for &quot;the mind viewed as separate from the body.&quot; The American Indian <span class="foreign"><strong>ghost dance</strong></span> is from 1890.
