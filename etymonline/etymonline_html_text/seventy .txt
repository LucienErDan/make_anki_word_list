<br/><br/>
English, like many other Germanic languages, retains traces of a base-12 number system. The most obvious instance is <span class="crossreference">eleven</span> and <span class="crossreference">twelve</span> which ought to be the first two numbers of the &quot;teens&quot; series. Their Old English forms, <span class="foreign">enleofan</span> and <span class="foreign">twel(eo)f(an)</span>, are more transparent: &quot;leave one&quot; and &quot;leave two.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Old English also had <span class="foreign">hund endleofantig</span> for &quot;110&quot; and <span class="foreign">hund twelftig</span> for &quot;120.&quot; <span class="foreign">One hundred</span> was <span class="foreign">hund teantig</span>. The <span class="foreign">-tig</span> formation ran through 12 cycles, and could have bequeathed us numbers <span class="foreign">*eleventy</span> (&quot;110&quot;) and <span class="foreign">*twelfty</span> (&quot;120&quot;) had it endured, but already during the Anglo-Saxon period it was being obscured.
<br/><br/>
