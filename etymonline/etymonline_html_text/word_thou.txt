and meaning of thou" href="/word/thou#etymonline_v_13251"><em>thou</em> (pron.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>2nd nominative singular personal pronoun, Old English <span class="foreign">þu</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*thu</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">thu</span>, Middle Dutch and Middle Low German <span class="foreign">du</span>, Old High German and German <span class="foreign">du</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">þu</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">þu</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*tu-</span>, second person singular pronoun (source also of Latin <span class="foreign">tu</span>, Irish <span class="foreign">tu</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">ti</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">su</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">tu</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">ty</span>, Sanskrit <span class="foreign">twa-m</span>).</p>
<p>Superseded in Middle English by plural form <span class="crossreference">you</span> (from a different root), but retained in certain dialects (e.g. early Quakers). The plural at first was used in addressing superior individuals, later also (to err on the side of propriety) strangers, and ultimately all equals. By c. 1450 the use of <span class="foreign">thou</span> to address inferiors gave it a tinge of insult unless addressed by parents to children, or intimates to one another. Hence the verb meaning &quot;to use &#x27;thou&#x27; to a person&quot; (mid-15c.).</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">Avaunt, caitiff, dost thou thou me! I am come of good kin, I tell thee!</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[&quot;Hickscorner,&quot; c. 1530]</blockquote>

<br/><br/>
Pronunciation of <span class="foreign">you</span> and the nominative form <span class="foreign">ye</span> gradually merged from 14c.; the distinction between them passed out of general usage by 1600. Widespread use of French in England after 12c. gave English <span class="foreign">you</span> the same association as French <span class="foreign">vous</span>, and it began to drive out singular nominative <span class="foreign">thou</span>, originally as a sign of respect (similar to the &quot;royal we&quot;) when addressing superiors, then equals and strangers, and ultimately (by c. 1575) becoming the general form of address. Through 13c. English also retained a dual pronoun <span class="foreign">ink</span> &quot;you two; your two selves; each other.&quot;</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/you#etymonline_v_4985"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of thee" href="/word/thee#etymonline_v_10720">thee (pron.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">þe</span> (accusative and dative singular of <span class="foreign">þu</span> &quot;thou&quot;), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*theke</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">thi</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">di</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">dih</span>, German <span class="foreign">dich</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">þik</span>, Norwegian <span class="foreign">deg</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">þuk</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*tege-</span>, accusative of root <span class="foreign">*tu-</span>, second person singular pronoun (see <span class="crossreference">thou</span>). The verb meaning &quot;to use the pronoun &#x27;thee&#x27; to someone&quot; is recorded from 1662, in connection with the rise of Quakerism.</p>
<p>In Middle English, people began to use plural forms in all cases, at first as a sign of respect to superiors, then as a courtesy to equals. By the 1600s, the singular forms had come to represent familiarity and lack of status, and fell from use except in the case of a few dialects, notably in the north of England. People in Lancashire north of the Rossendale Forest and Yorkshire formerly were noted for use of the singular second person pronouns <span class="foreign">tha</span> (nom.) and <span class="foreign">thee</span> (acc.). For religious reasons (Christian equality of persons, but also justified as grammatically correct), the Quakers also retained the familiar forms.</p>
<blockquote>Thou and Thee was a sore cut to proud flesh and them that sought self-honour, who, though they would say it to God and Christ, could not endure to have it said to themselves. So that we were often beaten and abused, and sometimes in danger of our lives, for using those words to some proud men, who would say, &quot;What! you ill-bred clown, do you Thou me?&quot; as though Christian breeding consisted in saying You to one; which is contrary to all their grammar and teaching books, by which they instructed their youth. [George Fox&#x27;s journal, 1661]</blockquote>

<blockquote>While the Quakers originally adopted &quot;thee&quot; and &quot;thou&quot; on account of their grammatical correctness, they soon fell into the careless habit of using &quot;thee,&quot; the objective, instead of &quot;thou,&quot; the nominative. <p>Related to words in Balto-Slavic (Lithuanian <span class="foreign">tūkstantis</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">tysashta</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">tysiąc</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">tysiacha</span>, Czech <span class="foreign">tisic</span>), and probably ultimately a compound with indefinite meaning &quot;great multitude, several hundred,&quot; literally &quot;swollen-hundred,&quot; with first element from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*teue-</span> &quot;to swell,&quot; second element from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*dekm-</span> &quot;ten.&quot;</p>
<p>Used to translate Greek <span class="foreign">khilias</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">mille</span>, hence the refinement into the precise modern meaning.
