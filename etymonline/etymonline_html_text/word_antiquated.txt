<p>In history, in the broadest sense, opposed to <span class="foreign">ancient</span> and <span class="foreign">medieval</span>, but often in more limited use. In Shakespeare, often with a sense of &quot;every-day, ordinary, commonplace.&quot; Meaning &quot;not antiquated or obsolete, in harmony with present ways&quot; is by 1808. Of languages, indicating the current form of Greek, etc., 1690s; <span class="foreign"><strong>modern languages</strong></span> as a department of study (1821) comprised those now living (i.e. not Latin or Greek) that were held to have literary or historical importance.</p>
