<p>It has largely displaced Old English <span class="foreign">ceorfan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">carve</span> (v.)), <span class="foreign">snian</span>, and <span class="foreign">scieran</span> (see <span class="crossreference">shear</span>). The past participle is also <span class="foreign">cut</span>, though <span class="foreign">cutted</span> sometimes has been used since Middle English.</p>
<p>From early 14c. as &quot;to make or fashion by cutting or carving.&quot; From c. 1400 as &quot;to intersect or cross.&quot; From early 15c. as &quot;abridge or shorten by omitting a part.&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to wound the sensibilities of&quot; is from 1580s (to <span class="foreign">cut the heart</span> in the same sense is attested from early 14c.). Sense of &quot;sever connection or relations with&quot; is from 1630s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to be absent without excuse&quot; is British university slang from 1794. Colloquial or slang sense of &quot;move off with directness and rapidity&quot; is from 1580s. Meaning &quot;divide (a deck of cards) at random into parts before the deal&quot; to prevent cheating is from 1530s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to dilute, adulterate&quot; (liquor, etc.) is by 1930. Colloquial sense of &quot;to divide or share&quot; is by 1928, perhaps an image from meat-carving at table. As a director&#x27;s call to halt recording or performing, by 1931 (in an article about Pete, the bulldog with the black-ringed eye in the Hal Roach studios shorts, who was said to know the word). The sense of &quot;perform, execute&quot; (c. 1600) is in <span class="foreign">cut capers</span> &quot;frisk about;&quot; <span class="foreign">cut a dash</span> &quot;make a display.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut down</strong></span> is from late 14c. as &quot;to fell;&quot; by 1821 as &quot;to slay&quot; (as with a sword); 1857 as &quot;to curtail.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (someone or something) down to size</strong></span> is from 1821 as &quot;reduce to suitable dimensions;&quot; the figurative sense, &quot;reduce to the proper level of importance,&quot; is by 1927.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut in</strong></span> &quot;enter suddenly and unceremoniously&quot; is from 1610s; sense of &quot;suddenly join in conversation, interrupt&quot; is by 1830. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut up</strong></span> &quot;cut in pieces&quot; is from 1570s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (something) short</strong></span> &quot;abridge, curtail, interrupt&quot; is from 1540s.</p>
<p>In nautical use to <span class="foreign"><strong>cut a feather</strong></span> (1620s) is to move so fast as to make water foam under the bow. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut and run</strong></span> (1704) also is originally nautical, &quot;cut cable and set sail immediately,&quot; as in an emergency, hence, generally, &quot;to make off suddenly.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut the teeth</strong></span> &quot;have the teeth grow through the gums&quot; as an infant is from 1670s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut both ways</strong></span> in the figurative sense of &quot;have a good and bad effect&quot; is from c. 1600. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut loose</strong></span> &quot;set (something) free&quot; is by 1828; intransitive sense &quot;begin to act freely&quot; is by 1909.</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">When you get &#x27;hot&#x27; about something and vow you are going to rip something or somebody up the back--cut it out.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">If you feel disposed to try the plan of building yourself up by tearing some one else down--cut it out.</blockquote>
