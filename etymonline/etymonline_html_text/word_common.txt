title="Origin and meaning of common" href="/word/common#etymonline_v_17239"><em>common</em> (adj.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;belonging to all, owned or used jointly, general, of a public nature or character,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">comun</span> &quot;common, general, free, open, public&quot; (9c., Modern French <span class="foreign">commun</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">communis</span> &quot;in common, public, shared by all or many; general, not specific; familiar, not pretentious.&quot; This is from a reconstructed PIE compound <span class="foreign">*ko-moin-i-</span> &quot;held in common,&quot; compound adjective formed from <span class="foreign">*ko-</span> &quot;together&quot; + <span class="foreign">*moi-n-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="crossreference">*mei-</span> (1) &quot;to change, go, move,&quot; hence literally &quot;shared by all.&quot;</p>
<p>The second element of the compound also is the source of Latin <span class="foreign">munia</span> &quot;duties, public duties, functions,&quot; those related to <span class="foreign">munia</span> &quot;office.&quot; Perhaps reinforced in Old French by the Germanic form of PIE <span class="foreign">*ko-moin-i-</span> (compare German <span class="foreign">gemein</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">gemne</span> &quot;common, public, general, universal;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">mean</span> (adj.)), which came to French via Frankish.</p>
<p>Used disparagingly of women and criminals since c. 1300. Meaning &quot;pertaining equally to or proceeding equally from two or more&quot; is from c. 1400. Meaning &quot;usual, not exceptional, of frequent occurrence&quot; is from late 14c. Sense of &quot;not distinguished, belonging to the general mass&quot; is from c. 1400; of things, &quot;ordinary, not excellent,&quot; late 14c.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Common pleas</strong></span> is 13c., from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">communs plets</span>, hearing civil actions by one subject against another as opposed to pleas of the crown. <span class="foreign"><strong>Common prayer</strong></span> is that done in public in unity with other worshipers; contrasted with private prayer. <span class="foreign">Common stock</span> is attested from 1888. <span class="foreign"><strong>Common speech</strong></span> (late 14c.) is the vernacular, as opposed to Latin. <span class="foreign"><strong>Common good</strong></span> (late 14c.) translates Latin <span class="foreign">bonum publicum</span> &quot;the common weal.&quot; The college <span class="foreign"><strong>common room</strong></span> (1660s) is one to which all members have common access. </p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/common#etymonline_v_17239"><span>Relate entries &amp;
