The whole group is of unknown origin.</p>
<p>The object was placed with others in a receptacle (such as a hat or helmet), which was shaken, the winner being the one whose name or mark was on the lot that fell out first. Hence the expression <span class="foreign"><strong>cast lots</strong></span>; to <span class="foreign"><strong>cast (one&#x27;s) lot with</strong></span> another (1530s, originally biblical) is to agree to share winnings. In some cases the lots were drawn by hand, hence to <span class="foreign"><strong>draw lots</strong></span>. The word was adopted from Germanic into the Romanic languages (Spanish <span class="foreign">lote</span>, and compare <span class="crossreference">lottery</span>, <span class="crossreference">lotto</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;choice resulting from the casting of lots&quot; first attested c. 1200. Meaning &quot;share or portion of life&quot; in any way, &quot;that which is given by fate, God or destiny&quot; is from c. 1300. Meaning &quot;number of persons or things of the same kind&quot; is from 1570s (compare Latin <span class="foreign">mala merx</span>, of persons, literally &quot;a bad lot&quot;). Sense of &quot;plot of land&quot; is first recorded 1630s, American English (distribution of the most desirable properties in new settlements often was determined by casting lots), then especially &quot;parcel of land set aside for a specified purpose&quot; (the Hollywood sense is from 1928). The common U.S. <span class="foreign"><strong>city lot</strong></span> was a rectangle 25 feet wide (along the street) by 100 deep; it was so universal as to be sometimes a unit of measure.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;group, collection&quot; is 1725, from the notion of auction lots. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lots</strong></span> in the generalized sense of &quot;great many&quot; is attested by 1812; <span class="foreign"><strong>lotsa</strong></span>, colloquial for &quot;lots of,&quot; is from 1927; <span class="foreign"><strong>lotta</strong></span> for &quot;lot of&quot; is by 1906.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/lot#etymonline_v_14542"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of tombola" href="/word/tombola#etymonline_v_15351">tombola (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Italian lotto-style lottery, 1880, from Italian <span class="foreign">tombola</span>, apparently from <span class="foreign">tombolare</span> &quot;to tumble, fall upside down,&quot; from a Germanic source (see <span class="crossreference">tumble</span> (v.)).</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/tombola#etymonline_v_15351"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of keno" href="/word/keno#etymonline_v_1812">keno (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">game of chance (akin to bingo), 1814, American English, probably from French <span class="foreign">quine</span> &quot;five winning numbers in a lottery,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">quini</span> &quot;five each,&quot; distributive of <span class="foreign">quinque</span> &quot;five&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*penkwe-</span> &quot;five&quot;).
