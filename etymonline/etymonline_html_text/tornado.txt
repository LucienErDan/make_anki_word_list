<blockquote>When, years ago, I first called public attention to this kind of straight blows peculiar to Iowa prairies in summer, I named them provisorily Iowa squalls. The term derecho, coined from the Spanish in analogy with the term tornado, and expressing the main feature of a straight blow, has been chosen to bring out the contrast with the tornado or whirl-wind to avoid further confusion of these two different storms. [Gustavus Hinrichs, &quot;Tornadoes in Iowa,&quot; in Report of the Iowa Weather Service for the Year 1888]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/derecho#etymonline_v_29617"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of butterfly" href="/word/butterfly#etymonline_v_18153">butterfly (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>common name of any lepidopterous insect active in daylight, Old English <span class="foreign">buttorfleoge</span>, evidently <span class="crossreference">butter</span> (n.) + <span class="crossreference">fly</span> (n.), but the name is of obscure signification. Perhaps based on the old notion that the insects (or, according to Grimm, witches disguised as butterflies) consume butter or milk that is left uncovered. Or, less creatively, simply because the pale yellow color of many species&#x27; wings suggests the color of butter. Another theory connects it to the color of the insect&#x27;s excrement, based on Dutch cognate <span class="foreign">boterschijte</span>. Also see <span class="crossreference">papillon</span>.</p>
<p>Applied to persons from c. 1600, originally in reference to vain and gaudy attire; by 1806 in reference to transformation from early lowly state; in reference to flitting tendencies by 1873. The swimming stroke so called from 1935. As a type of mechanical nut, 1869. <span class="foreign">Butterflies</span> &quot;light stomach spasms caused by anxiety&quot; is from 1908. &quot;Who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel?&quot; is from Pope.</p>
<blockquote>The butterfly effect is a deceptively simple insight extracted from a complex modern field. As a low-profile assistant professor in MIT&#x27;s department of meteorology in 1961, [Edward] Lorenz created an early computer program to simulate weather. One day he changed one of a dozen numbers representing atmospheric conditions, from .506127 to .506. That tiny alteration utterly transformed his long-term forecast, a point Lorenz amplified in his 1972 paper, &quot;Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly&#x27;s Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?&quot; [Peter Dizikes, &quot;The Meaning of the Butterfly,&quot; The Boston Globe, June 8, 2008]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/butterfly#etymonline_v_18153"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of typhoon" href="/word/typhoon#etymonline_v_18890">typhoon (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p><span class="foreign">Tiphon</span> &quot;violent storm, whirlwind, tornado,&quot; 1550s, from Greek <span class="foreign">typhon</span> &quot;whirlwind,&quot; personified as a giant, father of the winds, probably [Beekes] from or related to <span class="foreign">typhein</span> &quot;to smoke&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">typhus</span>), but according to Watkins from PIE <span class="foreign">*dheub-</span> &quot;deep, hollow,&quot; via notion of &quot;monster from the depths.&quot; The meaning &quot;cyclone, violent hurricane of India or the China Seas&quot; is first recorded 1588 in Thomas Hickock&#x27;s translation of an account in Italian of a voyage to the East Indies by Caesar Frederick, a merchant of Venice:</p>
<blockquote>concerning which Touffon ye are to vnderstand, that in the East Indies often times, there are not stormes as in other countreys; but euery 10. or 12. yeeres there are such tempests and stormes, that it is a thing incredible, but to those that haue seene it, neither do they know certainly what yeere they wil come. [&quot;The voyage and trauell of M. Caesar Fredericke, Marchant of Venice, into the East India, and beyond the Indies&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>This sense of the word, in reference to titanic storms in the East Indies, first appears in Europe in Portuguese in the mid-16th century. It apparently is from <span class="foreign">tufan</span>, a word in Arabic, Persian, and Hindi meaning &quot;big cyclonic storm.&quot; Yule [&quot;Hobson-Jobson,&quot; London, 1903] writes that &quot;the probability is that Vasco [da Gama] and his followers got the <span class="foreign">tufao</span> ... direct from the Arab pilots.&quot;</p>
<p>The Arabic word sometimes is said to be from Greek <span class="foreign">typhon</span>, but other sources consider it purely Semitic, though the Greek word might have influenced the form of the word in English. <span class="foreign">Al-tufan</span> occurs several times in the Koran for &quot;a flood or storm&quot; and also for Noah&#x27;s Flood. Chinese (Cantonese) <span class="foreign">tai fung</span> &quot;a great wind&quot; also might have influenced the form or sense of the word in English, and that term and the Indian one may have had some mutual influence; <span class="foreign">toofan</span> still means &quot;big storm&quot; in India.</p>

His brutish ass-head touched the stars, his vast wings darkened the sun, fire flashed from his eyes, and flaming rocks hurtled from his mouth. [Robert Graves, &quot;Typhon,&quot; in &quot;The Greek Myths&quot;]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
