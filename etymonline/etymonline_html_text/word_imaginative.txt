<p>Related to Greek <span class="foreign">idein</span> &quot;to see,&quot; and to <span class="foreign">eidenai</span> &quot;to know.&quot; In Middle English, not differentiated from <span class="crossreference">story</span> (n.1); sense of &quot;narrative record of past events&quot; probably first attested late 15c. <blockquote>History is the interpretation of the significance that the past has for us. [Johan Huizinga, &quot;The Task of the Cultural Historian&quot;]</blockquote>
<blockquote>History is more or less bunk [Henry Ford, &quot;Chicago Tribune,&quot; May 25, 1916]</blockquote>

It was a sequel to his <span class="foreign">&quot;Amadis de Gaula,&quot;</span> and was said to have been influential among Spanish explorers of the New World. It could have led them to misidentify Baja California as this mythical land and to mistake it for an island. The <span class="foreign">Amadis</span> tales are the Iberian equivalent of the Arthurian romances; they are older than 1510 (traces of them have been found mid-14c.) and were wildly popular. That conquistadors and sailors would have known the story in all its imaginative detail is hardly surprising.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span> ... set a fashion: all later Spanish writers of books of chivalry adopted the machinery of <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span>. Later knights were not less brave (they could not be braver than) Amadis; heroines were not less lovely (they could not be lovelier) than Oriana; there was nothing for it but to make the dragons more appalling, the giants larger, the wizards craftier, the magic castles more inaccessible, the enchanted lakes deeper. Subsequent books of chivalry are simple variants of the types in <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span>: Cervantes made his barber describe it as &#x27;the best of all books of this kind.&#x27; This verdict is essentially just. <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span> was read everywhere, especially in the French version of Herberay des Essarts. It was done into Hebrew during the sixteenth century, and attracted readers as different as St Ignatius of Loyola and Henry of Navarre. Its vogue perhaps somewhat exceeded its merit, but its merits are not inconsiderable. [James Fitzmaurice-Kelly, &quot;Spanish Literature,&quot; 1922 edition]</blockquote>
<p>Where Montalvo got the name and what it means, if anything, is a mystery. In reference to the native inhabitants, <span class="foreign">Californian</span> is attested from 1785 as an adjective, 1789 as a noun. <p>From late 14c. in English as &quot;book-learning,&quot; also &quot;a particular branch of knowledge or of learning;&quot; also &quot;skillfulness, cleverness; craftiness.&quot; From c. 1400 as &quot;experiential knowledge;&quot; also &quot;a skill, handicraft; a trade.&quot; From late 14c. as &quot;collective human knowledge&quot; (especially that gained by systematic observation, experiment, and reasoning). Modern (restricted) sense of &quot;body of regular or methodical observations or propositions concerning a particular subject or speculation&quot; is attested from 1725; in 17c.-18c. this concept commonly was called <span class="foreign">philosophy</span>. Sense of &quot;non-arts studies&quot; is attested from 1670s. </p>
<blockquote>Science, since people must do it, is a socially embedded activity. It progresses by hunch, vision, and intuition. Much of its change through time does not record a closer approach to absolute truth, but the alteration of cultural contexts that influence it so strongly. Facts are not pure and unsullied bits of information; culture also influences what we see and how we see it. Theories, moreover, are not inexorable inductions from facts. The most creative theories are often imaginative visions imposed upon facts; the source of imagination is also strongly cultural. [Stephen Jay Gould, introduction to &quot;The Mismeasure of Man,&quot; 1981]</blockquote>
<blockquote>In <span class="foreign">science</span> you must not talk before you know. In art you must not talk before you do. In literature you must not talk before you think. [John Ruskin, &quot;The Eagle&#x27;s Nest,&quot; 1872]</blockquote>
<p>The distinction is commonly understood as between theoretical truth (Greek <span class="foreign">epistemē</span>) and methods for effecting practical results (<span class="foreign">tekhnē</span>), but <span class="foreign">science</span> sometimes is used for practical applications and art for applications of skill.
