class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1400, &quot;unlucky, inauspicious,&quot; in <span class="foreign">dismal day</span>, earlier as a noun, <span class="foreign">in the dismal</span> (c. 1300) &quot;in days of misfortune or disaster, under inauspicious circumstances, at an unlucky time,&quot; from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">dismal</span> (mid-13c.), apparently from Old French <span class="foreign">(li) dis mals</span> &quot;(the) bad days,&quot; from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">dies mali</span> &quot;evil or unlucky days&quot; (also called <span <p>Through the Middle Ages, calendars marked as unlucky two days of each month (Jan. 1, 25; Feb. 4, 26; March 1, 28; April 10, 20; May 3, 25; June 10, 16; July 13, 22; Aug. 1, 30; Sept. 3, 21; Oct. 3, 22; Nov. 5, 28; Dec. 7, 22), supposedly based on the ancient calculations of Egyptian astrologers.</p>
<p>By 1580s the English word had been extended to &quot;gloomy, dreary, cheerless,&quot; and was used to describe physical surroundings, sounds, or anything else felt as tending to depress the spirits. In North America, it was the name given along the seacoast and sounds around North Carolina to tracts of swampy land and dead trees (1763). <p>Meaning &quot;graphite in a pencil&quot; is from 1816 (see <span class="crossreference">pencil</span> (n.)). <span class="foreign"><strong>Black lead</strong></span> was an old name for &quot;graphite,&quot; hence <span class="foreign"><strong>lead pencil</strong></span> (1680s) and the colloquial figurative phrase to have <span class="foreign"><strong>lead in one&#x27;s pencil</strong></span> &quot;be possessed of (especially male sexual) vigor,&quot; attested by 1902. <span class="foreign"><strong>White lead</strong></span> (1560s) was an old name for &quot;tin.&quot;</p>
<p>As a name of a dull bluish-gray color, 1610s. From 1590s as figurative for &quot;bullets.&quot; Lead oxide was much used in glazing, mirror-making, and pigments. In printing, &quot;thin strip of type-metal (often lead but sometimes brass) used in composition to separate lines&quot; from 1808, earlier <span class="foreign">space-line</span>. <span class="foreign"><strong>Lead-poisoning</strong></span> is from 1848; earlier <span class="foreign">lead-distemper</span> (1774).</p></section></object><a data-more="true"
