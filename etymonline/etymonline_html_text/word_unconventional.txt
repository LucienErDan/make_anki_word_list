<p>The French used <span class="foreign">bohemién</span> since 15c. to also mean &quot;Gypsy.&quot; The Roma were wrongly believed to have come from there, perhaps because their first appearance in Western Europe may have been immediately from Bohemia, or because they were confused with the 15c. <p>The transferred sense, in reference to unconventional living, is attested in French by 1834 and was popularized by Henri Murger&#x27;s stories from the late 1840s later collected as <span class="foreign">&quot;Scenes de la Vie de Boheme&quot;</span> (the basis of Puccini&#x27;s <span class="foreign">&quot;La Bohème&quot;</span>). <blockquote>The term &#x27;Bohemian&#x27; has come to be very commonly accepted in our day as the description of a certain kind of literary gipsey, no matter in what language he speaks, or what city he inhabits .... A Bohemian is simply an artist or littérateur who, consciously or unconsciously, secedes from conventionality in life and in art. [&quot;Westminster Review,&quot; 1862]</blockquote>
