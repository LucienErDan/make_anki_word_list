<p>Re-spelled after 1550 on Latin model. Cognate with the common term for the animal in Romanic and Germanic; Slavic words (for example Polish <span class="foreign">slon&#x27;</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">slonu</span>) are from a different word. Old English had it as <span class="foreign">elpend</span>, and compare <span class="foreign">elpendban</span>, <span class="foreign">elpentoð</span> &quot;ivory,&quot; but a confusion of exotic animals led to <span class="foreign">olfend</span> &quot;camel.&quot;</p>

<p>Herodotus mentions the (African) elephant, which in ancient times and until 7c. C.E. was found north of the Sahara as well. Frazer (notes to Pausanias&#x27;s &quot;Description of Greece,&quot; 1898) writes that &quot;Ptolemy Philadelphius, king of Egypt (283-247 B.C.), was first to tame the African elephant and use it in war; his elephants were brought from Nubia,&quot; and the Carthaginians probably borrowed the idea from him; &quot;for in the Carthaginian army which defeated Regulus in 255 B.C. there were about 100 elephants .... It was easy for the Carthaginians to procure elephants, since in antiquity the animal was found native in the regions of North Africa now known as Tripoli and Morocco (Pliny, N.H. viii.32).&quot;</p>

<blockquote>The word &quot;tacky&quot; is a Southern colloquialism. It was coined by a wealthier or more refined and educated class for general application to those who were not sheltered by the branches of a family tree, who were &quot;tainted.&quot; Those who were wealthy and yet had no great-grandfathers were &quot;tackies.&quot; The word was used both in contempt and in derision. It is now nearly obsolete in both senses. There are no aristocrats in the South now, and therefore no &quot;tackies.&quot; No man who has the instincts of a gentleman is spoken of as a &quot;tacky,&quot; whether he can remember the name of his grandfather&#x27;s uncle or not. But it has its uses. It is employed in describing persons of low ideas and vulgar manners, whether rich or poor. It may mean an absence of style. In dress, anything that is tawdry is &quot;tacky.&quot; A ribbon on the shopkeeper&#x27;s counter, a curtain in the bolt, a shawl or bonnet, a bolt of cloth fresh from the loom may be &quot;tacky,&quot; because it is cheap and yet pretentious.
