Aristotle, who was the first to use <span class="foreign">Graikhos</span> as equivalent to <span class="foreign">Hellenes</span> (<span class="foreign">&quot;Meteorologica&quot;</span> I.xiv), wrote that it was the name originally used by Illyrians for the Dorians in Epirus, from <span class="foreign">Graii</span>, native name of the people of Epirus.</p>
<p>But a modern theory (put forth by German classical historian Georg Busolt, 1850-1920), derives it from <span class="foreign">Graikhos</span> &quot;inhabitant of <span class="foreign">Graia</span>&quot; (literally &quot;gray,&quot; also &quot;old, withered&quot;), a town on the coast of Boeotia, which was the name given by the Romans to all Greeks, originally to the Greek colonists from Graia who helped found Cumae (9c. <p>The Germanic languages originally borrowed the word with an initial &quot;-k-&quot; sound (compare Old High German <span class="foreign">Chrech</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">Kreks</span>), which probably was their initial sound closest to the Latin &quot;-g-&quot; at the time; the word was later refashioned. From late 14c. as &quot;the Greek language.&quot; Meaning &quot;unintelligible speech, gibberish, any language of which one is ignorant&quot; is from c. 1600. Meaning &quot;member of a Greek-letter fraternity&quot; is student slang, 1884.</p>

<p>Greek <span class="foreign">barbaroi</span> (plural noun) meant &quot;all that are not Greek,&quot; but especially the Medes and Persians; originally it was not entirely pejorative, but its sense became moreso after the Persian wars. The Romans (technically themselves <span class="foreign">barbaroi</span>) took up the word and applied it to tribes or nations which had no Greek or Roman accomplishments.</p>
<p>Also in Middle English (c. 1400) &quot;native of the Barbary coast;&quot; meaning &quot;rude, wild person&quot; is from 1610s. Occasionally in 19c. English distinguished from <span class="foreign">savage</span> (n.) as being a step closer to civilization. Sometimes, in reference to Renaissance Italy, &quot;a non-Italian.&quot; It also was used to translate the usual Chinese word of contempt for foreigners.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Barbarian</span> applies to whatever pertains to the life of an uncivilized people, without special reference to its moral aspects. <span class="foreign">Barbarous</span> properly expresses the bad side of barbarian life and character, especially its inhumanity or cruelty: as, a <span class="foreign">barbarous</span> act. <span class="foreign">Barbaric</span> expresses the characteristic love of barbarians for adornment, magnificence, noise, etc., but it is not commonly applied to persons: it implies the lack of cultivated taste .... [Century Dictionary, 1889]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/barbarian#etymonline_v_52529"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of so long" href="/word/so%20long#etymonline_v_25685">so long (interj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>parting salutation, 1860, of unknown origin, perhaps from a German idiom (compare German parting salutation <span class="foreign">adieu so lange</span>, the full sense of which probably is something like &quot;farewell, whilst (we&#x27;re apart)&quot;); or perhaps from Hebrew <span class="foreign">shalom</span> (via Yiddish <span class="foreign">sholom</span>). Some have noted a similarity to Scandinavian leave-taking phrases, such as Norwegian <span class="foreign">Adjø så lenge, Farvel så lenge, Mor&#x27;n så lenge</span>, literally &quot;bye so long, farewell so long, morning so long;&quot; and Swedish <span class="foreign">Hej så länge</span> &quot;good-bye for now,&quot; with <span class="foreign">så länge</span> &quot;for now&quot; attested since 1850 according to Swedish sources. Most etymology sources seem to lean toward the German origin. <span class="foreign">So long</span> (adv.) &quot;for such a long time&quot; is from late Old English.</p>

<p>Earlier guesses that it was a sailors&#x27; corruption of a South Pacific form of Arabic <span class="foreign">salaam</span> are not now regarded as convincing. &quot;Dictionary of American Slang&quot; also adds to the list of candidates Irish <span class="foreign">slán</span> &quot;safe,&quot; said to be used as a salutation in parting. The phrase seems to have turned up simultaneously in America, Britain, and perhaps Canada, originally among lower classes. First attested use is in title and text of the last poem in Whitman&#x27;s &quot;Leaves of Grass&quot; in the 1860 edition.</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">An unknown sphere, more real than I dream&#x27;d, more direct, darts awakening rays about me -- So long!</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Remember my words -- I may again return,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">I love you -- I depart from materials;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">I am as one disembodied, triumphant, dead.</blockquote>

<p>Whitman&#x27;s friend and fan William Sloane Kennedy wrote in 1923:</p>
<blockquote>The salutation of parting -- &#x27;So long!&#x27; -- was, I believe, until recent years, unintelligible to the majority of persons in America, especially in the interior, and to members of the middle and professional classes. I had never heard of it until I read it in Leaves of Grass, but since then have quite often heard it used by the laboring class and other classes in New England cities. Walt wrote to me, defining &#x27;so long&#x27; thus: &quot;A salutation of departure, greatly used among sailors, sports, &amp; prostitutes -- the sense of it is &#x27;Till we meet again,&#x27; -- conveying an inference that somehow they will doubtless so meet, sooner or later.&quot; ... It is evidently about equivalent to our &#x27;See you later.&#x27; The phrase is reported as used by farm laborers near Banff, Scotland. In Canada it is frequently heard; &#x27;and its use is not entirely confined to the vulgar.&#x27; It is in common use among the working classes of Liverpool and among sailors at Newcastle-upon-Tyne, and in Dorsetshire. ... The London Globe suggests that the expression is derived from the Norwegian &#x27;Saa laenge,&#x27; a common form of &#x27;farewell,&#x27; au revoir. If so, the phrase was picked up from the Norwegians in America, where &#x27;So long&#x27; first was heard. The expression is now (1923) often used by the literary and artistic classes.</blockquote>
