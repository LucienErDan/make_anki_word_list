<blockquote>The orig. word, AS. <span class="foreign">bæddel</span>, ME. <span class="foreign">baddel</span>, on account of its sinister import, is scarcely found in literature, but, like other words of similar sense, it prob. flourished in vulgar speech as an indefinite term of abuse, and at length, divested of its original meaning, emerged in literary use as a mere adj., <span class="foreign">badde</span>, equiv. to the older <span class="foreign">evil</span>. [Century Dictionary, 1897]</blockquote>
<p>Comparable words in the other Indo-European languages tend to have grown from descriptions of specific qualities, such as &quot;ugly,&quot; &quot;defective,&quot; &quot;weak,&quot; &quot;faithless,&quot; &quot;impudent,&quot; &quot;crooked,&quot; &quot;filthy&quot; (such as Greek <span class="foreign">kakos</span>, probably from the word for &quot;excrement;&quot; Russian <span class="foreign">plochoj</span>, related to Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">plachu</span> &quot;wavering, timid;&quot; Persian <span class="foreign">gast</span>, Old Persian <span class="foreign">gasta-</span>, related to <span class="foreign">gand</span> &quot;stench;&quot; German <span class="foreign">schlecht</span>, originally &quot;level, straight, smooth,&quot; whence &quot;simple, ordinary,&quot; then &quot;bad&quot;).</p>
<p>Comparative and superlative forms <span class="foreign">badder</span>, <span class="foreign">baddest</span> were common 14c.-18c. and used as recently as Defoe (but not by Shakespeare), but yielded to comparative <span class="crossreference">worse</span> and superlative <span class="crossreference">worst</span> (which had belonged to <span class="foreign">evil</span> and <span class="foreign">ill</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;uncomfortable, sorry&quot; is 1839, American English colloquial. To <span class="foreign"><strong>go bad</strong></span> &quot;putrefy&quot; is from 1884. <span class="foreign"><strong>Not bad</strong></span> &quot;fairly good&quot; is by 1771. Ironic use as a word of approval is said to be at least since 1890s orally, originally in African-American vernacular, emerging in print 1928 in a jazz context. It might have emerged from the ambivalence of expressions like <span class="foreign">bad nigger</span>, used as a term of reproach by whites, but among blacks sometimes representing one who stood up to injustice, but in the U.S. West <span class="foreign">bad man</span> also had a certain ambivalence:</p>
<blockquote>These are the men who do most of the killing in frontier communities, yet it is a noteworthy fact that the men who are killed generally deserve their fate. [Farmer &amp; Henley]</blockquote>
<p>*Farsi has <span class="foreign">bad</span> in more or less the same sense as the English word, but this is regarded by linguists as a coincidence. The forms of the words diverge as they are traced back in time (Farsi <span class="foreign">bad</span> comes from Middle Persian <span class="foreign">vat</span>), and such accidental convergences exist across many languages, given the vast number of words in each and the limited range of sounds humans can make to signify them. Among other coincidental matches with English are Korean <span class="foreign">mani</span> &quot;many,&quot; Chinese <span class="foreign">pei</span> &quot;pay,&quot; Nahuatl (Aztecan) <span class="foreign">huel</span> &quot;well,&quot; Maya <span class="foreign">hol</span> &quot;hole.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
