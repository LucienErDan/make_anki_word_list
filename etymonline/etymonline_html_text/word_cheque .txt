Also c. 1300 in a generalized sense, &quot;harmful incident or event, hostile environment.&quot;</p>

<p>As &quot;an exposure of the king to a direct attack from an opposing piece&quot; early 15c. When his king is in check, a player&#x27;s choices are severely limited. From that notion come the many extended senses: From the notion of &quot;a sudden stoppage, hindrance, restraint&quot; (1510s) comes that of &quot;act or means of checking or restraining,&quot; also &quot;means of detecting or exposing or preventing error; a check against forgery or alteration.</p>

<p>&quot;Hence: &quot;a counter-register as a token of ownership used to check against, and prevent, loss or theft&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">hat check</span>, etc.), 1812.  Hence also the financial use for &quot;written order for money drawn on a bank, money draft&quot; (1798, often spelled <span class="foreign">cheque</span>), which was probably influenced by <span class="crossreference">exchequer</span>. Hence also &quot;mark put against names or items on a list indicating they have been verified or otherwise examined&quot; (by 1856).</p>
<blockquote>From its use in chess the word has been widely transferred in French and English. In the sense-extension, the sb. and vb. have acted and reacted on each other, so that it is difficult to trace and exhibit the order in which special senses arose [OED]</blockquote>
