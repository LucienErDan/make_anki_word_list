title="Origin and meaning of trivia" href="/word/trivia#etymonline_v_17849"><em>trivia</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;trivialities, bits of information of little consequence,&quot; by 1932, from the title of a popular book by U.S.-born British aphorist Logan Pearsall Smith (1865-1946) first published in 1902 but popularized in 1918 (with &quot;More Trivia&quot; following in 1921 and a collected edition including both in 1933), containing short essays often tied to observation of small things and commonplace moments. <span class="foreign">Trivia</span> is Latin, plural of <span class="foreign">trivium</span> &quot;place where three roads meet;&quot; in transferred use, &quot;an open place, a public place.&quot; The adjectival form of this, <span class="foreign">trivialis</span>, meant &quot;public,&quot; hence &quot;common, commonplace&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">trivial</span>).</p>
<p>The Romans also had <span class="foreign">trivius dea</span>, the &quot;goddess of three ways,&quot; another name for Hecate, perhaps originally in her triple aspect (Selene/Diana/Proserpine), but also as the especial divinity of crossroads (Virgil has &quot;Nocturnisque hecate triviis ululata per urbes&quot;). John Gay took this arbitrarily as the name of a goddess of streets and roads for his mock Georgic &quot;Trivia: Or, the Art of Walking the Streets of London&quot; (1716); Smith writes in his autobiography that he got the title from Gay.</p>
<blockquote>I KNOW too much; I have stuffed too many of the facts of History and Science into my intellectuals. My eyes have grown dim over books; believing in geological periods, cave dwellers, Chinese Dynasties, and the fixed stars has prematurely aged me. [&quot;Trivia,&quot; 1918 edition]</blockquote>
<p>Then noted c. 1965 as an informal fad game among college students wherein one asked questions about useless bits of information from popular culture (&quot;What was Donald Duck&#x27;s address?&quot;) and others vied to answer first.</p>
<blockquote>Nobody really wins in this game which concentrates on sports, comics and television. Everyone knows that Amos&#x27;s wife on the &quot;Amos &#x27;n&#x27; Andy Show&quot; is Ruby, but who knows that she is from Marietta, Georgia? Trivia players do. They also know the fourth man in the infield of Tinker-to-Evers-to-Chance, the Canadian who shot down Baron Von Richtofen, and can name ten Hardy Boy books. [&quot;Princeton Alumni Weekly,&quot; Nov. 9, 1965]</blockquote>
<p>The board game <span class="foreign">Trivial Pursuit</span> was released 1982 and was a craze in U.S. for several years thereafter.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/trivia#etymonline_v_17849"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of trivium" href="/word/trivium#etymonline_v_17851">trivium (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>by 1751, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">trivium</span> (9c.) &quot;grammar, rhetoric, and logic,&quot; the first three of the seven liberal arts, considered initiatory and foundational to the other four (arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, and music).
