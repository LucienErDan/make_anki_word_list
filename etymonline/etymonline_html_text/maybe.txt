<p>It is perhaps from Arabic <span class="foreign">maskharah</span> &quot;buffoon, mockery,&quot; from <span class="foreign">sakhira</span> &quot;be mocked, ridiculed.&quot; Or it may come via Provençal <span class="foreign">mascarar</span>, Catalan <span class="foreign">mascarar</span>, Old French <span class="foreign">mascurer</span> &quot;to black (the face),&quot; which is perhaps from a Germanic source akin to English <span class="crossreference">mesh</span> (q.v.). But it maybe a Provençal word originally: Compare Occitan <span class="foreign">mascara</span> &quot;to blacken, darken,&quot; derived from <span class="foreign">mask-</span> &quot;black,&quot; which is held to be from a pre-Indo-European language, and Old Occitan <span class="foreign">masco</span> &quot;witch,&quot; surviving in dialects; in Beziers it means &quot;dark cloud before the rain comes.&quot; [See Walther von Wartburg, <span class="foreign">&quot;Französisches Etymologisches Wörterbuch: Eine Darstellung galloromanischen sprachschatzes&quot;</span>].</p>

<p>Figurative meaning &quot;anything used or practiced for disguise or concealment&quot; is by 1570s.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mask#etymonline_v_9675"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of manifest" href="/word/manifest#etymonline_v_6804">manifest (adj.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;clearly revealed to the eye or the understanding, open to view or comprehension,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">manifest</span> &quot;evident, palpable,&quot; (12c.), or directly from Latin <span class="foreign">manifestus</span> &quot;plainly apprehensible, clear, apparent, evident;&quot; of offenses, &quot;proved by direct evidence;&quot; of offenders, &quot;caught in the act,&quot; probably from <span class="foreign">manus</span> &quot;hand&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*man-</span> (2) &quot;hand&quot;) + <span class="foreign">-festus</span>, which apparently is identical to the second element of <span class="crossreference">infest</span>.</p>
<p>De Vaan writes, &quot;If <span class="foreign">manifestus</span> may be interpreted as &#x27;caught by hand&#x27;, the meanings seem to point to &#x27;grabbing&#x27; or &#x27;attacking&#x27; for <span class="foreign">-festus</span>.&quot; But he finds none of the proposed ulterior connections compelling, and concludes that, regarding <span class="foreign">infestus</span> and <span class="foreign">manifestus</span>, &quot;maybe the two must be separated.&quot; If not, the sense development might be from &quot;caught by hand&quot; to &quot;in hand, palpable.&quot; </p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Manifest destiny</strong></span>, &quot;that which clearly appears destined to come to pass; a future state, condition, or event which can be foreseen with certainty, or is regarded as inevitable&quot; was much used in American politics from about the time of the Mexican War &quot;by those who believed that the United States were destined in time to occupy the entire continent&quot; [Century Dictionary].</p>
<blockquote>Other nations have tried to check ... the fulfillment of our manifest destiny to overspread the Continent allotted by Providence for the free development of our yearly multiplying millions. [John O&#x27;Sullivan (1813-1895), &quot;U.S. Magazine &amp; Democratic Review,&quot; July 1845]</blockquote>
<p>The phrase apparently is O&#x27;Sullivan&#x27;s coinage; the notion is as old as the republic.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/manifest#etymonline_v_6804"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of gay" href="/word/gay#etymonline_v_1327">gay (adj.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;full of joy, merry; light-hearted, carefree;&quot; also &quot;wanton, lewd, lascivious&quot; (late 12c. as a surname, <span class="foreign">Philippus de Gay</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">gai</span> &quot;joyful, happy; pleasant, agreeably charming; forward, pert; light-colored&quot; (12c.; compare Old Spanish <span class="foreign">gayo</span>, Portuguese <span class="foreign">gaio</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">gajo</span>, probably French loan-words). Ultimate origin disputed; perhaps from Frankish <span class="foreign">*gahi</span> (related to Old High German <span class="foreign">wahi</span> &quot;pretty&quot;), though not all etymologists accept this.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;stately and beautiful; splendid and showily dressed&quot; is from early 14c. Of things, &quot;sumptuous, showy, rich, ornate,&quot; mid-14c. of colors, etc., &quot;shining, glittering, gleaming, bright, vivid,&quot; late 14c.; of persons, &quot;dressed up, decked out in finery,&quot; late 14c.</p>
<p>In the English of Yorkshire and Scotland formerly it could mean &quot;moderately, rather, considerable&quot; (1796; compare sense development in <span class="foreign">pretty</span> (adj.)).</p>

<p>The word <span class="foreign">gay</span> by the 1890s had an overall tinge of promiscuity -- a <span class="foreign">gay house</span> was a brothel. The suggestion of immorality in the word can be traced back at least to the 1630s, if not to Chaucer:</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">But in oure bed he was so fressh and gay</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Whan that he wolde han my bele chose.</blockquote>

<p>Slang meaning &quot;homosexual&quot; (adj.) begins to appear in psychological writing late 1940s, evidently picked up from gay slang and not always easily distinguished from the older sense:</p>
<blockquote>After discharge A.Z. lived for some time at home. He was not happy at the farm and went to a Western city where he associated with a homosexual crowd, being &quot;gay,&quot; and wearing female clothes and makeup. He always wished others would make advances to him. [&quot;Rorschach Research Exchange and Journal of Projective Techniques,&quot; 1947, p.240]</blockquote>
<p>The association with (male) homosexuality likely got a boost from the term <span class="foreign">gay cat</span>, used as far back as 1893 in American English for &quot;young hobo,&quot; one who is new on the road, also one who sometimes does jobs.</p>
<blockquote>&quot;A Gay Cat,&quot; said he, &quot;is a loafing laborer, who works maybe a week, gets his wages and vagabonds about hunting for another &#x27;pick and shovel&#x27; job. Do you want to know where they got their monica (nickname) &#x27;Gay Cat&#x27;? See, Kid, cats sneak about and scratch immediately after chumming with you and then get gay (fresh). That&#x27;s why we call them &#x27;Gay Cats&#x27;.&quot; [Leon Ray Livingston (&quot;America&#x27;s Most Celebrated Tramp&quot;), &quot;Life and Adventures of A-no. 1,&quot; 1910]</blockquote>
<p>Quoting a tramp named Frenchy, who might not have known the origin. Gay cats were severely and cruelly abused by &quot;real&quot; tramps and bums, who considered them &quot;an inferior order of beings who begs of and otherwise preys upon the bum -- as it were a jackal following up the king of beasts&quot; [Prof. John J. McCook, &quot;Tramps,&quot; in &quot;The Public Treatment of Pauperism,&quot; 1893], but some accounts report certain older tramps would dominate a gay cat and employ him as a sort of slave. In &quot;Sociology and Social Research&quot; (1932-33) a paragraph on the &quot;gay cat&quot; phenomenon notes, &quot;Homosexual practices are more common than rare in this group,&quot; and <span class="foreign">gey cat</span> &quot;homosexual boy&quot; is attested in Noel Erskine&#x27;s 1933 dictionary of &quot;Underworld &amp; Prison Slang&quot; (<span class="foreign">gey</span> is a Scottish variant of <span class="foreign">gay</span>).</p>

<p>The &quot;Dictionary of American Slang&quot; reports that <span class="foreign">gay</span> (adj.) was used by homosexuals, among themselves, in this sense at least since 1920. Rawson [&quot;Wicked Words&quot;] notes a male prostitute using <span class="foreign">gay</span> in reference to male homosexuals (but also to female prostitutes) in London&#x27;s notorious Cleveland Street Scandal of 1889. Ayto [&quot;20th Century Words&quot;] calls attention to the ambiguous use of the word in the 1868 song &quot;The Gay Young Clerk in the Dry Goods Store,&quot; by U.S. female impersonator Will S. Hays, but the word evidently was not popularly felt in this sense by wider society until the 1950s at the earliest.</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Gay&quot; (or &quot;gai&quot;) is now widely used in French, Dutch, Danish, Japanese, Swedish, and Catalan with the same sense as the English. It is coming into use in Germany and among the English-speaking upper classes of many cosmopolitan areas in other countries. [John Boswell, &quot;Christianity, Social Tolerance, and Homosexuality,&quot; 1980]</blockquote>
<p>As a teen slang word meaning &quot;bad, inferior, undesirable,&quot; without reference to sexuality, from 2000.</p>
