<p>Visible only when near the sun, they were anciently regarded as omens of ruin, pestilence, and the overthrow of kingdoms. Related: <span class="foreign">Cometary; cometic; cometical</span>.</p>
<blockquote>Their sudden appearance in the heavens, and the imposing and astonishing aspect which they present, have, even in recent times, inspired alarm and terror. [&quot;The Leisure Hour,&quot; April 15, 1852]</blockquote>

<p>Meaning &quot;of common or low origin, inferior in rank or status&quot; (of persons) is attested from early 14c. Sense of &quot;ordinary, inferior in attainment or skill&quot; is from late 14c. James Stirling, in &quot;Letters from the Slave States&quot; [London, 1857], mentioning <span class="foreign">mean whites</span> (poor whites in the South who do manual labor and are looked down on by the slaves) notes, &quot; &#x27;Mean&#x27; is an Americanism for &#x27;poor,&#x27; &#x27;shabby.&#x27; They speak here of a &#x27;mean&#x27; hotel, a &#x27;mean&#x27; dinner, &amp;c.&quot;</p>

<p>The pejorative sense of &quot;without dignity of mind, destitute of honor, low-minded&quot; is from 1660s; the specific sense of &quot;stingy, niggardly&quot; is recorded by 1755; the weaker sense of &quot;disobliging, pettily offensive&quot; is from 1839, originally American English slang. <p>The inverted sense of &quot;remarkably good&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">plays a mean Rhythm Master</span>) first recorded c. 1900, perhaps from phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>no mean _______</strong></span> &quot;not inferior&quot; (1590s, also, &quot;not average,&quot; reflecting further confusion with <span class="foreign"><strong>mean</strong></span> (adj.2.)).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/mean#etymonline_v_12495"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of come" href="/word/come#etymonline_v_15860">come (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>elementary intransitive verb of motion, Old English <span class="foreign">cuman</span> &quot;to move with the purpose of reaching, or so as to reach, some point; to arrive by movement or progression;&quot; also &quot;move into view, appear, become perceptible; come to oneself, recover; arrive; assemble&quot; (class IV strong verb; past tense <span class="foreign">cuom</span>, <span class="foreign">com</span>, past participle <span class="foreign">cumen</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kwem-</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">cuman</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">kuma</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">comen</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">komen</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">queman</span>, German <span class="foreign">kommen</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">koma</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">qiman</span>), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwa-</span> &quot;to go, come.&quot;</p>
<p>The substitution of Middle English <span class="foreign">-o-</span> for Old English <span class="foreign">-u-</span> before <span class="foreign">-m-</span>, <span class="foreign">-n-</span>, or <span class="foreign">-r-</span> was a scribal habit before minims to avoid misreading the letters in the old style handwriting, which jammed them together. The practice similarly transformed <span class="crossreference">some</span>, <span class="crossreference">monk</span>, <span class="crossreference">tongue</span>, <span class="crossreference">worm</span>. Modern past tense form <span class="foreign">came</span> is Middle English, probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">kvam</span>, replacing Old English <span class="foreign">cuom</span>.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to happen, occur&quot; is from early 12c. (<span class="foreign">come to pass</span> &quot;happen, occur&quot; is from 1520s). As an invitation to action, c. 1300; as a call or appeal to a person (often in expanded forms: &quot;come, come,&quot; &quot;come, now&quot;), mid-14c. <span class="foreign">Come again?</span> as an off-hand way of asking &quot;what did you say?&quot; is attested by 1884. For sexual senses, see <span class="crossreference">cum</span>.</p>
<p>Remarkably productive with prepositions (NTC&#x27;s &quot;Dictionary of Phrasal Verbs&quot; lists 198 combinations); consider the varied senses in <span class="foreign">come to</span> &quot;regain consciousness,&quot; <span class="foreign">come over</span> &quot;possess&quot; (as an emotion), <span class="foreign">come at</span> &quot;attack,&quot; <span class="foreign">come on</span> (interj.) &quot;be serious,&quot; and <span class="foreign">come off</span> &quot;occur, have some level of success&quot; (1864). Among other common examples:</p>
