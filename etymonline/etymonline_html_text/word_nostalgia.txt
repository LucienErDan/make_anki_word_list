title="Origin and meaning of nostalgia" href="/word/nostalgia#etymonline_v_9811"><em>nostalgia</em> (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1770, &quot;severe homesickness considered as a disease,&quot; Modern Latin, coined 1688 in a dissertation on the topic at the University of Basel by scholar Johannes Hofer (1669-1752) as a rendering of German <span class="foreign">heimweh</span> &quot;homesickness&quot; (for which see <span class="crossreference">home</span> + <span class="crossreference">woe</span>). From Greek <span class="foreign">algos</span> &quot;pain, grief, distress&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">-algia</span>) + <span class="foreign">nostos</span> &quot;homecoming,&quot; from <span class="foreign">neomai</span> &quot;to reach some place, escape, return, get home,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*nes-</span> &quot;to return safely home&quot; (cognate with Old Norse <span class="foreign">nest</span> &quot;food for a journey,&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">nasate</span> &quot;approaches, joins,&quot; German <span class="foreign">genesen</span> &quot;to recover,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">ganisan</span> &quot;to heal,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">genesen</span> &quot;to recover&quot;). French <span class="foreign">nostalgie</span> is in French army medical manuals by 1754.</p>
<p>Originally in reference to the Swiss and said to be peculiar to them and often fatal, whether by its own action or in combination with wounds or disease. By 1830s the word was used of any intense homesickness: that of sailors, convicts, African slaves. &quot;The bagpipes produced the same effects sometimes in the Scotch regiments while serving abroad&quot; [Penny Magazine,&quot; Nov. 14, 1840]. It is listed among the &quot;endemic diseases&quot; in the &quot;Cyclopaedia of Practical Medicine&quot; [London, 1833, edited by three M.D.s], which defines it as &quot;The concourse of depressing symptoms which sometimes arise in persons who are absent from their native country, when they are seized with a longing desire of returning to their home and friends and the scenes their youth ....&quot; It was a military medical diagnosis principally, and was considered a serious medical problem by the North in the American Civil War:</p>
<blockquote>In the first two years of the war, there were reported 2588 cases of nostalgia, and 13 deaths from this cause. These numbers scarcely express the real extent to which nostalgia influenced the sickness and mortality of the army. To the depressing influence of home-sickness must be attributed the fatal result in many cases which might otherwise have terminated favorably. [&quot;Sanitary Memoirs of the War,&quot; U.S. Sanitary Commission, N.Y.: 1867]</blockquote>
<p>Transferred sense (the main modern one) of &quot;wistful yearning for the past&quot; first recorded 1920, perhaps from such use of <span class="foreign">nostalgie</span> in French literature. The longing for a distant place also necessarily involves a separation in time.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/nostalgia#etymonline_v_9811"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of nostalgic" href="/word/nostalgic#etymonline_v_30294">nostalgic (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">1806, from <span class="crossreference">nostalgia</span> + <span class="crossreference">-ic</span>. Klein says the name is literally &quot;one who blesses,&quot; and is related to <span class="foreign">nostimos</span> &quot;blessed;&quot; Watkins connects it with the root of the first element in <span class="crossreference">nostalgia</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Nestor#etymonline_v_6876"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of harness" href="/word/harness#etymonline_v_6173">harness (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1300, &quot;personal fighting equipment, body armor,&quot; also &quot;armor or trappings of a war-horse,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">harnois</span>, a noun of broad meaning: &quot;arms, equipment; harness; male genitalia; tackle; household equipment&quot; (12c.), of uncertain origin, perhaps from Old Norse <span class="foreign">*hernest</span> &quot;provisions for an army,&quot; from <span class="foreign">herr</span> &quot;army&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">harry</span> (v.)) + <span class="foreign">nest</span> &quot;provisions&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">nostalgia</span>). See <span class="crossreference">ninety</span>.</p>
<blockquote>Many still live who remember those days; if the old men cannot tell you the exact date, they will say: &#x27;It were in the nineties;&#x27; (etc.) [&quot;Chambers&#x27;s Journal,&quot; Nov. 1, 1856]</blockquote>
<p>In Britain, the <span class="foreign">naughty nineties</span> was a popular name 1920s-30s for the 1890s, based on the notion of a relaxing of morality and mood in contrast to earlier Victorian times. In U.S., <span class="foreign">gay nineties</span> in reference to the same decade is attested from 1927, and was the title of a regular nostalgia feature in &quot;Life&quot; magazine about that time.</p>
<blockquote>The long, dreary blue-law Sunday afternoons were periods of the Nineties which no amount of rosy retrospect will ever be able to recall as gay, especially to a normal healthy boy to whom all activities were taboo except G. A. Henty and the bound volumes of <span class="foreign">Leslie&#x27;s Weekly</span> of the Civil War. [Life, Sept. 1, 1927]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/nineties#etymonline_v_40709"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa
