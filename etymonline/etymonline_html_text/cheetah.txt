In some French dialects, including that of Paris (but not that of Picardy), Latin <span class="foreign">ca-</span> became French &quot;tsha.&quot; This was introduced to English after the Norman Conquest, in words borrowed from Old French such as <span class="crossreference">chaste</span>, <span class="crossreference">charity</span>, <span class="crossreference">chief</span> (adj.). Under French influence, <span class="foreign">-ch-</span> also was inserted into Anglo-Saxon words that had the same sound (such as <span class="crossreference">bleach</span>, <span class="crossreference">chest</span>, <span class="crossreference">church</span>) which in Old English still was written with a simple <span class="foreign">-c-</span>, and into those that had formerly been spelled with a <span class="foreign">-c-</span> and pronounced &quot;k&quot; such as <span class="crossreference">chin</span> and <span class="crossreference">much</span>.</p>

<p>As French evolved, the &quot;t&quot; sound dropped out of <span class="foreign">-ch-</span>, so in later loan-words from French -<span class="foreign">ch-</span> has only the sound &quot;sh-&quot; (<span class="crossreference">chauffeur</span>, <span class="crossreference">machine</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">chivalry</span>, etc.).</p>

<p>It turns up as well in words from classical languages (<span class="crossreference">chaos</span>, <span class="crossreference">echo</span>, etc.). Most uses of <span class="foreign">-ch-</span> in Roman Latin were in words from Greek, which in Greek would be pronounced correctly as /k/ + /h/, as in modern <span class="foreign">blockhead</span>, but most Romans would have said merely /k/, and this was the regular pronunciation in English. Before c. 1500 such words were regularly spelled with a <span class="foreign">-c-</span> (<span class="foreign">Crist, cronicle, scoole</span>), but Modern English has preserved or restored the etymological spelling in most of them (<span class="foreign">chemical</span>, <span class="foreign">chorus</span>, <span class="foreign">monarch</span>). </p>

<p>Sometimes <span class="foreign">ch-</span> is written to keep <span class="foreign">-c-</span> hard before a front vowel, as still in modern Italian. In some languages (Welsh, Spanish, Czech) <span class="foreign">ch-</span> can be treated as a separate letter and words in it are alphabetized after <span class="foreign">-c-</span> (or, in Czech and Slovak, after <span class="foreign">-h-</span>). The sound also is heard in words from more distant languages (as in <span class="crossreference">cheetah</span>, <span class="crossreference">chintz</span>), and the digraph also is used to represent               $('.backtop').show('fast')
            } else {
              $('.backtop').hide('fast')
            }
          })
          $('.backtop').click(function() {
            $('body,html').animate({
              scrollTop: 0
            }, 500)
          })
        </script>
  </body>
  </html>
