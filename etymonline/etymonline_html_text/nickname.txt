<p>In late Middle English <span class="foreign">a</span> and <span class="foreign">an</span> commonly were joined to the following noun, if that word began with a vowel, which caused confusion over how such words ought to be divided when written separately. In <span class="crossreference">nickname</span>, <span class="crossreference">newt</span>, and British dialectal <span class="foreign">naunt</span>, the <span class="foreign">-n-</span> belongs to a preceding indefinite article <span class="foreign">an</span> or possessive pronoun <span class="foreign">mine</span>.</p>
<p>Other examples of this from Middle English manuscripts include <span class="foreign">a neilond</span> (&quot;an island,&quot; early 13c.), <span class="foreign">a narawe</span> (&quot;an arrow,&quot; c. 1400), <span class="foreign">a nox</span> (&quot;an ox,&quot; c. 1400), <span class="foreign">a noke</span> (&quot;an oak,&quot; early 15c.), <span class="foreign">a nappyle</span> (&quot;an apple,&quot; early 15c.), <span class="foreign">a negge</span> (&quot;an egg,&quot; 15c.), <span class="foreign">a nynche</span> (&quot;an inch,&quot; c. 1400). A manuscript from c. 1500 has <span class="foreign">a nylle</span> for &quot;an isle.&quot; <span class="foreign">My naunt</span> for <span class="foreign">mine aunt</span> is recorded from 13c.-17c. <span class="foreign">Natomy</span> or <span class="foreign">atomy</span> was common in Elizabethan English for <span class="foreign">anatomy</span>. In 16c., <span class="foreign">an idiot</span> sometimes became <span class="foreign">a nidiot</span>, which, with still-common casual pronunciation, became <span class="foreign">nidget</span>, which, alas, has not survived. Marlowe (1590) has <span class="foreign">Natolian</span> for <span class="foreign">Anatolian</span>. Also compare <span class="crossreference">nonce</span>.</p>
<p>The process also worked in surnames, from oblique cases of Old English <span class="foreign">at</span> &quot;by, near,&quot; as in <span class="foreign">Nock</span>/<span class="foreign">Nokes</span>/<span class="foreign">Noaks</span> from <span class="foreign">atten Oke</span> &quot;by the oak;&quot; <span class="foreign">Nye</span> from <span class="foreign">atten ye</span> &quot;near the lowland;&quot; and see <span class="crossreference">Nashville</span>. (Elision of the vowel of the definite article also took place and was standard in Chancery English of the 15c.: <span class="foreign">þarchebisshop</span> for &quot;the archbishop.&quot;)</p>
<p>But it is more common for an English word to lose an <span class="foreign">-n-</span> to a preceding <span class="foreign">a</span>: <span class="crossreference">apron</span>, <span class="crossreference">auger</span>, <span class="crossreference">adder</span>, <span class="crossreference">umpire</span>, <span class="crossreference">humble pie</span>, etc.</p>
