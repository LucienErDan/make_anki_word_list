Not found in Scandinavian (Danish has <span class="foreign">faar</span> for &quot;sheep&quot;) or Gothic (which uses <span class="foreign">lamb</span>), and with no known cognates outside Germanic. The more usual Indo-European word for the animal is represented in English by <span class="crossreference">ewe</span>.
<br/><br/>
The plural form was leveled with the singular in Old English, but Old Northumbrian had a plural <span class="foreign">scipo</span>. Used since Old English as a type of timidity and figuratively of those under the guidance of God. The meaning &quot;stupid, timid person&quot; is attested from 1540s. The image of the <span class="foreign">wolf in sheep&#x27;s clothing</span> was in Old English (from Matthew vii.15); that of <span class="foreign">separating the sheep from the goats</span> is from Matthew xxv.33. To <span class="foreign">count sheep</span> in a bid to induce sleep is recorded from 1854 but seems not to have been commonly written about until 1870s. It might simply be a type of a tedious activity, but an account of shepherd life from Australia from 1849 [&quot;Sidney&#x27;s Emigrant&#x27;s Journal&quot;] describes the night-shepherd (&quot;hut-keeper&quot;) taking a count of the sheep regularly at the end of his shift to protect against being answerable for any animals later lost or killed. 
<br/><br/>
<span class="foreign">Sheep&#x27;s eyes</span> &quot;loving looks&quot; is attested from 1520s (compare West Frisian <span class="foreign">skiepseach</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">schaapsoog</span>, German <span class="foreign">Schafsauge</span>). It was taken to mean &quot;man who allows himself to be molded,&quot; but that probably was not the original image. The source is an 1820 speech by John Randolph of Roanoke, in the wake of the Missouri Compromise.</p>
<blockquote>Randolph, mocking the northerners intimidated by the South, referred to a children&#x27;s game in which the players daubed their faces with dough and then looked in a mirror and scared themselves. [Daniel Walker Howe, &quot;What Hath God Wrought,&quot; 2007]</blockquote>
<p>Randolph had used the term <span class="foreign">dough-face</span> in the sense &quot;mask of dough&quot; in Congressional debates as far back as February 1809 (&quot;... it is something like dressing ourselves up in a dough-face and winding-sheet to frighten others ....&quot;).</p>
<p>However, the expression has been explained as referring to &quot;the pale doughy faces of his frightened opponents&quot; [Craigie], to a &quot;person who is pliable and, as it were, made of dough&quot; [Century Dictionary], or even &quot;to liken them in timidity to female deer,&quot; which is frightened at her own shadow [The Port Folio, 1820]. <span class="foreign">Dough-faced</span> in the sense &quot;cowardly&quot; is attested in a text from 1773, so there might be a convergence of senses.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/doughface#etymonline_v_37632"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of hypochondria" href="/word/hypochondria#etymonline_v_16133">hypochondria (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;unfounded belief that one is sick,&quot; by 1816; a narrowing from the earlier sense &quot;depression or melancholy without real cause&quot; (1660s); from Middle English medical term <span class="foreign">ipocondrie</span> &quot;lateral regions of the upper abdomen&quot; (late 14c.). This is from Late Latin <span class="foreign">hypochondria</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">hypokhondria</span> (neuter plural of <span class="foreign">hypokhondrios</span>), from <span class="foreign">hypo-</span> &quot;under&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">hypo-</span>) + <span class="foreign">khondros</span> &quot;cartilage&quot; (in this case, of the false ribs); see <span class="crossreference">chondro-</span>.</p>
<p>The sense &quot;morbid melancholy&quot; reflects the ancient belief that the viscera of the hypochondria (liver, gall bladder, spleen) were the seat of melancholy and the source of the vapors that caused such feelings. The attempt to put it on a scientific bases passes through <span class="crossreference">hypochondriasis</span>. Also see <span class="crossreference">hype</span> (n.). The poet Cowper is an oft-cited example in late 18c. literature. The focus of sense on the particular symptom &quot;unfounded belief that one is sick&quot; seems to begin 1790s with William Cullen, M.D., professor of physic in the University of Edinburgh, who made a specialty of the topic:</p>
<blockquote>A languor, listlessness, or want of resolution and activity, with respect to all undertakings; a disposition to seriousness, sadness, and timidity; as to all future events, an apprehension of the worst or most unhappy state of them; and, therefore, often upon slight grounds an apprehension of great evil. Such persons are particularly attentive to the state of their own health, to every the smallest change of feeling in their bodies; and from any unusual sensation, perhaps of the slightest kind, they apprehend great danger, and even death itself. In respect to these feelings and fears, there is commonly the most obstinate belief and persuasion. [Cullen, &quot;First Lines of the Practice of Physic,&quot; Edinburgh, 1791]</blockquote>
<p>Though to Cullen the clinical definition of <span class="foreign">hypochondria</span> also included physical symptoms and pains as well as these mental delusions. As the old medical beliefs faded, the word dropped from clinical use but remained in popular use for &quot;groundless morbid fear for one&#x27;s health.&quot; In the 1830s <span class="foreign">hypochondria</span> could mean merely &quot;morbid melancholy,&quot; also &quot;apprehension of evil respecting health, without sufficient cause,&quot; and &quot;upper abdomen.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/hypochondria#etymonline_v_16133"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of frank" href="/word/frank#etymonline_v_11874">frank (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;free, liberal, generous;&quot; 1540s, &quot;outspoken,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">franc</span> &quot;free (not servile); without hindrance, exempt from; sincere, genuine, open, gracious, generous; worthy, noble, illustrious&quot; (12c.), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">francus</span> &quot;free, at liberty, exempt from service,&quot; as a noun, &quot;a freeman, a Frank&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">Frank</span>).</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Frank</span>, literally, <span class="foreign">free</span>; the freedom may be in regard to one&#x27;s own opinions, which is the same as <span class="foreign">openness</span>, or in regard to things belonging to others, where the freedom may go so far as to be unpleasant, or it may disregard conventional ideas as to reticence. Hence, while <span class="foreign">openness</span> is consistent with timidity, <span class="foreign">frankness</span> implies some degree of boldness. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>A generalization of the tribal name; the connection is that Franks, as the conquering class, alone had the status of freemen in a world that knew only free, captive, or slave. For sense connection of &quot;being one of the nation&quot; and &quot;free,&quot; compare Latin <span class="foreign">liber</span> &quot;free,&quot; from the same root as German <span class="foreign">Leute</span> &quot;nation, people&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">liberal</span> (adj.)) and Slavic &quot;free&quot; words (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">svobodi</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">swobodny</span>, Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">slobodan</span>) which are cognates of the first element in English <span class="crossreference">sibling</span> &quot;brother, sister&quot; (in Old English used more generally: &quot;relative, kinsman&quot;).
