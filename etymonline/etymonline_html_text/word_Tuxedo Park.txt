<br/><br/>
Internal evidence suggests the West Germanic word is pre-4c. and originally meant the fencing, not the place enclosed. Found also in Medieval Latin as <span class="foreign">parricus</span> &quot;enclosure, park&quot; (8c.), which likely is the direct source of the Old French word, as well as Italian <span class="foreign">parco</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">parque</span>, etc. Some claim the Medieval Latin word as the source of the West Germanic, but the reverse seems more likely. Some later senses in English represent later borrowings from French. OED discounts notion of a Celtic origin. Welsh <span class="foreign">parc</span>, Gaelic <span class="foreign">pairc</span> are from English.
<br/><br/>
Meaning &quot;enclosed lot in or near a town, for public recreation&quot; is first attested 1660s, originally in reference to London; the sense evolution is via royal parks in the original, hunting sense being overrun by the growth of London and being opened to the public. Applied to sporting fields in American English from 1867.
<br/><br/>
New York&#x27;s <span class="foreign">Park Avenue</span> as an adjective meaning &quot;luxurious and fashionable&quot; (1956) was preceded in the same sense by London&#x27;s <span class="foreign">Park Lane</span> (1880). As a surname, <span class="foreign">Parker</span> &quot;keeper of a park&quot; is attested in English from mid-12c.
