Also in some cases from Latin <span class="foreign">-atorem</span> (nominative <span class="foreign">-ator</span>).
<br/><br/>
In U.S., via Noah Webster, <span class="foreign">-or</span> is nearly universal (but not in <span class="crossreference">glamour</span>, <span class="crossreference">curious</span>, <span class="crossreference">generous</span>), while in Britain <span class="foreign">-our</span> is used in most cases (but with many exceptions: <span class="foreign">author, error, senator, ancestor, horror</span> etc.). The <span class="foreign">-our</span> form predominated after c. 1300, but Mencken reports that the first three folios of Shakespeare&#x27;s plays used both spellings indiscriminately and with equal frequency; only in the Fourth Folio of 1685 does <span class="foreign">-our</span> become consistent.
<br/><br/>
A partial revival of <span class="foreign">-or</span> on the Latin model took place from 16c. (<span class="foreign">governour</span> began to lose its <span class="foreign">-u-</span> 16c. and it was gone by 19c.), and also among phonetic spellers in both England and America (John Wesley wrote that <span class="foreign">-or</span> was &quot;a fashionable impropriety&quot; in England in 1791).
<br/><br/>
Webster criticized the habit of deleting <span class="foreign">-u-</span> in <span class="foreign">-our</span> words in his first speller (&quot;A Grammatical Institute of the English Language,&quot; commonly called the Blue-Black Speller) in 1783. His own deletion of the <span class="foreign">-u-</span> began with the revision of 1804, and was enshrined in the influential &quot;Comprehensive Dictionary of the English Language&quot; (1806), which also established in the U.S. <span class="foreign">-ic</span> for British <span class="foreign">-ick</span> and <span class="foreign">-er</span> for <span class="foreign">-re</span>, along with many other attempts at reformed spelling which never caught on (such as <span class="foreign">masheen</span> for <span class="foreign">machine</span>). His attempt to justify them on the grounds of etymology and the custom of great writers does not hold up.
<br/><br/>
Fowler notes the British drop the <span class="foreign">-u-</span> when forming adjectives ending in <span class="foreign">-orous</span> (<span class="foreign">humorous</span>) and derivatives in <span class="foreign">-ation</span> and <span class="foreign">-ize</span>, in which cases the Latin origin is respected (such as <span class="foreign">vaporize</span>). When the Americans began to consistently spell it one way, however, the British reflexively hardened their insistence on the other. &quot;The American abolition of <span class="foreign">-our</span> in such words as <span class="foreign">honour</span> and <span class="foreign">favour</span> has probably retarded rather than quickened English progress in the same direction.&quot;
