English <span class="foreign">cwen</span> &quot;queen, female ruler of a state, woman, wife,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kwoeniz</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">quan</span> &quot;wife,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">kvaen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">quens</span>), ablaut variant of <span class="foreign">*kwenon</span> (source of <span class="crossreference">quean</span>), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwen-</span> &quot;woman.&quot;
<br/><br/>
The original sense seems to have been &quot;wife,&quot; specialized by Old English to &quot;wife of a king.&quot; In Old Norse, still mostly of a wife generally, as in <span class="foreign">kvan-fang</span> &quot;marriage, taking of a wife,&quot; <span class="foreign">kvanlauss</span> &quot;unmarried, widowed,&quot; <span class="foreign">kvan-riki</span> &quot;the domineering of a wife.&quot; English is one of the few Indo-European languages to have a word for &quot;queen&quot; that is not a feminine derivative of a word for &quot;king.&quot; The others are Scandinavian: Old Norse <span class="foreign">drottning</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">dronning</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">drottning</span> &quot;queen,&quot; in Old Norse also &quot;mistress,&quot; but these also are held to be ultimately from male words, such as Old Norse <span class="foreign">drottinn</span> &quot;master.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Used of chess piece from mid-15c. (as a verb in chess, in reference to a pawn that has reached the last rank, from 1789), of playing card from 1570s. Of bees from c. 1600 (until late 17c., they generally were thought to be kings; as in &quot;Henry V,&quot; I.ii, but the Anglo-Saxons knew better: their word was <span class="foreign">beomodor</span>); <span class="foreign">queen bee</span> in a figurative sense is from 1807. Meaning &quot;male homosexual&quot; (especially a feminine and ostentatious one) first certainly recorded 1924; probably here an alteration of <span class="crossreference">quean</span>, which is earlier in this sense. <span class="foreign">Queen Anne</span> first used 1878 for &quot;style characteristic of the time of Queen Anne of Great Britain and Ireland,&quot; who reigned 1702-14.
