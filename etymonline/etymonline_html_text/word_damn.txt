and meaning of damn" href="/word/damn#etymonline_v_752"><em>damn</em> (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Middle English <span class="foreign">dampnen</span>, also <span class="foreign">damnen, dammen</span>, late 13c. as a legal term, &quot;to condemn, declare guilty, convict;&quot; c. 1300 in the theological sense of &quot;doom to punishment in a future state,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">damner</span> &quot;damn, condemn; convict, blame; injure,&quot; derivative of Latin <span class="foreign">damnare</span> &quot;to adjudge guilty; to doom; to condemn, blame, reject,&quot; from noun <span class="foreign">damnum</span> &quot;damage, hurt, harm; loss, injury; a fine, penalty,&quot; from Proto-Italic <span class="foreign">*dapno-</span>, possibly from an ancient religious term from PIE <span class="foreign">*dap-</span> &quot;to apportion in exchange&quot; [Watkins] or <span class="foreign">*dhp-no-</span> &quot;expense, investment&quot; [de Vaan]. The <span class="foreign">-p-</span> in the English word disappeared 16c.</p>
<p>The legal meaning &quot;pronounce judgment upon&quot; evolved in the Latin word. The optative expletive use likely is as old as the theological sense. <span class="foreign">Damn</span> and its derivatives generally were avoided in print from 18c. to 1930s (the famous line in the film version of &quot;Gone with the Wind&quot; was a breakthrough and required much effort by the studio). Meaning &quot;judge or pronounce (a work) to be bad by public expression&quot; is from 1650s; to <span class="foreign"><strong>damn with faint praise</strong></span> is from Pope.</p>
<p>The noun is recorded from 1610s, &quot;utterance of the word &#x27;damn.&#x27;&quot; To be <span class="foreign"><strong>not worth a damn</strong></span> is from 1817. To <span class="foreign"><strong>not give</strong></span><strong> (or </strong><span class="foreign"><strong>care</strong></span><strong>) </strong><span class="foreign"><strong>a damn</strong></span> is by 1760. The adjective is 1775, short for <span class="crossreference">damned</span>; <span class="foreign"><strong>Damn Yankee</strong></span>, the characteristic Southern U.S. term for &quot;Northerner,&quot; is attested by 1812 (as <span class="foreign">damned</span>).
