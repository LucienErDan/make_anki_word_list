<p>In the Middle Ages, the Latin word was used in the Guidonian system for &quot;the lowest note of a scale,&quot; which is its basis (see <span class="crossreference">keynote</span>). <br/><br/>
Perhaps it is related to Middle Low German <span class="foreign">keie</span> &quot;lance, spear&quot; on notion of &quot;tool to cleave with,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ki-</span> &quot;to cleave, split&quot; (cognates: German <span class="foreign">Keil</span> &quot;wedge,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">us-kijans</span> &quot;come forth,&quot; said of seed sprouts, <span class="foreign">keinan</span> &quot;to germinate&quot;). But Liberman writes, &quot;The original meaning of <span class="foreign">*kaig-jo-</span> was presumably &#x27;*pin with a twisted end.&#x27; Words with the root <span class="foreign">*kai-</span> followed by a consonant meaning &#x27;crooked, bent; twisted&#x27; are common only in the North Germanic languages.&quot; Compare also Sanskrit <span class="foreign">kuncika-</span> &quot;key,&quot; from <span class="foreign">kunc-</span> &quot;make crooked.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Modern pronunciation is a northern variant predominating from c. 1700; earlier and in Middle English it often was pronounced &quot;kay.&quot; Meaning &quot;that which holds together other parts&quot; is from 1520s. Meaning &quot;explanation of a solution&quot; (to a set problem, code, etc.) is from c.1600. 
<br/><br/>
The musical sense originally was &quot;tone, note&quot; (mid-15c.). In music theory, the sense developed 17c. to &quot;sum of the melodic and harmonic relationships in the tones of a scale,&quot; also &quot;melodic and harmonic relationships centering on a given tone.&quot; Probably this is based on a translation of Latin <span class="foreign">clavis</span> &quot;key,&quot; used by Guido for &quot;lowest tone of a scale,&quot; or French <span class="foreign">clef</span> (see <span class="crossreference">clef</span>; also see <span class="crossreference">keynote</span>). Sense of &quot;mechanism on a musical instrument operated by the player&#x27;s fingers&quot; is from c. 1500, probably also suggested by uses of <span class="foreign">clavis</span>.
