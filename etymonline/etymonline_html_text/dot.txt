The Latin neuter past participle <span class="foreign">punctum</span> was used as a noun, meaning &quot;small hole made by pricking,&quot; subsequently extended to anything that looked like one, hence, &quot;dot, particle,&quot; <br/><br/>
Meanwhile the Latin fem. past participle of <span class="foreign">pungere</span> was <span class="foreign">puncta</span>, which was used in Medieval Latin to mean &quot;sharp tip,&quot; and became Old French <span class="foreign">pointe</span> &quot;point of a weapon, vanguard of an army,&quot; which also passed into English, early 14c.
<br/><br/>
The senses have merged in English, but remain distinct in French. Extended senses are from the notion of &quot;minute, single, or separate items in an extended whole.&quot; Meaning &quot;small mark, dot&quot; in English is mid-14c. Meaning &quot;distinguishing feature&quot; is recorded from late 15c. Meaning &quot;a unit of score in a game&quot; is first recorded 1746. As a typeface unit (in Britain and U.S., one twelfth of a pica), it went into use in U.S. 1883. As a measure of weight for precious stones (one one-hundredth of a carat) it is recorded from 1931.

<p>The dot on the &quot;small&quot; letter <span class="foreign">-i-</span> began to appear in 11c.
