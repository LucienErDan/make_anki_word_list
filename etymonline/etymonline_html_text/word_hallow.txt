poem (1785, and he attached a footnote explaining it), but it probably dates to 17c. in Scotland and is attested as the name of a tune in 1724. The tune is mentioned again in an English-Scots songbook (&quot;The Chearful Companion&quot;) in 1783, and Burns was not the first to describe the customs in print.</p>
<blockquote>Hallow-E&#x27;en, or Holy Eve, is the evening previous to the celebration of All Saints. That it is propitious to the rites of divination, is an opinion still common in many parts of Scotland. [John Main, footnote to his poem &quot;Hallow-E&#x27;en,&quot; Glasgow, 1783]</blockquote>
<p>It is a Scottish shortening of <span class="foreign">Allhallow-even</span> &quot;Eve of All Saints, last night of October&quot; (1550s), the last night of the year in the old Celtic calendar, where it was <span class="foreign">Old Year&#x27;s Night</span>, a night for witches. A pagan holiday given a cursory baptism. Otherwise obsolete <span class="foreign">hallow</span> (n.) &quot;holy person, saint,&quot; is from the source of <span class="crossreference">hallow</span> (v.). Also see <span class="crossreference">even</span> (n.), and compare <span class="crossreference">hallows</span>. <span class="foreign">Hallow-day</span> for &quot;All-Saints Day&quot; is from 1590s; earlier was <span class="foreign">halwemesse day</span> (late 13c.).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Halloween#etymonline_v_1399"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of bless" href="/word/bless#etymonline_v_13599">bless (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">Old English <span class="foreign">bletsian</span>, <span class="foreign">bledsian</span>, Northumbrian <span class="foreign">bloedsian</span> &quot;to consecrate by a religious rite, make holy, give thanks,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*blodison</span> &quot;hallow with blood, mark with blood,&quot; from <span class="foreign">*blotham</span> &quot;blood&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">blood</span> (n.)). Originally a blood sprinkling on pagan altars.
<br/><br/>
This word was chosen in Old English bibles to translate Latin <span class="foreign">benedicere</span> and Greek <span class="foreign">eulogein</span>, both of which have a ground sense of &quot;to speak well of, to praise,&quot; but were used in Scripture to translate Hebrew <span class="foreign">brk</span> &quot;to bend (the knee), worship, praise, invoke blessings.&quot; L.R. Palmer (&quot;The Latin Language&quot;) writes, &quot;There is nothing surprising in the semantic development of a word denoting originally a special ritual act into the more generalized meanings to &#x27;sacrifice,&#x27; &#x27;worship,&#x27; &#x27;bless,&#x27; &quot; and he compares Latin <span class="foreign">immolare</span> (see <span class="crossreference">immolate</span>).
<br/><br/>
The meaning shifted in late Old English toward &quot;pronounce or make happy, prosperous, or fortunate&quot; by resemblance to unrelated <span class="crossreference">bliss</span>. Meaning &quot;invoke or pronounce God&#x27;s blessing upon&quot; is from early 14c. No cognates in other languages. Related: <span class="crossreference">Blessed</span>; <span class="crossreference">blessing</span>.
