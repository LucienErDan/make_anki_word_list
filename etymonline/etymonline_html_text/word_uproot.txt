<p>Early 14c. as &quot;to pick, pull off, gather&quot; (fruit, flowers, berries, leaves, petals, etc.); mid-14c. as &quot;to uproot, pull up&quot; (of teeth, weeds, etc.). Sense of &quot;to draw (to oneself), attract&quot; is from c. 1400; sense of &quot;to pluck at with the fingers&quot; is from c. 1400; meaning &quot;tear to pieces&quot; is mid-15c. By late 16c. it had replaced <span class="crossreference">draw</span> (v.) in these senses. Common in slang 19c.-20c.; Bartlett (1859) has <span class="foreign">to pull foot</span> &quot;walk fast; run;&quot; <span class="foreign">pull it</span> &quot;to run.&quot; To <span class="foreign">pull up</span> &quot;check a course of action&quot; is from 1808, figurative of the lifting of the reins in horse-riding.</p>

<p>To <span class="foreign">pull (someone&#x27;s) chain</span> in figurative sense is from 1974, perhaps on the notion of a captive animal; the expression was also used for &quot;to contact&quot; (someone), on the notion of the chain that operates a signaling mechanism. To <span class="foreign">pull (someone&#x27;s) leg</span> is from 1882, perhaps on notion of &quot;playfully tripping&quot; (compare <span class="foreign">pull the long bow</span> &quot;exaggerate,&quot; 1830, and pulling someone&#x27;s leg also sometimes was described as a way to awaken a sleeping person in a railway compartment, ship&#x27;s berth, etc.). Thornton&#x27;s &quot;American Glossary&quot; (1912) has <span class="foreign">pull</span> (n.) &quot;a jest&quot; (<span class="foreign">to have a pull at</span> (someone)), which it identifies as &quot;local&quot; and illustrates with an example from the Massachusetts &quot;Spy&quot; of May 21, 1817, which identifies it as &quot;a Georgian phrase.&quot; To <span class="foreign">pull (one&#x27;s) punches</span> is from 1920 in pugilism, from 1921 figuratively.</p>

<p>To <span class="foreign">pull in</span> &quot;arrive&quot; (1892) and <span class="foreign">pull out</span> &quot;depart&quot; (1868) are from the railroads. To <span class="foreign">pull (something) off</span> &quot;accomplish, succeed at&quot; is originally in sporting, &quot;to win the prize money&quot; (1870). To <span class="foreign">pull (something) on (someone)</span> is from 1916; to <span class="foreign">pull (something) out of one&#x27;s ass</span> is Army slang from 1970s.
