<p>This is apparently from a root <span class="foreign">*nauti-</span> &quot;death, to be exhausted,&quot; source also of Old English <span class="foreign">ne, neo</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">na</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">naus</span> &quot;corpse;&quot; Old Irish <span class="foreign">naunae</span> &quot;famine, shortage,&quot; Old Cornish <span class="foreign">naun</span> &quot;corpse;&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">navi</span> &quot;corpse,&quot; <span class="foreign">nazda</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">nuzda</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">nędza</span> &quot;misery, distress;&quot; Old Prussian <span class="foreign">nowis</span> &quot;corpse,&quot; <span class="foreign">nautin</span> &quot;need, distress,&quot; <span class="foreign">nawe</span> &quot;death;&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">novyti</span> &quot;to torture, kill,&quot; <span class="foreign">nove</span> &quot;death.&quot; As it is attested only in Germanic, Celtic, and Balto-Slavic, it might be non-PIE, from a regional substrate language.</p>

<p>From 12c. as &quot;lack of something that is necessary or important; state or condition of needing something;&quot; also &quot;a necessary act, required work or duty.&quot; Meaning &quot;extreme poverty, destitution, want of means of subsistence&quot; is from early 14c. </p>

<p>The more common Old English word for &quot;need, necessity, want&quot; was <span class="foreign">ðearf</span>, but they were connected via a notion of &quot;trouble, pain,&quot; and the two formed a compound, <span class="foreign">niedðearf</span> &quot;need, necessity, compulsion, thing needed.&quot; <span class="foreign">Nied</span> also might have been influenced by Old English <span class="foreign">neod</span> &quot;desire, longing,&quot; which often was spelled the same. <span class="foreign">Nied</span> was common in Old English compounds, such as <span class="foreign">niedfaru</span> &quot;compulsory journey,&quot; a euphemism for &quot;death;&quot; <span class="foreign">niedhæmed</span> &quot;rape&quot; (the second element being an Old English word meaning &quot;sexual intercourse&quot;);  <span class="foreign">niedling</span> &quot;slave.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/need#etymonline_v_2366"><span>Relate entries &amp; Others suggest a possible Scandinavian etymology from North Germanic <span class="foreign">*kut-</span> (source also of Swedish dialectal <span class="foreign">kuta</span> &quot;to cut,&quot; <span class="foreign">kuta</span> &quot;knife,&quot; <p>It has largely displaced Old English <span class="foreign">ceorfan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">carve</span> (v.)), <span class="foreign">snian</span>, and <span class="foreign">scieran</span> (see <span class="crossreference">shear</span>). The past participle is also <span class="foreign">cut</span>, though <span class="foreign">cutted</span> sometimes has been used since Middle English.</p>
<p>From early 14c. as &quot;to make or fashion by cutting or carving.&quot; From c. 1400 as &quot;to intersect or cross.&quot; From early 15c. as &quot;abridge or shorten by omitting a part.&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to wound the sensibilities of&quot; is from 1580s (to <span class="foreign">cut the heart</span> in the same sense is attested from early 14c.). Sense of &quot;sever connection or relations with&quot; is from 1630s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to be absent without excuse&quot; is British university slang from 1794. Colloquial or slang sense of &quot;move off with directness and rapidity&quot; is from 1580s. Meaning &quot;divide (a deck of cards) at random into parts before the deal&quot; to prevent cheating is from 1530s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to dilute, adulterate&quot; (liquor, etc.) is by 1930. Colloquial sense of &quot;to divide or share&quot; is by 1928, perhaps an image from meat-carving at table. As a director&#x27;s call to halt recording or performing, by 1931 (in an article about Pete, the bulldog with the black-ringed eye in the Hal Roach studios shorts, who was said to know the word). The sense of &quot;perform, execute&quot; (c. 1600) is in <span class="foreign">cut capers</span> &quot;frisk about;&quot; <span class="foreign">cut a dash</span> &quot;make a display.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut down</strong></span> is from late 14c. as &quot;to fell;&quot; by 1821 as &quot;to slay&quot; (as with a sword); 1857 as &quot;to curtail.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (someone or something) down to size</strong></span> is from 1821 as &quot;reduce to suitable dimensions;&quot; the figurative sense, &quot;reduce to the proper level of importance,&quot; is by 1927.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut in</strong></span> &quot;enter suddenly and unceremoniously&quot; is from 1610s; sense of &quot;suddenly join in conversation, interrupt&quot; is by 1830. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut up</strong></span> &quot;cut in pieces&quot; is from 1570s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut back</strong></span> is from 1871 as &quot;prune by cutting off shoots,&quot; 1913 in cinematography, &quot;return to a previous scene by repeating a part of it,&quot; 1943 as &quot;reduce, decrease&quot; (of expenditures, etc.). To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (something) short</strong></span> &quot;abridge, curtail, interrupt&quot; is from 1540s.</p>
<p>In nautical use to <span class="foreign"><strong>cut a feather</strong></span> (1620s) is to move so fast as to make water foam under the bow. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut and run</strong></span> (1704) also is originally nautical, &quot;cut cable and set sail immediately,&quot; as in an emergency, hence, generally, &quot;to make off suddenly.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut the teeth</strong></span> &quot;have the teeth grow through the gums&quot; as an infant is from 1670s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut both ways</strong></span> in the figurative sense of &quot;have a good and bad effect&quot; is from c. 1600. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut loose</strong></span> &quot;set (something) free&quot; is by 1828; intransitive sense &quot;begin to act freely&quot; is by 1909.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Cut it out</strong></span> &quot;remove (something) by or as if by cutting&quot; yielded a figurative use in the command <span class="foreign">cut it out!</span> &quot;Stop! That&#x27;s enough!&quot; by 1933. The evolution seems to have begun earlier. A piece attributed to the Chicago Live Stock World that made the rounds in trade publications 1901-02 begins:</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">When you get &#x27;hot&#x27; about something and vow you are going to rip something or somebody up the back--cut it out.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">If you feel disposed to try the plan of building yourself up by tearing some one else down--cut it out.</blockquote>
