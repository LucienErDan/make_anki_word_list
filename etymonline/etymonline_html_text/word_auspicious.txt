<p>The planet was discovered and identified as such in 1781 by Sir William Herschel (it had been observed before, but mistaken for a star; in 1690 John Flamsteed cataloged it as 34 Tauri); Herschel proposed calling it <span class="foreign">Georgium Sidus</span>, literally &quot;George&#x27;s Star,&quot; in honour of his patron, King George III of England.</p>
<blockquote>I cannot but wish to take this opportunity of expressing my sense of gratitude, by giving the name of Georgium Sidus ... to a star which (with respect to us) first began to shine under His auspicious reign. [Sir William Herschel, 1783]</blockquote>
<p>The planet was known in English in 1780s as the <span class="foreign">Georgian Planet</span>; French astronomers began calling <span class="foreign">Herschel</span>, and ultimately German astronomer Johann Bode proposed <span class="foreign">Uranus</span> as in conformity with other planet names. However, the name didn&#x27;t come into common usage until c. 1850.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Uranus#etymonline_v_25116"><span>Relate
