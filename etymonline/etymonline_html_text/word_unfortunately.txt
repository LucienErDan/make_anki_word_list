<blockquote>The passage on the nature of comedy in the <span class="foreign">Poetic</span> of Aristotle is unfortunately lost, but if we can trust stray hints on the subject, his definition of comedy (which applied mainly to Menander) ran parallel to that of tragedy, and described the art as a purification of certain affections of our nature, not by terror and pity, but by laughter and ridicule. [Rev. J.P. Mahaffy, &quot;A History of Classical Greek Literature,&quot; London, 1895]</blockquote>
<p>The origin of Greek <span class="foreign">komos</span> is uncertain; perhaps it is from a PIE <span class="foreign">*komso-</span> &quot;praise,&quot; and cognate with Sanskrit <span class="foreign">samsa</span> &quot;praise, judgment.&quot; Beekes suggests Pre-Greek. The old derivation from <span class="foreign">kome</span> &quot;village&quot; is not now regarded.</p>
<p>The classical sense of the word was &quot;amusing play or performance with a happy ending,&quot; which is similar to the modern one, but in the Middle Ages the word meant poems and stories generally (albeit ones with happy endings), such as Dante&#x27;s <span class="foreign">&quot;Commedia.&quot;</span> The revival of learning 16c. recovered the ancient comedies and shifted the sense of the word to &quot;branch of drama addressing primarily the humorous and ridiculous&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">tragedy</span>). In 18c. this was somewhat restricted to &quot;humorous, but not grossly comical, drama&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">farce</span>).</p>

<blockquote><span class="foreign">Comedy</span> aims at entertaining by the fidelity with which it presents life as we know it, <span class="foreign">farce</span> at raising laughter by the outrageous absurdity of the situation or characters exhibited, &amp; <span class="foreign">burlesque</span> at tickling the fancy of the audience by caricaturing plays or actors with whose style it is familiar. [Fowler]</blockquote>

<p>Meaning &quot;comic play or drama&quot; is from 1550s (the first modern comedy in English was said to be Nicholas Udall&#x27;s &quot;Roister Doister&quot;). Extended sense &quot;humorous or comic incident or events in life&quot; is from 1560s. Generalized sense of &quot;quality of being amusing&quot; dates from 1877. </p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/comedy#etymonline_v_15861"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of turkey" href="/word/turkey#etymonline_v_18833">turkey (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1540s, originally &quot;guinea fowl&quot; (<span class="foreign">Numida meleagris</span>), a bird imported from Madagascar via Turkey, and called <span class="foreign">guinea fowl</span> when brought by Portuguese traders from West Africa. The larger North American bird (<span class="foreign">Meleagris gallopavo</span>) was domesticated by the Aztecs, introduced to Spain by conquistadors (1523) and thence to wider Europe. The word <span class="foreign">turkey</span> first was applied to it in English 1550s because it was identified with or treated as a species of the guinea fowl, and/or because it got to the rest of Europe from Spain by way of North Africa, then under Ottoman (Turkish) rule. Indian corn was originally <span class="foreign">turkey corn</span> or <span class="foreign">turkey wheat</span> in English for the same reason.</p>
<p>The Turkish name for it is <span class="foreign">hindi</span>, literally &quot;Indian,&quot; probably influenced by Middle French <span class="foreign">dinde</span> (c. 1600, contracted from <span class="foreign">poulet d&#x27;inde</span>, literally &quot;chicken from India,&quot; Modern French <span class="foreign">dindon</span>), based on the then-common misconception that the New World was eastern Asia.</p>
<blockquote>After the two birds were distinguished and the names differentiated, <span class="foreign">turkey</span> was erroneously retained for the American bird, instead of the African. From the same imperfect knowledge and confusion <span class="foreign">Melagris</span>, the ancient name of the African fowl, was unfortunately adopted by Linnæus as the generic name of the American bird. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>The New World bird itself reputedly reached England by 1524 at the earliest estimate, though a date in the 1530s seems more likely. The wild turkey, the North American form of the bird, was so called from 1610s. By 1575, turkey was becoming the usual main course at an English Christmas. Meaning &quot;inferior show, failure,&quot; is 1927 in show business slang, probably from the bird&#x27;s reputation for stupidity. Meaning &quot;stupid, ineffectual person&quot; is recorded from 1951. <span class="foreign">Turkey shoot</span> &quot;something easy&quot; is World War II-era, in reference to marksmanship contests where turkeys were tied behind a log with their heads showing as targets. To <span class="foreign">talk turkey</span> (1824) supposedly comes from an old tale of a Yankee attempting to swindle an Indian in dividing up a turkey and a buzzard as food.</p></section></object><a
