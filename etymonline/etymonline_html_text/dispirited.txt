This is not found in Old English, so the word is probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">lagr</span> &quot;low, low-down, short; humble,&quot; or a similar Scandinavian source (compare Swedish <span class="foreign">låg</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">lav</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*lega-</span> &quot;lying flat, low&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">lech</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">lage</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">laag</span> &quot;low,&quot; dialectal German <span class="foreign">läge</span> &quot;flat&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*legh-</span> &quot;to lie down, lay.&quot;</p>
<p>In reference to sounds, &quot;not loud,&quot; also &quot;having a deep pitch,&quot; from c. 1300. Sense of &quot;dejected, dispirited&quot; is attested from 1737. Of prices, from c. 1400. In geographical usage, <span class="foreign">low</span> refers to the part of a country near the sea-shore (c. 1300), as in <span class="foreign"><strong>Low Countries</strong></span> &quot;Holland, Belgium, Luxembourg&quot; (1540s). <span class="foreign"><strong>Low German</strong></span> languages (1845) are so called for being spoken in the lower elevations of old Germany.</p>
