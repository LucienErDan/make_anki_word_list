For first element see <span class="crossreference">arctic</span>; second element is Greek <span class="foreign">ouros</span> &quot;watcher, guardian, ward,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*wer-</span> (3) &quot;perceive, watch out for.&quot; It is fourth-brightest of the fixed stars. The double nature of the great bear/wagon (see <span class="crossreference">Big Dipper</span>) has given two different names to the constellation that follows it: <span class="foreign">Arktouros</span> &quot;bear-ward&quot; and <span class="foreign">Bootes</span> &quot;the wagoner&quot; (from Greek, ultimately from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwou-</span> &quot;ox, bull, cow&quot;).<br/>
<br/>
<span class="foreign">Arcturus</span> in the Bible (Job ix.9 and xxxviii.32) is a mistranslation by Jerome (continued in KJV) of Hebrew <span class="foreign">&#x27;Ayish</span>, which refers to what we see as the &quot;bowl&quot; of the Big Dipper. In Israel and Arabia, the seven stars of the Great Bear seem to have been a bier (the &quot;bowl&quot;) followed by three mourners. In the Septuagint it was translated as <span class="foreign">Pleiada</span>, which is equally incorrect.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Arcturus#etymonline_v_16967"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of bear" href="/word/bear#etymonline_v_8204">bear (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;large carnivorous or omnivorous mammal of the family <span class="foreign">Ursidae</span>,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">bera</span> &quot;a bear,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bero</span>, literally &quot;the brown (one)&quot; (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">björn</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bere</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">beer</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">bero</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bär</span>), usually said to be from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*bher-</span> (2) &quot;bright; brown.&quot; There was perhaps a PIE <span class="foreign">*bheros</span> &quot;dark animal&quot; (compare <span class="crossreference">beaver</span> (n.1) and Greek <span class="foreign">phrynos</span> &quot;toad,&quot; literally &quot;the brown animal&quot;).</p>
<p>Greek <span class="foreign">arktos</span> and Latin <span class="foreign">ursus</span> retain the PIE root word for &quot;bear&quot; (<span class="foreign">*rtko</span>; see <span class="crossreference">arctic</span>), but it is believed to have been ritually replaced in the northern branches because of hunters&#x27; taboo on names of wild animals (compare the Irish equivalent &quot;the good calf,&quot; Welsh &quot;honey-pig,&quot; Lithuanian &quot;the licker,&quot; Russian <span class="foreign">medved</span> &quot;honey-eater&quot;). Others connect the Germanic word with Latin <span class="foreign">ferus</span> &quot;wild,&quot; as if it meant &quot;the wild animal (<span class="foreign">par excellence</span>) of the northern woods.&quot;</p>
<p>Symbolic of Russia since 1794. Used of rude, gruff, uncouth men since 1570s. Stock market meaning &quot;speculator for a fall&quot; is 1709 shortening of <span class="foreign"><strong>bearskin jobber</strong></span> (from the proverb <span class="foreign">sell the bearskin before one has caught the bear</span>); i.e. &quot;one who sells stock for future delivery, expecting that meanwhile prices will fall.&quot; Paired with <span class="foreign">bull</span> from c. 1720. <span class="foreign"><strong>Bear claw</strong></span> as a type of large pastry is from 1942, originally chiefly western U.S. <span class="foreign"><strong>Bear-garden</strong></span> (1590s) was a place where bears were kept for the amusement of spectators.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/bear#etymonline_v_8204"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Charles&#x27;s Wain" href="/word/Charles&#x27;s%20Wain#etymonline_v_8444">Charles&#x27;s Wain (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">Carles wægn</span>, a star-group associated in medieval times with <span class="foreign">Charlemagne</span>, but originally with the nearby bright star <span class="crossreference">Arcturus</span>, which is linked by folk etymology to Latin <span class="foreign">Arturus</span> &quot;Arthur.&quot; Which places the seven-star asterism at the crux of the legendary association (or confusion) of Arthur and Charlemagne. Evidence from Dutch (cited in Grimm, &quot;Teutonic Mythology&quot;) suggests that it might originally have been Woden&#x27;s wagon. More recent names for it are <span class="foreign">the Plough</span> (by 15c., chiefly British) and <span class="foreign">the Dipper</span> (1833, chiefly American).<br/>
<br/>
It is called &quot;the Wagon&quot; in a Mesopotamian text from 1700 B.C.E., and it is mentioned in the Biblical Book of Job. The seven bright stars in the modern constellation Ursa Major have borne a dual identity in Western history at least since Homer&#x27;s time, being seen as both a wagon and a bear: as in Latin <span class="foreign">plaustrum</span> &quot;freight-wagon, ox cart&quot; and <span class="foreign">arctos</span> &quot;bear,&quot; both used of the seven-star pattern, as were equivalent Greek <span class="foreign">amaxa</span> (Attic <span class="foreign">hamaxa</span>) and <span class="foreign">arktos</span>.<br/>
<br/>
The identification with a wagon is easy to see, with four stars as the body and three as the pole. The identification with a bear is more difficult, as the figure has a tail longer than its body. As Allen writes, &quot;The conformation of the seven stars in no way resembles the animal,--indeed the contrary ....&quot; But he suggests the identification &quot;may have arisen from Aristotle&#x27;s idea that its prototype was the only creature that dared invade the frozen north.&quot; The seven stars never were below the horizon in the latitude of the Mediterranean in Homeric and classical times (though not today, due to precession of the equinoxes). See also <span class="crossreference">arctic</span> for the identification of the bear and the north in classical times.<br/>
<br/>
A variety of French and English sources from the early colonial period independently note that many native North American tribes in the northeast had long seen the seven-star group as a bear tracked by three hunters (or a hunter and his two dogs).<br/>
<br/>
Among the Teutonic peoples, it seems to have been only a wagon, not a bear. A 10c. Anglo-Saxon astronomy manual uses the Greek-derived <span class="foreign">Aretos</span>, but mentions that &quot;unlearned men&quot; call it &quot;Charles&#x27;s Wain&quot;:</p>
<blockquote>Arheton hatte an tungol on norð dæle, se haefð seofon steorran, &amp; is for ði oþrum naman ge-hatan septemtrio, þone hatað læwede meon carles-wæn. [&quot;Anglo-Saxon Manual of Astronomy&quot;] </blockquote>
<p>[<span class="foreign">Septemtrio</span>, the seven oxen, was yet another Roman name.] The star picture was not surely identified as a bear in English before late 14c.<br/>
<br/>
The unlearned of today are corrected that the seven stars are not the Great Bear but form only a part of that large constellation. But those who applied the name &quot;Bear&quot; apparently did so originally only to these seven stars, and from Homer&#x27;s time down to Thales, &quot;the Bear&quot; meant just the seven stars. From Rome to Anglo-Saxon England to Arabia to India, ancient astronomy texts mention a supposed duplicate constellation to the northern bear in the Southern Hemisphere, never visible from the north. This perhaps is based on sailors&#x27; tales of the Southern Cross.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
