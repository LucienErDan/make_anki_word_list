<br/><br/>
Etymologically, this word belongs to <span class="foreign">ingenuous</span>, but in 17c. <span class="crossreference">ingenious</span> &quot;intellectual, talented&quot; and <span class="foreign">ingenuous</span> so often were confused (even by Shakespeare) that <span class="foreign">ingenuity</span> in English has come to mean only &quot;capacity for invention or construction.&quot; That sense of this word is first attested 1640s; the word for it in Middle English was <span class="foreign">ingeniosity</span> (the native word is <span class="foreign">craftiness</span>). French <span class="foreign">ingénuité</span>, meanwhile, has evolved through &quot;natural and graceful freedom of manners&quot; to &quot;graceful simplicity&quot; (compare <span class="crossreference">ingenue</span>); for the sense &quot;ingeniousness,&quot; French uses <span class="foreign">ingéniosité</span>.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/ingenuity#etymonline_v_9247"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of frank" href="/word/frank#etymonline_v_11874">frank (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;free, liberal, generous;&quot; 1540s, &quot;outspoken,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">franc</span> &quot;free (not servile); without hindrance, exempt from; sincere, genuine, open, gracious, generous; worthy, noble, illustrious&quot; (12c.), from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">francus</span> &quot;free, at liberty, exempt from service,&quot; as a noun, &quot;a freeman, a Frank&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">Frank</span>).</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Frank</span>, literally, <span class="foreign">free</span>; the freedom may be in regard to one&#x27;s own opinions, which is the same as <span class="foreign">openness</span>, or in regard to things belonging to others, where the freedom may go so far as to be unpleasant, or it may disregard conventional ideas as to reticence. Hence, while <span class="foreign">openness</span> is consistent with timidity, <span class="foreign">frankness</span> implies some degree of boldness. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>A generalization of the tribal name; the connection is that Franks, as the conquering class, alone had the status of freemen in a world that knew only free, captive, or slave. For sense connection of &quot;being one of the nation&quot; and &quot;free,&quot; compare Latin <span class="foreign">liber</span> &quot;free,&quot; from the same root as German <span class="foreign">Leute</span> &quot;nation, people&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">liberal</span> (adj.)) and Slavic &quot;free&quot; words (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">svobodi</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">swobodny</span>, Serbo-Croatian <span class="foreign">slobodan</span>) which are cognates of the first element in English <span class="crossreference">sibling</span> &quot;brother, sister&quot; (in Old English used more generally: &quot;relative, kinsman&quot;).
