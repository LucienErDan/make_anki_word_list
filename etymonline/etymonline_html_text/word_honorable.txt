(mid-13c. as a surname, <span class="foreign">Walter le Onorable</span>, also known as <span class="foreign">Walter Honurable</span>), &quot;worthy of respect or reverence, respectable,&quot; also &quot;signifying or rendering distinction or respect; ensuring good repute or honor,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">onorable</span>, <span class="foreign">honorable</span> &quot;respectable, respectful, civil, courteous,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">honorabilis</span> &quot;that procures honor, estimable, honorable,&quot; from <span class="foreign">honorare</span> &quot;to honor,&quot; from <span class="foreign">honor</span> (see <span class="crossreference">honor</span> (n.)). <blockquote>&quot;Now, George, you must divide the cake honorably with your brother Charlie.&quot;--George: &quot;What is &#x27;honorably,&#x27; mother?&quot; &quot;It means that you must give him the largest piece.&quot;--George: &quot;Then, mother, I should rather Charlie would cut it.&quot; [&quot;Smart Sayings of Bright Children,&quot; collected by Howard Paul, 1886]</blockquote>
<p>As an epithet before the name of a peer, Church or civil official, guild officer, etc., from c.1400. As a noun, &quot;honorable person,&quot; late 14c. Alternative adjective <span class="foreign">honorous</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">honoros</span>) seems not to have survived Middle English.
