Century Dictionary calls the whole thing &quot;probably arbitrary.&quot; Klein thinks the word of Hindu origin (but he credits its introduction into English, mistakenly, to Kipling). Boy Scouts use is from 1920. It is noted earlier as a term in cribbage:</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Jamboree</span> signifies the combination of the five highest cards, as, for example, the two Bowers [jacks], Ace, King, and Queen of trumps in one hand, which entitles the holder to count <span class="foreign">sixteen</span> points. The holder of such a hand, simply announces the fact, as no play is necessary; but should he play the hand as a Jambone, he can count only eight points, whereas he could count sixteen if he played it, or announced it as a Jamboree. <blockquote>The passage on the nature of comedy in the <span class="foreign">Poetic</span> of Aristotle is unfortunately lost, but if we can trust stray hints on the subject, his definition of comedy (which applied mainly to Menander) ran parallel to that of tragedy, and described the art as a purification of certain affections of our nature, not by terror and pity, but by laughter and ridicule. [Rev. J.P. Mahaffy, &quot;A History of Classical Greek Literature,&quot; London, 1895]</blockquote>
<p>The origin of Greek <span class="foreign">komos</span> is uncertain; perhaps it is from a PIE <span class="foreign">*komso-</span> &quot;praise,&quot; and cognate with Sanskrit <span class="foreign">samsa</span> &quot;praise, judgment.&quot; Beekes suggests Pre-Greek. The old derivation from <span class="foreign">kome</span> &quot;village&quot; is not now regarded.</p>
<p>The classical sense of the word was &quot;amusing play or performance with a happy ending,&quot; which is similar to the modern one, but in the Middle Ages the word meant poems and stories generally (albeit ones with happy endings), such as Dante&#x27;s <span class="foreign">&quot;Commedia.&quot;</span> The revival of learning 16c. recovered the ancient comedies and shifted the sense of the word to &quot;branch of drama addressing primarily the humorous and ridiculous&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">tragedy</span>). In 18c. this was somewhat restricted to &quot;humorous, but not grossly comical, drama&quot; (opposed to <span class="foreign">farce</span>).</p>

<blockquote><span class="foreign">Comedy</span> aims at entertaining by the fidelity with which it presents life as we know it, <span class="foreign">farce</span> at raising laughter by the outrageous absurdity of the situation or characters exhibited, &amp; <span class="foreign">burlesque</span> at tickling the fancy of the audience by caricaturing plays or actors with whose style it is familiar. [Fowler]</blockquote>

<p>Meaning &quot;comic play or drama&quot; is from 1550s (the first modern comedy in English was said to be Nicholas Udall&#x27;s &quot;Roister Doister&quot;). Extended sense &quot;humorous or comic incident or events in life&quot; is from 1560s. Generalized sense of &quot;quality of being amusing&quot; dates from 1877. </p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
