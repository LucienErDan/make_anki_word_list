<p>The religious sense is often said to derive from conservative rural adherence to the old gods after the Christianization of Roman towns and cities; but the word in this sense predates that period in Church history, and it is more likely derived from the use of <span class="foreign">paganus</span> in Roman military jargon for &quot;civilian, incompetent soldier,&quot; which Christians (Tertullian, c. 202; Augustine) picked up with the military imagery of the early Church (such as <span class="foreign">milites</span> &quot;soldier of Christ,&quot; etc.). <blockquote><span class="foreign">Pagan</span> and <span class="foreign">heathen</span> are primarily the same in meaning; but <span class="foreign">pagan</span> is sometimes distinctively applied to those nations that, although worshiping false gods, are more cultivated, as the Greeks and Romans, and <span class="foreign">heathen</span> to uncivilized idolaters, as the tribes of Africa. <p>Perhaps literally &quot;dweller on the heath, one inhabiting uncultivated land;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">heath</span> + <span class="crossreference">-en</span> (2).
