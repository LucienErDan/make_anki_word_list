title="Origin and meaning of curtain" href="/word/curtain#etymonline_v_498"><em>curtain</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, <span class="foreign">curtine</span>, &quot;hanging screen of textile fabric used to close an opening or shut out light, enclose a bed, or decorate an altar,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">cortine</span> &quot;curtain, tapestry, drape, blanket,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">cortina</span> &quot;curtain,&quot; but in classical Latin &quot;round vessel, cauldron,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">cortem</span> (older <span class="foreign">cohortem</span>) &quot;enclosure, courtyard&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cohort</span>).</p>
<p>The meaning shift apparently begins with <span class="foreign">cortina</span> being used as a loan-translation of Greek <span class="foreign">aulaia</span> (&quot;curtain&quot;) in the Vulgate (to render Hebrew <span class="foreign">yeriah</span> in Exodus xxvi:1, etc.). The Greek word was connected to <span class="foreign">aule</span> &quot;court,&quot; perhaps because the &quot;door&quot; that led out to the courtyard of a Greek house was a hung cloth.</p>
<p>Figuratively from early 15c. as &quot;something that conceals or screens.&quot; From 1590s as &quot;large sheet used to conceal the stage in a theater.&quot; Many of the figurative senses are from stage plays: <span class="foreign"><strong>Behind the curtain</strong></span> &quot;concealed&quot; is from 1670s; <span class="foreign"><strong>curtains</strong></span> &quot;the end&quot; is by 1912. The theatrical <span class="foreign"><strong>curtain call</strong></span> &quot;appearance of individual performers on stage at the end of a performance to be recognized by the audience&quot; is from 1884. To <span class="foreign"><strong>draw the curtain</strong></span> is from c. 1500 in opposite senses: &quot;to conceal,&quot; and &quot;to reveal.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>Curtain-rod</strong></span> is from c. 1500. An Old English word for &quot;curtain&quot; was <span class="foreign">fleonet</span> &quot;fly-net.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/curtain#etymonline_v_498"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of curtain" href="/word/curtain#etymonline_v_53717"><em>curtain</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;to enclose with or as if with a curtain,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">cortiner</span>, from <span class="foreign">cortine</span> (see <span class="crossreference">curtain</span> (n.)). <blockquote>The new and exquisitely beautiful theatre of Drury-lane has the peculiar contrivance of an <span class="foreign">iron-curtain</span> to secure the audience from all danger, in case of fire on the stage. Miss Farren, in the occasional epilogue, delivered on opening this new theatre, pleasantly informs the spectators that, should flames burst out in the part appropriated to the representation, they may comfort themselves with thinking that nothing can be burnt but the scenery and the actors. [&quot;The Monthly Review,&quot; June 1794]</blockquote>
<p>From 1819 in the figurative sense &quot;impenetrable barrier.&quot; In reference to the Soviet occupation of Eastern Europe, famously coined by Winston Churchill March 5, 1946, in speech at Westminster College, Fulton, Missouri, but it had been used earlier in this context (for example by U.S. bureaucrat Allen W. Dulles at a meeting of the Council on Foreign Relations, Dec. 3, 1945). The phrase had been used in the sense of &quot;barrier at the edge of the sphere of influence of the Soviet Union&quot; from 1920. During World War II, Goebbels used it in German (<span class="foreign">ein eiserner Vorhang</span>) in the same sense. But its popular use in the U.S. dates from Churchill&#x27;s speech.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/iron%20curtain#etymonline_v_12233"><span>Relate entries &amp;
