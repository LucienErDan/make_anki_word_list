<blockquote><strong>shade, shadow</strong>, nn. It seems that the difference in form is fairly to be called an accidental one, the first representing the nominative &amp; the second the oblique cases of the same word. The meanings are as closely parallel or intertwined as might be expected from this original identity, the wonder being that, with a differentiation so vague, each form should have maintained its existence by the side of the other. [Fowler]</blockquote>
<p>Figurative use in reference to comparative obscurity is from 1640s. Meaning &quot;a ghost&quot; is from 1610s; dramatic (or mock-dramatic) expression <span class="foreign">&quot;shades of _____&quot;</span> to invoke or acknowledge a memory is from 1818, from the &quot;ghost&quot; sense. Meaning &quot;lamp cover&quot; is from 1780. Sense of &quot;window blind&quot; first recorded 1845. Meaning &quot;cover to protect the eyes&quot; is from 1801. Meaning &quot;grade of color&quot; first recorded 1680s; that of &quot;degree or gradiation of darkness in a color&quot; is from 1680s (compare <span class="crossreference">nuance</span>, from French <span class="foreign">nue</span> &quot;cloud&quot;). <p>Meaning &quot;not barbarous, civilized&quot; is from 1550s. Specifically &quot;relating to the commonwealth as secularly organized&quot; (as opposed to <span class="foreign">military</span> or <span class="foreign">ecclesiastical</span>) by 1610s. Meaning &quot;relating to the citizen in his relation to the commonwealth or to fellow citizens&quot; also is from 1610s.</p>
<blockquote>The word civil has about twelve different meanings; it is applied to all manner of objects, which are perfectly disparate. As opposed to <span class="foreign">criminal</span>, it means all law not criminal. As opposed to ecclesiastical, it means all law not ecclesiastical: as opposed to military, it means all law not military, and so on. [John Austin, &quot;Lectures on Jurisprudence,&quot; 1873]</blockquote>
<p>The sense of &quot;polite&quot; was in classical Latin, but English did not pick up this nuance of the word until late 16c., and it has tended to descend in meaning to &quot;meeting minimum standards of courtesy.&quot; &quot;<span class="foreign">Courteous</span> is thus more commonly said of superiors, <span class="foreign">civil</span> of inferiors, since it implies or suggests the possibility of incivility or rudeness&quot; [OED].</p>
