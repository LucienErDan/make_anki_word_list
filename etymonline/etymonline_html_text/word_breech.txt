c. 1200, a double plural (also <span class="foreign">breechen</span>, and singular <span class="foreign">breech</span>), from Old English <span class="foreign">brec</span> &quot;breeches,&quot; which already was plural of <span class="foreign">broc</span> &quot;garment for the legs and trunk,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*brokiz</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">brok</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">broek</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">brog</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">bruoh</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bruch</span>, obsolete since 18c. except in Swiss dialect), perhaps from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*bhreg-</span> &quot;to break.&quot; The etymological notion would be of a garment &quot;forked&quot; or &quot;split.&quot; The singular <span class="foreign">breech</span> survived into 17c., but the word is now always used in the plural.</p>
<p>The Proto-Germanic word is a parallel form to Celtic <span class="foreign">*bracca</span>, source (via Gaulish) of Latin <span class="foreign">braca</span> (source of French <span class="foreign">braies</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">braca</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">braga</span>). Some propose that the Germanic word group is borrowed from Gallo-Latin, others that the Celtic was from Germanic, but OED writes that the Proto-Germanic noun &quot;has all the markings of an original Teutonic word.&quot;</p>
<p>Classical <span class="foreign">bracae</span> were part of the characteristic garb of Gauls and Orientals; they were not worn by Greeks or Romans until the end of the republic. After 1 c. they came into use at first among military forces stationed in cold climates and were adopted generally toward the end of the empire, though they never seem to have been much in favor in Rome proper.</p>
<p>Expanded sense of &quot;lower part of the body, part of the body covered by breeches, posterior&quot; led to senses in childbirthing (1670s) and gunnery (&quot;the part of a firearm behind the bore,&quot; 1570s).
