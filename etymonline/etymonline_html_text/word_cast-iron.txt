<p>This perhaps is an early borrowing of Celtic <span class="foreign">*isarnon</span> (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">iarn</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">haiarn</span>), which Watkins suggests is from PIE <span class="foreign">*is-(e)ro-</span> &quot;powerful, holy,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*eis</span> &quot;strong&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">isirah</span> &quot;vigorous, strong,&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">ieros</span> &quot;strong&quot;), on the notion of &quot;holy metal&quot; or &quot;strong metal&quot; (in contrast to softer bronze).</p>
<p>It was both an adjective and a noun in Old English, but in form it is an adjective. The alternative <span class="foreign">isen</span> survived into early Middle English as <span class="foreign">izen</span>. In southern England the Middle English word tended to be <span class="foreign">ire</span>, <span class="foreign">yre</span>, with loss of <span class="foreign">-n</span>, perhaps regarded as an inflection; in the north and Scotland, however, the word tended to be contracted to <span class="foreign">irn</span>, <span class="foreign">yrn</span>, still detectable in dialect.</p>
<blockquote>Right so as whil that Iren is hoot men sholden smyte. [Chaucer, c. 1386]</blockquote>
<p>Chemical symbol <span class="foreign">Fe</span> is from the Latin word for the metal, <span class="foreign">ferrum</span> (see <span class="crossreference">ferro-</span>). Meaning &quot;metal device used to press or smooth clothes&quot; is from 1610s. Meaning &quot;golf club with an iron head, 1842. To <span class="foreign">have (too) many irons in the fire</span> &quot;to be doing too much at once&quot; is from 1540s. <span class="foreign">Iron lung</span> &quot;artificial respiration tank&quot; is from 1932. The <span class="foreign">iron crown</span> was that of the ancient kings of Lombardy, with a thin band of iron in the gold, said to have been forged from a nail of Christ&#x27;s Cross. <span class="foreign">Iron horse</span> &quot;railroad locomotive&quot; is from an 1839 poem. <span class="foreign">Iron maiden</span>, instrument of torture, is from 1837 (probably translating German <span class="foreign">eiserne jungfrau</span>). The unidentified French political prisoner known as the <span class="foreign">man in the iron mask</span> died in the Bastille in 1703. In British history, Wellington was called the <span class="foreign">Iron Duke</span> by 1832.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/iron#etymonline_v_12229"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of cast" href="/word/cast#etymonline_v_46988"><em>cast</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-13c., &quot;a throw, an act of throwing,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">cast</span> (v.). <br/>
The sense of &quot;a throw&quot; carried an idea of &quot;the form the thing takes after it has been thrown,&quot; which led to widespread and varied meanings, such as &quot;group of actors in a play&quot; (1630s). OED finds 42 distinct noun meaning and 83 verbal ones, with many sub-definitions. Many of the figurative senses converged in a general meaning &quot;sort, kind, style&quot; (mid-17c.). Meaning &quot;model made from taking an impression of an object&quot; is from c. 1500. A <span class="foreign">cast</span> in the eye &quot;slight squint&quot; (early 14c.) preserves the older verbal sense of &quot;warp, turn,&quot; via the notion of &quot;permanent motion or turn.&quot; As &quot;plaster molded around an injured or diseased part,&quot; by 1883.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/cast#etymonline_v_46988"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of cast" href="/word/cast#etymonline_v_5451"><em>cast</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, &quot;to throw, throw violently, fling, hurl,&quot; from a Scandinavian source akin to Old Norse <span class="foreign">kasta</span> &quot;to throw&quot; (cognate with Swedish <span class="foreign">kasta</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">kaste</span>, North Frisian <span class="foreign">kastin</span>), of uncertain origin.
