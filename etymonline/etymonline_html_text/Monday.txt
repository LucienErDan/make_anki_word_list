class="foreign">monedai</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">mōndæg</span>, contraction of <span class="foreign">mōnandæg</span> &quot;Monday,&quot; literally &quot;day of the moon,&quot; A common Germanic name (compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">manandagr</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">monendei</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">maandag</span>, German <span class="foreign">Montag</span>). All are loan-translations of Late Latin <span class="foreign">Lunæ dies</span>, which also is the source of the day name in Romance languages (French <span class="foreign">lundi</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">lunedi</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">lunes</span>), itself a loan-translation of Greek <span class="foreign">Selēnēs hēmera</span>. The name for this day in Slavic tongues generally means &quot;day after Sunday.&quot;</p>

<p>Not originally applied to humorous moods or speech or conduct, yet the word had a much wider senses in Middle English than modern: &quot;pleasant-sounding&quot; (of animal voices), &quot;fine&quot; (of weather), &quot;handsome&quot; (of dress), &quot;pleasant-tasting&quot; (of herbs). The evolution of the modern senses is probably via the meaning &quot;pleased by a certain event or situation or state of things&quot; (c. 1200). Of persons, &quot;cheerful by disposition or nature; playfully cheerful, enlivened with gladness or good spirits,&quot; by mid-14c.</p>
