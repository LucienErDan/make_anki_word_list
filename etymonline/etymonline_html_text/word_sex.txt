<blockquote><span class="foreign">Secus</span> seems the more original formation, but it is strange that the older texts only know <span class="foreign">sexus</span>. The modern meaning of <span class="foreign">sectiō</span> &#x27;division&#x27; suggests that <span class="foreign">sec/xus</span> might derive from <span class="foreign">secāre</span> &#x27;to sever&#x27;, but the morphology remains unclear: does <span class="foreign">sexus</span> go back to an s-present <span class="foreign">*sek-s-</span> &#x27;to cut up&#x27;, or was it derived from a form <span class="foreign">*sek-s-</span> of the putative s-stem underlying <span class="foreign">secus</span>?  [Michiel de Vaan, &quot;Etymological Dictionary of Latin and the other Italic Languages,&quot; Leiden, 2008]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;quality of being male or female&quot; first recorded 1520s. Meaning &quot;sexual intercourse&quot; first attested 1929 (in writings of D.H. Lawrence); meaning &quot;genitalia&quot; is attested from 1938. <span class="foreign">Sex appeal</span> attested by 1904.</p>
<blockquote>For the raw sex appeal of the burlesque &quot;shows&quot; there is no defense, either. These &quot;shows&quot; should be under official supervision, at the least, and boys beneath the age of eighteen forbidden, perhaps, to attend their performance, just as we forbid the sale of liquors to minors. [Walter Prichard Eaton, &quot;At the New Theatre and Others: The American Stage, Its Problems and Performances,&quot; Boston, 1910]</blockquote>
