<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Dark meat</strong></span> and <span class="foreign"><strong>light meat</strong></span> in reference to the meat of fowls, based on the color when cooked, were popularized 19c., supposedly as euphemisms for <span class="foreign">leg</span> or <span class="foreign">thigh</span> and <span class="foreign">breast</span>, but earliest sources use both sets of terms without apparent embarrassment.</p>
<blockquote>The choicest parts of a turkey are the side bones, the breast, and the thigh bones. The breast and wings are called light meat; the thigh-bones and side-bones dark meat. When a person declines expressing a preference, it is polite to help to both kinds. [Lydia Maria Child, &quot;The American Frugal Housewife,&quot; Boston, 1835]</blockquote>
<p>First record of <span class="foreign"><strong>meat loaf</strong></span> is from 1876. <span class="foreign"><strong>Meat-market</strong></span> &quot;place where one looks for sex partners&quot; is from 1896 (<span class="foreign">meat</span> in various sexual senses of &quot;penis, vagina, body regarded as a sex object, prostitute&quot; are attested from 1590s; Old English for &quot;meat-market&quot; was <span class="foreign">flæsccyping</span> (&#x27;flesh-cheaping&#x27;)); slang <span class="foreign"><strong>meat wagon</strong></span> &quot;ambulance&quot; is from 1920, American English slang, said to date from World War I (in a literal sense by 1857). <span class="foreign"><strong>Meat-grinder</strong></span> is by 1858 in the literal sense &quot;device for grinding meat;&quot; in the figurative sense it is attested by 1951. <span class="foreign"><strong>Meat-hook</strong></span> is by 1812; in the colloquial transferred sense &quot;arm&quot; it is attested by
