title="Origin and meaning of ideology" href="/word/ideology#etymonline_v_1485"><em>ideology</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1796, &quot;science of ideas,&quot; originally &quot;philosophy of the mind which derives knowledge from the senses&quot; (as opposed to <span class="foreign">metaphysics</span>), from French <span class="foreign">idéologie</span> &quot;study or science of ideas,&quot; coined by French philosopher Destutt de Tracy (1754-1836) from <span class="foreign">idéo-</span> &quot;of ideas,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">idea</span> (see <span class="crossreference">idea</span>) + <span class="foreign">-logie</span> (see <span class="crossreference">-logy</span>). With connective <span class="foreign">-o-</span> because the elements are Greek and the Greek combining vowel is <span class="foreign">-o-</span> for nouns of all declensions. Destutt published his <span class="foreign">Eléments d&#x27;idéologie</span> 1801-1815.</p>
<blockquote>The term <span class="foreign">ideology</span> did not become widely employed in the nineteenth century, however, and I have not found that Emerson ever used it. It was only after the appearance of Karl Marx&#x27;s long unpublished <span class="foreign">The German Ideology</span> and Karl Mannheim&#x27;s <span class="foreign">Ideology and Utopia</span> in the period between the world wars of the twentieth century that the term became an omnipresent one. [Lewis P. Simpson, &quot;Mind and the American Civil War,&quot; 1989]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;systematic set of ideas, doctrines through which the world is interpreted&quot; was in use in English by 1907, earliest in socialist and communist writing, with reference to class; from 1918 it came to be used of socialism and communism themselves (along with fascism) and later more broadly still.</p>

<p>As a noun, &quot;one who is at home all over the world, a cosmopolite,&quot; 1640s. As the name of a vodka-based cocktail popular in 1990s (due to &quot;Sex and the City&quot; TV program) from late 1980s (the drink itself seems to date to the 1970s).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Cosmopolitanism</span> in reference to an ideology that considers all humans as a single community is recorded by 1828. It took on a negative tinge in mid-20c., suggesting an undermining of indigenous and national societies and often tied to the supposed influence of the Jews.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/cosmopolitan#etymonline_v_19127"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Herr" href="/word/Herr#etymonline_v_9200">Herr </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>German equivalent of <span class="foreign">Mister</span> (but also used without a name), 1650s, originally &quot;nobler, superior,&quot; from Middle High German <span class="foreign">herre</span>, from Old High German <span class="foreign">herro</span>, comparative of <span class="foreign">hēr</span> &quot;noble, worthy, important, exalted,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*kei-</span> (2), a color adjective (see <span class="crossreference">hue</span> (n.1)), in suffixed form <span class="foreign">*koi-ro-</span> here meaning &quot;gray, hoary,&quot; hence &quot;gray-haired, venerable.&quot; Cognate with Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hera</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">heer</span>; perhaps in this usage a loan-translation of Latin <span class="foreign">senior</span> in the High German area that spread into other Germanic languages. Hence also <span class="foreign">Herrenvolk</span> &quot;master race,&quot; the concept of the German people in Nazi ideology.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Herr#etymonline_v_9200"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Aryan" href="/word/Aryan#etymonline_v_17058">Aryan </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1600, as a term in classical history, from Latin <span class="foreign">Arianus</span>, <span class="foreign">Ariana</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">Aria</span>, <span class="foreign">Areia</span>, names applied in classical times to the eastern part of ancient Persia and to its inhabitants. Ancient Persians used the name in reference to themselves (Old Persian <span class="foreign">ariya-</span>), hence <span class="crossreference">Iran</span>. Ultimately from Sanskrit <span class="foreign">arya-</span> &quot;compatriot;&quot; in later language &quot;noble, of good family.&quot;</p>
<p>Also the name Sanskrit-speaking invaders of India gave themselves in the ancient texts. Thus it was the word early 19c. European philologists (Friedrich Schlegel, 1819, who linked it with German <span class="foreign">Ehre</span> &quot;honor&quot;) applied to the ancient people we now call Indo-Europeans, suspecting that this is what they called themselves. This use is attested in English from 1851. In German from 1845 it was specifically contrasted to <span class="foreign">Semitic</span> (Lassen).</p>
<p>German philologist Max Müller (1823-1900) popularized <span class="foreign">Aryan</span> in his writings on comparative linguistics, recommending it as the name (replacing <span class="foreign">Indo-European</span>, <span class="foreign">Indo-Germanic</span>, <span class="crossreference">Caucasian</span>, <span class="crossreference">Japhetic</span>) for the group of related, inflected languages connected with these peoples, mostly found in Europe but also including Sanskrit and Persian. The spelling <span class="foreign">Arian</span> was used in this sense from 1839 (and is more philologically correct), but it caused confusion with <span class="crossreference">Arian</span>, the term in ecclesiastical history.</p>
<blockquote>The terms for God, for house, for father, mother, son, daughter, for dog and cow, for heart and tears, for axe and tree, identical in all the Indo-European idioms, are like the watchwords of soldiers. We challenge the seeming stranger; and whether he answer with the lips of a Greek, a German, or an Indian, we recognize him as one of ourselves. [Müller, &quot;History of Ancient Sanskrit Literature,&quot; 1859]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Aryan</span> was gradually replaced in comparative linguistics c. 1900 by <span class="crossreference">Indo-European</span>, except when used to distinguish Indo-European languages of India from non-Indo-European ones. From the 1920s <span class="foreign">Aryan</span> began to be used in Nazi ideology to mean &quot;member of a Caucasian Gentile race of Nordic type.&quot; As an ethnic designation, however, it is properly limited to Indo-Iranians (most justly to the latter) and has fallen from general academic use since the Nazis adopted it.</p></section></object><a data-more="true"
