title="Origin and meaning of body" href="/word/body#etymonline_v_13683"><em>body</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">bodig</span> &quot;trunk of a man or beast, physical structure of a human or animal; material frame, material existence of a human; main or principal part of anything,&quot; related to Old High German <span class="foreign">botah</span>, but otherwise of unknown origin. Not elsewhere in Germanic, and the word has died out in German (replaced by <span class="foreign">Leib</span>, originally &quot;life,&quot; and <span class="foreign">Körper</span>, from Latin), &quot;but in English <span class="foreign">body</span> remains as a great and important word&quot; [OED].</p>
<p>Extension to &quot;a person, a human being&quot; is from c. 1300. Meaning &quot;main part&quot; of anything was in late Old English, hence its use in reference to vehicles (1520s). From 1580s as &quot;part of the dress which covers the body.&quot; From 1590s as &quot;main part of a group, any number of individuals spoken of collectively.&quot; From 1660s as &quot;main portion of a document.&quot; Contrasted with <span class="foreign">soul</span> at least since mid-13c. Meaning &quot;corpse&quot; (&quot;dead body&quot;) is from c. 1200. Transferred to matter generally in Middle English (as in <span class="foreign">heavenly body</span>, late 14c.).</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Body politic</strong></span> &quot;the nation, the state, whole body of people living under an organized government&quot; first recorded late 15c., with French word order. <span class="foreign"><strong>Body image</strong></span> was coined 1934. <span class="foreign"><strong>Body count</strong></span> &quot;number of enemy killed in battle or otherwise&quot; is from 1968, from the Vietnam War. <span class="foreign"><strong>Body language</strong></span> is attested from 1967, perhaps from French <span class="foreign">langage corporel</span> (1966). <span class="foreign"><strong>Body-snatcher</strong></span> &quot;one who secretly disinters the bodies of the recently dead for dissection&quot; is from 1834.
