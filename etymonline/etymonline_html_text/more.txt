and meaning of more" href="/word/more#etymonline_v_18358"><em>more</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">mara</span> &quot;greater, relatively greater, more, stronger, mightier,&quot; used as a comparative of <span class="foreign">micel</span> &quot;great&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">mickle</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*maiz</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">mera</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">meiri</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">mara</span>, Middle <p>Sometimes used as an adverb in Old English (&quot;in addition&quot;), but Old English generally used related <span class="foreign">ma</span> &quot;more&quot; as adverb and noun. This became Middle English <span class="foreign">mo</span>, but <span class="foreign">more</span> in this sense began to predominate in later Middle English.</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">&quot;Take some more tea,&quot; the March Hare said to Alice, very earnestly.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">&quot;I&#x27;ve had nothing yet,&quot; Alice replied in an offended tone, &quot;so I can&#x27;t take more.&quot;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">&quot;You mean you can&#x27;t take less,&quot; said the Hatter: &quot;it&#x27;s very easy to take more than nothing.&quot;</blockquote>

<p>As a noun, &quot;a greater quantity, amount, or number,&quot; in Old English. <span class="foreign"><strong>More and more</strong></span> &quot;larger and larger amounts&quot; is from 12c. <span class="foreign"><strong>More or less</strong></span> &quot;in a greater or lesser degree&quot; is from early 13c.; appended to a statement to indicate nearness but not precision, from 1580s. <span class="foreign"><strong>The more the merrier</strong></span> &quot;the larger the company the greater the enjoyment&quot; is from late 14c. <p>Original sense of &quot;greatest&quot; survives in phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>for the most part</strong></span> (mid-14c.; late Old English had <span class="foreign">þa mæste dæl</span>). Slang <span class="foreign"><strong>the most</strong></span> meaning &quot;the best, extremely good&quot; is attested from 1953. Also used as an adverb in Old English and in late Old English as a noun, &quot;the greatest or greater number.&quot; The sense of &quot;greatest value or advantage&quot; in the phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>make the most of</strong></span> (something) is by 1520s. <p>Double superlative <span class="foreign"><strong>mostest</strong></span> &quot;greatest amount or degree&quot; is by 1849 in U.S. Southern and African-American vernacular.
