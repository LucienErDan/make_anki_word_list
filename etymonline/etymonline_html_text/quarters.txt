<p>Used of the phases of the moon from early 15c. The use of <span class="foreign">quarter of an hour</span> is attested from mid-15c. In Middle English <span class="foreign">quarter</span> also meant &quot;one of the four divisions of a 12-hour night&quot; (late 14c.), and <span class="foreign">the quarter of the night</span> meant &quot;nine o&#x27;clock p.m.&quot; (early 14c.).</p>

<p>From late 14c. as &quot;one of the four quadrants of the heavens;&quot; hence, from the notion of the winds, &quot;a side, a direction&quot; (c. 1400). In heraldry from mid-14c. as &quot;one of the four divisions of a shield or coat of arms.&quot; The word&#x27;s connection with &quot;four&quot; loosened in Middle English and by 15c. expressions such as <span class="foreign">six-quartered</span> for &quot;six-sided&quot; are found. Meaning &quot;region, locality, area, place&quot; is from c. 1400. Meaning &quot;portion of a town&quot; (identified by the class or race of people who live there) is first attested 1520s. For military sense, see <span class="crossreference">quarters</span>. As a period of time in a football game, from 1911. <span class="foreign">Quarter horse</span>, bred strong for racing on quarter-mile tracks, first recorded 1834.</p>

<p>The coin (one fourth of a dollar) is peculiar to U.S., first recorded 1783. But <span class="foreign">quarter</span> could mean &quot;a farthing&quot; in Middle English (late 14c.), and compare British <span class="foreign">quadrant</span> &quot;a farthing&quot; (c. 1600), and classical Latin <span class="foreign">quadrans</span>, the name of a coin worth a quarter of an <span class="foreign">as</span> (the basic unit of Roman currency).</p>

<p><span class="foreign">Quarter days</span> (mid-15c.), designated as days when rents were paid and contracts and leases began or expired, were, in England, Lady day (March 25), Midsummer day (June 24), Michaelmas day (Sept. 29), and Christmas day (Dec. 25); in Scotland, keeping closer to the pagan Celtic calendar, they were Candlemas (Feb. 2), Whitsunday (May 15), Lammas (Aug. 1), and Martinmas (Nov. 11). <span class="foreign">Quarter</span> in the sense &quot;period of three months; one of the four divisions of a year&quot; is recorded from late 14c.</p>
