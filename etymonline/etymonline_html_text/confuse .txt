<p>The Latin past participle also was used as an adjective, with reference to mental states, &quot;troubled, embarrassed,&quot; and this passed into Old French as <span class="foreign">confus</span> &quot;dejected, downcast, undone, defeated, discomfited in mind or feeling,&quot; which passed to Middle English as <span class="foreign">confus</span> (14c.; for example Chaucer&#x27;s &quot;I am so confus, that I may not seye&quot;), which then was assimilated to the English past-participle pattern by addition of <span class="foreign">-ed</span>. By mid-16c., the word evolved a back-formed verb in <span class="crossreference">confuse</span>. Few English etymologies are more confusing. </p>
<p>Of individuals, &quot;discomfited in mind, perplexed,&quot; from mid-14c.; in logic, &quot;indistinct, indistinguishable from other ideas from which it ought to be different,&quot; 1610s.
