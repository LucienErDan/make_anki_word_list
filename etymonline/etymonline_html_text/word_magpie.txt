title="Origin and meaning of magpie" href="/word/magpie#etymonline_v_2200"><em>magpie</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>popular name of a common bird of Europe, Asia, and America, known for its chattering, acquisitiveness, curiosity, and mimicry, c. 1600, earlier simply <span class="foreign">pie</span> (early 13c.). The first element is <span class="crossreference">Mag</span>, nickname for <span class="foreign">Margaret</span>, long used in proverbial and slang English for qualities associated generally with women, especially in this case &quot;idle chattering&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">Magge tales</span> &quot;tall tales, nonsense,&quot; early 15c.; also compare French <span class="foreign">margot</span> &quot;magpie,&quot; from <span class="foreign">Margot</span>, pet form of <span class="foreign">Marguerite</span>). The name <span class="foreign">Margaret</span>, and its reduced forms <span class="foreign">Mag</span>, <span class="foreign">Madge</span>, diminutive <span class="foreign">Maggie</span>, also has long been familiarly applied to birds.</p>
<p>The second element, <span class="foreign">pie</span>, is the earlier name of the bird, from Old French <span class="foreign">pie</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">pica</span> &quot;magpie,&quot; fem. of <span class="foreign">picus</span> &quot;woodpecker,&quot; from PIE root <span class="foreign">*(s)peik-</span> &quot;woodpecker, magpie&quot; (source also of Umbrian <span class="foreign">peica</span> &quot;magpie,&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pikah</span> &quot;Indian cuckoo,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">spætr</span>, German <span class="foreign">Specht</span> &quot;woodpecker&quot;); possibly from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pi-</span>, denoting pointedness, of the beak, perhaps, but the magpie also has a long, pointed tail.</p>
<p>The birds are proverbial for pilfering and hoarding, can be taught to speak, and have been regarded since the Middle Ages as ill omens.</p>
<blockquote>Whan pyes chatter vpon a house it is a sygne of ryghte euyll tydynges. [1507]</blockquote>
<p>Divination by numbering magpies is attested from c. 1780 in Lincolnshire; the rhyme varies from place to place, the only consistency being that one is bad, two are good.</p>

<blockquote>The councils which magpies appear to hold together, at particular seasons, commonly called &quot;folkmotes,&quot; are associated in the minds of many with superstitious and ominous notions. The innocent objects of terror, while meeting together most probably for the purpose of choosing mates, are supposed to be conspiring and clubbing their wits, for the weal or woe of the inhabitants of the neighbouring village. If they are of an even number and carry on their cheerful, noisy chatter, it is supposed to betoken good to old and young--but if there is an odd magpie perched apart from the rest, silent, and disconsolate, the reverse of this is apprehended, and mischievous consequences are inevitably expected. <p>The coin may have been so called for its marking; Gamillscheg writes the word is from French <span class="foreign">gai</span> (see <span class="crossreference">jay</span>). The general story of the origin of the word is broadly accepted, but there are many variations in the details:</p>
<blockquote>We are indebted to the Italians for the idea of newspapers. The title of their <span class="foreign">gazettas</span> was, perhaps, derived from <span class="foreign">gazzera</span>, a magpie or chatterer; or, more probably, from a farthing coin, peculiar to the city of Venice, called <span class="foreign">gazetta</span>, which was the common price of the newspapers. Another etymologist is for deriving it from the Latin <span class="foreign">gaza</span>, which would colloquially lengthen into <span class="foreign">gazetta</span>, and signify a little treasury of news. The Spanish derive it from the Latin <span class="foreign">gaza</span>, and likewise their <span class="foreign">gazatero</span>, and our <span class="foreign">gazetteer</span>, for a writer of the <span class="foreign">gazette</span> and, what is peculiar to themselves, <span class="foreign">gazetista</span>, for a lover of the gazette. [Isaac Disraeli, &quot;Curiosities of Literature,&quot; 1835]</blockquote>

<blockquote><strong>Gazzetta</strong> It., Sp. <span class="foreign">gazeta</span>, Fr. E. <span class="foreign">gazette</span>; prop. the name of a Venetian coin (from <span class="foreign">gaza</span>), so in Old English. Others derive <span class="foreign">gazette</span> from <span class="foreign">gazza</span> a magpie, which, it is alleged, was the emblem figured on the paper; but it does not appear on any of the oldest Venetian specimens preserved at Florence. The first newspapers appeared at Venice about the middle of the 16th century during the war with Soliman II, in the form of a written sheet, for the privilege of reading which a <span class="foreign">gazzetta</span> (= a <span class="foreign">crazia</span>) was paid. Hence the name was transferred to the news-sheet. [T.C. Donkin, &quot;Etymological Dictionary of the Romance Languages&quot; (based on Diez), 1864]</blockquote>

<blockquote>GAZETTE. A paper of public intelligence and news of divers countries, first printed at Venice, about the year 1620, and so called (some say) because <span class="foreign">una gazetta</span>, a small piece of Venetian coin, was given to buy or read it.
