One guess [OED] leans toward an unrecorded Old English <span class="foreign">*gyrele</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*gurwilon-</span>, diminutive of <span class="foreign">*gurwjoz</span> (apparently also represented by Low German <span class="foreign">gære</span> &quot;boy, girl,&quot; Norwegian dialectal <span class="foreign">gorre</span>, Swedish dialectal <span class="foreign">gurre</span> &quot;small child,&quot; though the exact relationship, if any, between all these is obscure), from PIE <span class="foreign">*ghwrgh-</span>, also found in Greek <span class="foreign">parthenos</span> &quot;virgin.&quot; But this involves some objectionable philology. Liberman (2008) writes:</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Girl</span> does not go back to any Old English or Old Germanic form. It is part of a large group of Germanic words whose root begins with a g or k and ends in r. The final consonant in <span class="foreign">girl</span> is a diminutive suffix. The <span class="foreign">g-r</span> words denote young animals, children, and all kinds of creatures considered immature, worthless, or past their prime.</blockquote>
<p>Another candidate is Old English <span class="foreign">gierela</span> &quot;garment&quot; (for possible sense evolution in this theory, compare <span class="crossreference">brat</span>). Like <span class="crossreference">boy</span>, <span class="crossreference">lass</span>, <span class="crossreference">lad</span> it is of more or less obscure origin. &quot;Probably most of them arose as jocular transferred uses of words that had originally different meaning&quot; [OED]. Specific meaning of &quot;female child&quot; is late 14c. Applied to &quot;any young unmarried woman&quot; since mid-15c. Meaning &quot;sweetheart&quot; is from 1640s. <span class="foreign">Old girl</span> in reference to a woman of any age is recorded from 1826. <span class="foreign">Girl next door</span> as a type of unflashy attractiveness is recorded by 1953.</p>
