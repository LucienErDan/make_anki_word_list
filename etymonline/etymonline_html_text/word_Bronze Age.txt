title="Origin and meaning of bronze" href="/word/bronze#etymonline_v_17157"><em>bronze</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1721, &quot;alloy of copper and (usually) a smaller amount of tin,&quot; from French <span class="foreign">bronze</span>, from Italian <span class="foreign">bronzo</span>, from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">bronzium</span>, which is of uncertain origin. Perhaps cognate (via notion of color) with Venetian <span class="foreign">bronza</span> &quot;glowing coals,&quot; or German <span class="foreign">brunst</span> &quot;fire.&quot; Perhaps influenced by Latin <span class="foreign">Brundisium</span> the Italian town of Brindisi (Pliny writes of <span class="foreign">aes Brundusinum</span>). Perhaps ultimately from Persian <span class="foreign">birinj</span> &quot;copper.&quot;</p>
<p>In Middle English, the distinction between bronze (copper-tin alloy) and brass (copper-zinc alloy) was not clear, and both were called <span class="foreign">bras</span> (see <span class="crossreference">brass</span> (n.)). Used historically for bells, cannons, statuary, and fine mechanical works. Also from French are Dutch <span class="foreign">brons</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bronze</span>, etc., and ultimately from the Medieval Latin word are Spanish <span class="foreign">bronce</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">bronza</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">bronc</span>, Albanian <span class="foreign">brunze</span>, etc.</p>
<p>A <span class="foreign"><strong>bronze medal</strong></span> has been given to a third-place finisher at least since 1852.
