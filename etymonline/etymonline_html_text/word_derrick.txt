title="Origin and meaning of derrick" href="/word/derrick#etymonline_v_5637"><em>derrick</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1600, &quot;a hangman,&quot; also &quot;a gallows,&quot; from the surname of a hangman at London&#x27;s Tyburn gallows, c. 1606-1608, who is often referred to in contemporary plays. The name represents a late borrowing from the Low Countries (compare Dutch <span class="foreign">Diederik</span>) of Old High German <span class="foreign">Theodric</span> (see <span class="crossreference">Dietrich</span>).</p>
<p>As &quot;a hoisting apparatus for lifting and moving heavy weights,&quot; 1727. &quot;It is similar to the crane, but differs from it in having the boom, which corresponds to the jib of the crane, pivoted at the lower end so that it may take different inclinations from the perpendicular&quot; [Century Dictionary]. As the word for a structure over an oil well to support the drilling apparatus, 1861, American English.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/derrick#etymonline_v_5637"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Jack" href="/word/Jack#etymonline_v_1615">Jack </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">masc. proper name, attested by 1218, probably via Anglo-French <span class="foreign">Jake</span>, <span class="foreign">Jaikes</span>, from Old French <span class="foreign">Jacques</span> (which was a diminutive of Latin <span class="foreign">Jacobus</span>; see <span class="crossreference">Jacob</span>), but in English the name always has been regarded as a familiar form of <span class="crossreference">John</span>, and some have argued that it is a native formation. In Middle English spelled <span class="foreign">Jakke</span>, <span class="foreign">Jacke</span>, etc., and pronounced as two syllables (&quot;Jackie&quot;).
<br/><br/>
In England, <span class="foreign">Jack</span> became a generic name applied familiarly or contemptuously to anybody (especially a young man of the lower classes) from late 14c. Later used especially of sailors (1650s; <span class="foreign">Jack-tar</span> is from 1781); <span class="foreign">Jack-ashore</span> (adj.) &quot;drinking and in high spirits, recklessly spending&quot; (1875) also is an image from sailors (1840 as a book title). In U.S., as a generic name addressed to an unknown stranger, attested from 1889. <span class="foreign">Every man Jack</span> &quot;everyone&quot; is from 1812. Also see <span class="crossreference">jack</span> (n.).
<br/><br/>
Used in male personifications from 15c.; first record of <span class="foreign">jack-of-all-trades</span> &quot;person handy at any kind of work or business&quot; is from 1610s; <span class="foreign">Jack Frost</span> is from 1826; <span class="foreign">Jack-nasty</span> &quot;a sneak or sloven&quot; is from 1833 (<span class="foreign">Jack-nasty-face</span>, a sea-term for a common sailor, is from 1788). <span class="foreign">Jack Sprat</span> for a small, light man is from 1560s (his opposite was <span class="foreign">Jack Weight</span>). <span class="foreign">Jack-pudding</span> &quot;comical clown, buffoon&quot; is from 1640s. <span class="foreign">Jack-Spaniard</span> is from 1703 as a Spaniard, 1833 as &quot;a hornet&quot; in the West Indies. Other personifications listed in Farmer &amp; Henley include <span class="foreign">jack-snip</span> &quot;a botching tailor,&quot; <span class="foreign">Jack-in-office</span> &quot;overbearing petty official&quot; (1680s), <span class="foreign">Jack-on-both-sides</span> &quot;a neutral,&quot; <span class="foreign">Jack-out-of-doors</span> &quot;a vagrant&quot; (1630s), <span class="foreign">jack-sauce</span> &quot;impudent fellow&quot; (1590s).
<br/><br/>
The U.S. plant <span class="foreign">jack-in-the-pulpit</span> (Indian turnip) is attested by 1833. <span class="foreign">Jack the Ripper</span> was active in London 1888. The Scottish form is <span class="crossreference">Jock</span> (compare <span class="crossreference">jockey</span> (n.)). Alliterative coupling of <span class="foreign">Jack and Jill</span> is from 15c. (<span class="foreign">Iakke and Gylle</span>, <span class="foreign">Ienken and Iulyan</span>). <span class="foreign">Jack Ketch</span> for &quot;hangman, executioner&quot; (1670s) is said to be from the name of a public executioner in the time of James II (compare <span class="crossreference">Derrick</span>); or a similar Germanic source, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*witanan</span> &quot;to look after, guard, ascribe to, reproach&quot; (source also of German <span class="foreign">weisen</span> &quot;to show, point out,&quot; c. 1600, perhaps from <span class="foreign">Dirk</span>, the proper name, which was used in Scandinavian for &quot;a picklock.&quot; But the earliest spellings were <span class="foreign">dork</span>, <span class="foreign">durk</span> (Johnson, 1755, seems to be responsible for the modern spelling). The earliest association is with Highlanders, however there seems to be no such word in Gaelic, where the proper word is <span class="foreign">biodag</span>. Another candidate is German <span class="foreign">dolch</span> &quot;dagger.&quot;</p>
