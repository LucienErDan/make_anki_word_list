They were divided into the <span class="foreign">trivium</span> -- grammar, logic, rhetoric (see <span class="crossreference">trivial</span>) -- and the <span class="foreign">quadrivium</span> -- arithmetic, geometry, music, astronomy. <blockquote>The study of [the classics] is fitly called a <span class="foreign">liberal</span> education, because it emancipates the mind from every narrow provincialism, whether of egoism or tradition, and is the apprenticeship that every one must serve before becoming a free brother of the guild which passes the torch of life from age to age. The more precise adjectival meaning &quot;tending to excite laughter by ludicrous contrast between the subject and the manner of treating it&quot; is attested in English by 1700.</p>
<blockquote>The two great branches of ridicule in writing are comedy and burlesque. The first ridicules persons by drawing them in their proper characters; the other, by drawing them quite unlike themselves. Burlesque is therefore of two kinds; the first represents mean persons in accoutrements of heroes, the other describes great persons acting and speaking like the basest among the people. [Addison, &quot;Spectator,&quot; Dec. 15, 1711]</blockquote>
<p>By 1880s it typically meant &quot;travesties on the classics and satires on accepted ideas&quot; and vulgar comic opera. Modern sense of &quot;variety show featuring striptease&quot; is American English, evolved after 1870 and predominant from 1920s, probably from the earlier sense &quot;sketches at the end of minstrel shows&quot; (1857).</p>
