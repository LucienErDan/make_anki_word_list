and meaning of boo" href="/word/boo#etymonline_v_15597"><em>boo</em> (interj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 15c., <span class="foreign">boh</span>, &quot;A combination of consonant and vowel especially fitted to produce a loud and startling sound&quot; [OED, which compares Latin <span class="foreign">boare</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">boaein</span> &quot;to cry aloud, roar, shout&quot;]; as an expression of disapproval, 1884 (n.); hence, the verb meaning &quot;shower (someone) with boos&quot; (1885).</p>
<p>Booing was common late 19c. among London theater audiences and at British political events; in Italy, Parma opera-goers were notorious boo-birds. But the custom seems to have been little-known in America before c. 1910. To <span class="foreign">say boo</span> &quot;open one&#x27;s mouth, speak,&quot; originally was to <span class="foreign">say boo to a goose</span>.</p>
<blockquote>To be able to say Bo! to a goose is to be not quite destitute of courage, to have an inkling of spirit, and was probably in the first instance used of children. A little boy who comes across some geese suddenly will find himself hissed at immediately, and a great demonstration of defiance made by them, but if he can pluck up heart to cry &#x27;bo!&#x27; loudly and advance upon them, they will retire defeated. The word &#x27;bo&#x27; is clearly selected for the sake of the explosiveness of its first letter and the openness and loudness of its vowel. [Walter W.
