<br/><br/>
Now largely superseded by <span class="crossreference">time</span> except in formulaic constructions (such as <span class="foreign">all the while</span>). Middle English sense of &quot;short space of time spent in doing something&quot; now only preserved in <span class="crossreference">worthwhile</span> and phrases such as <span class="foreign">worth (one&#x27;s) while</span>. As a conjunction, &quot;during or in the time that; as long as&quot; (late Old English), it represents Old English <span class="foreign">þa hwile þe</span>, literally &quot;the while that.&quot; Form <span class="foreign">whiles</span> is recorded from early 13c.; <span class="foreign">whilst</span> is from late 14c., with unetymological <span class="foreign">-st</span> as in <span class="foreign">amongst</span>, <span class="crossreference">amidst</span>. The earliest use is as a slang word for &quot;the whining speech of beggars asking for alms&quot; (1640s), from the verb in this sense (1560s), from Old North French <span class="foreign">canter</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">chanter</span>) &quot;to sing, chant,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">cantare</span>, frequentative of <span class="foreign">canere</span> &quot;to sing&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kan-</span> &quot;to sing&quot;).</p>
<p>Century Dictionary notes the ecclesiastical use of <span class="foreign">cantus</span> in Medieval Latin, and writes, &quot;The word <span class="foreign">cant</span> may thus have become associated with beggars; but there may have been also an allusion to a perfunctory performance of divine service and hence a hypocritical use of religious phrases.&quot; The sense in English expanded after 1680 to mean &quot;the jargon of criminals and vagabonds,&quot; and from thence the word was applied contemptuously by any sect or school to the phraseology of its rival.</p>
<blockquote>... Slang is universal, whilst Cant is restricted in usage to certain classes of the community: thieves, vagrom men, and -- well, their associates. ... Slang boasts a quasi-respectability denied to Cant, though Cant is frequently more enduring, its use continuing without variation of meaning for many generations. [John S. <p>Also used by c. 1200 of an animal or plant much below the ordinary size of its species.&quot; The use of <span class="foreign">dwarf</span> in the Germanic mythological sense, &quot;a diminished and generally deformed being, dwelling in rocks and hills and skilled in working metals,&quot; seems to have faded after Middle English and been revived after c. 1770 from German.</p>
<blockquote>Whilst in this and other ways the dwarfs do at times have dealings with mankind, yet on the whole they seem to shrink from man; they give the impression of a downtrodden afflicted race, which is on the point of abandoning its ancient home to new and more powerful invaders. There is stamped on their character something <span class="foreign">shy</span> and something <span class="foreign">heathenish</span>, which estranges them from intercourse with christians. They chafe at human faithlessness, which no doubt would primarily mean the apostacy from heathenism. In the poems of the Mid. Ages, <span class="foreign">Laurin</span> is expressly set before us as a <span class="foreign">heathen</span>. It goes sorely against the dwarfs to see churches built, <span class="foreign">bell-ringing</span> ... disturbs their ancient privacy; they also hate the clearing of forests, agriculture, new fangled pounding-machinery for ore. [&quot;Teutonic Mythology,&quot; Jakob Grimm, transl. Stallybrass, 1883]</blockquote>
<p>The shift of the Old English guttural at the end of the word to modern <span class="foreign">-f</span> is typical (compare <span class="crossreference">enough</span>, <span class="crossreference">draft</span>) and begins to appear early 14c. In Middle English it also was <span class="foreign">dwerþ</span>, <span class="foreign">dwerke</span>. Old English plural <span class="foreign">dweorgas</span> became Middle English <span class="foreign">dwarrows</span>, later leveled down to <span class="foreign">dwarfs</span>. The use of <span class="foreign">dwarves</span> for the legendary race was popularized by J.R.R. Tolkien. As an adjective, from 1590s.</p>

<p>The use of <span class="foreign">giant</span> and <span class="foreign">dwarf</span> in reference to stars of the highest and lowest luminosity is attested by 1914, said to have been suggested by Danish astronomer Ejnar Hertzsprung, (1873-1967); hence <span class="foreign">red dwarf</span> (attested by 1922), <span class="foreign">white dwarf</span>, <span class="foreign">black dwarf</span> &quot;dead and lightless star&quot; (1966).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/dwarf#etymonline_v_16019"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i
