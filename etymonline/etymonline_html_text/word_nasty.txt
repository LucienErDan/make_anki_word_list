and meaning of nasty" href="/word/nasty#etymonline_v_2302"><em>nasty</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., <span class="foreign">nasti</span>, &quot;foul, filthy, dirty, unclean,&quot; literally or figuratively, a word of uncertain origin. Middle English Compendium says from Old Norse (compare Swedish dialectal and Danish <span class="foreign">naskug, nasket</span> &quot;dirty, nasty&quot;) with Middle English adjectival suffix <span class="foreign">-i</span>. There was a variant <span class="foreign">nasky</span> in early Modern English.</p>

<p>Barnhart suggests Old French <span class="foreign">nastre</span> &quot;miserly, envious, malicious, spiteful,&quot; shortened form of <span class="foreign">villenastre</span> &quot;infamous, bad,&quot; from <span class="foreign">vilain</span> &quot;villain&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">villain</span>) + <span class="foreign">-astre</span>, pejorative suffix, from Latin <span class="foreign">-aster</span>. Another alternative etymology [mentioned in OED] is from Dutch <span class="foreign">nestig</span> &quot;dirty,&quot; literally &quot;like a bird&#x27;s nest.&quot;</p>

<p>From c. 1600 as &quot;indecent, obscene&quot; (&quot;morally filthy&quot;). Of weather, &quot;foul, stormy,&quot; from 1630s; of things generally, &quot;unpleasant, offensive; troublesome, annoying,&quot; from 1705. Of people, &quot;ill-tempered, mean,&quot; from 1825. The noun meaning &quot;something nasty&quot; is from 1935. Related: <span class="foreign">Nastily</span>; <span class="foreign">nastiness</span>.</p>

<p>Long regarded as a number of perfection (<span class="foreign">seven wonders</span>; <span class="foreign">seven sleepers</span>, the latter translating Latin <span class="foreign">septem dormientes</span>; <span class="foreign">seven against Thebes</span>, etc.), but that notion is late in Old English and in German a nasty, troublesome woman could be <span class="foreign">eine böse Sieben</span> &quot;an evil seven&quot; (1662).</p>
<p>Magical power or healing skill associated since 16c. with the <span class="foreign">seventh son</span> [&quot;The seuenth Male Chyld by iust order (neuer a Gyrle or Wench being borne betweene),&quot; Thomas Lupton, &quot;A Thousand Notable Things,&quot; 1579]. The typical number for &quot;very great, strong,&quot; as in <span class="foreign">seven-league boots</span> in the fairy story of Hop o&#x27;my Thumb. The <span class="foreign">Seven Years&#x27; War</span> (1756-63) is also the Third Silesian War.</p>
<p>The <span class="foreign">Seven Stars</span> (Old English <span class="foreign">sibunsterri</span>), usually refers to the Pleiades, though in 15c. and after this name occasionally was given to the Big Dipper (which also has seven stars), or the seven planets of classical astronomy. Popular as a tavern sign, it might also (with six in a circle, one in the center) be a Masonic symbol.</p>
