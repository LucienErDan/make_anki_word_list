In Hungary, the thing and the name for it date from 15c., and forms are found since 16c. in most European languages (Spanish and Portuguese <span class="foreign">coche</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">cocchino</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">koets</span>). Vehicles often were named for the place of their invention or first use (compare <span class="foreign">berlin, landau, surrey</span>). Applied to railway passenger cars by 1866, American English. Sense of &quot;economy or tourist class&quot; is from 1949.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;instructor/trainer&quot; is c. 1830 Oxford University slang for a private tutor who &quot;carries&quot; a student through an exam (compare <span class="crossreference">pony</span> in the student slang sense &quot;translation&quot;). Transferred sense in sports, &quot;person employed to train athletes for a contest&quot; is attested from 1861. A more classical word for an athletic trainer was <span class="foreign">agonistarch</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">agonistarkhes</span> &quot;one who trains (someone) to compete in the public games and contests.&quot;</p>

<blockquote>The extraordinary chance which had saved three lives was a holy miracle or a comic accident according to the religion of the beholder .... Murder or no murder, the <span class="foreign">coup d&#x27;état</span> was complete, and since Thurn had overruled many of his supporters in demanding death it was well for the conscience of his allies that a pile of mouldering filth in the courtyard of the Hradschin had made soft falling for the governors.
