<p>Originally &quot;life on earth, this world (as opposed to the afterlife),&quot; sense extended to &quot;the known world,&quot; then to &quot;the physical world in the broadest sense, the universe&quot; (c. 1200). In Old English gospels, the commonest word for &quot;the physical world,&quot; was <span class="foreign">Middangeard</span> (Old Norse <span class="foreign">Midgard</span>), literally &quot;the middle enclosure&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">yard</span> (n.1)), which is rooted in Germanic cosmology. Greek <span class="foreign">kosmos</span> in its ecclesiastical sense of &quot;world of people&quot; sometimes was rendered in Gothic as <span class="foreign">manaseþs</span>, literally &quot;seed of man.&quot; The usual Old Norse word was <span class="foreign">heimr</span>, literally &quot;abode&quot; (see home). Words for &quot;world&quot; in some other Indo-European languages derive from the root for &quot;bottom, foundation&quot; (such as Irish <span class="foreign">domun</span>, Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">duno</span>, related to English <span class="foreign">deep</span>); the Lithuanian word is <span class="foreign">pasaulis</span>, from <span class="foreign">pa-</span> &quot;under&quot; + <span class="foreign">saulė</span> &quot;sun.&quot;</p>
<p>Original sense in <span class="foreign">world without end</span>, translating Latin <span class="foreign">saecula saeculorum</span>, and in <span class="crossreference">worldly</span>. Latin <span class="foreign">saeculum</span> can mean both &quot;age&quot; and &quot;world,&quot; as can Greek <span class="foreign">aiōn</span>. Meaning &quot;a great quantity or number&quot; is from 1580s. <span class="foreign">Out of this world</span> &quot;surpassing, marvelous&quot; is from 1928; earlier it meant &quot;dead.&quot; <span class="foreign">World Cup</span> is by 1951; U.S. baseball <span class="foreign">World Series</span> is by 1893 (originally often <span class="foreign">World&#x27;s Series</span>). <span class="foreign">World power</span> in the geopolitical sense first recorded 1900. <span class="foreign">World-class</span> is attested from 1950, originally of Olympic athletes.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/world#etymonline_v_10847"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of world war" href="/word/world%20war#etymonline_v_10848"><em>world</em> war (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>attested by 1898 as a speculation.</p>
