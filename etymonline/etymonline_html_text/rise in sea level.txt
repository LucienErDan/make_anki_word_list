<p>Germanic languages also use the general Indo-European word (represented by English <span class="crossreference">mere</span> (n.1)), but have no firm distinction between &quot;sea&quot; and &quot;lake,&quot; either by size, by inland or open, or by salt vs. fresh. This may reflect the Baltic geography where the languages are thought to have originated. The two words are used more or less interchangeably in Germanic, and exist in opposite senses (such as Gothic <span class="foreign">saiws</span> &quot;lake, marshland,&quot; <span class="foreign">marei</span> &quot;sea;&quot; but Dutch <span class="foreign">zee</span> &quot;sea,&quot; <span class="foreign">meer</span> &quot;lake&quot;). German <span class="foreign">See</span> is &quot;sea&quot; (fem.) or &quot;lake&quot; (masc.).</p>

<p>Boutkan writes that the <span class="foreign">sea</span> words in Germanic likely were originally &quot;lake,&quot; and the older word for &quot;sea&quot; is represented by <span class="crossreference">haff</span>.
