Applied to the aboriginal native inhabitants of the Americas from at least 1553 as a noun (1610s as an adjective), reflecting Spanish and Portuguese use, on the mistaken notion that America was the eastern end of Asia (it was also used occasionally 18c.-19c. of inhabitants of the Philippines and indigenous peoples of Australia and New Zealand). The Old English adjective was <span class="foreign">Indisc</span>, and <span class="foreign">Indish</span> (adj.) was common in 16c.</p>

<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Red Indian</strong></span>, to distinguish the native Americans from inhabitants of India, is first attested 1831 in British English (Carlyle) but was not commonly used in North America. More than 500 modern phrases include <span class="foreign">Indian</span>, most of them U.S. and most impugning honesty or intelligence, such as <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian gift</strong></span><span class="foreign">:</span></p>
<blockquote>An Indian gift is a proverbial expression, signifying a present for which an equivalent return is expected. [Thomas Hutchinson, &quot;History of Massachusetts Bay,&quot; 1765]</blockquote>
<p>Hence <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian giver</strong></span> &quot;one who gives a gift and then asks for it back&quot; (1848). <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian elephant</strong></span> is from c. 1600; <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian corn</strong></span> is from 1620s; to walk <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian file</strong></span> is from 1758. <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian club</strong></span> is from 1824 as a weapon, 1825 as exercise equipment (clubs were noted noted in Lewis &amp; Clark, etc., as characteristic weapons of native warriors in the American West). <span class="foreign"><strong>Indian-head</strong></span> (adj.) in reference to U.S. copper pennies with a portrait of an Indian in profile, from 1862.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Indian#etymonline_v_6376"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of butterfly" href="/word/butterfly#etymonline_v_18153">butterfly (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>common name of any lepidopterous insect active in daylight, Old English <span class="foreign">buttorfleoge</span>, evidently <span class="crossreference">butter</span> (n.) + <span class="crossreference">fly</span> (n.), but the name is of obscure signification. Perhaps based on the old notion that the insects (or, according to Grimm, witches disguised as butterflies) consume butter or milk that is left uncovered. Or, less creatively, simply because the pale yellow color of many species&#x27; wings suggests the color of butter. Another theory connects it to the color of the insect&#x27;s excrement, based on Dutch cognate <span class="foreign">boterschijte</span>. Also see <span class="crossreference">papillon</span>.</p>
<p>Applied to persons from c. 1600, originally in reference to vain and gaudy attire; by 1806 in reference to transformation from early lowly state; in reference to flitting tendencies by 1873. The swimming stroke so called from 1935. As a type of mechanical nut, 1869. <span class="foreign">Butterflies</span> &quot;light stomach spasms caused by anxiety&quot; is from 1908. &quot;Who breaks a butterfly upon a wheel?&quot; is from Pope.</p>
<blockquote>The butterfly effect is a deceptively simple insight extracted from a complex modern field. As a low-profile assistant professor in MIT&#x27;s department of meteorology in 1961, [Edward] Lorenz created an early computer program to simulate weather. One day he changed one of a dozen numbers representing atmospheric conditions, from .506127 to .506. That tiny alteration utterly transformed his long-term forecast, a point Lorenz amplified in his 1972 paper, &quot;Predictability: Does the Flap of a Butterfly&#x27;s Wings in Brazil Set Off a Tornado in Texas?&quot; [Peter Dizikes, &quot;The Meaning of the Butterfly,&quot; The Boston Globe, June 8, 2008]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3"
