and meaning of merry" href="/word/merry#etymonline_v_14683"><em>merry</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Middle English <span class="foreign">mirie</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">myrge</span> &quot;pleasing, agreeable, pleasant, sweet, exciting feelings of enjoyment and gladness&quot; (said of grass, trees, the world, music, song); also as an adverb, &quot;pleasantly, melodiously,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*murgijaz</span>, which probably originally meant &quot;short-lasting,&quot; (compare Old High German <span class="foreign">murg</span> &quot;short,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">gamaurgjan</span> &quot;to shorten&quot;), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*mregh-u-</span> &quot;short.&quot; The only exact cognate for meaning outside English was Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">mergelijc</span> &quot;joyful.&quot;</p>

<p>The connection to &quot;pleasure&quot; likely was via the notion of &quot;making time fly, that which makes the time seem to pass quickly&quot; (compare German <span class="foreign">Kurzweil</span> &quot;pastime,&quot; literally &quot;a short time;&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">skemta</span> &quot;to amuse, entertain, amuse oneself,&quot; from <span class="foreign">skamt</span>, neuter of <span class="foreign">skammr</span> &quot;short&quot;). There also was a verbal form in Old English, <span class="foreign">myrgan</span> &quot;be merry, rejoice.&quot; For vowel evolution, see <span class="crossreference">bury</span> (v.).</p>

<p>Not originally applied to humorous moods or speech or conduct, yet the word had a much wider senses in Middle English than modern: &quot;pleasant-sounding&quot; (of animal voices), &quot;fine&quot; (of weather), &quot;handsome&quot; (of dress), &quot;pleasant-tasting&quot; (of herbs). The evolution of the modern senses is probably via the meaning &quot;pleased by a certain event or situation or state of things&quot; (c. 1200). Of persons, &quot;cheerful by disposition or nature; playfully cheerful, enlivened with gladness or good spirits,&quot; by mid-14c.</p>
