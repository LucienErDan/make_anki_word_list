title="Origin and meaning of compose" href="/word/compose#etymonline_v_17279"><em>compose</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1400, <span class="foreign">compousen</span>, &quot;to write&quot; (a book), from Old French <span class="foreign">composer</span> &quot;put together, compound; adjust, arrange; write&quot; a work (12c.), from <span class="foreign">com-</span> &quot;with, together&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">com-</span>) + <span class="foreign">poser</span> &quot;to place,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">pausare</span> &quot;to cease, lay down&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">pause</span> (n.)).</p>
<p>Meaning influenced in Old French by <span class="foreign">componere</span> &quot;to arrange, direct&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">composite</span>; also see <span class="crossreference">compound</span> (v.), <span class="crossreference">pose</span> (v.)), which gradually was replaced in French by <span class="foreign">composer</span>. Similar confusion is found in <span class="crossreference">expose</span>, <span class="crossreference">oppose</span>, <span class="crossreference">repose</span> (v.2), <span class="crossreference">transpose</span>, etc.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to make or form by uniting two or more things&quot; is from late 15c. Sense of &quot;be the substance or elements of, make up&quot; is from 1540s. Sense of &quot;invent and put (music) into proper form&quot; is from 1590s. From c. 1600 as &quot;bring into a composed state, to cal, quiet;&quot; from 1650s as &quot;place (parts or elements) in proper form, arrange.&quot;</p>
<p>In painting, &quot;combine into an arrangement with artistic effect&quot; (1782). In printing, &quot;put into type&quot; (1630s), but the usual term among printers was <span class="foreign">set</span>. Related: <span class="crossreference">Composed</span>; <span class="foreign">composing</span>.
