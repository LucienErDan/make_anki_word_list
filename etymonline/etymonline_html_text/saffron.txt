<p>Not used as a color word until 1540s (colors similar to modern orange in Middle English might be called <span class="foreign">citrine</span> or <span class="foreign">saffron</span>). <p>OED says of uncertain origin; Liberman says &quot;None of the proposed etymologies of <span class="foreign">witch</span> is free from phonetic or semantic difficulties.&quot; Klein suggests connection with Old English <span class="foreign">wigle</span> &quot;divination,&quot; and <span class="foreign">wig, wih</span> &quot;idol.&quot; Watkins says the nouns represent a Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*wikkjaz</span> &quot;necromancer&quot; (one who wakes the dead), from PIE <span class="foreign">*weg-yo-</span>, from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*weg-</span> &quot;to be strong, be lively.&quot;</p>
<p>That <span class="foreign">wicce</span> once had a more specific sense than the later general one of &quot;female magician, sorceress&quot; perhaps is suggested by the presence of other words in Old English describing more specific kinds of magical craft. In the Laws of Ælfred (c.890), witchcraft was specifically singled out as a woman&#x27;s craft, whose practitioners were not to be suffered to live among the West Saxons:</p>
<blockquote>Ða fæmnan þe gewuniað onfon gealdorcræftigan &amp; scinlæcan &amp; wiccan, ne læt þu ða libban.</blockquote>
<p>The other two words combined with it here are <span class="foreign">gealdricge</span>, a woman who practices &quot;incantations,&quot; and <span class="foreign">scinlæce</span> &quot;female wizard, woman magician,&quot; from a root meaning &quot;phantom, evil spirit.&quot; Another word that appears in the Anglo-Saxon laws is <span class="foreign">lyblæca</span> &quot;wizard, sorcerer,&quot; but with suggestions of skill in the use of drugs, because the root of the word is <span class="foreign">lybb</span> &quot;drug, poison, charm&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">leaf</span> (n.)). <span class="foreign">Lybbestre</span> was a fem. word meaning &quot;sorceress,&quot; and <span class="foreign">lybcorn</span> was the name of a certain medicinal seed (perhaps wild saffron). Weekley notes possible connection to Gothic <span class="foreign">weihs</span> &quot;holy&quot; and German <span class="foreign">weihan</span> &quot;consecrate,&quot; and writes, &quot;the priests of a suppressed religion naturally become magicians to its successors or opponents.&quot; In Anglo-Saxon glossaries, <span class="foreign">wicca</span> renders Latin <span class="foreign">augur</span> (c. 1100), and <span class="foreign">wicce</span> stands for &quot;pythoness, <span class="foreign">divinatricem</span>.&quot; In the &quot;Three Kings of Cologne&quot; (c. 1400) <span class="foreign">wicca</span> translates <span class="foreign">Magi</span>:</p>
<blockquote>Þe paynyms ... cleped þe iij kyngis <span class="foreign">Magos</span>, þat is to seye wicchis.</blockquote>
<p>The glossary translates Latin <span class="foreign">necromantia</span> (<span class="foreign">&quot;demonum invocatio&quot;</span>) with <span class="foreign">galdre, wiccecræft</span>. The Anglo-Saxon poem called &quot;Men&#x27;s Crafts&quot; (also &quot;The Gifts of Men&quot;) has <span class="foreign">wiccræft</span>, which appears to be the same word, and by its context means &quot;skill with horses.&quot; In a c. 1250 translation of &quot;Exodus,&quot; <span class="foreign">witches</span> is used of the Egyptian midwives who save the newborn sons of the Hebrews: &quot;Ðe wicches hidden hem for-ðan, Biforen pharaun nolden he ben.&quot; <span class="foreign">Witch</span> in reference to a man survived in dialect into 20c., but the fem. form was so dominant by 1601 that <span class="foreign">men-witches</span> or <span class="foreign">he-witch</span> began to be used.
