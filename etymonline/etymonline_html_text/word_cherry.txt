title="Origin and meaning of cherry" href="/word/cherry#etymonline_v_11229"><em>cherry</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>pulpy drupe of a well-known type of tree, c. 1300, earlier in surname <span class="foreign">Chyrimuth</span> (1266, literally &quot;Cherry-mouth&quot;); from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">cherise</span>, from Old North French <span class="foreign">cherise</span> (Old French, Modern French <span class="foreign">cerise</span>, 12c.), from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*ceresia</span>, from late Greek <span class="foreign">kerasian</span> &quot;cherry,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">kerasos</span> &quot;cherry tree,&quot; possibly from a language of Asia Minor. Mistaken in Middle English for a plural and stripped of its <span class="foreign">-s</span> (compare <span class="crossreference">pea</span>).</p>
<p>Old English had <span class="foreign">ciris</span> &quot;cherry&quot; from a West Germanic borrowing of the Vulgar Latin word (cognate with German <span class="foreign">Kirsch</span>), but it died out after the Norman invasion and was replaced by the French word. Short for <span class="foreign">cherry-tree</span> from 1620s. As an adjective, &quot;of the color of a cherry,&quot; mid-15c.</p>
