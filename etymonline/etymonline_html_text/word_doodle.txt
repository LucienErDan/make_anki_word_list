<p>The long-standing speculation is that this Latin word is altered (probably by influence of <span class="foreign">turbare</span> &quot;to disturb, confuse&quot;) from <span class="foreign">*manstuprare</span>, from <span class="foreign">manu</span>, ablative of <span class="foreign">manus</span> &quot;hand&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">manual</span>) + <span class="foreign">stuprare</span> &quot;defile&quot; (oneself), from <span class="foreign">stuprum</span> &quot;defilement, dishonor,&quot; related to <span class="foreign">stupere</span> &quot;to be stunned, stupefied&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">stupid</span>). <p>Originally known as a leading food of Italy (especially Naples and Genoa), it was used in English by 1769 to mean &quot;a fop, a dandy&quot; (&quot;typical of elegant young men&quot; would be the sense in &quot;Yankee Doodle&quot;) because it was an exotic dish in England at a time when certain young men who had traveled the continent were affecting French and Italian fashions and accents (and were much mocked for it).</p>
<p>There is said to have been a <span class="foreign">Macaroni Club</span> in Britain by 1764, composed of young men who sought to introduce elegancies of dress and bearing from the continent, which was the immediate source of this usage in English.
