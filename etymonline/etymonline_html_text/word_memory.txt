title="Origin and meaning of memory" href="/word/memory#etymonline_v_12571"><em>memory</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 13c., &quot;recollection (of someone or something); remembrance, awareness or consciousness (of someone or something),&quot; also &quot;fame, renown, reputation;&quot; from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">memorie</span> (Old French <span class="foreign">memoire</span>, 11c., &quot;mind, memory, remembrance; memorial, record&quot;) and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">memoria</span> &quot;memory, remembrance, faculty of remembering,&quot; abstract noun from <span class="foreign">memor</span> &quot;mindful, remembering,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*(s)mer-</span> (1) &quot;to remember.&quot;</p>

<p>Sense of &quot;commemoration&quot; (of someone or something) is from c. 1300. Meaning &quot;faculty of remembering; the mental capacity of retaining unconscious traces of conscious impressions or states, and of recalling these to consciousness in relation to the past,&quot; is late 14c. in English. Meaning &quot;length of time included in the consciousness or observation of an individual&quot; is from 1520s. </p>
<blockquote>I am grown old and my memory is not as active as it used to be. When I was younger I could remember anything, whether it had happened or not; but my faculties are decaying now and soon I shall be so I cannot remember any but the things that never happened. It is sad to go to pieces like this, but we all have to do it. [Mark Twain, &quot;Autobiography&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;that which is remembered; anything fixed in or recalled to the mind&quot; is by 1817, though the correctness of this use was disputed in 19c. The word was extended, with more or less of figurativeness, in 19c. to analogous physical processes. Computer sense, &quot;device which stores information,&quot; is from 1946.
