title="Origin and meaning of father" href="/word/father#etymonline_v_1154"><em>father</em> (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">fæder</span> &quot;he who begets a child, nearest male ancestor;&quot; also &quot;any lineal male ancestor; the Supreme Being,&quot; and by late Old English, &quot;one who exercises parental care over another,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fader</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fadar</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">feder</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">vader</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">faðir</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fatar</span>, German <span class="foreign">vater</span>; in Gothic usually expressed by <span class="foreign">atta</span>), from PIE <span class="foreign">*pəter-</span> &quot;father&quot; (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">pitar-</span>, Greek <span class="foreign">pater</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">pater</span>, Old Persian <span class="foreign">pita</span>, Old Irish <span class="foreign">athir</span> &quot;father&quot;), presumably from baby-speak sound &quot;pa.&quot; The ending formerly was regarded as an agent-noun affix.</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">My heart leaps up when I behold</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">  A rainbow in the sky:</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">So was it when my life began;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">So is it now I am a man;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">So be it when I shall grow old,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">  Or let me die!</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">The Child is father of the Man;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">I could wish my days to be</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Bound each to each by natural piety.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[Wordsworth, 1802]</blockquote>
<p>The classic example of Grimm&#x27;s Law, where PIE &quot;p-&quot; becomes Germanic &quot;f-.&quot; Spelling with <span class="foreign">-th-</span> (15c.) reflects widespread phonetic shift in Middle English that turned <span class="foreign">-der</span> to <span class="foreign">-ther</span> in many words, perhaps reinforced in this case by Old Norse forms; spelling caught up to pronunciation in 1500s (compare <span class="crossreference">mother</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">weather</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">hither</span>, <span class="crossreference">gather</span>). As a title of various Church dignitaries from c. 1300; meaning &quot;creator, inventor, author&quot; is from mid-14c.; that of &quot;anything that gives rise to something else&quot; is from late 14c. As a respectful title for an older man, recorded from 1550s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Father-figure</strong></span> is from 1954. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fathers</strong></span> &quot;leading men, elders&quot; is from 1580s.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/father#etymonline_v_1154"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of father" href="/word/father#etymonline_v_39234"><em>father</em> (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1400, from <span class="crossreference">father</span> (n.).
