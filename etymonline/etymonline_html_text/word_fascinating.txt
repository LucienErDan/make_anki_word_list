<blockquote>To <span class="foreign">fascinate</span> is to bring under a spell, as by the power of the eye; to <span class="foreign">enchant</span> and to <span class="foreign">charm</span> are to bring under a spell by some more subtle and mysterious power. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>Possibly from Greek <span class="foreign">baskanos</span> &quot;slander, envy, malice,&quot; later &quot;witchcraft, sorcerery,&quot; with form influenced by Latin <span class="foreign">fari</span> &quot;speak&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">fame</span> (n.)), but others say the resemblance of the Latin and Greek words is accidental. The Greek word might be from a Thracian equivalent of Greek <span class="foreign">phaskein</span> &quot;to say;&quot; compare <span class="crossreference">enchant</span>, and German <span class="foreign">besprechen</span> &quot;to charm,&quot; from <span class="foreign">sprechen</span> &quot;to speak.&quot; Watkins suggests the Latin word is perhaps from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhasko-</span> &quot;band, bundle&quot; via a connecting sense of &quot;amulet in the form of a phallus&quot; (compare Latin <span class="foreign">fascinum</span> &quot;human penis; artificial phallus; dildo&quot;). Related: <span class="foreign">Fascinated</span>; <span class="crossreference">fascinating</span>.</p>
<blockquote>If <span class="foreign">[baskanos]</span> and <span class="foreign">fascinum</span> are indeed related, they would point to a meaning &#x27;curse, spell&#x27; in a loanword from an unknown third language.
