title="Origin and meaning of disciple" href="/word/disciple#etymonline_v_11384"><em>disciple</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">discipul</span> (fem. <span class="foreign">discipula</span>), &quot;one who follows another for the purpose of learning,&quot; especially &quot;the personal followers of Jesus Christ during his life, the twelve Apostles chosen or called by him to be his immediate associates,&quot; a Biblical borrowing from Latin <span class="foreign">discipulus</span> &quot;pupil, student, follower,&quot; which is of uncertain origin.</p>
<p>In OED and Watkins it is said to be from <span class="foreign">discere</span> &quot;to learn,&quot; from a reduplicated form of the PIE root <span class="crossreference">*dek-</span> &quot;to take, accept.&quot; But according to Barnhart and Klein, it is from a lost compound <span class="foreign">*discipere</span> &quot;to grasp intellectually, analyze thoroughly,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dis-</span> &quot;apart&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">dis-</span>) + <span class="foreign">capere</span> &quot;to take, take hold of,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*kap-</span> &quot;to grasp.&quot; De Vaan finds the ending <span class="foreign">-pulus</span> &quot;difficult to explain&quot; in the former theory and the latter &quot;semantically not compelling.&quot;</p>
<p>It was not common in Old English, where the usual word was <span class="foreign">leorningcniht</span>, and in some cases <span class="foreign">þegn</span> (see <span class="crossreference">thane</span>). The pre-Christian Latin sense of &quot;scholar, pupil, student&quot; is rare in English.
