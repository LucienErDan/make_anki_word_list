canonical hours; the daily service at the canonical hours;&quot; c. 1300, &quot;time of day appointed for prayer, one of the seven canonical hours,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">ore</span>, <span class="foreign">hore</span> &quot;canonical hour; one-twelfth of a day&quot; (sunrise to sunset), from Latin <span class="foreign">hora</span> &quot;an hour;&quot; poetically &quot;time of year, season,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">hōra</span> a word used to indicate any limited time within a year, month, or day, from PIE <span <p>Church sense is oldest in English. Meaning &quot;one of the 24 equal parts of a natural solar day (time from one sunrise to the next), equal hour; definite time of day or night reckoned in equal hours,&quot; and that of &quot;one of the 12 equal parts of an artificial day (sunrise to sunset) or night, varying in duration according to the season; definite time of day or night reckoned in unequal hours&quot; are from late 14c. In the Middle Ages the planets were held to rule over the unequal hours. As late as 16c. distinction sometimes was made in English between <span class="foreign">temporary</span> (unequal) hours and <span class="foreign">sidereal</span> (equal) ones. Meaning &quot;time of a particular happening; the time for a given activity&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">hour of death</span>) is mid-14c.</p>
<p>The <span class="foreign">h-</span> has persisted in this word despite not being pronounced since Roman times. Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">tid</span>, literally &quot;time&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">tide</span> (n.)) and <span class="foreign">stund</span> &quot;period of time, point of time, hour,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stundo</span> (compare German <span class="foreign">Stunde</span> &quot;hour&quot;), which is of uncertain origin. German <span class="foreign">Uhr</span> likewise is from French.</p>
<p>Greek <span class="foreign">hora</span> could mean &quot;a season; &#x27;the season&#x27; (spring or summer).&quot; In classical times it sometimes meant &quot;a part of the day,&quot; such as morning, evening, noon, night. The Greek astronomers apparently borrowed the notion of dividing the day into twelve parts (mentioned in Herodotus) from the Babylonians. Night continued to be divided into four watches (see <span class="crossreference">watch</span> (n.)); but because the amount of daylight changed throughout the year, the hours were not fixed or of equal length.</p>
<p>As a measure of distance (&quot;the distance that can be covered in an hour&quot;) it is recorded from 1785. <span class="foreign"><strong>At all hours</strong></span> &quot;at all times&quot; is from early 15c. In Phoenician it originally had a rough guttural sound like German <span class="foreign">Reich</span> or Scottish <span class="foreign">loch</span>. In Greek at first it had the value of Modern English <span class="foreign">-h-</span>, and with this value it passed into the Latin alphabet via Greek colonies in Italy. Subsequently in Greek it came to be used for a long &quot;e&quot; sound; the &quot;h&quot; sound being indicated by a fragment of the letter, which later was reduced to the aspiration mark. In Germanic it was used for the voiceless breath sound when at the beginning of words, and in the middle or at the end of words for the rough guttural sound, which later came to be written <span class="foreign">-gh</span>.
<br/><br/>
The sound became totally silent in Vulgar Latin and in the languages that emerged from it; thus the letter was omitted in Old French and Italian, but it was restored pedantically in French and Middle English spelling, and often later in English pronunciation. Thus Modern English has words ultimately from Latin with missing <span class="foreign">-h-</span> (<span class="crossreference">able</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">habile</span>); with a silent <span class="foreign">-h-</span> (<span class="crossreference">heir</span>, <span class="crossreference"> hour</span>); with a formerly silent <span class="foreign">-h-</span> now often vocalized (<span class="crossreference">humble</span>, <span class="crossreference">humor</span>, <span class="crossreference">herb</span>); and even a few with an unetymological <span class="foreign">-h-</span> fitted in confusion to words that never had one (<span class="crossreference">hostage</span>, <span class="crossreference">hermit</span>). Relics of the formerly unvoiced <span class="foreign">-h-</span> persist in pedantic insistence on <span class="foreign">an historical (object)</span> and in obsolete <span class="foreign">mine host</span>. 
<br/><br/>
The pronunciation &quot;aitch&quot; was in Old French (<span class="foreign">ache</span> &quot;name of the letter H&quot;), and is from a presumed Late Latin <span class="foreign">*accha</span> (compare Italian <span class="foreign">effe</span>, <span class="foreign">elle</span>, <span class="foreign">emme</span>), with the central sound approximating the rough, guttural value of the letter in Germanic. In earlier Latin the letter was called <span class="foreign">ha</span>. The use in digraphs (as in <span class="foreign">-sh-</span>, <span class="foreign">-th-</span>) goes back to the ancient Greek alphabet, which used it in <span class="foreign">-ph-</span>, <span class="foreign">-th-</span>, <span class="foreign">-kh-</span> until <span class="foreign">-H-</span> took on the value of a long &quot;e&quot; and the digraphs acquired their own characters.
