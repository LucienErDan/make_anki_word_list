[Horace Boies Hawthorn, &quot;The Sociology of Rural Life,&quot; 1926]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/local#etymonline_v_12364"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of coast" href="/word/coast#etymonline_v_45970">coast (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;to skirt, to go around the sides, to go along the border&quot; of something (as a ship does the coastline), from Anglo-French <span class="foreign">costien</span>, from the French source of <span class="crossreference">coast</span> (n.).</p>
<p>The meaning &quot;sled downhill,&quot; first attested 1834 in American English, is a separate borrowing or a new development from the noun. In bicycle-riding, &quot;descend a hill with the feet off the pedals,&quot; from 1879. Of motor vehicles, &quot;to move without thrust from the engine,&quot; from 1896; figurative use, of persons, &quot;not to exert oneself,&quot; by 1934. Related: <span class="foreign">Coasted</span>; <span class="foreign">coasting</span>.</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Coasting&quot; consists in throwing the legs up over the handles and allowing the bicycle to rush of its own impetus down hill. It can only be done with safety where the road is perfectly smooth, hard, and free from obstructions; but, under such conditions, bicycle coasting affords one of the most glorious and exhilarating of sensations, and, next to ballooning, its motion most nearly resembles the flight of a bird. [Harper&#x27;s Weekly, Dec. 20, 1879]</blockquote>
