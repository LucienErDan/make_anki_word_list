<p>Apparently felt as inadequate in its basic sense, leading to the more distinctive formation <span class="foreign">wifman</span> (source of <span class="crossreference">woman</span>). Dutch <span class="foreign">wijf</span> now means, in slang, &quot;girl, babe,&quot; having softened somewhat from earlier sense of &quot;bitch.&quot; The Modern German cognate (<span class="foreign">Weib</span>) also tends to be slighting or derogatory; Middle High German <span class="foreign">wip</span> in early medieval times was &quot;woman, female person,&quot; <span class="foreign">vrouwe</span> (<span class="foreign">Frau</span>) being retained for &quot;woman of gentle birth, lady;&quot; but from c. 1200 <span class="foreign">wip</span> &quot;took on a common, almost vulgar tone that restricted its usage in certain circles&quot; and largely has been displaced by <span class="foreign">Frau</span>.</p>
<p>The more usual Indo-European word is represented in English by <span class="crossreference">queen</span>/<span class="crossreference">quean</span>. Words for &quot;woman&quot; also double for &quot;wife&quot; in some languages. Some proposed PIE roots for <span class="foreign">wife</span> include <span class="foreign">*weip-</span> &quot;to twist, turn, wrap,&quot; perhaps with sense of &quot;veiled person&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">vibrate</span>); and more recently <span class="foreign">*ghwibh-</span>, a proposed root meaning &quot;shame,&quot; also &quot;pudenda,&quot; but the only examples of it would be the Germanic words and Tocharian (a lost IE language of central Asia) <span class="foreign">kwipe</span>, <span class="foreign">kip</span> &quot;female pudenda.&quot;</p>
<p>The modern sense of &quot;female spouse&quot; began as a specialized sense in Old English; the general sense of &quot;woman&quot; is preserved in <span class="crossreference">midwife</span>, <span class="foreign">old wives&#x27; tale</span>, etc. Middle English sense of &quot;mistress of a household&quot; survives in <span class="crossreference">housewife</span>; and the later restricted sense of &quot;tradeswoman of humble rank&quot; in <span class="crossreference">fishwife</span>. <p>Perhaps ultimately &quot;provided with a <span class="foreign">*mari</span>,&quot; a young woman, from PIE <span class="foreign">*mari-, *mori-</span> &quot;young wife, young woman&quot; (source also of Welsh <span class="foreign">morwyn</span> &quot;girl, maiden,&quot; Middle Welsh <span class="foreign">merch</span> &quot;daughter&quot;), akin to <span class="foreign">*meryo-</span> &quot;young man&quot; (source of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">marya-</span> &quot;young man, suitor&quot;).</p>
<p>By early 14c. as &quot;to take (someone) in marriage, take for a husband or wife;&quot; by late 14c. as &quot;become husband and wife according to law or custom; get married (to one another).&quot; Transitive sense, of a priest, etc., who performs the rite of marriage, &quot;to unite in wedlock or matrimony,&quot; by 1520s.</p>
<p>Figurative meaning &quot;unite intimately or by some close bond of connection&quot; is from early 15c. Related: <span class="crossreference">Married</span>; <span class="foreign">marrying</span>. <p>In some Indo-European languages there were distinct &quot;marry&quot; verbs for men and women, though some of these have become generalized. <blockquote>It is notable that it was thought necessary to join <span class="foreign">wif</span>, a neuter noun, representing a female person, to <span class="foreign">man</span>, a masc. noun representing either a male or female person, to form a word denoting a female person exclusively. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>The formation is peculiar to English and Dutch. Replaced older Old English <span class="foreign">wif</span> and <span class="foreign">quean</span> as the word for &quot;female human being.&quot; The pronunciation of the singular altered in Middle English by the rounding influence of <span class="foreign">-w-</span>; the plural retains the original vowel. Meaning &quot;wife,&quot; now largely restricted to U.S. dialectal use, is attested from mid-15c.</p>
<p>In American English, <span class="crossreference">lady</span> is &quot;In loose and especially polite usage, a woman&quot; [Craigie, &quot;Dictionary of American English&quot;]. The effect is odd.&quot; [Harriet Martineau, 1837]</p>
