title="Origin and meaning of silly" href="/word/silly#etymonline_v_23518"><em>silly</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">gesælig</span> &quot;happy, fortuitous, prosperous&quot; (related to <span class="foreign">sæl</span> &quot;happiness&quot;), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*sæligas</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">sæll</span> &quot;happy,&quot; Old Saxon <span class="foreign">salig</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">salich</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">salig</span>, German <span class="foreign">selig</span> &quot;blessed, happy, blissful,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">sels</span> &quot;good, kindhearted&quot;).</p>
<blockquote>This is one of the few instances in which an original long <span class="foreign">e</span> (<span class="foreign">ee</span>) has become shortened to <span class="foreign">i</span>. The same change occurs in <span class="foreign">breeches</span>, and in the American pronunciation of <span class="foreign">been</span>, with no change in spelling. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>The word&#x27;s considerable sense development moved from &quot;happy&quot; to &quot;blessed&quot; to &quot;pious,&quot; to &quot;innocent&quot; (c. 1200), to &quot;harmless,&quot; to &quot;pitiable&quot; (late 13c.), &quot;weak&quot; (c. 1300), to &quot;feeble in mind, lacking in reason, foolish&quot; (1570s). Further tendency toward &quot;stunned, dazed as by a blow&quot; (1886) in <span class="foreign">knocked silly,</span> etc. <span class="foreign">Silly season</span> in journalism slang is from 1861 (August and September, when newspapers compensate for a lack of hard news by filling up with trivial stories). <span class="foreign">Silly Putty</span> trademark claims use from July 1949.</p>
<blockquote>It is a widespread phenomenon that the words for &#x27;innocent&#x27;, apart from their legal use, develop, through &#x27;harmless, guileless&#x27;, a disparaging sense &#x27;credulous, naive, simple, foolish.&#x27; [Buck]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/silly#etymonline_v_23518"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of silliness" href="/word/silliness#etymonline_v_48667">silliness (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;foolishness,&quot; <p>Connection between Old English and Middle English words is uncertain, but if they are the same, meaning had shifted by c. 1400 to &quot;manly, gallant,&quot; and later moved via &quot;attractive, skillfully made,&quot; to &quot;fine,&quot; to &quot;beautiful in a slight way&quot; (mid-15c.). Ironical use from 1530s. For sense evolution, compare <span class="crossreference">nice</span>, <span class="crossreference">silly</span>. Also used of bees (c. 1400). &quot;After the OE. period the word is unknown till the 15th c., when it becomes all at once frequent in various senses, none identical with the OE., though derivable from it&quot; [OED].</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;not a few, considerable&quot; is from late 15c. With a sense of &quot;moderately,&quot; qualifying adjectives and adverbs, since 1560s. <span class="foreign">Pretty please</span> as an emphatic plea is attested from 1902. A <span class="foreign">pretty penny</span> &quot;lot of money&quot; is first recorded 1768.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Pretty</span> applies to that which has symmetry and delicacy, a diminutive beauty, without the higher qualities of gracefulness, dignity, feeling, purpose, etc. A thing not small of its kind may be called <span class="foreign">pretty</span> if it is of little dignity or consequence: as a <span class="foreign">pretty</span> dress or shade of color; but <span class="foreign">pretty</span> is not used of men or their belongings, except in contempt.
