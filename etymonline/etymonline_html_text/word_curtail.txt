<p>Closely related to <span class="crossreference">breach</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">brake</span> (n.1), <span class="crossreference">brick</span> (n.). The old past tense <span class="foreign">brake</span> is obsolete or archaic; past pariticiple is <span class="crossreference">broken</span>, but shortened form <span class="foreign">broke</span> is attested from 14c. and was &quot;exceedingly common&quot; [OED] 17c.-18c.</p>
<p>Of bones in Old English. Formerly also of cloth, paper, etc. Meaning &quot;escape by breaking an enclosure&quot; is from late 14c. Intransitive sense &quot;be or become separated into fragments or parts under action of some force&quot; is from late 12c. Meaning &quot;lessen, impair&quot; is from late 15c. Meaning &quot;make a first and partial disclosure&quot; is from early 13c. Meaning &quot;destroy continuity or completeness&quot; in any way is from 1741. In reference to the heart from early 13c. (intransitive); to <span class="foreign"><strong>break (someone&#x27;s) heart</strong></span> is late 14c.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Break bread</strong></span> &quot;share food&quot; (with) is from late 14c. To <span class="foreign"><strong>break ground</strong></span> is from 1670s as &quot;to dig, plow,&quot; from 1709 in the figurative sense &quot;begin to execute a plan.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>break the ice</strong></span> &quot;overcome the feeling of restraint in a new acquaintanceship&quot; is from c. 1600, in reference to the &quot;coldness&quot; of encounters of strangers. <span class="foreign"><strong>Break wind</strong></span> first attested 1550s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>break (something) out</strong></span> (1890s) probably is an image from dock work, of freeing cargo before unloading it.</p>
<p>The ironic theatrical good luck formula <span class="foreign"><strong>break a leg</strong></span> (by 1948, said to be from at least 1920s) has parallels in German <span class="foreign">Hals- und Beinbruch</span> &quot;break your neck and leg,&quot; and Italian <span class="foreign">in bocca al lupo</span>. Evidence of a highly superstitious craft (see <span class="crossreference">Macbeth</span>). According to Farmer &amp; Henley, in 17c. the expression was used euphemistically, of a woman, &quot;to have a bastard.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/break#etymonline_v_17079"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of cut" href="/word/cut#etymonline_v_509">cut (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;to make, with an edged tool or instrument, an incision in; make incisions for the purpose of dividing into two or more parts; remove by means of a cutting instrument;&quot; of an implement, &quot;have a cutting edge,&quot; according to Middle English Compendium from a presumed Old English <span class="foreign">*cyttan</span>, &quot;since ME has the normal regional variants of the vowel.&quot; Others suggest a possible Scandinavian etymology from North Germanic <span class="foreign">*kut-</span> (source also of Swedish dialectal <span class="foreign">kuta</span> &quot;to cut,&quot; <span class="foreign">kuta</span> &quot;knife,&quot; Old Norse <span class="foreign">kuti</span> &quot;knife&quot;), or that it is from Old French <span class="foreign">couteau</span> &quot;knife.&quot;</p>
<p>It has largely displaced Old English <span class="foreign">ceorfan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">carve</span> (v.)), <span class="foreign">snian</span>, and <span class="foreign">scieran</span> (see <span class="crossreference">shear</span>). The past participle is also <span class="foreign">cut</span>, though <span class="foreign">cutted</span> sometimes has been used since Middle English.</p>
<p>From early 14c. as &quot;to make or fashion by cutting or carving.&quot; From c. 1400 as &quot;to intersect or cross.&quot; From early 15c. as &quot;abridge or shorten by omitting a part.&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to wound the sensibilities of&quot; is from 1580s (to <span class="foreign">cut the heart</span> in the same sense is attested from early 14c.). Sense of &quot;sever connection or relations with&quot; is from 1630s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to be absent without excuse&quot; is British university slang from 1794. Colloquial or slang sense of &quot;move off with directness and rapidity&quot; is from 1580s. Meaning &quot;divide (a deck of cards) at random into parts before the deal&quot; to prevent cheating is from 1530s.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;to dilute, adulterate&quot; (liquor, etc.) is by 1930. Colloquial sense of &quot;to divide or share&quot; is by 1928, perhaps an image from meat-carving at table. As a director&#x27;s call to halt recording or performing, by 1931 (in an article about Pete, the bulldog with the black-ringed eye in the Hal Roach studios shorts, who was said to know the word). The sense of &quot;perform, execute&quot; (c. 1600) is in <span class="foreign">cut capers</span> &quot;frisk about;&quot; <span class="foreign">cut a dash</span> &quot;make a display.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut down</strong></span> is from late 14c. as &quot;to fell;&quot; by 1821 as &quot;to slay&quot; (as with a sword); 1857 as &quot;to curtail.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (someone or something) down to size</strong></span> is from 1821 as &quot;reduce to suitable dimensions;&quot; the figurative sense, &quot;reduce to the proper level of importance,&quot; is by 1927.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut in</strong></span> &quot;enter suddenly and unceremoniously&quot; is from 1610s; sense of &quot;suddenly join in conversation, interrupt&quot; is by 1830. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut up</strong></span> &quot;cut in pieces&quot; is from 1570s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut back</strong></span> is from 1871 as &quot;prune by cutting off shoots,&quot; 1913 in cinematography, &quot;return to a previous scene by repeating a part of it,&quot; 1943 as &quot;reduce, decrease&quot; (of expenditures, etc.). To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut (something) short</strong></span> &quot;abridge, curtail, interrupt&quot; is from 1540s.</p>
<p>In nautical use to <span class="foreign"><strong>cut a feather</strong></span> (1620s) is to move so fast as to make water foam under the bow. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut and run</strong></span> (1704) also is originally nautical, &quot;cut cable and set sail immediately,&quot; as in an emergency, hence, generally, &quot;to make off suddenly.&quot;</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut the teeth</strong></span> &quot;have the teeth grow through the gums&quot; as an infant is from 1670s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut both ways</strong></span> in the figurative sense of &quot;have a good and bad effect&quot; is from c. 1600. To <span class="foreign"><strong>cut loose</strong></span> &quot;set (something) free&quot; is by 1828; intransitive sense &quot;begin to act freely&quot; is by 1909.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Cut it out</strong></span> &quot;remove (something) by or as if by cutting&quot; yielded a figurative use in the command <span class="foreign">cut it out!</span> &quot;Stop! That&#x27;s enough!&quot; by 1933. The evolution seems to have begun earlier. A piece attributed to the Chicago Live Stock World that made the rounds in trade publications 1901-02 begins:</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">When you get &#x27;hot&#x27; about something and vow you are going to rip something or somebody up the back--cut it out.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">If you feel disposed to try the plan of building yourself up by tearing some one else down--cut it out.</blockquote>
