<p>According to Watkins, literally &quot;standard that one follows,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*sekw-no-</span>, from root <span class="crossreference">*sekw-</span> (1) &quot;to follow.&quot; But de Vaan has it from PIE <span class="foreign">*sekh-no-</span> &quot;cut,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*sek-</span> &quot;to cut&quot; He writes: &quot;The etymological appurtenance to <span class="foreign">seco</span> &#x27;to cut&#x27; implies a semantic shift of <span class="foreign">*sek-no-</span> &#x27;what is cut out&#x27;, &#x27;carved out&#x27; &gt; &#x27;sign&#x27;.&quot; But he also also compares Hebrew <span class="foreign">sakkin</span>, Aramaic <span class="foreign">sakkin</span> &quot;slaughtering-knife,&quot; and mentions a theory that &quot;both words are probably borrowed from an unknown third source.&quot;</p>

<p>It has ousted native <span class="crossreference">token</span>. Meaning &quot;token or signal of some condition&quot; (late 13c.) is behind <span class="foreign"><strong>sign of the times</strong></span> (1520s).
