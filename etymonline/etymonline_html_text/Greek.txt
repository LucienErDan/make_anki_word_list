and meaning of Greek" href="/word/Greek#etymonline_v_11949"><em>Greek</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">Grecas</span>, <span class="foreign">Crecas</span> (plural) &quot;Greeks, inhabitants of Greece,&quot; early Germanic borrowing from Latin <span class="foreign">Graeci</span> &quot;the Hellenes,&quot; apparently from Greek <span class="foreign">Graikoi</span>. Aristotle, who was the first to use <span class="foreign">Graikhos</span> as equivalent to <span class="foreign">Hellenes</span> (<span class="foreign">&quot;Meteorologica&quot;</span> I.xiv), wrote that it was the name originally used by Illyrians for the Dorians in Epirus, from <span class="foreign">Graii</span>, native name of the people of Epirus.</p>
<p>But a modern theory (put forth by German classical historian Georg Busolt, 1850-1920), derives it from <span class="foreign">Graikhos</span> &quot;inhabitant of <span class="foreign">Graia</span>&quot; (literally &quot;gray,&quot; also &quot;old, withered&quot;), a town on the coast of Boeotia, which was the name given by the Romans to all Greeks, originally to the Greek colonists from Graia who helped found Cumae (9c. B.C.E.), the important city in southern Italy where the Latins first encountered Greeks. Under this theory, it was reborrowed in this general sense by the Greeks.</p>
<p>The Germanic languages originally borrowed the word with an initial &quot;-k-&quot; sound (compare Old High German <span class="foreign">Chrech</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">Kreks</span>), which probably was their initial sound closest to the Latin &quot;-g-&quot; at the time; the word was later refashioned. From late 14c. as &quot;the Greek language.&quot; Meaning &quot;unintelligible speech, gibberish, any language of which one is ignorant&quot; is from c. 1600. Meaning &quot;member of a Greek-letter fraternity&quot; is student slang, 1884.</p>
