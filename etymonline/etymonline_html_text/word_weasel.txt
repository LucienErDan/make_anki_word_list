title="Origin and meaning of weasel" href="/word/weasel#etymonline_v_43949"><em>weasel</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;to deprive (a word or phrase) of its meaning,&quot; 1900, from <span class="crossreference">weasel</span> (n.); so used because the weasel sucks out the contents of eggs, leaving the shell intact. Both this and <span class="foreign">weasel-word</span> are first attested in &quot;The Stained-Glass Political Platform,&quot; a short story by Stewart Chaplin, first printed in &quot;Century Magazine,&quot; June 1900:</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Why, weasel words are words that suck all the life out of the words next to them, just as a weasel sucks an egg and leaves the shell. <p>They were picked up at once in American political slang. The sense of &quot;extricate oneself (from a difficult place) like a weasel&quot; is first recorded 1925; that of &quot;to evade and equivocate&quot; is from 1956.
