</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/clock#etymonline_v_13831"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fire" href="/word/fire#etymonline_v_5966">fire (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">fyr</span> &quot;fire, a fire,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*fūr-</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">fiur</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">fiur</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">fürr</span>, Middle Dutch and Dutch <span class="foreign">vuur</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">fiur</span>, German <span class="foreign">Feuer</span> &quot;fire&quot;), from PIE <span class="foreign">*perjos</span>, from root <span class="crossreference">*paewr-</span> &quot;fire.&quot; Current spelling is attested as early as 1200, but did not fully displace Middle English <span class="foreign">fier</span> (preserved in <span class="crossreference">fiery</span>) until c. 1600.</p>
<p>PIE apparently had two roots for fire: <span class="foreign">*paewr-</span> and <span class="foreign">*egni-</span> (source of Latin <span class="foreign">ignis</span>). The former was &quot;inanimate,&quot; referring to fire as a substance, and the latter was &quot;animate,&quot; referring to it as a living force (compare <span class="crossreference">water</span> (n.1)).</p>
<blockquote>Brend child fuir fordredeþ [&quot;The Proverbs of Hendyng,&quot; c. 1250]</blockquote>
<p>English <span class="foreign">fire</span> was applied to &quot;ardent, burning&quot; passions or feelings from mid-14c. Meaning &quot;discharge of firearms, action of guns, etc.&quot; is from 1580s. To be <span class="foreign">on fire</span> is from c. 1500 (<span class="foreign">in fire</span> attested from c. 1400, as is <span class="foreign">on a flame</span> &quot;on fire&quot;). To <span class="foreign"><strong>play with fire</strong></span> in the figurative sense &quot;risk disaster, meddle carelessly or ignorantly with a dangerous matter&quot; is by 1861, from the common warning to children. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>where&#x27;s the fire?</strong></span>, said to one in an obvious hurry, is by 1917, American English.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Fire-bell</strong></span> is from 1620s; <span class="foreign"><strong>fire-alarm</strong></span> as a self-acting, mechanical device is from 1808 as a theoretical creation; practical versions began to appear in the early 1830s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire-escape</strong></span> (n.) is from 1788 (the original so-called was a sort of rope-ladder disguised as a small settee); <span class="foreign"><strong>fire-extinguisher</strong></span> is from 1826. A <span class="foreign"><strong>fire-bucket</strong></span> (1580s) carries water to a fire. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire-house</strong></span> is from 1899; <span class="foreign"><strong>fire-hall</strong></span> from 1867, <span class="foreign"><strong>fire-station</strong></span> from 1828. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire company</strong></span> &quot;men for managing a fire-engine&quot; is from 1744, American English. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire brigade</strong></span> &quot;firefighters organized in a body in a particular place&quot; is from 1838. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire department</strong></span>, usually a branch of local government, is from 1805. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire-chief</strong></span> is from 1877; <span class="foreign"><strong>fire-ranger</strong></span> from 1909.</p>
<p>Symbolic <span class="foreign"><strong>fire and the sword</strong></span> is by c. 1600 (translating Latin <span class="foreign">flamma ferroque absumi</span>); earlier <span class="foreign">yron and fyre</span> (1560s), <span class="foreign">with suerd &amp; flawme</span> (mid-15c.), <span class="foreign">mid fure &amp; mid here</span> (&quot;with fire and armed force&quot;), c. 1200. <span class="foreign"><strong>Fire-breathing</strong></span> is from 1590s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>set the river on fire</strong></span>, &quot;accomplish something surprising or remarkable&quot; (usually with a negative and said of one considered foolish or incompetent) is by 1830, often with the name of a river, varying according to locality, but the original is <span class="foreign">set the Thames on fire</span> (1796). The hypothetical feat was mentioned as the type of something impossibly difficult by 1720; it circulated as a theoretical possibility under some current models of chemistry c. 1792-95, which may have contributed to the rise of the expression.</p>
<blockquote>[A]mong other fanciful modes of demonstrating the practicability of conducting the gas wherever it might be required, he anchored a small boat in the stream about 50 yards from the shore, to which he conveyed a pipe, having the end turned up so as to rise above the water, and forcing the gas through the pipe, lighted it just above the surface, observing to his friends &quot;that he had now set the river on fire.&quot; [&quot;On the Origins and Progress of Gas-lighting,&quot; in &quot;Repertory of Patent Inventions,&quot; vol.
