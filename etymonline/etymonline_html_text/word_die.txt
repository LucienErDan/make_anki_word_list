and meaning of die" href="/word/die#etymonline_v_8549"><em>die</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-12c., <span class="foreign">dien, deighen</span>, of sentient beings, &quot;to cease to live,&quot; possibly from Old Danish <span class="foreign">døja</span> or Old Norse <span class="foreign">deyja</span> &quot;to die, pass away,&quot; both from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*dawjan</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">deja</span> &quot;to kill,&quot; Old Saxon <span class="foreign">doian</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">touwen</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">diwans</span> &quot;mortal&quot;), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*dheu-</span> (3) &quot;to pass away, die, become senseless&quot; (source also of Old Irish <span class="foreign">dith</span> &quot;end, death,&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">daviti</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">davit&#x27;</span> &quot;to choke, suffer&quot;).</p>
<p>It has been speculated that Old English had <span class="foreign">*diegan</span>, from the same source, but it is not in any of the surviving texts and the preferred words were <span class="foreign">steorfan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">starve</span>), <span class="foreign">sweltan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">swelter</span>), <span class="foreign">wesan dead</span> (&quot;become dead&quot;), also <span class="foreign">forðgan</span> and other euphemisms.</p>
<p>Languages usually don&#x27;t borrow words from abroad for central life experiences, but &quot;die&quot; words are an exception; they often are hidden or changed euphemistically out of superstitious dread. A Dutch euphemism translates as &quot;to give the pipe to Maarten.&quot;</p>
<p>Regularly spelled <span class="foreign">dege</span> through 15c., and still pronounced &quot;dee&quot; by some in Lancashire and Scotland. Of plants, &quot;become devitalized, wither,&quot; late 14c.; in a general sense of &quot;come to an end&quot; from mid-13c. Meaning &quot;be consumed with a great longing or yearning&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">dying to go</span>) is colloquial, from 1709. Used figuratively (of sounds, etc.) from 1580s; to <span class="foreign"><strong>die away</strong></span> &quot;diminish gradually&quot; is from 1670s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>die down</strong></span> &quot;subside&quot; is by 1834. Related: <span class="foreign">Died</span>; <span class="foreign">dies</span>.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>die out</strong></span> &quot;become extinct&quot; is from 1865. To <span class="foreign"><strong>die game</strong></span> &quot;preserve a bold, resolute, and defiant spirit to the end&quot; (especially of one facing the gallows) is from 1793. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>never say die</strong></span> &quot;don&#x27;t give up or in&quot; is by 1822; the earliest contexts are in sailors&#x27; jargon.</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Never look so cloudy about it messmate,&quot; the latter continued in an unmoved tone--&quot;Cheer up man, the rope is not twisted for your neck yet. <p>The numbers on the opposite sides always add up to seven; otherwise there is no uniformity to their arrangement. Sense of &quot;engraved stamping block or tool used for stamping a softer material&quot; is from 1690s. Perhaps so called because they often were used in pairs (to impress on both sides, as of a coin).
