<p>Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">andwlita</span> &quot;face, countenance&quot; (from root of <span class="foreign">wlitan</span> &quot;to see, look&quot;) and <span class="foreign">ansyn</span>, <span class="foreign">ansien</span>, the usual word (from the root of <span class="foreign">seon</span> &quot;see&quot;). <p>From late 14c. as &quot;outward appearance (as contrasted to some other reality);&quot; also from late 14c. as &quot;forward part or front of anything;&quot; also &quot;surface (of the earth or sea), extent (of a city).&quot; Typographical sense of &quot;part of the type which forms the letter&quot; is from 1680s.</p>

<p>Now &quot;a large and important town,&quot; but originally in early Middle English a walled town, a capital or cathedral town. Distinction from <span class="crossreference">town</span> is early 14c. OED calls it &quot;Not a native designation, but app[arently] at first a somewhat grandiose title, used instead of the OE. <span class="foreign">burh&quot; </span>(see <span class="crossreference">borough</span>).</p>
<p>Between Latin and English the sense was transferred from the inhabitants to the place. The Latin word for &quot;city&quot; was <span class="foreign">urbs</span>, but a resident was <span class="foreign">civis</span>. <span class="foreign">Civitas</span> seems to have replaced <span class="foreign">urbs</span> as Rome (the ultimate <span class="foreign">urbs</span>) lost its prestige. <p>London is <span class="foreign">the city</span> from 1550s.
