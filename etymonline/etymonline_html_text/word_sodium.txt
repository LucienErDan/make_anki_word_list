Another theory, considered far-fetched in some quarters, traces it to Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">sodanum</span> &quot;a headache remedy,&quot; ultimately from Arabic <span class="foreign">suda</span> &quot;splitting headache.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Soda is found naturally in alkaline lakes, in deposits where such lakes have dried, and from ash produced by burning various seaside plants. A major trading commodity in the medieval Mediterranean, since commercial manufacture of it began in France in late 18c., these other sources have been abandoned. <span class="foreign">Washing soda</span> (sodium carbonate) is commonly distinguished from <span class="foreign">baking soda</span> (sodium bicarbonate). A <span class="foreign">soda-cracker</span> (1863) has baking soda as an ingredient. 
<br/><br/>

Since 19c. typically flavored and sweetened with syrups.  First record of <span class="foreign">soda pop</span> is from 1863, and the most frequent modern use of the word is as a shortening of this or other terms for &quot;flavored, sweetened soda water.&quot; Compare <span class="crossreference">pop</span> (n.1). <span class="foreign">Soda fountain</span> is from 1824; <span class="foreign">soda jerk</span> first attested 1915 (<span class="foreign">soda-jerker</span> is from 1883).
