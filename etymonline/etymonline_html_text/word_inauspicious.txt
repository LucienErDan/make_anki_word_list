<p>Through the Middle Ages, calendars marked as unlucky two days of each month (Jan. 1, 25; Feb. 4, 26; March 1, 28; April 10, 20; May 3, 25; June 10, 16; July 13, 22; Aug. 1, 30; Sept. 3, 21; Oct. 3, 22; Nov. 5, 28; Dec. 7, 22), supposedly based on the ancient calculations of Egyptian astrologers.</p>
<p>By 1580s the English word had been extended to &quot;gloomy, dreary, cheerless,&quot; and was used to describe physical surroundings, sounds, or anything else felt as tending to depress the spirits. In North America, it was the name given along the seacoast and sounds around North Carolina to tracts of swampy land and dead trees (1763). Legally, in U.S., it hinged on &quot;whether to the average person, applying contemporary community standards, the dominant theme of the material taken as a whole appeals to a prurient interest.&quot; [Justice William Brennan, &quot;Roth v. United States,&quot; June 24, 1957]; refined in 1973 by &quot;Miller v. California&quot;:</p>
<blockquote>The basic guidelines for the trier of fact must be: (a) whether &#x27;the average person, applying contemporary community standards&#x27; would find that the work, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest, (b) whether the work depicts or describes, in a patently offensive way, sexual conduct specifically defined by the applicable state law; and (c) whether the work, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value.</blockquote>

This is from <span class="foreign">euphemizein</span> &quot;speak with fair words, use words of good omen,&quot; from <span class="foreign">eu-</span> &quot;good, well&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">eu-</span>) + <span class="foreign">pheme</span> &quot;speech, voice, utterance, a speaking,&quot; from <span class="foreign">phanai</span> &quot;speak&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*bha-</span> (2) &quot;to speak, tell, say&quot;). Related: <span class="foreign">Euphemistic</span>; <span class="foreign">euphemistically</span>.</p>
<blockquote>All the ancients, but most of all the Athenians, were careful not to use ill-omened words; so they called the prison &#x27;the chamber,&#x27; and the executioner &#x27;the public man,&#x27; and the Furies (Erinyes) they called &#x27;Eumenides&#x27; (&#x27;the kindly ones&#x27;) or &#x27;the Venerable Goddesses.&#x27; &quot; [Helladius of Antinoopolis, 4 c. C.E., quoted by Photius]</blockquote>

<blockquote>Thus, in our dialect, a vicious man is a man of pleasure, a sharper is one that plays the whole game, a lady is said to have an affair, a gentleman to be a gallant, a rogue in business to be one that knows the world.
