<p>The little balls (which left white marks) were substitutes for the small sugar-plum candies that traditionally were thrown during Italian carnivals; the custom was adopted in England by early 19c. for weddings and other occasions, with symbolic tossing of little bits of paper (which are called <span class="foreign">confetti</span> by 1846).</p>
<blockquote>The chief amusement of the Carnival consists in throwing the <span class="foreign">confetti</span>--a very ancient practice, and which, with a little research, may be traced up through the Italian Chronicles to the time of the Romans. The <span class="foreign">confetti</span> were originally of sugar, and the nobility still pique themselves on adhering to so costly a material. The people have degraded them to small balls of lime, which allows more sport, and takes in a much greater number of combatants. [Dr. Abraham Eldon, &quot;The Continental Traveller&#x27;s Oracle; or, Maxims for Foreign Locomotion,&quot; London, 1828]</blockquote>

<blockquote>[The Roman ladies] are generally provided with a small basket of <span class="foreign">confetti</span>, and as their acquaintance and admirers pass in review, they must be prepared to receive a volley of them. It is thought quite the supreme <span class="foreign">bon ton</span> for a Roman beau, to mark how many distinguished beauties he is in favour with, by having both his coat and hat covered as white as a miller with the flour of these <span class="foreign">confetti</span>. [John Bramsen, &quot;Letters of a Prussian Traveller,&quot; 1818]</blockquote>

<p>At first with a sense of betrayal or malicious exposure (<span class="foreign">discoverer</span> originally meant &quot;informant&quot;). Also in Middle English used in lteral senses, such as &quot;to remove&quot; (one&#x27;s hat, the roof from a building). The meaning &quot;to obtain the first knowledge or sight of what was before not known,&quot; the main modern sense, is by 1550s.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Discover, Invent</span>, agree in signifying to find out; but we <span class="foreign">discover</span> what already exists, though to us unknown; we <span class="foreign">invent</span> what did not before exist: as, to <span class="foreign">discover</span> the applicability of steam to the purposes of locomotion, and to <span class="foreign">invent</span> the machinery necessary to use steam for these ends. ... Some things are of so mixed a character that either word may be applied to them. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>Sense of &quot;make famous or fashionable&quot; is by 1908. Related: <span class="foreign">Discovered</span>; <span class="foreign">discovering</span>.</p>
