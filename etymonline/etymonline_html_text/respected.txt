The first element probably is cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">haga</span> &quot;enclosure, portion of woodland marked off for cutting&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">hedge</span> (n.)). Old Norse had <span class="foreign">tunriða</span> and Old High German <span class="foreign">zunritha</span>, both literally &quot;hedge-rider,&quot; used of witches and ghosts. The second element in the prehistoric compound may be connected with Norwegian <span class="foreign">tysja</span> &quot;fairy; crippled woman,&quot; Gaulish <span class="foreign">dusius</span> &quot;demon,&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">dvasia</span> &quot;spirit,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*dhewes-</span> &quot;to fly about, smoke, be scattered, vanish.&quot;
<br/><br/>
One of the magic words for which there is no male form, suggesting its original meaning was close to &quot;diviner, soothsayer,&quot; which were always female in northern European paganism, and <span class="foreign">hægtesse</span> seem at one time to have meant &quot;woman of prophetic and oracular powers&quot; (Ælfric uses it to render the Greek &quot;pythoness,&quot; the voice of the Delphic oracle), a figure greatly feared and respected. Later, the word was used of village wise women.
<br/><br/>
<span class="foreign">Haga</span> is also the <span class="foreign">haw-</span> in <span class="crossreference">hawthorn</span>, which is an important tree in northern European pagan religion. There may be several layers of folk etymology here. Confusion or blending with <span class="foreign">heathenish</span> is suggested by Middle English <span class="foreign">hæhtis</span>, <span class="foreign">hægtis</span> &quot;hag, witch, fury, etc.,&quot; and <span class="foreign">haetnesse</span> &quot;goddess,&quot; used of Minerva and Diana.
<br/><br/>
If the <span class="foreign">hægtesse</span> once was a powerful supernatural woman (in Norse it is an alternative word for <span class="crossreference">Norn</span>, any of the three weird sisters, the equivalent of the Fates), it might originally have carried the hawthorn sense. Later, when the pagan magic was reduced to local scatterings, it might have had the sense of &quot;hedge-rider,&quot; or &quot;she who straddles the hedge,&quot; because the hedge was the boundary between the civilized world of the village and the wild world beyond. The <span class="foreign">hægtesse</span> would have a foot in each reality. Even later, when it meant the local healer and root collector, living in the open and moving from village to village, it may have had the mildly pejorative Middle English sense of <span class="foreign">hedge-</span> (<span class="foreign">hedge-priest</span>, etc.), suggesting an itinerant sleeping under bushes. <br/><br/>
A partial revival of <span class="foreign">-or</span> on the Latin model took place from 16c. (<span class="foreign">governour</span> began to lose its <span class="foreign">-u-</span> 16c. and it was gone by 19c.), and also among phonetic spellers in both England and America (John Wesley wrote that <span class="foreign">-or</span> was &quot;a fashionable impropriety&quot; in England in 1791).
<br/><br/>
Webster criticized the habit of deleting <span class="foreign">-u-</span> in <span class="foreign">-our</span> words in his first speller (&quot;A Grammatical Institute of the English Language,&quot; commonly called the Blue-Black Speller) in 1783. His own deletion of the <span class="foreign">-u-</span> began with the revision of 1804, and was enshrined in the influential &quot;Comprehensive Dictionary of the English Language&quot; (1806), which also established in the U.S. <span class="foreign">-ic</span> for British <span class="foreign">-ick</span> and <span class="foreign">-er</span> for <span class="foreign">-re</span>, along with many other attempts at reformed spelling which never caught on (such as <span class="foreign">masheen</span> for <span class="foreign">machine</span>). His attempt to justify them on the grounds of etymology and the custom of great writers does not hold up.
<br/><br/>
Fowler notes the British drop the <span class="foreign">-u-</span> when forming adjectives ending in <span class="foreign">-orous</span> (<span class="foreign">humorous</span>) and derivatives in <span class="foreign">-ation</span> and <span class="foreign">-ize</span>, in which cases the Latin origin is respected (such as <span class="foreign">vaporize</span>). When the Americans began to consistently spell it one way, however, the British reflexively hardened their insistence on the other. &quot;The American abolition of <span class="foreign">-our</span> in
