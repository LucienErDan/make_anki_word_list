Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stab-</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">staf</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">stafr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">stav</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">stef</span>, Middle Low German and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">staf</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">stab</span>, German <span <p>As &quot;pole from which a flag is flown,&quot; 1610s. In musical notation from 1660s. Sense of &quot;group of military officers that assists a commander&quot; is attested from 1702, apparently from German, from the notion of the &quot;baton&quot; that is a badge of office or authority (a sense attested in English from 1530s); hence <span class="foreign">staff officer</span> (1702), <span class="foreign">staff-sergeant</span> (1811). Meaning &quot;group of employees (as at an office or hospital)&quot; is first found 1837. <span class="foreign">Staff of life</span> &quot;bread&quot; is from the Biblical phrase <span class="foreign">break the staff of bread</span> meaning &quot;cut off the supply of food&quot; (Leviticus xxvi.26), translating Hebrew <span class="foreign">matteh lekhem</span>.</p>
