and meaning of about" href="/word/about#etymonline_v_63"><em>about</em> (adv., prep.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Middle English <span class="foreign">aboute</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">abutan</span> (adv., prep.), earlier <span class="foreign">onbutan</span> &quot;on the outside of; around the circumference of, enveloping; in the vicinity of, near; hither and thither, from place to place,&quot; also &quot;with a rotating or spinning motion,&quot; in late Old English &quot;near in time, number, degree, etc., approximately;&quot; a compound or contraction of <span class="foreign">on</span> (see <span class="crossreference">on</span>; also see <span class="crossreference">a-</span> (1)) + <span class="foreign">be</span> &quot;by&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">by</span>) + <span class="foreign">utan</span> &quot;outside,&quot; from <span class="foreign">ut</span> (see <span class="crossreference">out</span> (adv.)).</p>

<p>By c. 1300 it had developed senses of &quot;around, in a circular course, round and round; on every side, so as to surround; in every direction;&quot; also &quot;engaged in&quot; (<span class="foreign">Wist ye not that I must be about my Father&#x27;s business?</span>), and gradually it forced out Old English <span class="foreign">ymbe</span>, <span class="foreign">ymbutan</span> (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*ambhi-</span> &quot;around&quot;) in the sense &quot;round about, in the neighborhood of.&quot;</p>

<p>From mid-13c. as &quot;in the matter, in connection with.&quot; From early 14c. as &quot;in partial rotation, so as to face in a different direction.&quot; From late 14c. as &quot;near at hand, about one&#x27;s person.&quot; &quot;In a circuitous course,&quot; hence &quot;on the move&quot; (late 13c.), and in Middle English &quot;be about to do, be busy in preparation for,&quot; hence its use as a future participle in <span class="foreign">(to be) about to</span> &quot;in readiness, intending.&quot; <span class="foreign">Abouts</span> (late 14c.), with adverbial genitive, still found in <span class="foreign">hereabouts</span>, etc., probably is a northern dialectal form.</p>

<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>bring about</strong></span> &quot;cause or affect&quot; and to <span class="foreign"><strong>come about</strong></span> &quot;happen&quot; are from late 14c. <span class="foreign"><strong>About face</strong></span> as a military command (short for <span class="foreign">right about face</span>) is first attested 1861, American English.</p>
