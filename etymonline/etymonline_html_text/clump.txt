<p>The <span class="foreign">club</span> suit in the deck of cards (1560s) bears the correct name (Spanish <span class="foreign">basto</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">bastone</span>), but the pattern adopted on English decks is the French trefoil. <p>The sense &quot;company of persons organized to meet for social intercourse or to promote some common object&quot; (1660s) apparently evolved from this word from the verbal sense &quot;gather in a club-like mass&quot; (1620s), then, as a noun, &quot;association of people&quot; (1640s).</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">We now use the word clubbe for a sodality in a tavern. [John Aubrey, 1659]</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Admission to membership of clubs is commonly by ballot. Clubs are now an important feature of social life in all large cities, many of them occupying large buildings containing reading-rooms, libraries, restaurants, etc. [Century Dictionary, 1902]</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">I got a good mind to join a club and beat you over the head with it. [Rufus T. Firefly] </blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Join the club</span> &quot;become one of a number of people having a common experience&quot; is by 1944.<span class="foreign"> Club soda</span> is by 1881, originally a proprietary name (Cantrell &amp; Cochrane, Dublin). <span class="foreign">Club car</span> is from 1890, American English, originally one well-appointed and reserved for members of a club run by the railway company; later of any railway car fitted with chairs instead of benches and other amenities (1917). Hence <span class="foreign">club</span> for &quot;class of fares between first-class and transit&quot; (1978).</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">The club car is one of the most elaborate developments of the entire Commuter idea. It is a comfortable coach, which is rented to a group of responsible men coming either from a single point or a chain of contiguous points. The railroad charges from $250 to $300 a month for the use of this car in addition to the commutation fares, and the &quot;club&quot; arranges dues to cover this cost and the cost of such attendants and supplies as it may elect to place on its roving house. [Edward Hungerford, &quot;The Modern Railroad,&quot; 1911]</blockquote>
