title="Origin and meaning of church" href="/word/church#etymonline_v_11358"><em>church</em> (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">cirice</span>, <span class="foreign">circe</span> &quot;place of assemblage set aside for Christian worship; the body of Christian believers, Christians collectively; ecclesiastical authority or power,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kirika</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">kirika</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">kirkja</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">zerke</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">kerke</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">kerk</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kirihha</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kirche</span>).</p>
<p>This is probably [see extensive note in OED] borrowed via an unrecorded Gothic word from Greek <span class="foreign">kyriake (oikia)</span>, <span class="foreign">kyriakon doma</span> &quot;the Lord&#x27;s (house),&quot; from <span class="foreign">kyrios</span> &quot;ruler, lord,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*keue-</span> &quot;to swell&quot; (&quot;swollen,&quot; hence &quot;strong, powerful&quot;). </p>
<p>Greek <span class="foreign">kyriakon</span> (adj.) &quot;of the Lord&quot; was used of houses of Christian worship since c.300, especially in the East, though it was less common in this sense than <span class="foreign">ekklesia</span> or <span class="foreign">basilike</span>. An example of the direct Greek-to-Germanic transmission of many Christian words, via the Goths; probably it was used by West Germanic people in their pre-Christian period.</p>
<p>The word also was picked up by the Slavic tongues, probably via Germanic (Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">criky</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">cerkov</span>). Finnish <span class="foreign">kirkko</span>, Estonian <span class="foreign">kirrik</span> are from Scandinavian. Romance and Celtic languages use variants of Latin <span class="foreign">ecclesia</span> (such as French <span class="foreign">église</span>, 11c.).</p>
<p>Phonetic spelling from c. 1200, established by 16c. For vowel evolution, see <span class="crossreference">bury</span>. After the Reformation, <span class="foreign">church</span> was used for any particular Christian denomination agreeing on doctrine and forms of worship.</p>
<p>As an adjective, &quot;pertaining to a church,&quot; from 1570s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Church-bell</strong></span> was in late Old English. <span class="foreign"><strong>Church-goer</strong></span> is from 1680s. <span class="foreign"><strong>Church-key</strong></span> &quot;key of a church door&quot; is from early 14c.; slang use for &quot;can or bottle opener&quot; is by 1954, probably originally U.S. college student slang. <span class="foreign"><strong>Church-mouse</strong></span> (1731) &quot;a mouse supposed to live in a church&quot; (where there is nothing for it to eat) is proverbial in many languages for poverty.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/church#etymonline_v_11358"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of church" href="/word/church#etymonline_v_47182"><em>church</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;to bring or lead to church,&quot; mid-14c., from <span class="crossreference">church</span> (n.).
