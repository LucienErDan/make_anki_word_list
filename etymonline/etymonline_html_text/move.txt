<p>Of the physical meanings, the earliest in English (late 13c.) is the intransitive one of &quot;change one&#x27;s place or posture, stir, shift; move the body; move from one&#x27;s place, change position. That of &quot;to go (from one place to another), journey, travel; set out, proceed&quot; is from c. 1300. The transitive sense of &quot;cause to change place or position; shift; dislodge; set in motion&quot; is from late 14c., as is that of &quot;impart motion to, impel; set or sustain in motion.&quot; The intransitive sense of &quot;pass from place to place; journey; travel; change position continuously or occasionally&quot; is from c. 1300.</p>
<p>The emotional, figurative, and non-material senses also are mostly from Middle English: The earliest is &quot;excite to action; influence; induce; incite; arouse; awaken&quot; the senses or mental faculties or emotions (late 13c.); specifically &quot;affect (someone) emotionally, rouse to pity or tenderness&quot; by early 14c. Hence also &quot;influence (someone, to do something), guide, prompt or impel toward some action&quot; (late 14c.).</p>
<p>The sense of &quot;propose; bring forward; offer formally; submit,&quot; as a motion for consideration by a deliberative assembly&quot; is by early 15c. Sense of &quot;to change one&#x27;s place of residence&quot; is from 1707. In chess, checkers, and similar games, &quot;to change the position of a piece in the course of play,&quot; late 15c. Commercial sense of &quot;sell, cause to be sold&quot; is by 1900.</p>
<p>The policeman&#x27;s order to <span class="foreign"><strong>move on</strong></span> is attested by 1831. To <span class="foreign"><strong>move heaven and earth</strong></span> &quot;make extraordinary efforts&quot; is by 1798.
