<p>In late Old English or early Middle English the sense was extended to include &quot;table;&quot; hence the transferred meaning &quot;food&quot; (early 14c.), as &quot;that which is served upon a table,&quot; especially &quot;daily meals provided at a place of lodging&quot; (late 14c.). Compare <span class="crossreference">boarder</span>, <span class="crossreference">boarding</span>, and Old Norse <span class="foreign">borð</span>, which also had a secondary sense of &quot;table&quot; and an extended sense &quot;maintenance at table.&quot; Hence also <span class="foreign"><strong>above board</strong></span> &quot;honest, open&quot; (1610s; compare modern <span class="foreign">under the table</span> &quot;dishonest&quot;). A further extension is to &quot;table where council is held&quot; (1570s), then transferred to &quot;leadership council, persons having the management of some public or private concern&quot; (1610s), as in <span class="foreign">board of directors</span> (1712).</p>
<blockquote>&quot;Bow to the board,&quot; said Bumble. Oliver brushed away two or three tears that were lingering in his eyes; and seeing no board but the table, fortunately bowed to that.</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;table upon which public notices are written&quot; is from mid-14c. Meaning &quot;table upon which a game is played&quot; is from late 14c. <p>If not etymologically related to <span class="foreign">board</span> (n.1), the two forms represented in English by these words were nonetheless confused at an early date in most Germanic languages, a situation made worse in English because this Germanic word also was adopted in Medieval Latin as <span class="foreign">bordus</span> (source of Italian and Spanish <span class="foreign">bordo</span>) and entered Old French as <span class="foreign">bort</span> &quot;beam, board, plank; side of a ship&quot; (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">bord</span>), via either Medieval Latin or Frankish, and from thence it came over with the Normans to mingle with its native cousins. By now the senses are inextricably tangled. Some etymology dictionaries treat them as having been the same word all along.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>go by the board</strong></span> originally was &quot;fall overboard&quot; (1757), of a mast, etc., hence, generally, &quot;be completely lost or destroyed&quot; (1835).
