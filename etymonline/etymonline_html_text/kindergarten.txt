class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1852, from German <span class="foreign">Kinder-Garten</span> (1840), literally &quot;children-garden, garden of children,&quot; a metaphoric name from <span class="foreign">Kinder</span> &quot;children&quot; (plural of <span class="foreign">Kind</span> &quot;child;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">kin</span> (n.)) + <span class="foreign">Garten</span> &quot;garden&quot; (from suffixed form of PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gher-</span> (1) &quot;to grasp, enclose&quot;). Coined by German educator Friedrich Fröbel (1782-1852) in reference to his method of developing intelligence in young children. Compare the double sense in <span class="crossreference">nursery</span></p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Kindergarten</span> means a garden of children, and Froebel, the inventor of it, or rather, as he would prefer to express it, <span class="foreign">the discoverer of the method of Nature</span>, meant to symbolize by the name the spirit and plan of treatment. How does the gardener treat his plants? He studies their individual natures, and puts them into such circumstances of soil and atmosphere as enable them to grow, flower, and bring forth fruit,-- also to renew their manifestation year after year. [Mann, Horace, and Elizabeth P. Peabody, &quot;Moral Culture of Infancy and Kindergarten Guide,&quot; Boston, 1863]</blockquote>
<p>The first in England was established 1850 by Johannes Ronge, German Catholic priest; in America, 1868, by Elizabeth Peabody of Boston, Mass. Taken into English untranslated, whereas other nations that borrowed the institution nativized the name (Danish <span class="foreign">börnehave</span>, Modern Hebrew <span class="foreign">gan yeladim</span>, literally &quot;garden of children&quot;).
