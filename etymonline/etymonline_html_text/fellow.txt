title="Origin and meaning of fellow" href="/word/fellow#etymonline_v_5838"><em>fellow</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;companion, comrade,&quot; c. 1200, from Old English <span class="foreign">feolaga</span> &quot;partner, one who shares with another,&quot; from Old Norse <span class="foreign">felagi</span>, from <span class="foreign">fe</span> &quot;money&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">fee</span>) + <span class="foreign">lag</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*lagam</span>, from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*legh-</span> &quot;to lie down, lay.&quot; The etymological sense of <span class="foreign">fellow</span> seems to be &quot;one who puts down money with another in a joint <br/><br/>
Meaning &quot;one of the same kind&quot; is from early 13c.; that of &quot;one of a pair&quot; is from c. 1300. Used familiarly since mid-15c. for &quot;any man, male person,&quot; but not etymologically masculine (it is used of women, for example, in Judges xi.37 in the King James version: &quot;And she said unto her father, Let this thing be done for me: let me alone two months, that I may go up and down upon the mountains, and bewail my virginity, I and my fellows&quot;). Its use can be contemptuous or dignified in English and American English, and at different times in its history, depending on who used it to whom, it has carried a tinge of condescension or insult. University senses (mid-15c., corresponding to Latin <span class="foreign">socius</span>) evolved from notion of &quot;one of the corporation who constitute a college&quot; and who are paid from its revenues. <span class="foreign">Fellow well-met</span> &quot;boon companion&quot; is from 1580s, hence <span class="foreign">hail-fellow-well-met</span> as a figurative phrase for &quot;on intimate terms.&quot;
<br/><br/>
In compounds, with a sense of &quot;co-, joint-,&quot; from 16c., and by 19c. also denoting &quot;association with another.&quot; Hence <span class="foreign">fellow-traveler</span>, 1610s in a literal sense but in 20c. with a specific extended sense of &quot;one who sympathizes with the Communist movement but is not a party member&quot; (1936, translating Russian <span class="foreign">poputchik</span>).
<br/><br/>
