<p>Sappho&#x27;s particular association with erotic love between women (with or without concurrent relations with men) dates to at least 1732 in writing in English, though the continuous use of <span class="foreign">lesbian</span> and the modern words formed from it are from late 19c. The use of <span class="foreign">lesbian</span> as a noun and an adjective in this sense seems to follow the same pattern.  </p>
<blockquote>In another Place the same commentator conjectures, that <span class="foreign">Myra</span> is a Corruption of <span class="foreign">Myrrhina</span> а famous Courtesan of <span class="foreign">Athens</span>, who first practis&#x27;d and taught in that City <span class="foreign">Sappho&#x27;s</span> Manner and the <span class="foreign">Lesbian</span> Gambols. <p>Before this, the principal figurative use of <span class="foreign">Lesbian</span> was <span class="foreign">lesbian rule</span> (c. 1600 and especially common in 17c.) a mason&#x27;s rule of lead, of a type used in ancient times on Lesbos, which could be bent to fit the curves of a molding; hence, figuratively, &quot;pliant morality or judgment.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>And this is the nature of the equitable, a correction of law where it is defective owing to its universality. ... For when the thing is indefinite the rule also is indefinite, like the leaden rule used in making the Lesbian moulding; the rule adapts itself to the shape of the stone and is not rigid, and so too the decree is adapted to the facts. [Aristotle, &quot;Nicomachean Ethics&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>It also was used in English from 1775 in reference to wines from Lesbos.
