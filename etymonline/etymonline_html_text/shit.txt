and meaning of shit" href="/word/shit#etymonline_v_23415"><em>shit</em> (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">scitan</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*skit-</span> (source also of North Frisian <span class="foreign">skitj</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">schijten</span>, German <span class="foreign">scheissen</span>), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*skei-</span> &quot;to cut, split.&quot; The notion is of &quot;separation&quot; from the body (compare Latin <span class="foreign">excrementum</span>, from <span class="foreign">excernere</span> &quot;to separate,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">scearn</span> &quot;dung, muck,&quot; from <span class="foreign">scieran</span> &quot;to cut, shear;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">sharn</span>). It is thus a cousin to <span class="crossreference">science</span> and <span class="crossreference">conscience</span>.</p>
<p><a href="http://www.etymonline.com/baloney.php"><strong>&quot;Shit&quot; is not an acronym</strong></a>. The notion that it is a recent word might be partly because it was taboo from c. 1600 and rarely appeared in print (neither Shakespeare nor the KJV has it), and even in &quot;vulgar&quot; publications of the late 18c. it is disguised by dashes. It drew the wrath of censors as late as 1922 (&quot;Ulysses&quot; and &quot;The Enormous Room&quot;), scandalized magazine subscribers in 1957 (a Hemingway story in &quot;Atlantic Monthly&quot;) and was omitted from some dictionaries as recently as 1970 (&quot;Webster&#x27;s New World&quot;).</p>
<p>Extensive slang usage; meaning &quot;to lie, to tease&quot; is from 1934; that of &quot;to disrespect&quot; is from 1903. <span class="foreign">Shite</span>, now a jocular or slightly euphemistic and chiefly British variant of the noun, formerly a dialectal variant, reflects the vowel in the Old English verb (compare German <span class="foreign">scheissen</span>); the modern verb has been influenced by the noun. <span class="foreign">Shat</span> is a humorous past tense form, not etymological, first recorded 18c. To <span class="foreign">shit bricks</span> &quot;be very frightened&quot; attested by 1961. The connection between fear and involuntary defecation has generated expressions in English since 14c. (the image also is in Latin), and probably also is behind <span class="foreign">scared shitless</span> (1936).</p>

Sense of &quot;excrement&quot; dates from 1580s (Old English had <span class="foreign">scytel</span>, Middle English <span class="foreign">shitel</span> for &quot;dung, excrement;&quot; the usual 14c. noun seems to have been <span class="crossreference">turd</span>). Use for &quot;obnoxious person&quot; is at least since 1508; meaning &quot;misfortune, trouble&quot; is attested from 1937. <span class="foreign">Shit-faced</span> &quot;drunk&quot; is 1960s student slang; <span class="foreign">shit list</span> is from 1942. <span class="foreign">Up shit creek</span> &quot;in trouble&quot; is from 1937 (compare <span class="crossreference">salt river</span>). To <span class="foreign">not give a shit</span> &quot;not care&quot; is from 1922. Pessimistic expression <span class="foreign">Same shit different day</span> attested by 1989. <span class="foreign">Shitload</span> (also <span class="foreign">shit-load</span>) for &quot;a great many&quot; is by 1970. <span class="foreign">Shitticism</span> is Robert Frost&#x27;s word for scatological writing.</p>

Returning, he found everyone had gone except the bartender, who was cowering behind the bar.
