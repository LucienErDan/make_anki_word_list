The oldest use of the verbal phrase <span class="foreign">shake down</span> is &quot;cause to totter and fall&quot; (c. 1400).</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/shake-down#etymonline_v_24066"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of farce" href="/word/farce#etymonline_v_1125">farce (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;force-meat, stuffing;&quot; 1520s, in the dramatic sense &quot;ludicrous satire; low comedy,&quot; from Middle French <span class="foreign">farce</span> &quot;comic interlude in a mystery play&quot; (16c.), literally &quot;stuffing,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">farcir</span> &quot;to stuff,&quot; (13c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">farcire</span> &quot;to stuff, cram,&quot; which is of uncertain origin, perhaps from PIE <span class="foreign">*bhrekw-</span> &quot;to cram together,&quot; and thus related to <span class="foreign">frequens</span> &quot;crowded.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>... for a farce is that in poetry which grotesque is in a picture. The persons and action of a farce are all unnatural, and the manners false, that is, inconsisting with the characters of mankind. [Dryden, &quot;A Parallel of Poetry and Painting&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>According to OED and other sources, the pseudo-Latin <span class="foreign">farsia</span> was applied 13c. in France and England to praise phrases inserted into liturgical formulae (for example between <span class="foreign">kyrie</span> and <span class="foreign">eleison</span>) at the principal festivals, then in Old French <span class="foreign">farce</span> was extended to the impromptu buffoonery among actors that was a feature of religious stage plays. Generalized sense of &quot;a ridiculous sham&quot; is from 1690s in English.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/farce#etymonline_v_1125"><span>Relate
