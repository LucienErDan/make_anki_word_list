and meaning of ape" href="/word/ape#etymonline_v_15453"><em>ape</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">apa</span> (fem. <span class="foreign">ape</span>) &quot;an ape, a monkey,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*apan</span> (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">apo</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">api</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">aap</span>, German <span class="foreign">affe</span>), probably a borrowed word, perhaps from Celtic (compare Old Irish <span class="foreign">apa</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">epa</span>) or Slavic (compare Old Bohemian <span class="foreign">op</span>, Slovak <span class="foreign">opitza</span>), and the whole group probably is ultimately from an Eastern or non-Indo-European language.</p>

<p>The common word until the emergence of <span class="crossreference">monkey</span> in 16c. More technically, in zoology, &quot;a simian; tail-less, man-like monkey&quot; 1690s. The only native apes in Europe are the Barbary apes of Gibraltar, intelligent and docile, and these were the showman&#x27;s apes of the Middle Ages. Apes were noted in medieval times for mimicry of human action, hence, perhaps, the other figurative use of the word, to mean &quot;a fool&quot; (c. 1300).</p>

<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>go ape</strong></span> (in emphatic form, <span class="foreign">go apeshit</span>) &quot;go crazy&quot; is 1955, U.S. slang, said to be from the armed forces. To <span class="foreign"><strong>lead apes in hell</strong></span> (1570s) was the fancied fate of one who died an old maid. Middle English plural was occasionally <span class="foreign">apen</span>. <p>The Old French form of the name is <span class="foreign">Monequin</span> (recorded as <span class="foreign">Monnekin</span> in a 14c. version from Hainault), which could be a diminutive of some personal name, or it could be from the general Romanic word, which may be ultimately from Arabic <span class="foreign">maimun</span> &quot;monkey,&quot; literally &quot;auspicious,&quot; a euphemistic usage because the sight of apes was held by the Arabs to be unlucky [Klein]. The word would have been influenced in Italian by folk etymology from <span class="foreign">monna</span> &quot;woman,&quot; a contraction of <span class="foreign">ma donna</span> &quot;my lady.&quot;</p>
<p>In general, any one of the primates except man and lemurs; in more restricted use, &quot;an anthropoid ape or baboon;&quot; but popularly used especially of the long-tailed species often kept as pets. <span class="foreign">Monkey</span> has been used affectionately or in pretended disapproval of a child since c. 1600. As the name of a type of modern popular dance, it is attested from 1964.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Monkey suit</strong></span> is from 1876 as a type of child&#x27;s suit; by 1918 as slang for &quot;fancy dress clothes or uniform.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>make a monkey of</strong></span> &quot;make a fool of&quot; is attested from 1851.
