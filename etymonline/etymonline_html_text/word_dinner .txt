undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;first big meal of the day&quot; (eaten between 9 a.m. and noon), from Old French <span class="foreign">disner</span> &quot;breakfast&quot; (11c.), noun use of infinitive <span class="foreign">disner</span> (Modern French <span class="foreign">dîner</span>) &quot;take the first meal of the day,&quot; from stem of Gallo-Roman <span class="foreign">*desjunare</span> &quot;to break one&#x27;s fast,&quot; from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*disjejunare</span>, from <span class="foreign">dis-</span> &quot;undo, do the opposite of&quot; (see <span food&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">jejune</span>).</p>
<p>Always used in English for the main meal of the day, but the time of that has gradually shifted later.</p>
<blockquote>In medieval and modern Europe the common practice, down to the middle of the eighteenth century, was to take this meal about midday, or in more primitive times even as early as 9 or 10 A.M. In France, under the old régime, the dinner-hour was at 2 or 3 in the afternoon; but when the Constituent Assembly moved to Paris, since it sat until 4 or 5 o&#x27;clock, the hour for dining was postponed. The custom of dining at 6 o&#x27;clock or later has since become common, except in the country, where early dinner is still the general practice. [Century Dictionary, 1897]</blockquote>
<p>The change from midday to evening began with the fashionable classes. Compare <span class="crossreference">dinette</span>.</p>
