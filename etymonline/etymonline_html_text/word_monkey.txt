title="Origin and meaning of monkey" href="/word/monkey#etymonline_v_17446"><em>monkey</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1520s, also <span class="foreign">monkie, munkie, munkye</span>, etc., not found in Middle English (where <span class="crossreference">ape</span> was the usual word); of uncertain origin, but likely from an unrecorded Middle Low German <span class="foreign">*moneke</span> or Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">*monnekijn</span>, a colloquial word for &quot;monkey,&quot; originally a diminutive of some Romanic word, compare French <span class="foreign">monne</span> (16c.); Middle Italian <span class="foreign">monnicchio</span>, from Old Italian <span class="foreign">monna</span>; Spanish <span class="foreign">mona</span> &quot;ape, monkey.&quot; In a 1498 Low German version of the popular medieval beast story <span class="foreign">Roman de Renart</span> (&quot;Reynard the Fox&quot;), <span class="foreign">Moneke</span> is the name given to the son of Martin the Ape; transmission of the word to English might have been via itinerant entertainers from the German states.</p>
<p>The Old French form of the name is <span class="foreign">Monequin</span> (recorded as <span class="foreign">Monnekin</span> in a 14c. version from Hainault), which could be a diminutive of some personal name, or it could be from the general Romanic word, which may be ultimately from Arabic <span class="foreign">maimun</span> &quot;monkey,&quot; literally &quot;auspicious,&quot; a euphemistic usage because the sight of apes was held by the Arabs to be unlucky [Klein]. The word would have been influenced in Italian by folk etymology from <span class="foreign">monna</span> &quot;woman,&quot; a contraction of <span class="foreign">ma donna</span> &quot;my lady.&quot;</p>
<p>In general, any one of the primates except man and lemurs; in more restricted use, &quot;an anthropoid ape or baboon;&quot; but popularly used especially of the long-tailed species often kept as pets. <span class="foreign">Monkey</span> has been used affectionately or in pretended disapproval of a child since c. 1600. As the name of a type of modern popular dance, it is attested from 1964.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Monkey suit</strong></span> is from 1876 as a type of child&#x27;s suit; by 1918 as slang for &quot;fancy dress clothes or uniform.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>make a monkey of</strong></span> &quot;make a fool of&quot; is attested from 1851. To <span class="foreign"><strong>have a monkey on one&#x27;s back</strong></span> &quot;be addicted&quot; is 1930s narcotics slang, though the same phrase in the 1860s meant &quot;to be angry.&quot; There is a story in the Sinbad cycle about a tormenting ape-like creature that mounts a man&#x27;s shoulders and won&#x27;t get off, which may be the root of the term. In 1890s British slang, to <span class="foreign">have a monkey up the chimney</span> meant &quot;to have a mortgage on one&#x27;s house.&quot; The Japanese <span class="foreign">three wise monkeys</span> (&quot;see no evil,&quot; etc.) are attested in English by 1891.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/monkey#etymonline_v_17446"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of monkey" href="/word/monkey#etymonline_v_44984"><em>monkey</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1859, &quot;to mock, mimic&quot; (as a monkey does), from <span class="crossreference">monkey</span> (n.). <p>The common word until the emergence of <span class="crossreference">monkey</span> in 16c. More technically, in zoology, &quot;a simian; tail-less, man-like monkey&quot; 1690s. The only native apes in Europe are the Barbary apes of Gibraltar, intelligent and docile, and these were the showman&#x27;s apes of the Middle Ages. Apes were noted in medieval times for mimicry of human action, hence, perhaps, the other figurative use of the word, to mean &quot;a fool&quot; (c. 1300).</p>
