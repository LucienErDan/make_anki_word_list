<p>Sense of &quot;opposite of right&quot; is from the left being usually the weaker hand), a derived sense also found in cognate Middle Dutch and Low German <span class="foreign">luchter</span>, <span class="foreign">luft</span>. Compare Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kairys</span> &quot;left&quot; and Lettish <span class="foreign">kreilis</span> &quot;left hand&quot; both from a root that yields words for &quot;twisted, crooked.&quot;</p>
<p>The usual Old English <span class="foreign">winstre</span>/<span class="foreign">winestra</span> &quot;left&quot; (adj.); &quot;left hand,&quot; literally &quot;friendlier,&quot; a euphemism used superstitiously to avoid invoking the unlucky forces connected with the left side (compare <span class="crossreference">sinister</span>). The Kentish word itself might have been originally a taboo replacement, if instead it represents PIE <span class="foreign">*laiwo-</span> &quot;considered conspicuous&quot; (represented in Greek <span class="foreign">laios</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">laevus</span>, and Russian <span class="foreign">levyi</span>). <p>Meaning &quot;being on the left-hand side&quot; is from c. 1300. As an adverb from early 14c. For political senses, see <span class="crossreference">left</span> (n.). To have <span class="foreign"><strong>two left feet</strong></span> &quot;be clumsy&quot; is attested by 1902.</p>
<p>Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>out in left field</strong></span> &quot;out of touch with pertinent realities&quot; is attested from 1944, from the baseball fielding position that tends to be far removed from the play (<span class="foreign">left field</span> in baseball attested by 1867).
