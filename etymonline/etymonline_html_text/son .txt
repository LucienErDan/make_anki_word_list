<p>&quot;Probably the most common American vulgarity from about the middle of the eighteenth century to the middle of the twentieth&quot; [Rawson].</p>
<blockquote>Our maid-of-all-work in that department [indecency] is <span class="foreign">son-of-a-bitch</span>, which seems as pale and ineffectual to a Slav or a Latin as <span class="foreign">fudge</span> does to us. There is simply no lift in it, no shock, no sis-boom-ah. The dumbest policeman in Palermo thinks of a dozen better ones between breakfast and the noon whistle. [H.L. Mencken, &quot;The American Language,&quot; 4th ed., 1936, p.317-8]</blockquote>
<p>Elsewhere, complaining of the tepidity of the American vocabulary of profanity, Mencken writes that the toned-down form <span class="foreign">son-of-a-gun</span> &quot;is so lacking in punch that the Italians among us have borrowed it as a satirical name for an American: <span class="foreign">la sanemagogna</span> is what they call him, and by it they indicate their contempt for his backwardness in the art that is one of their great glories.&quot;</p>

Also compare <span class="foreign">gonnilde gnoste</span> &quot;spark or flame used to fire a cannon&quot; (early 14c.). The woman&#x27;s name is from Old Norse <span class="foreign">Gunnhildr</span>, a compound of <span class="foreign">gunnr</span> and <span class="foreign">hildr</span>, both meaning &quot;war, battle.&quot; First element from PIE <span class="foreign">*gwhen-</span> &quot;to strike, kill&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">bane</span>); for second, see <span class="crossreference">Hilda</span>.</p>

<p>The identification of women with powerful weapons is common historically (such as <span class="foreign">Big Bertha</span>, <span class="foreign">Brown Bess</span>, <span class="foreign">Mons Meg</span>, etc.). Or perhaps directly from Old Norse <span class="foreign">gunnr</span> &quot;battle.&quot; The word was perhaps influenced by or confirmed by (or possibly from) Old French <span class="foreign">engon</span>, dialectal variant of <span class="foreign">engin</span> &quot;engine.&quot;</p>

<p>Meaning grew with technology, from cannons to firearms as they developed 15c.; popularly applied to pistols and revolvers from 1744. In modern military use the word is restricted to cannons (which must be mounted), especially long ones used for high velocity and long trajectory. Hence <span class="foreign"><strong>great guns</strong></span> (1884 as an exclamation) distinguished from <span class="foreign">small guns</span> (such as muskets) from c. 1400. Meaning &quot;thief, rascal&quot; is from 1858. For <span class="foreign"><strong>son of a gun</strong></span>, see <span class="crossreference">son</span>. To <span class="foreign"><strong>jump the gun</strong></span> (1912, American English) is a figurative use from track and field. <span class="foreign"><strong>Guns</strong></span> &quot;a woman&#x27;s breasts&quot; (especially if prominent) attested by 2006.</p>
