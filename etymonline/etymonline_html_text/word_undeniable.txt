(in Hendyng&#x27;s &quot;Proverbs&quot; -- <span class="foreign">ʒeve þi cunte to cunni[n]g, And crave affetir wedding</span>), akin to Old Norse <span class="foreign">kunta</span>, Old Frisian, Middle Dutch, and Middle Low German <span class="foreign">kunte</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kunton</span>, which is of uncertain origin. Some suggest a link with Latin <span class="foreign">cuneus</span> &quot;wedge&quot; (which is of unknown origin), others to PIE root <span class="foreign">*geu-</span> &quot;hollow place,&quot; still others to PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwen-</span> &quot;woman.&quot;</p>
<p>The form is similar to Latin <span class="foreign">cunnus</span> &quot;female pudenda&quot; (also, vulgarly, &quot;a woman&quot;), which is likewise of disputed origin, perhaps literally &quot;gash, slit&quot; (from PIE <span class="foreign">*sker-</span> &quot;to cut&quot;) or &quot;sheath&quot; (Watkins, from PIE <span class="foreign">*(s)keu-</span> &quot;to conceal, hide&quot;). De Vaan rejects this, however, and traces it to &quot;a root <span class="foreign">*kut- </span>meaning &#x27;bag&#x27;, &#x27;scrotum&#x27;, and metaphorically also &#x27;female pudenda,&#x27; &quot; source also of Greek <span class="foreign">kysthos</span> &quot;vagina; buttocks; pouch, small bag&quot; (but Beekes suspects this is a Pre-Greek word), Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kutys</span> &quot;(money) bag,&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">hodo</span> &quot;testicles.&quot;</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Hec vulva: a cunt. Hic cunnus: idem est.</span> [from Londesborough Illustrated Nominale, c. 1500, in &quot;Anglo-Saxon and Old English Vocabularies,&quot; eds. Wright and Wülcker, vol. 1, 1884]</blockquote>
<p>First known reference in English apparently is in a compound, Oxford street name <span class="foreign">Gropecuntlane</span> cited from c. 1230 (and attested through late 14c.) in &quot;Place-Names of Oxfordshire&quot; (Gelling &amp; Stenton, 1953), presumably a haunt of prostitutes. Used in medical writing c. 1400, but avoided in public speech since 15c.; considered obscene since 17c.</p>
<p>in Middle English also <span class="foreign">conte</span>, <span class="foreign">counte</span>, and sometimes <span class="foreign">queinte</span>, <span class="foreign">queynte</span> (for this, see <span class="crossreference">Q</span>). Chaucer used <span class="foreign">quaint</span> and <span class="foreign">queynte</span> in &quot;Canterbury Tales&quot; (late 14c.), and Andrew Marvell might be punning on <span class="crossreference">quaint</span> in &quot;To His Coy Mistress&quot; (1650).</p>
<blockquote>&quot;What eyleth yow to grucche thus and grone? Is it for ye wolde haue my queynte allone?&quot; [Wife of Bath&#x27;s Tale]</blockquote>

[A sampling: <span class="foreign">Botany Bay, chum, coffee-shop, cookie, End of the Sentimental Journey, fancy bit, Fumbler&#x27;s Hall, funniment, goatmilker, heaven, hell, Itching Jenny, jelly-bag, Low Countries, nature&#x27;s tufted treasure, penwiper, prick-skinner, seminary, tickle-toby, undeniable, wonderful lamp</span>, and <span class="foreign">aphrodisaical tennis court</span>, and, in a separate listing, <span class="foreign">Naggie</span>. Dutch cognate <span class="foreign">de kont</span> means &quot;a bottom, an arse,&quot; but Dutch also has attractive poetic slang ways of expressing this part, such as <span class="foreign">liefdesgrot</span>, literally &quot;cave of love,&quot; and <span class="foreign">vleesroos</span> &quot;rose of flesh.&quot;</p>
