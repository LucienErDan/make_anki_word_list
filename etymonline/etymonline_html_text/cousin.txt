title="Origin and meaning of cousin" href="/word/cousin#etymonline_v_19187"><em>cousin</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>early 13c., &quot;a collateral blood relative more remote than a brother or sister&quot; (mid-12c. as a surname), from Old French <span class="foreign">cosin</span> &quot;nephew; kinsman; cousin&quot; (12c., Modern French <span class="foreign">cousin</span>), from Latin <span class="foreign">consobrinus</span> &quot;cousin,&quot; originally &quot;mother&#x27;s sister&#x27;s son,&quot; from assimilated form of <span class="foreign">com</span> &quot;with, together&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">com-</span>) + <span class="foreign">sobrinus</span> (earlier <span class="foreign">*sosrinos</span>) &quot;cousin on mother&#x27;s side,&quot; from <span class="foreign">soror</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">sororis</span>) &quot;sister&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">sister</span>).</p>
<p>Specific modern usage, &quot;the son or daughter of an uncle or aunt,&quot; is attested by c. 1300, but throughout Middle English the word also was used of grandchildren, godchildren, etc. Extended sense of &quot;closely related thing&quot; is from late 14c.</p>
<p>Italian <span class="foreign">cugino</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">kusine</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">kuzyn</span> also are from French. German <span class="foreign">vetter</span> is from Old High German <span class="foreign">fetiro</span> &quot;uncle,&quot; perhaps on the notion of &quot;child of uncle.&quot; Words for <span class="foreign">cousin</span> tend to drift to &quot;nephew&quot; on the notion of &quot;father&#x27;s nephew.&quot;</p>
<p>Many IE languages (including Irish, Sanskrit, Slavic, and some of the Germanic tongues) have or had separate words for some or all of the eight possible &quot;cousin&quot; relationships, such as Latin, which along with <span class="foreign">consobrinus</span> had <span class="foreign">consobrina</span> &quot;mother&#x27;s sister&#x27;s daughter,&quot; <span class="foreign">patruelis</span> &quot;father&#x27;s brother&#x27;s son,&quot; <span class="foreign">atruelis</span> &quot;mother&#x27;s brother&#x27;s son,&quot; <span class="foreign">amitinus</span> &quot;father&#x27;s sister&#x27;s son,&quot; etc. Old English distinguished <span class="foreign">fæderan sunu</span> &quot;father&#x27;s brother&#x27;s son,&quot; <span class="foreign">modrigan sunu</span> &quot;mother&#x27;s sister&#x27;s son,&quot; etc.</p>
<p>Used familiarly as a term of address since early 15c., especially in Cornwall.
