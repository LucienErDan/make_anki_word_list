It also arrived (without the final <span class="foreign">-e</span>) from Old French <span class="foreign">defens</span>, from Latin <span class="foreign">defensum</span> &quot;thing protected or forbidden,&quot; neuter past participle of <span class="foreign">defendere</span>.</p>

<p>Middle English <span class="foreign">defens</span> was assimilated into <span class="foreign">defense</span>, but not before it inspired the alternative spelling <span class="foreign">defence</span>, via the same tendency that produced <span class="crossreference">hence</span> (<span class="foreign">hennis</span>), <span class="crossreference">pence</span> (<span class="foreign">penies</span>), <span class="crossreference">dunce</span> (<span class="foreign">Duns</span>). Webster made the <span class="foreign">-se</span> form standard in U.S., but British has preferred <span class="foreign">defence</span>, and compare <span class="crossreference">fence</span> (n.).</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;a speech or writing intended to repel or disprove a charge or accusation&quot; is from late 14c., as is the sense of &quot;method adopted by one against whom a lawsuit has been brought.&quot; Meaning &quot;science of defense against attack&quot; (in fencing, boxing, etc.) is from c. 1600. Used by 1935 as a euphemism for &quot;national military resources,&quot; but the notion (non-euphemistic) was in Middle English: <span class="foreign">man of defense</span> &quot;warrior,&quot; <span class="foreign">ship of defense</span> &quot;warship.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>Defenses</strong></span> &quot;natural weapons of an animal&quot; is by 1889. <span class="foreign"><strong>Defense mechanism</strong></span> in psychology is from 1913.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/defense#etymonline_v_905"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of apology" href="/word/apology#etymonline_v_15478">apology (n.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">early 15c., &quot;defense, justification,&quot; from Late Latin <span class="foreign">apologia</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">apologia</span> &quot;a speech in defense,&quot; from <span class="foreign">apologeisthai</span> &quot;to speak in one&#x27;s defense,&quot; from <span class="foreign">apologos</span> &quot;an account, story,&quot; from <span class="foreign">apo</span> &quot;away from, off&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">apo-</span>) + <span class="foreign">logos</span> &quot;speech&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">Logos</span>).

In classical Greek, &quot;a well-reasoned reply; a &#x27;thought-out response&#x27; to the accusations made,&quot; as that of Socrates. The original English sense of &quot;self-justification&quot; yielded a meaning &quot;frank expression of regret for wrong done,&quot; first recorded 1590s, but this was not the main sense until 18c. Johnson&#x27;s dictionary defines it as &quot;Defence; excuse,&quot; and adds, &quot;<span class="foreign">Apology</span> generally signifies rather excuse than vindication, and tends rather to extenuate the fault, than prove innocence,&quot; which might indicate the path of the sense shift. The old sense has tended to shift to the Latin form <span class="crossreference">apologia</span> (1784), known from early Christian writings in defense of the faith.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/apology#etymonline_v_15478"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of henchman" href="/word/henchman#etymonline_v_9156">henchman (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-14c., <span class="foreign">hengestman</span>, later <span class="foreign">henshman</span> (mid-15c.) &quot;high-ranking servant (usually of gentle birth), attendant upon a king, nobleman, etc.,&quot; originally &quot;groom,&quot; probably from <span class="crossreference">man</span> (n.) + Old English <span class="foreign">hengest</span> &quot;horse, stallion, gelding,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hangistas</span> (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hengst</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">hengest</span>, German <span class="foreign">Hengst</span> &quot;stallion&quot;), perhaps literally &quot;best at springing,&quot; from PIE <span class="foreign">*kenku-</span> (source also of Greek <span class="foreign">kekiein</span> &quot;to gush forth;&quot; Lithuanian <span class="foreign">šokti</span> &quot;to jump, dance;&quot; Breton <span class="foreign">kazek</span> &quot;a mare,&quot; literally &quot;that which belongs to a stallion&quot;).</p>
<p>Perhaps modeled on Old Norse compound <span class="foreign">hesta-maðr</span> &quot;horse-boy, groom.&quot; The word became obsolete in England 17c., but it was retained in Scottish as &quot;personal attendant of a Highland chief,&quot; in which sense Scott revived it in literary English from 1810. Sense of &quot;obedient or unscrupulous follower&quot; is first recorded 1839, probably somehow a misunderstanding of the word as used by Scott.</p>

<blockquote>&quot;A colonist&quot; says he, &quot;who lives two hundred leagues up the country, arrives at the Cape, to complain that the Caffrees have taken all his cattle; and intreats a commando, which is a permission to go, with the help of his neighbours, to retake his property; the governor, who either does not, or feigns not to understand the trick, adheres strictly to the facts expressed in the petition: a preamble of regular information would occasion long delays; a permission is easily given--tis but a word--the fatal word is written, which proves a sentence of death to a thousand poor savages, who have no such defence or resources as their persecutors.&quot; [George Carter, &quot;A Narrative of the Loss of the Grosvenor,&quot; 1791]</blockquote>
