title="Origin and meaning of tongue" href="/word/tongue#etymonline_v_15361"><em>tongue</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">tunge</span> &quot;tongue, organ of speech; speech, a people&#x27;s language,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*tungō</span> (source also of Old Saxon and Old Norse <span class="foreign">tunga</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">tunge</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">tonghe</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">tong</span>, Old High German <span <p>For substitution of <span class="foreign">-o-</span> for <span class="foreign">-u-</span>, see <span class="crossreference">come</span>. The spelling of the ending of the word apparently is a 14c. attempt to indicate proper pronunciation, but the result is &quot;neither etymological nor phonetic, and is only in a very small degree historical&quot; [OED]. In the &quot;knowledge of a foreign language&quot; sense in the Pentecostal miracle, from 1520s. <span class="foreign">Tongue-tied</span> is first recorded 1520s. To <span class="foreign">hold (one&#x27;s) tongue</span> &quot;refrain from speaking&quot; was in Old English. Johnson has <span class="foreign">tonguepad</span> &quot;A great talker.&quot;</p>
