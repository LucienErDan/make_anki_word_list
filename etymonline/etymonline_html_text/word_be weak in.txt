etc.</p>
<p>The modern verb <span class="foreign">to be</span> in its entirety represents the merger of two once-distinct verbs, the &quot;b-root&quot; represented by <span class="foreign">be</span> and the <span class="foreign">am/was</span> verb, which was itself a conglomerate. Roger Lass (&quot;Old English&quot;) describes the verb as &quot;a collection of semantically related paradigm fragments,&quot; while Weekley calls it &quot;an accidental conglomeration from the different Old English dial[ect]s.&quot; It is the most irregular verb in Modern English and the most common. Collective in all Germanic languages, it has eight different forms in Modern English:</p>
<p><strong>BE</strong> (infinitive, subjunctive, imperative); <strong>AM</strong> (present 1st person singular); <strong>ARE</strong> (present 2nd person singular and all plural); <strong>IS</strong> (present 3rd person singular); <strong>WAS</strong> (past 1st and 3rd persons singular); <strong>WERE</strong> (past 2nd person singular, all plural; subjunctive); <strong>BEING</strong> (progressive &amp; present participle; gerund); <strong>BEEN</strong> (perfect participle).</p>
<p>The paradigm in Old English was: <span class="foreign">eom, beo</span> (present 1st person singular); <span class="foreign">eart, bist</span> (present 2nd person singular); <span class="foreign">is, bið</span> (present 3rd person singular);  <span class="foreign">sind, sindon, beoð</span> (present plural in all persons); <span class="foreign">wæs</span> (past 1st and 3rd person singular); <span class="foreign">wære</span> (past 2nd person singular); <span class="foreign">wæron</span> (past plural in all persons); <span class="foreign">wære</span> (singular subjunctive preterit); <span class="foreign">wæren</span> (plural subjunctive preterit).</p>
<p>The &quot;b-root&quot; had no past tense in Old English, but often served as future tense of <span class="foreign">am/was</span>. In 13c. it took the place of the infinitive, participle and imperative forms of <span class="foreign">am/was</span>. Later its plural forms (<span class="foreign">we beth, ye ben, they be</span>) became standard in Middle English and it made inroads into the singular (<span class="foreign">I be, thou beest, he beth</span>), but forms of <span class="foreign">are</span> claimed this turf in the 1500s and replaced <span class="foreign">be</span> in the plural. For the origin and evolution of the <span class="foreign">am/was</span> branches of this tangle, see <span class="crossreference">am</span> and <span class="crossreference">was</span>.</p>

<br/><br/>
The Old English prefix also was used to make transitive verbs and as a privative prefix (as in <span class="foreign">behead</span>). The sense &quot;on all sides, all about&quot; naturally grew to include intensive uses (as in <span class="foreign">bespatter</span> &quot;spatter about,&quot; therefore &quot;spatter very much,&quot; <span class="foreign">besprinkle</span>, etc.). <span class="foreign">Be-</span> also can be causative, or have just about any sense required. The prefix was productive 16c.-17c. in forming useful words, many of which have not survived, such as <span class="foreign">bethwack</span> &quot;to thrash soundly&quot; (1550s) and <span class="foreign">betongue</span> &quot;to assail in speech, to scold&quot; (1630s).</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/be-#etymonline_v_8193"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of in" href="/word/in#etymonline_v_6283"><em>in</em> (adv., prep.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>a Middle English merger of Old English <span class="foreign">in</span> (prep.) &quot;in, into, upon, on, at, among; about, during;&quot; and Old English <span class="foreign">inne</span> (adv.) &quot;within, inside,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*in</span> (source also of Old Frisian, Dutch, German, Gothic <span class="foreign">in</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">i</span>), from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*en</span> &quot;in.&quot; The simpler form took on both senses in Middle English.</p>

<p>Sense distinction between <span class="foreign">in</span> and <span class="crossreference">on</span> is from later Middle English, and nuances in use of <span class="foreign">in</span> and <span class="foreign">at</span> still distinguish British and American English (<span class="foreign">in school</span>/<span class="foreign">at school</span>). Sometimes in Middle English shortened to <span class="foreign">i</span>.</p>

<p>The noun sense of &quot;influence, access (to power or authorities),&quot; as in <span class="foreign">have an in with</span>, is first recorded 1929 in American English. to be <span class="foreign">in for it</span> &quot;certain to meet with something unpleasant&quot; is from 1690s. <br/><br/>
In Old French and Middle English often <span class="foreign">en-</span>, but most of these forms have not survived in Modern English, and the few that do (<span class="crossreference">enemy</span>, for instance) no longer are felt as negative. The rule of thumb in English has been to use <span class="foreign">in-</span> with obviously Latin elements, <span class="foreign">un-</span> with native or nativized ones.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/in-#etymonline_v_6284"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of in-" href="/word/in-#etymonline_v_6285"><em>in</em>- (2)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>element meaning &quot;into, in, on, upon&quot; (also <span class="foreign">im-</span>, <span class="foreign">il-</span>, <span class="foreign">ir-</span> by assimilation of <span class="foreign">-n-</span> with following consonant), from Latin <span class="foreign">in-</span> &quot;in,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*en</span> &quot;in.&quot;</p>

<p>In Old French (and hence in Middle English) this often became <span class="foreign">en-</span>, which in English had a strong tendency to revert to Latin <span class="foreign">in-</span>, but not always, which accounts for pairs such as <span class="crossreference">enquire</span>/<span class="crossreference">inquire</span>. There was a native form, which in West Saxon usually appeared as <span class="foreign">on-</span> (as in Old English <span class="foreign">onliehtan</span> &quot;to enlighten&quot;), and some of those verbs survived into Middle English (such as <span class="foreign">inwrite</span> &quot;to inscribe&quot;), but all now seem to be extinct.</p>
<p>Not related to <span class="crossreference">in-</span> (1) &quot;not,&quot; which also was a common prefix in Latin, causing confusion: to the Romans <span class="foreign">impressus</span> could mean &quot;pressed&quot; or &quot;unpressed;&quot; <span class="foreign">inaudire</span> meant &quot;to hear,&quot; but <span class="foreign">inauditus</span> meant &quot;unheard of;&quot; in Late Latin <span class="foreign">investigabilis</span> could mean &quot;that may be searched into&quot; or &quot;that cannot be searched into.&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">invocatus</span> was &quot;uncalled, uninvited,&quot; but <span class="foreign">invocare</span> was &quot;to call, appeal to.&quot;</p>

<p>The trouble has continued in English; the hesitation over what is meant by <span class="foreign">inflammable</span> being a commonly cited example. <span class="foreign">Implume</span> (1610s) meant &quot;to feather,&quot; but <span class="foreign">implumed</span> (c. 1600) meant &quot;unfeathered.&quot; <span class="foreign">Impliable</span> can mean &quot;capable of being implied&quot; (1865) or &quot;inflexible&quot; (1734). <span class="foreign">Impartible</span> in 17c. could mean &quot;incapable of being divided&quot; or &quot;capable of being imparted.&quot; <span class="foreign">Impassionate</span> can be &quot;free from passion&quot; or it can mean &quot;strongly stirred by passion.&quot; <span class="foreign">Inanimate</span> (adj.) is &quot;lifeless,&quot; but Donne uses <span class="foreign">inanimate</span> (v.) to mean &quot;infuse with life or vigor.&quot; <span class="foreign">Irruption</span> is &quot;a breaking in,&quot; but <span class="foreign">irruptible</span> is &quot;unbreakable.&quot;</p>

<p>In addition to <span class="foreign">improve</span> &quot;use to one&#x27;s profit,&quot; Middle English also had a verb <span class="foreign">improve</span> meaning &quot;to disprove&quot; (15c.). To <span class="foreign">inculpate</span> is &quot;to accuse,&quot; but <span class="foreign">inculpable</span> means &quot;not culpable, free from blame.&quot; <span class="foreign">Infestive</span> has meant &quot;troublesome, annoying&quot; (1560s, from <span class="foreign">infest</span>) and &quot;not festive&quot; (1620s). In Middle English <span class="foreign">inflexible</span> could mean &quot;incapable of being bent&quot; or &quot;capable of being swayed or moved.&quot; In 17c., <span class="foreign">informed</span> could mean &quot;current in information,&quot; formed, animated,&quot; or &quot;unformed, formless&quot; (&quot;This was an awkward use&quot; [OED]). <span class="foreign">Inhabited</span> has meant &quot;dwelt in&quot; (1560s) and &quot;uninhabited&quot; (1610s); <span class="foreign">inhabitable</span> likewise has been used on opposite senses, a confusion that goes back to Late Latin.</p>
