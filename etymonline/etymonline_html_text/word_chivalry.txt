title="Origin and meaning of chivalry" href="/word/chivalry#etymonline_v_11292"><em>chivalry</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1300, &quot;body or host of knights; In some French dialects, including that of Paris (but not that of Picardy), Latin <span class="foreign">ca-</span> became French &quot;tsha.&quot; This was introduced to English after the Norman Conquest, in words borrowed from Old French such as <span class="crossreference">chaste</span>, <span class="crossreference">charity</span>, <span class="crossreference">chief</span> (adj.). Under French influence, <span class="foreign">-ch-</span> also was inserted into Anglo-Saxon words that had the same sound (such as <span class="crossreference">bleach</span>, <span class="crossreference">chest</span>, <span class="crossreference">church</span>) which in Old English still was written with a simple <span class="foreign">-c-</span>, and into those that had formerly been spelled with a <span class="foreign">-c-</span> and pronounced &quot;k&quot; such as <span class="crossreference">chin</span> and <span class="crossreference">much</span>.</p>

<p>As French evolved, the &quot;t&quot; sound dropped out of <span class="foreign">-ch-</span>, so in later loan-words from French -<span class="foreign">ch-</span> has only the sound &quot;sh-&quot; (<span class="crossreference">chauffeur</span>, <span class="crossreference">machine</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">chivalry</span>, etc.).</p>

<p>It turns up as well in words from classical languages (<span class="crossreference">chaos</span>, <span class="crossreference">echo</span>, etc.). Most uses of <span class="foreign">-ch-</span> in Roman Latin were in words from Greek, which in Greek would be pronounced correctly as /k/ + /h/, as in modern <span class="foreign">blockhead</span>, but most Romans would have said merely /k/, and this was the regular pronunciation in English. Before c. 1500 such words were regularly spelled with a <span class="foreign">-c-</span> (<span class="foreign">Crist, cronicle, scoole</span>), but Modern English has preserved or restored the etymological spelling in most of them (<span class="foreign">chemical</span>, <span class="foreign">chorus</span>, <span class="foreign">monarch</span>). </p>

<p>Sometimes <span class="foreign">ch-</span> is written to keep <span class="foreign">-c-</span> hard before a front vowel, as still in modern Italian. In some languages (Welsh, Spanish, Czech) <span class="foreign">ch-</span> can be treated as a separate letter and words in it are alphabetized after <span class="foreign">-c-</span> (or, in Czech and Slovak, after <span class="foreign">-h-</span>). The sound also is heard in words from more distant languages (as in <span class="crossreference">cheetah</span>, <span class="crossreference">chintz</span>), and the digraph also is used to represent the sound in Scottish <span class="crossreference">loch</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/ch#etymonline_v_8383"><span>Relate entries &amp; chivalry, chivalrous conduct; elegance of manners, politeness,&quot; also &quot;a courteous act, act of civility or respect,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">curteisie, cortoisie</span> &quot;courtliness, noble sentiments; <p>From c. 1300 as &quot;good will, kindness,&quot; also &quot;a reward, a gift;&quot; mid-14c. as &quot;refinement, gentlemanly conduct.&quot; A specialized sense of <span class="foreign">curteisie</span> is the source of English <span class="crossreference">curtsy</span>. <p>No one seems to consider that it might be a corruption and misdivision of <span class="foreign">to then anes</span>, literally &quot;for the one (purpose or occasion),&quot; a similar construction to the one that yielded <span class="crossreference">nonce</span> (q.v.).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/nines#etymonline_v_6959"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of cordon" href="/word/cordon#etymonline_v_19071">cordon (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-15c., &quot;cord, lace, or ribbon of fine material worn as an ornament or token of victory,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">cordon</span> &quot;ribbon, cord,&quot; diminutive of <span class="foreign">corde</span> &quot;cord&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cord</span>). <p>The original sense is preserved in <span class="foreign">cordon bleu</span> (1727) &quot;the highest distinction,&quot; literally &quot;blue ribbon,&quot; for the sky-blue ribbon worn by the old French order of Knights-grand-cross of the Holy Ghost (the highest order of chivalry under the Bourbons). <p><span class="foreign">Cordon sanitaire</span> (1857) is French, a line of troops or military posts set around an infected district to keep the disease from spreading.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/cordon#etymonline_v_19071"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of California" href="/word/California#etymonline_v_25807">California </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>name of an imaginary realm in <span class="foreign">&quot;Las sergas de Esplandián&quot;</span> (&quot;Exploits of Espladán&quot;), a romance by Spanish writer Garci Ordóñez de Montalvo, published in 1510. It was a sequel to his <span class="foreign">&quot;Amadis de Gaula,&quot;</span> and was said to have been influential among Spanish explorers of the New World. It could have led them to misidentify Baja California as this mythical land and to mistake it for an island. The <span class="foreign">Amadis</span> tales are the Iberian equivalent of the Arthurian romances; they are older than 1510 (traces of them have been found mid-14c.) and were wildly popular. That conquistadors and sailors would have known the story in all its imaginative detail is hardly surprising.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span> ... set a fashion: all later Spanish writers of books of chivalry adopted the machinery of <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span>. Later knights were not less brave (they could not be braver than) Amadis; heroines were not less lovely (they could not be lovelier) than Oriana; there was nothing for it but to make the dragons more appalling, the giants larger, the wizards craftier, the magic castles more inaccessible, the enchanted lakes deeper. Subsequent books of chivalry are simple variants of the types in <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span>: Cervantes made his barber describe it as &#x27;the best of all books of this kind.&#x27; This verdict is essentially just. <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span> was read everywhere, especially in the French version of Herberay des Essarts. It was done into Hebrew during the sixteenth century, and attracted readers as different as St Ignatius of Loyola and Henry of Navarre. Its vogue perhaps somewhat exceeded its merit, but its merits are not inconsiderable. [James Fitzmaurice-Kelly, &quot;Spanish Literature,&quot; 1922 edition]</blockquote>
<p>Where Montalvo got the name and what it means, if anything, is a mystery. In reference to the native inhabitants, <span class="foreign">Californian</span> is attested from 1785 as an adjective, 1789 as a noun.
