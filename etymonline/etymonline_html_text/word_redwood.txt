Of various types of New World trees that yield such wood, from 1716; specifically of the California <span class="foreign">Sequoia sempervirens</span> from 1819. But as the product came to Europe via India perhaps this is a folk-etymology of some word in Arabic or another Asian language (an Old Italian form, <span class="foreign">verzino</span>, suggests to some a possible connection with Arabic <span class="foreign">wars</span> &quot;saffron&quot;).
<br/><br/>
The same word for the same stuff entered Portuguese and Spanish (<span class="foreign">brasil</span>) and Italian (<span class="foreign">brasile</span>). The South American country was named <span class="foreign">Santa Cruz</span> by its &quot;discoverer,&quot; Pedro Alvarez Cabral (1500), but within a decade on maps it began to be called <span class="foreign">terra de brasil</span> &quot;red-dyewood land&quot; because it produced a valuable red dyewood similar to East Indian type, and that name predominated from 1550s.
<br/><br/>
Complicating matters is <span class="foreign">Hy Brasil</span>, a name attested since early 14c. for a legendary island or rock in the North Atlantic off the west coast of Ireland. It is so-called perhaps from the &quot;red dyewood&quot; word by association with Pliny&#x27;s <span class="foreign">Insulae Purpurariae</span> (&quot;Purple Islands&quot;) in the Atlantic off the coast of Morocco.</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/Brazil#etymonline_v_17075"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of sequoia" href="/word/sequoia#etymonline_v_23250">sequoia (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">large American coniferous tree, 1857, from Modern Latin tree genus name given 1847 by Austrian botanist Stephan Endlicher (1804-1849), originally to a different tree, the coast redwood, apparently in honor of <span class="foreign">Sequoya</span> (a.k.a. George Guess, 1760-1843), Cherokee man who invented a system of writing for his people&#x27;s language, whose name is from Cherokee (Iroquoian) <span class="foreign">Sikwayi</span>, a word of unknown etymology.
<br/><br/>
Endlicher was a specialist in conifers, and he also was a philologist. But he never gave an etymology of this name and a search of his papers discovered no mention of Sequoya or the Cherokee writing system, and the connection is an assumption that some botanists have challenged, though no better candidate for a source has yet been found.
<br/><br/>
The giant sequoia was unseen by Europeans until 1833 and unknown to scientists until 1852. In May 1855, a pair of American botanists named it <span class="foreign">Taxodium giganteum</span>, but that name was deemed inappropriate for several scientific reasons. Meanwhile, English botanist John Lindley, who had never been to California, in 1853 named it <span class="foreign">Wellingtonia</span> in honor of the Duke of Wellington. &quot;As high as Wellington towers above his contemporaries, as high towers this California tree above the forest surrounding it. Therefore, it shall bear for all time to come the name Wellingtonia gigantea.&quot; This sat poorly with the Americans, and much ink was spilled until a French botanist provided the solution by transferring Endlicher&#x27;s name.
