<p>Apparently the sense of the first element shifted to intensive in Medieval Latin. From late 15c. as &quot;to affect the interest of, be of importance to;&quot; hence the meaning &quot;to worry, disturb, make uneasy or anxious&quot; (17c.). Reflexive use &quot;busy, occupy, engage&quot; (&quot;concern oneself&quot;) is from 1630s. Related: <span class="crossreference">Concerned</span>; <span class="foreign">concerning</span>.</p>
<p>Used imperatively from 1803 (compare similar use of <span class="crossreference">confound</span>); often rendered in dialect as <span class="foreign">consarn</span> (1832), probably a euphemism for <span class="foreign">damn</span> (compare <span class="foreign">concerned</span>). <blockquote>The management of the sails, etc., the holding of the assigned course by proper steering, and the working of the ship generally pertain rather to seamanship, though necessary to successful navigation. The two fundamental problems of navigation are the determination of the ship&#x27;s position at a given moment, and the decision of the most advantageous course to be steered in order to reach a given point. <p>From c. 1300 in the transitive sense &quot;bring into physical contact,&quot; also &quot;pertain to.&quot; Other senses attested from 14c. are &quot;perceive by physical contact, examine by sense of touch,&quot; also &quot;be or come into physical contact with; come to rest on; border on, be contiguous with;&quot; also &quot;use the sense of touch,&quot; and &quot;mention, describe.&quot; From early 14c. as &quot;affect or move mentally or emotionally,&quot; with notion of to &quot;touch&quot; the heart or mind. Also from early 14c. as &quot;have sexual contact with.&quot; Meaning &quot;to get or borrow money&quot; first recorded 1760.</p>
