meaning of chorus" href="/word/chorus#etymonline_v_11327"><em>chorus</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1560s, in drama, &quot;person who speaks the prologue and explains or comments on events on stage,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">chorus</span> &quot;a dance in a circle, the persons singing and dancing, the chorus of a tragedy,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">khoros</span> &quot;round dance; dancing-place; band of dancers; company of persons in a play, under a leader, who take part in dialogue with the actors and sing their sentiments at intervals.&quot;</p>
<p>The Greek word is of uncertain origin, because the original sense is unknown. Perhaps it is from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gher-</span> (1) &quot;to grasp, enclose,&quot; if the original sense of the Greek word is &quot;enclosed dancing floor,&quot; or <span class="crossreference">*gher-</span> (2) &quot;to like, want,&quot; if the original notion is &quot;to rejoice.&quot;</p>
<p>Extension from dance to voice is because Attic drama arose from tales inserted in the intervals of the dance. In Attic tragedy, the <span class="foreign">khoros</span> (of 12 or 15 (tragic) or 24 (comedic) persons) gave expression, between the acts, to the moral and religious sentiments evoked by the actions of the play. English 16c. theater adopted a stripped-down version of this.</p>
<blockquote>When a Poet wished to bring out a piece, he asked a Chorus from the Archon, and the expenses, being great, were defrayed by some rich citizen (the <span class="foreign">khoregos</span>): it was furnished by the Tribe and trained originally by the Poet himself [Liddell &amp; Scott]</blockquote>
<p>The meaning &quot;an organized company of singers&quot; is from 1650s. Meaning &quot;the refrain of a song&quot; (which the audience joins in singing) is from 1590s; that of &quot;a song to be sung by a (large) chorus&quot; is from 1744.  Meaning &quot;main part of a modern popular song&quot; (as distinguished from the <span class="crossreference">verse</span>, q.v.) is by 1926, originally in jazz. As a verb, 1703, from the noun. <span class="foreign">Chorus girl</span> &quot;young woman who sings and dances in a stage chorus&quot; is by 1852.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/chorus#etymonline_v_11327"><span>Relate entries &amp;
