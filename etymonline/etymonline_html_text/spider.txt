class="foreign">spydyr</span>, <span class="foreign">spither</span>, from earlier <span class="foreign">spiþre</span>, <span class="foreign">spiþur</span>, <span class="foreign">spiþer</span> (mid-14c.), from Old English <span class="foreign">spiðra</span>, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*spin-thron-</span> (cognate with Danish <span class="foreign">spinder</span>), literally &quot;spinner,&quot; from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*(s)pen-</span> &quot;to draw, stretch, spin&quot; + formative or agential <span class="foreign">*-thro</span>. The connection with the root is more transparent in other Germanic cognates (such as Middle Low German, Middle Dutch, Middle High German, German <span class="foreign">spinne</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">spin</span> &quot;spider&quot;).</p>
<blockquote>The male is commonly much smaller than the female, and in impregnating the female runs great risk of being devoured. The difference in sizes is as if the human female should be some 60 or 70 feet tall. [Century Dictionary]</blockquote>
<p>The loss of <span class="foreign">-n-</span> before spirants is regular in Old English (compare <span class="crossreference">goose</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">tooth</span>). For shift of <span class="foreign">-th-</span> to <span class="foreign">-d-</span> compare <span class="crossreference">murder</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">burden</span> (n.), <span class="crossreference">rudder</span>.</p>
<p>Not the common word in Old or Middle English, which identified the creatures as <span class="foreign">loppe</span> (Chaucer&#x27;s usual word), <span class="foreign">lobbe</span>. Old English also had <span class="foreign">atorcoppe</span> (Middle English <span class="foreign">attercop</span>, literally &quot;poison-head&quot;), and (from Latin <span class="foreign">aranea</span>), <span class="foreign">renge</span>; Middle English had <span class="foreign">araine</span>, &quot;spider,&quot; via Old French from the same Latin word; see <span class="crossreference">arachnid</span>). Another Old English word was <span class="foreign">gangewifre</span> &quot;a weaver as he goes.&quot;</p>
<p>In literature, often a figure of cunning, skill, and industry as well as venomnous predation; in 17c. English used figuratively for venomousness and thread-spinning but also sensitivity (to vibrations), lurking, independence. As the name for a type of two-pack solitaire, it is attested from 1890, probably based on resemblance of the layout of the decks in the original form of the game (see &quot;Tarbart,&quot; &quot;Games of Patience,&quot; 1901, p. 49). <span class="foreign">Spider crab</span> is from 1710, used of various species; <span class="foreign">spider monkey</span> is from 1764, so called for its long limbs.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/spider#etymonline_v_24014"><span>Relate entries &amp;
