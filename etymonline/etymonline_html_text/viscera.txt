<br/><br/>
Transferred sense of &quot;the viscera as the seat of emotions&quot; is from late 14c.; especially &quot;inner parts as the seat of pity or kindness,&quot; hence &quot;tenderness, compassion.&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">splankhnon</span> (from the same PIE root as <span class="crossreference">spleen</span>) was a word for the principal internal organs, which also were felt in ancient times to be the seat of various emotions. Greek poets, from Aeschylus down, regarded the bowels as the seat of the more violent passions such as anger and love, but by the Hebrews they were seen as the seat of tender affections, especially kindness, benevolence, and compassion. <span class="foreign">Splankhnon</span> was used in Septuagint to translate a Hebrew word, and from thence early Bibles in English rendered it in its literal sense as <span class="foreign">bowels</span>, which thus acquired in English a secondary meaning of &quot;pity, compassion&quot; (late 14c.). But in later editions the word often was translated as <span class="crossreference">heart</span>. <span class="foreign">Bowel movement</span> is attested by 1874.

This is from Late Latin <span class="foreign">hypochondria</span>, from Greek <span class="foreign">hypokhondria</span> (neuter plural of <span class="foreign">hypokhondrios</span>), from <span class="foreign">hypo-</span> &quot;under&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">hypo-</span>) + <span class="foreign">khondros</span> &quot;cartilage&quot; (in this case, of the false ribs); see <span class="crossreference">chondro-</span>.</p>
<p>The sense &quot;morbid melancholy&quot; reflects the ancient belief that the viscera of the hypochondria (liver, gall bladder, spleen) were the seat of melancholy and the source of the vapors that caused such feelings. The attempt to put it on a scientific bases passes through <span class="crossreference">hypochondriasis</span>. Also see <span class="crossreference">hype</span> (n.). The poet Cowper is an oft-cited example in late 18c. literature. The focus of sense on the particular symptom &quot;unfounded belief that one is sick&quot; seems to begin 1790s with William Cullen, M.D., professor of physic in the University of Edinburgh, who made a specialty of the topic:</p>
<blockquote>A languor, listlessness, or want of resolution and activity, with respect to all undertakings; a disposition to seriousness, sadness, and timidity; as to all future events, an apprehension of the worst or most unhappy state of them; and, therefore, often upon slight grounds an apprehension of great evil. Such persons are particularly attentive to the state of their own health, to every the smallest change of feeling in their bodies; and from any unusual sensation, perhaps of the slightest kind, they apprehend great danger, and even death itself. In respect to these feelings and fears, there is commonly the most obstinate belief and persuasion. [Cullen, &quot;First Lines of the Practice of Physic,&quot; Edinburgh, 1791]</blockquote>
<p>Though to Cullen the clinical definition of <span class="foreign">hypochondria</span> also included physical symptoms and pains as well as these mental delusions.
