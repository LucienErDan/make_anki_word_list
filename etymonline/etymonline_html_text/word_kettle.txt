title="Origin and meaning of kettle" href="/word/kettle#etymonline_v_1827"><em>kettle</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;metal vessel used for boiling or heating liquids over a flame,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">cetil</span>, <span class="foreign">citel</span> (Mercian), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*katilaz</span> (compare Old Saxon <span class="foreign">ketel</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">zetel</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">ketel</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">kezzil</span>, German <span class="foreign">Kessel</span>), which usually is said to be derived from Latin <span class="foreign">catillus</span> &quot;deep pan or dish for cooking,&quot; diminutive of <span class="foreign">catinus</span> &quot;deep vessel, bowl, dish, pot,&quot; from Proto-Italic <span class="foreign">*katino-</span>.</p>
<blockquote>This word has been connected with Greek forms such as [<span class="foreign">kotylē</span>] &quot;bowl, dish.&quot; Yet the Greek word is no perfect formal match, and words for types of vessels are very often loanwords. It seems best to assume this for <span class="foreign">catinus</span> too. [de Vaan]</blockquote>
<p>One of the few Latin loan-words in Proto-Germanic, along with <span class="foreign">*punda-</span> &quot;measure of weight or money&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">pound</span> (n.1)) and a word relating to &quot;merchant&quot; that yielded <span class="crossreference">cheap</span> (adj.). &quot;[I]t is striking that all have something to do with trade&quot; [Don Ringe, &quot;From Proto-Indo-European to Proto-Germanic,&quot; Oxford 2006]. Perhaps the Latin word was confused with a native Germanic one.</p>

<p>Spelling with a <span class="foreign">-k-</span> (c. 1300) probably is from influence of Old Norse cognate <span class="foreign">ketill</span>. The smaller sense of &quot;tea-kettle&quot; is attested by 1769.</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Kettle of fish</span> &quot;complicated and bungled affair&quot; (1715), sometimes is said to be from a Scottish custom of a kettle full of fish cooked al fresco at a boating party or picnic, but this custom is not attested by that phrase until 1790. Perhaps it is rather a variant of <span class="foreign">kittle</span>/<span class="foreign">kiddle</span> &quot;weir or fence with nets set in rivers or along seacoasts for catching fish&quot; (c. 1200, in the Magna Charta as Anglo-Latin <span class="foreign">kidellus</span>), from Old French <span class="foreign">quidel</span>, probably from Breton <span class="foreign">kidel</span> &quot;a net at the mouth of a stream.&quot; <span class="foreign">Kettle</span> was used in geology for &quot;deep circular hollow in a river bed or other eroded area, pothole&quot; (1866), hence <span class="foreign">kettle moraine</span> (1883), characterized by such features.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/kettle#etymonline_v_1827"><span>Relate entries &amp;
