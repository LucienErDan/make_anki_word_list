<p>Used disparagingly, &quot;worthless woman, strumpet&quot; from 1590s; sometimes also playfully, &quot;saucy or flirtatious woman&quot; (1670s). <span class="foreign">Emotional baggage</span> &quot;detrimental unresolved feelings and issues from past experiences&quot; is attested by 1957.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/baggage#etymonline_v_197"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of faggot" href="/word/faggot#etymonline_v_1078">faggot (n.2)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;male homosexual,&quot; 1914, American English slang, probably from earlier contemptuous term for &quot;woman&quot; (1590s), especially an old and unpleasant one, in reference to <span class="crossreference">faggot</span> (n.1) &quot;bundle of sticks,&quot; as something awkward that has to be carried (compare <span class="crossreference">baggage</span> &quot;worthless woman,&quot; 1590s). <blockquote>He [the prefect] used to fag me to blow the chapel organ for him. [&quot;Boy&#x27;s Own Paper,&quot; 1889]</blockquote>
<p>Other obsolete British senses of <span class="foreign">faggot</span> were &quot;man hired into military service merely to fill out the ranks at muster&quot; (1700) and &quot;vote manufactured for party purposes&quot; (1817).</p>
<p>The explanation that male homosexuals were called <span class="foreign">faggots</span> because they were burned at the stake as punishment is an etymological urban legend. Burning sometimes was a punishment meted out to homosexuals in Christian Europe (on the suggestion of the Biblical fate of Sodom and Gomorrah), but in England, where parliament had made homosexuality a capital offense in 1533, hanging was the method prescribed. Use of <span class="foreign">faggot</span> in connection with public executions had long been obscure English historical trivia by the time the word began to be used for &quot;male homosexual&quot; in 20th century American slang, whereas the contemptuous slang word for &quot;woman&quot; (in common with the other possible sources or influences listed here) was in active use early 20c., by D.H. OED rejects connection to other Germanic words for &quot;bellows, belly&quot; as without evidence and finds a Celtic origin untenable. In some senses perhaps from Old French <span class="foreign">bague</span>, which is also from Germanic.</p>
<p>As disparaging slang for &quot;woman&quot; it dates from 1924 in modern use (but various specialized senses of this are much older, and compare <span class="crossreference">baggage</span>). Meaning &quot;person&#x27;s area of interest or expertise&quot; is 1964, from African-American vernacular, from jazz sense of &quot;category,&quot; probably via notion of putting something in a bag. Meaning &quot;fold of loose skin under the eye&quot; is by 1867. Related: <span class="foreign">bags</span>.</p>
<p>To be <span class="foreign"><strong>left holding the bag</strong></span> (and presumably nothing else), &quot;cheated, swindled&quot; is attested by 1793. Many figurative senses, such as the verb meaning &quot;to kill game&quot; (1814) and its colloquial extension to &quot;catch, seize, steal&quot; (1818) are from the notion of the <span class="foreign">game bag</span> (late 15c.) into which the product of the hunt was placed. This also probably explains modern slang <span class="foreign"><strong>in the bag</strong></span> &quot;assured, certain&quot; (1922, American English).</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>let the cat out of the bag</strong></span> &quot;reveal the secret&quot; is from 1760. The source is probably the French expression <span class="foreign">Acheter chat en poche</span> &quot;buy a cat in a bag,&quot; which is attested in 18c. French and explained in Bailey&#x27;s &quot;Universal Etymological English Dictionary&quot; (1736), under the entry for <span class="foreign">To buy a pig in a poke</span> as &quot;to buy a Thing without looking at it, or enquiring into the Value of it.&quot; (Similar expressions are found in Italian and German.) Thus to <span class="foreign">let the cat out of the bag</span> would be to inadvertently reveal the hidden truth of a matter one is attempting to pass off as something better or different, which is in line with the earliest uses in English.</p>
