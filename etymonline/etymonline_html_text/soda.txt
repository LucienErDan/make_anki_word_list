and meaning of soda" href="/word/soda#etymonline_v_23825"><em>soda</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">late 15c., &quot;sodium carbonate,&quot; an alkaline substance extracted from certain ashes (now made artificially), from Italian <span class="foreign">sida</span> (or Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">soda</span>) &quot;a kind of saltwort,&quot; from which soda was obtained, of uncertain origin. Perhaps it is from a Catalan <span class="foreign">sosa</span>, attested from late 13c., of uncertain origin. Proposed Arabic sources in a name of a variety of saltwort have not been attested and that theory is no longer considered valid. Another theory, considered far-fetched in some quarters, traces it to Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">sodanum</span> &quot;a headache remedy,&quot; ultimately from Arabic <span class="foreign">suda</span> &quot;splitting headache.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Soda is found naturally in alkaline lakes, in deposits where such lakes have dried, and from ash produced by burning various seaside plants. A major trading commodity in the medieval Mediterranean, since commercial manufacture of it began in France in late 18c., these other sources have been abandoned. <span class="foreign">Washing soda</span> (sodium carbonate) is commonly distinguished from <span class="foreign">baking soda</span> (sodium bicarbonate). A <span class="foreign">soda-cracker</span> (1863) has baking soda as an ingredient. 
<br/><br/>
The meaning &quot;carbonated water&quot; is first recorded 1834, a shortening of <span class="foreign">soda water</span> (1802) &quot;water into which carbonic acid has been forced under pressure.&quot; &quot;It rarely contains soda in any form; but the name originally applied when sodium carbonate was contained in it has been retained&quot; [Century Dictionary, 1902]. Since 19c. typically flavored and sweetened with syrups.  First record of <span class="foreign">soda pop</span> is from 1863, and the most frequent modern use of the word is as a shortening of this or other terms for &quot;flavored, sweetened soda water.&quot; Compare <span class="crossreference">pop</span> (n.1). <span class="foreign">Soda fountain</span> is from 1824; <span class="foreign">soda jerk</span> first attested 1915 (<span class="foreign">soda-jerker</span> is from 1883).
