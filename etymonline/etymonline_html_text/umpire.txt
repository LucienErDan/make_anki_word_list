<p>In late Middle English <span class="foreign">a</span> and <span class="foreign">an</span> commonly were joined to the following noun, if that word began with a vowel, which caused confusion over how such words ought to be divided when written separately. In <span class="crossreference">nickname</span>, <span class="crossreference">newt</span>, and British dialectal <span class="foreign">naunt</span>, the <span class="foreign">-n-</span> belongs to a preceding indefinite article <span class="foreign">an</span> or possessive pronoun <span class="foreign">mine</span>.</p>
<p>Other examples of this from Middle English manuscripts include <span class="foreign">a neilond</span> (&quot;an island,&quot; early 13c.), <span class="foreign">a narawe</span> (&quot;an arrow,&quot; c. 1400), <span class="foreign">a nox</span> (&quot;an ox,&quot; c. 1400), <span class="foreign">a noke</span> (&quot;an oak,&quot; early 15c.), <span class="foreign">a nappyle</span> (&quot;an apple,&quot; early 15c.), <span class="foreign">a negge</span> (&quot;an egg,&quot; 15c.), <span class="foreign">a nynche</span> (&quot;an inch,&quot; c. 1400). <p>But it is more common for an English word to lose an <span class="foreign">-n-</span> to a preceding <span class="foreign">a</span>: <span class="crossreference">apron</span>, <span class="crossreference">auger</span>, <span class="crossreference">adder</span>, <span class="crossreference">umpire</span>, <span class="crossreference">humble pie</span>, etc.</p>
