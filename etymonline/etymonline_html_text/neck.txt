and meaning of neck" href="/word/neck#etymonline_v_2355"><em>neck</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;that part of an animal body between the head and the trunk and which connects those parts,&quot; Middle English <span class="foreign">nekke</span>, from Old English <span class="foreign">hnecca</span> &quot;neck, nape, back of the neck&quot; (a fairly rare word) from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*hnekk-</span> &quot;the nape of the neck&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">hnekka</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">necke</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">nek</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hnakkr</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">hnach</span>, German <span class="foreign">Nacken</span> &quot;neck&quot;), with no certain cognates outside Germanic, though Klein&#x27;s sources suggest PIE <span class="foreign">*knok-</span> &quot;high point, ridge&quot; (source of Old Irish <span class="foreign">cnocc</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">cnwch</span>, Old Breton <span class="foreign">cnoch</span> &quot;hill&quot;).</p>
<p>The more usual Old English words were <span class="foreign">hals</span> (the general Germanic word, cognate with Gothic, Old Norse, Danish, Swedish, Dutch, German <span class="foreign">hals</span>), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*halsaz</span>, which is perhaps cognate with Latin <span class="foreign">collum</span> (see <span class="crossreference">collar</span> (n.)); and <span class="foreign">sweora</span>, <span class="foreign">swira</span> &quot;neck, nape,&quot; probably also from a PIE root meaning &quot;column&quot; (cognate with Old English <span class="foreign">swer</span> &quot;column,&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">svaru-</span> &quot;post&quot;).</p>
<p>Oxen and other draught animals being yoked by the neck, it became a symbol of burdens, of submission or subjugation, and also resistance or obstinacy (compare <span class="crossreference">stiff-necked</span>). Figuratively, &quot;life&quot; (late 15c.) from the breaking or severing of the neck in legal executions. Meaning &quot;narrow part at the top of a bottle&quot; is from late 14c.; meaning &quot;part of a garment which covers the neck&quot; is from 1520s. Meaning &quot;long, slender part of a stringed musical instrument&quot; is from 1610s.</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;isthmus, long, narrow strip of land connecting two larger ones&quot; is from 1550s. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>neck of the woods</strong></span> (American English) is attested from 1780 in the sense of &quot;narrow stretch of woods;&quot; 1839 with meaning &quot;settlement in a wooded region.&quot; To <span class="foreign"><strong>stick (one&#x27;s) neck out</strong></span> &quot;take a risk&quot; is recorded by 1919, American English. Horses running <span class="foreign"><strong>neck and neck</strong></span> &quot;at an equal pace&quot; is attested from 1799; to <span class="foreign"><strong>win by a neck</strong></span> is from 1823.
