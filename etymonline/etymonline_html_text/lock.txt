and meaning of lock" href="/word/lock#etymonline_v_12371"><em>lock</em> (n.1)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;means of fastening,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">loc</span> &quot;bolt, appliance for fastening a door, lid, etc.; barrier, enclosure; bargain, agreement, settlement, conclusion,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*lukana-</span>, a verbal root meaning &quot;to close&quot; (source also of Old Frisian <span class="foreign">lok</span> &quot;enclosure, prison, concealed place,&quot; Old Norse <span <span class="foreign">Loch</span> &quot;opening, hole,&quot; Dutch <span class="foreign">luik</span> &quot;shutter, trapdoor&quot;).</p>
<p>Ordinary mechanical locks work by means of an internal bolt or bar which slides and catches in an opening made to receive it. &quot;The great diversity of meaning in the Teut. words seems to indicate two or more independent but formally identical substantival formations from the root&quot; [OED]. The Old English sense &quot;barrier, enclosure&quot; led to the specific meaning &quot;barrier on a stream or canal&quot; (c. 1300), and the more specific sense &quot;gate and sluice system on a water channel used as a means of raising and lowering boats&quot; (1570s).</p>
<p>From 1540s as &quot;a fastening together,&quot; hence &quot;a grappling in wrestling&quot; (c. 1600). In firearms, the part of the mechanism which explodes the charge (1540s, probably so called for its resemblance to a door-latching device), hence figurative phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>lock, stock, and barrel</strong></span> (which add up to the whole firearm) &quot;the whole of something&quot; (1842).
