use as an adjective), from Church Latin <span class="foreign">clericus</span> &quot;clergyman, priest,&quot; noun use of adjective meaning &quot;priestly, belonging to the <span class="foreign">clerus</span>;&quot; from Ecclesiastical Greek <span class="foreign">klērikos</span> &quot;pertaining to an inheritance,&quot; but in Greek Christian jargon by 2c., &quot;of the <p><span class="foreign">Klēros</span> was used by early Greek Christians for matters relating to ministry, based on Deuteronomy xviii.2 reference to Levites as temple assistants: &quot;Therefore shall they have no inheritance among their brethren: the Lord is their inheritance&quot; (<span class="foreign">klēros</span> being used as a translation of Hebrew <span class="foreign">nahalah</span> &quot;inheritance, lot&quot;). <p>Modern bureaucratic usage is a reminder of the time when clergy alone could read and write and were employed as scribes and account-keepers by secular authorities. In late Old English the word also can mean &quot;king&#x27;s scribe; keeper of accounts.&quot; And by c. 1200 <span class="foreign">clerk</span> took on a secondary sense in Middle English (as the cognate word did in Old French) of &quot;man of letters, anyone who can read or write.&quot;</p>
<p>This led to the senses &quot;assistant in a public or private business&quot; (c. 1500), originally a keeper of accounts, also &quot;officer of a court, municipality, etc. whose duty it is to keep its records and perform its routine business&quot; (1520s), and later, especially in American English, &quot;a retail salesman&quot; (1790).
