<p>The earliest sense in Middle English was &quot;cord used by builders for taking measurements;&quot; extended late 14c. to &quot;a thread-like mark&quot; (from sense &quot;cord used by builders for making things level,&quot; mid-14c.), also &quot;track, course, direction.&quot; Meaning &quot;limit, boundary&quot; (of a county, etc.) is from 1590s. The mathematical sense of &quot;length without breadth&quot; is from 1550s. From 1530s as &quot;a crease of the face or palm of the hand.&quot; From 1580s as &quot;the equator.&quot;</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;things or people arranged in a straight line&quot; is from 1550s. Now considered American English, where British English uses <span class="crossreference">queue</span> (n.), but the sense appears earliest in English writers. Sense of &quot;chronologically continuous series of persons&quot; (<span class="foreign">a line of kings</span>, etc.) is from late 14c.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;one&#x27;s occupation, branch of business&quot; is from 1630s, according to OED probably from misunderstood KJV translation of II Corinthians x.16, &quot;And not to boast in another mans line of things made ready to our hand,&quot; where <span class="foreign">line</span> translates Greek <span class="foreign">kanon</span> which probably meant &quot;boundary, limit;&quot; the phrase &quot;in another man&#x27;s line&quot; being parenthetical.</p>
<p>Commercial meaning &quot;class of goods in stock&quot; is from 1930, so called from being goods received by the merchant on a <span class="foreign">line</span> in the specific sense &quot;order given to an agent&quot; for particular goods (1834). Insurance underwriting sense is from 1899. <span class="foreign"><strong>Line of credit</strong></span> is from 1958.</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;series of public conveyances&quot; (coaches, later ships) is from 1786; meaning &quot;continuous part of a railroad&quot; is from 1825. Meaning &quot;telegraph wire between stations&quot; is from 1847 (later &quot;telephone wire&quot;). Meaning &quot;cord bearing hooks used in fishing&quot; is from c. 1300. Meaning &quot;policy or set of policies of a political faction&quot; is 1892, American English, from notion of a procession of followers; this is the sense in the political <span class="foreign"><strong>party line</strong></span>, and, deteriorated, it is the slang <span class="foreign">line</span> that means &quot;glib and plausible talk meant to deceive.&quot;</p>
<p>In British army, <span class="foreign"><strong>the Line</strong></span> (1802) is the regular, numbered troops, as distinguished from guards, auxiliaries, militia, etc. In the Navy (1704) it refers to the battle line (the sense in <span class="foreign"><strong>ship of the line</strong></span>, which is attested from 1706).</p>
<p>Dutch <span class="foreign">lijn</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">lina</span>, German <span class="foreign">Leine</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">lina</span> &quot;a cord, rope,&quot; are likewise from Latin. Spanish and Italian have the word in the learned form <span class="foreign">linea</span>. In continental measurements, a subdivision of an inch (one-tenth or one-twelfth in England), attested in English from 1660s but never common. Also see <span class="crossreference">lines</span>.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>get a line on</strong></span> &quot;acquire information about&quot; is from 1903. To <span class="foreign"><strong>lay it on the line</strong></span> is from 1929 as &quot;to pay money;&quot; by 1954 as &quot;speak plainly.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>End of the line</strong></span> &quot;as far as one can go&quot; is from 1948.
