<p>Political sense &quot;the democratic or liberal party&quot; arose from the custom of assigning those members of a legislative body to the left side of a chamber. This usage is first attested in English in 1837 (by Carlyle, in reference to the French Revolution), and probably is a loan-translation of French <span class="foreign">la gauche</span> (1791), said to have originated during the seating of the French National Assembly in 1789 in which the nobility took the seats on the President&#x27;s right and left the Third Estate to sit on the left. <p>Sense of &quot;opposite of right&quot; is from the left being usually the weaker hand), a derived sense also found in cognate Middle Dutch and Low German <span class="foreign">luchter</span>, <span class="foreign">luft</span>. Compare Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kairys</span> &quot;left&quot; and Lettish <span class="foreign">kreilis</span> &quot;left hand&quot; both from a root that yields words for &quot;twisted, crooked.&quot;</p>
<p>The usual Old English <span class="foreign">winstre</span>/<span class="foreign">winestra</span> &quot;left&quot; (adj.); &quot;left hand,&quot; literally &quot;friendlier,&quot; a euphemism used superstitiously to avoid invoking the unlucky forces connected with the left side (compare <span class="crossreference">sinister</span>). The Kentish word itself might have been originally a taboo replacement, if instead it represents PIE <span class="foreign">*laiwo-</span> &quot;considered conspicuous&quot; (represented in Greek <span class="foreign">laios</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">laevus</span>, and Russian <span class="foreign">levyi</span>). Greek also uses a euphemism for &quot;left,&quot; <span class="foreign">aristeros</span> &quot;the better one&quot; (compare also Avestan <span class="foreign">vairyastara-</span> &quot;to the left,&quot; from <span class="foreign">vairya-</span> &quot;desirable&quot;).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;being on the left-hand side&quot; is from c. 1300. As an adverb from early 14c. For political senses, see <span class="crossreference">left</span> (n.). Used at least since c. 1600 in various senses of &quot;irregular, illicit;&quot; earlier proverbial sense was &quot;opposite of what is expressed&quot; (mid-15c.), for example <span class="foreign"><strong>over the left (shoulder)</strong></span> &quot;not at all,&quot; added to a statement to negate or neglect what was just said (1705). To have <span class="foreign"><strong>two left feet</strong></span> &quot;be clumsy&quot; is attested by 1902.</p>
<p>Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>out in left field</strong></span> &quot;out of touch with pertinent realities&quot; is attested from 1944, from the baseball fielding position that tends to be far removed from the play (<span class="foreign">left field</span> in baseball attested by 1867). The Parisian <span class="foreign"><strong>Left Bank</strong></span> (of the River Seine) has been associated with intellectual and artistic culture at least since 1893; <span class="foreign"><strong>Left Coast</strong></span> &quot;Pacific Coast of the U.S.&quot; is by 1980s.</p>
<p>German <span class="foreign">link</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">linker</span> &quot;left&quot; are said to be not directly related to these, being instead from Old High German <span class="foreign">slinc</span> and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">slink</span> &quot;left,&quot; related to Swedish <span class="foreign">linka</span> &quot;limp,&quot; <span class="foreign">slinka</span> &quot;dangle,&quot; and Old English <span class="foreign">slincan</span> &quot;crawl&quot; (Modern English <span class="crossreference">slink</span>).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_6652"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of lace-wing" href="/word/lace-wing#etymonline_v_51744">lace-<em>wing</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">also <span class="foreign">lacewing</span>, type of insect, 1847; see <span class="crossreference">lace</span> (n.) + <span class="crossreference">wing</span> (n.).
