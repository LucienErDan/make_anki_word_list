Until the Lanham Acts of 1948 changed federal patent regulations to allow protection for registered collective marks, the National Association fought and won sixteen cases on the local and state levels to protect its symbolic property. <p>Latin and Sanskrit also have words for &quot;writing&quot; that are based on tree names (&quot;birch&quot; and &quot;ash,&quot; respectively). And compare French <span class="foreign">livre</span> &quot;book,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">librum</span>, originally &quot;the inner bark of trees&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">library</span>). The Old English word originally meant any written document. The sense gradually narrowed by early Middle English to &quot;a written work covering many pages fastened together and bound,&quot; also &quot;a literary composition&quot; in any form, of however many volumes. Later also &quot;bound pages,&quot; whether written on or not. In 19c. it also could mean &quot;a magazine;&quot; in 20c. a telephone directory.</p>
<p>From c. 1200 as &quot;a main subdivision of a larger work.&quot; Meaning &quot;libretto of an opera&quot; is from 1768. A betting <span class="foreign">book</span> &quot;record of bets made&quot; is from 1812. Meaning &quot;sum of criminal charges&quot; is from 1926, hence slang phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>throw the book at</strong></span> (1932). <span class="foreign"><strong>Book of Life</strong></span> &quot;the roll of those chosen for eternal life&quot; is from mid-14c. <span class="foreign"><strong>Book of the month</strong></span> is from 1926. To do something <span class="foreign"><strong>by the book</strong></span> &quot;according to the rules&quot; is from 1590s.</p>
