Of taxation, from 1889; of jazz, from 1947. Meaning &quot;characterized by striving for change and innovation, avant-garde, liberal&quot; is from 1908.
<br/><br/>
In the socio-political sense &quot;favoring reform; radically liberal,&quot; it emerged in various British contexts from the 1880s; in the U.S. it was active as a movement in the 1890s and a generation thereafter, the name being taken again from time to time, most recently by some more liberal Democrats and other social activists, by c. 2000. The noun in the sense &quot;one who favors social and political change in the name of progress&quot; is first attested 1865 (originally in Christianity). Earlier in a like sense were <span class="foreign">progressionist</span> (1849, adjective; 1884, noun), <span class="foreign">progressist</span> (1848).
