and meaning of rat" href="/word/rat#etymonline_v_3387"><em>rat</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late Old English <span class="foreign">ræt</span> &quot;rat,&quot; of uncertain origin. Similar words are found in Celtic (Gaelic <span class="foreign">radan</span>), Romanic (Italian <span class="foreign">ratto</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">rata</span>, French <span class="foreign">rat</span>) and Germanic (Old Saxon <span class="foreign">ratta</span>; Dutch <span class="foreign">rat</span>; German <span class="foreign">Ratte</span>, dialectal <span class="foreign">Ratz</span>; Swedish <span class="foreign">råtta</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">rotte</span>) languages, but connection is uncertain and origin unknown. In all this it is very much like <span class="crossreference">cat</span>.<br/>
<br/>
Perhaps from Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*rattus</span>, but Weekley thinks this is of Germanic origin, &quot;the animal having come from the East with the race-migrations&quot; and the word passing thence to the Romanic languages. American Heritage and Tucker connect Old English <span class="foreign">ræt</span> to Latin <span class="foreign">rodere</span> and thus from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*red-</span> &quot;to scrape, scratch, gnaw,&quot; source of <span class="crossreference">rodent</span> (q.v.). Klein says there is no such connection and suggests a possible cognate in Greek <span class="foreign">rhine</span> &quot;file, rasp.&quot; Weekley connects them with a question mark and Barnhart writes, &quot;the relationship to each other of the Germanic, Romance, and Celtic words for <span class="foreign">rat</span> is uncertain.&quot; OED says it is &quot;probable&quot; the <span class="foreign">rat</span> word spread from Germanic to Romanic, but takes no position on further etymology. Middle English common form was <span class="foreign">ratton</span>, from augmented Old French form <span class="foreign">raton</span>.</p>
<blockquote>RATS. Of these there are the following kinds: a black rat and a grey rat, a py-rat and a cu-rat. [&quot;Dictionary of the Vulgar Tongue,&quot; Grose, 1788] </blockquote>
<p>Sense of &quot;one who abandons his associates&quot; (1620s) is from belief that rats leave a ship about to sink or a house about to fall and led to meaning &quot;traitor, informant&quot; (1902; verb 1910). Interjection <span class="foreign">rats</span> is American English, 1886.
