Meaning &quot;coarse, low, ill-bred&quot; is first recorded 1640s, probably from earlier use (with reference to people) with meaning &quot;belonging to the ordinary class&quot; (1530). Related: <span class="foreign">Vulgarly</span>.</p>
<blockquote>What we have added to human depravity is again a thoroughly Roman quality, perhaps even a Roman invention: vulgarity. That word means the mind of the herd, and specifically the herd in the city, the gutter, and the tavern. [Guy Davenport, &quot;Wheel Ruts&quot;]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/vulgar#etymonline_v_7897"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of inexpressible" href="/word/inexpressible#etymonline_v_42465">inexpressible (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1620s, from <span class="crossreference">in-</span> (1) &quot;not&quot; + <span class="foreign">expressible</span> (see <span class="crossreference">express</span> (v.)). <span class="foreign">Inexpressibles</span> &quot;trousers&quot; is from 1790. Related: <span class="foreign">Inexpressibly</span>.</p>

[footnote in &quot;Poems Miscellaneous and Humorous,&quot; by Edward Nairne, Canterbury, 1791]</blockquote>
<p><span class="foreign">Inexpressibles</span> is the earliest recorded and thus seems to have begotten the trend: <span class="foreign">Unmentionables</span> (1806); <span class="foreign">indispensibles</span> (1820); <span class="foreign">ineffables</span> (1823); <span class="foreign">unutterables</span> (1826); <span class="foreign">innominables</span> (1827); and <span class="foreign">inexplicables</span> (1829) followed.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/inexpressible#etymonline_v_42465"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of son of a bitch" href="/word/son%20of%20a%20bitch#etymonline_v_25707">son of a bitch </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1707 as a direct phrase, but implied much earlier, and Old Norse had <span class="foreign">bikkju-sonr</span>. Abbreviated form <span class="foreign">SOB</span> from 1918; form <span class="foreign">sumbitch</span> attested in writing by 1969.</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">Abide þou þef malicious!</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Biche-sone þou drawest amis</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">þou schalt abigge it ywis!</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[&quot;Of Arthour &amp; of Merlin,&quot; c. 1330]</blockquote>

<p>&quot;Probably the most common American vulgarity from about the middle of the eighteenth century to the middle of the twentieth&quot; [Rawson].</p>
<blockquote>Our maid-of-all-work in that department [indecency] is <span class="foreign">son-of-a-bitch</span>, which seems as pale and ineffectual to a Slav or a Latin as <span class="foreign">fudge</span> does to us. There is simply no lift in it, no shock, no sis-boom-ah. The dumbest policeman in Palermo thinks of a dozen better ones between breakfast and the noon whistle. [H.L. Mencken, &quot;The American Language,&quot; 4th ed., 1936, p.317-8]</blockquote>
<p>Elsewhere, complaining of the tepidity of the American vocabulary of profanity, Mencken writes that the toned-down form <span class="foreign">son-of-a-gun</span> &quot;is so lacking in punch that the Italians among us have borrowed it as a satirical name for an American: <span class="foreign">la sanemagogna</span> is what they call him, and by it they indicate their contempt for his backwardness in the art that is one of their great glories.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>It was in 1934 also that the New York <span class="foreign">Daily News</span>, with commendable frankness, in reporting a hearing in Washington at which Senator Huey P. Long featured, forsook the old-time dashes and abbreviations and printed the complete epithet &quot;son of a bitch.&quot; [Stanley Walker, &quot;City Editor,&quot; 1934]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/son%20of%20a%20bitch#etymonline_v_25707"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of Three Rs" href="/word/Three%20Rs#etymonline_v_49027">Three Rs (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>1824; said to have been given as a toast by Sir William Curtis (1752-1829), a beloved lord mayor of London in the 1820s, who seems to have been a figure of fun to whom many mangled phrases were attributed. Among the toasts he is alleged to have given at public dinners were &quot;The Female Ladies of London;&quot; &quot;The three C&#x27;s--Cox, King, and Curtis;&quot; and &quot;The three R&#x27;s--Reading, Writing, and Rithmetic.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>It has been very much the fashion amongst a class of persons to attribute to Sir W. C. ... a vulgarity and ignorance of speech which are by no means consistent with his character and conduct. The worthy and hospitable baronet has a rapid mode of speech, but it is always correct ; and although some eccentricities are mixed up in his composition, he is highly honourable, and has been a very useful member of society, particularly to his London constituents. [&quot;The Mirror,&quot; Jan. 29, 1825]</blockquote>
<p>After listing some examples, the article continues:</p>
<blockquote class="poetry">It is, however, very certain, that at a city festival some years ago, having indulged very freely, he fell asleep, when some wag, choosing to consider him dead, wrote his epitaph, which was found next morning pinned to the baronet&#x27;s dress coat:--</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">&quot;Here lies the great Curtis,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Of London, Lord May&#x27;r:</blockquote>
