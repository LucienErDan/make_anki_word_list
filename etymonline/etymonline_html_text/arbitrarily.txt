<p>The Latin word also is the source of French <span class="foreign">mille</span>, Italian <span class="foreign">miglio</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">milla</span>. The Scandinavian words (Old Norse <span class="foreign">mila</span>, etc.) are from English. An ancient Roman mile was 1,000 double paces (one step with each foot), for about 4,860 feet, but many local variants developed, in part in an attempt to reconcile the mile with the agricultural system of measurements. Consequently, old European miles were of various lengths. The medieval English mile was 6,610 feet; the old London mile was 5,000 feet. In Germany, Holland, and Scandinavia in the Middle Ages, the Latin word was applied arbitrarily to the ancient Germanic <span class="foreign">rasta</span>, a measure of from 3.25 to 6 English miles. In England the ordinary mile was set by legal act at 320 perches (5,280 feet) by statute in Elizabeth&#x27;s reign.</p>

<p>In Middle English the word also was a unit of time, &quot;about 20 minutes,&quot; roughly what was required to walk a mile.
