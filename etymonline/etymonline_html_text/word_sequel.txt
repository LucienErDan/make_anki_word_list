<p>Many Indo-European languages show signs of once having distinguished the horizontal back of an animal (or a mountain range) from the upright back of a human. <p>By synecdoche, &quot;the whole body,&quot; especially with reference to clothing. Meaning &quot;upright part of a chair&quot; is from 1520s. <p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>know (something) like the back of one&#x27;s hand</strong></span>, implying familiarity, is first attested 1893 in a dismissive speech made to a character in Robert Louis Stevenson&#x27;s &quot;Catriona&quot;:</p>
<blockquote>If I durst speak to herself, you may be certain I would never dream of trusting it to you; because I know you like the back of my hand, and all your blustering talk is that much wind to me.</blockquote>
<p>The story, a sequel to &quot;Kidnapped,&quot; has a Scottish setting and context, and <span class="foreign">the back of my hand to you</span> was noted in the late 19th century as a Scottish expression meaning &quot;I will have nothing to do with you&quot; [see Longmuir&#x27;s edition of Jamieson&#x27;s Scottish dictionary]. It was a sequel to his <span class="foreign">&quot;Amadis de Gaula,&quot;</span> and was said to have been influential among Spanish explorers of the New World. It could have led them to misidentify Baja California as this mythical land and to mistake it for an island. The <span class="foreign">Amadis</span> tales are the Iberian equivalent of the Arthurian romances; they are older than 1510 (traces of them have been found mid-14c.) and were wildly popular. That conquistadors and sailors would have known the story in all its imaginative detail is hardly surprising.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span> ... set a fashion: all later Spanish writers of books of chivalry adopted the machinery of <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span>. Later knights were not less brave (they could not be braver than) Amadis; heroines were not less lovely (they could not be lovelier) than Oriana; there was nothing for it but to make the dragons more appalling, the giants larger, the wizards craftier, the magic castles more inaccessible, the enchanted lakes deeper. Subsequent books of chivalry are simple variants of the types in <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span>: Cervantes made his barber describe it as &#x27;the best of all books of this kind.&#x27; This verdict is essentially just. <span class="foreign">Amadis de Gaula</span> was read everywhere, especially in the French version of Herberay des Essarts. It was done into Hebrew during the sixteenth century, and attracted readers as different as St Ignatius of Loyola and Henry of Navarre. Its vogue perhaps somewhat exceeded its merit, but its merits are not inconsiderable. [James Fitzmaurice-Kelly, &quot;Spanish Literature,&quot; 1922 edition]</blockquote>
