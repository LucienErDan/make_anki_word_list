<p>The sense of &quot;several, sundry, more than one but not a great number&quot; emerged by c. 1400, referring &quot;originally and in form to the variety of objects; but, as variety implies number, becoming an indefinite numeral word expressing multiplicity&quot; [OED].</p>
<p>Middle English used the spellings <span class="foreign">divers</span> and <span class="foreign">diverse</span> indifferently. In later use, <span class="foreign">diverse</span> became more associated with Latin <span class="foreign">diversus</span> &quot;turned different ways,&quot; and after c.1700 the differentiation in form and sense was complete.</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Divers</span> implies difference only, and is always used with a plural noun; <span class="foreign">diverse</span> (with either a singular or a plural noun) denotes difference with opposition. Thus, the evangelists narrate the same events in <span class="foreign">divers</span> manners, but not in <span class="foreign">diverse</span>.
