class="foreign">sæterdæg</span>, <span class="foreign">sæternesdæg</span>, literally &quot;day of the planet Saturn,&quot; from <span class="foreign">Sæternes</span> (genitive of <span class="foreign">Sætern</span>; see <span class="crossreference">Saturn</span>) + Old English <span class="foreign">dæg</span> (see <span class="crossreference">day</span>). Partial loan-translation of Latin <span class="foreign">Saturni dies</span> &quot;Saturn&#x27;s day&quot; (compare Dutch <span class="foreign">Zaterdag</span>, Old Frisian <span class="foreign">Saterdi</span>, Middle Low German <span class="foreign">Satersdach</span>; Irish <span class="foreign">dia Sathuirn</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">dydd Sadwrn</span>). The Latin word itself is a loan-translation of Greek <span class="foreign">kronou hemera</span>, literally &quot;the day of Cronus.&quot;
<br/><br/>
Unlike other English day names, no god substitution seems to have been attempted, perhaps because the northern European pantheon lacks a clear corresponding figure to Roman Saturn. A homely ancient Nordic custom, however, seems to be preserved in Old Norse <span class="foreign">laugardagr</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">lørdag</span>, Swedish <span class="foreign">lördag</span> &quot;Saturday,&quot; literally &quot;bath day&quot; (Old Norse <span class="foreign">laug</span> &quot;bath&quot;).
<br/><br/>
German <span class="foreign">Samstag</span> (Old High German <span class="foreign">sambaztag</span>) appears to be from a Greek <span class="foreign">*sambaton</span>, a nasalized colloquial form of <span class="foreign">sabbaton</span> &quot;sabbath,&quot; also attested in Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">sabota</span>, Polish <span class="foreign">sobota</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">subbota</span>, Hungarian <span class="foreign">szombat</span>, French <span class="foreign">samedi</span>.

<p>The Babylonians regarded seventh days as unlucky, and avoided certain activities then; the Jewish observance might have begun as a similar custom. The change was driven by Christians&#x27; celebration of the Lord&#x27;s resurrection on the first day of the week, a change completed during the Reformation.</p>
