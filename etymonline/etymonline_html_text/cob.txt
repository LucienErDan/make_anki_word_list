seeming to derive from notions of &quot;heap, lump, rounded object,&quot; also &quot;head,&quot; and metaphoric extensions of both. With its cognates in other Germanic languages, of uncertain origin and development.</p>
<p>&quot;The N.E.D. recognizes eight nouns <span class="foreign">cob</span>, with numerous sub-groups. Like other monosyllables common in the dial[ect] its hist[ory] is inextricable&quot; [Weekley]. In the 2nd print edition, the number stands at 11. Some senses are probably from Old English <span class="foreign">copp</span> &quot;top, head,&quot; others probably from Old Norse <span class="foreign">kubbi</span> or Low German, all the words perhaps trace to a Proto-Germanic base <span class="foreign">*kubb-</span> &quot;something rounded.&quot;</p>
<p>Among the earliest attested English senses are &quot;headman, chief,&quot; and &quot;male swan,&quot; both early 15c., but the surname <span class="foreign">Cobb</span> (1066) suggests Old English used a form of the word as a nickname for &quot;big, leading man.&quot;
