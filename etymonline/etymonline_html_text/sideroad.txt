Showing results for <strong><a href="/search?q=sidereal">sidereal</a>.</strong></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of sidereal" href="/word/sidereal#etymonline_v_23489"><em>sidereal</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>also <span class="foreign">siderial</span>, 1630s, &quot;star-like;&quot; 1640s, &quot;of or pertaining to the stars,&quot; earlier <span class="foreign">sideral</span> (1590s), from French <span class="foreign">sidereal</span> (16c.), from Latin <span class="foreign">sidereus</span> &quot;starry, astral, of the constellations,&quot; from <span class="foreign">sidus</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">sideris</span>) &quot;star, group of stars, constellation,&quot; which is of uncertain origin, perhaps from PIE root <span class="foreign">*sweid-</span> &quot;to shine&quot; (source also of Lithuanian <span class="foreign">svidus</span> &quot;shining, bright&quot;).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Sidereal time</span> is measured by the apparent diurnal motion of the fixed stars. The sidereal day begins and ends with the passage of the vernal equinox over the meridian and is about four minutes shorter than the solar day, measured by the passage of the sun over the meridian.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/sidereal#etymonline_v_23489"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of considerable" href="/word/considerable#etymonline_v_28678">considerable (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-15c., &quot;capable of being considered, conceivable,&quot; from Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">considerabilis</span> &quot;worthy to be considered,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">considerare</span> &quot;to look at closely, observe,&quot; probably literally &quot;to observe the stars,&quot; from assimilated form of <span class="foreign">com</span> &quot;with, together&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">con-</span>) + <span class="foreign">sidus</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">sideris</span>) &quot;heavenly body, star, constellation&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">sidereal</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;pretty large&quot; is from 1650s (implied in <span class="foreign">considerably</span>), from now-archaic earlier sense of &quot;Worthy of regard or attention&quot; (1610s).</p>
<blockquote>CONSIDERABLE. This word is still frequently used in the manner pointed out by Dr. Witherspoon in the following remark: &quot;He is <span class="foreign">considerable</span> of a surveyor; <span class="foreign">considerable</span> of it may be found in the country. This manner of speaking prevails in the northern parts.&quot; [Pickering, 1816]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/considerable#etymonline_v_28678"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of consider" href="/word/consider#etymonline_v_18238">consider (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;to fix the mind upon for careful examination, meditate upon,&quot; also &quot;view attentively, scrutinize; not to be negligent of,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">considerer</span> (13c.) &quot;reflect on, consider, study,&quot; from Latin <span class="foreign">considerare</span> &quot;to look at closely, observe,&quot; probably literally &quot;to observe the stars,&quot; from assimilated form of <span class="foreign">com</span> &quot;with, together&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">con-</span>) + <span class="foreign">sidus</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">sideris</span>) &quot;heavenly body, star, constellation&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">sidereal</span>).</p>
<p>Perhaps a metaphor from navigation, but more likely reflecting Roman obsession with divination by astrology. Tucker doubts the connection with <span class="foreign">sidus</span>, however, because it is &quot;quite inapplicable to <span class="foreign">desiderare</span>,&quot; and suggests derivation instead from the PIE root of English <span class="foreign">side</span> meaning &quot;stretch, extend,&quot; and a sense for the full word of &quot;survey on all sides&quot; or &quot;dwell long upon.&quot;</p>
<p>From 1530s as &quot;to regard in a particular light.&quot; Related: <span class="foreign">Considered</span>; <span class="foreign">considering</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/consider#etymonline_v_18238"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of hour" href="/word/hour#etymonline_v_14502">hour (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, &quot;divine office prescribed for each of the seven canonical hours; the daily service at the canonical hours;&quot; c. 1300, &quot;time of day appointed for prayer, one of the seven canonical hours,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">ore</span>, <span class="foreign">hore</span> &quot;canonical hour; one-twelfth of a day&quot; (sunrise to sunset), from Latin <span class="foreign">hora</span> &quot;an hour;&quot; poetically &quot;time of year, season,&quot; from Greek <span class="foreign">hōra</span> a word used to indicate any limited time within a year, month, or day, from PIE <span class="foreign">*yor-a-</span>, from root <span class="foreign">*yer-</span> &quot;year, season&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">year</span>).</p>
<p>Church sense is oldest in English. Meaning &quot;one of the 24 equal parts of a natural solar day (time from one sunrise to the next), equal hour; definite time of day or night reckoned in equal hours,&quot; and that of &quot;one of the 12 equal parts of an artificial day (sunrise to sunset) or night, varying in duration according to the season; definite time of day or night reckoned in unequal hours&quot; are from late 14c. In the Middle Ages the planets were held to rule over the unequal hours. As late as 16c. distinction sometimes was made in English between <span class="foreign">temporary</span> (unequal) hours and <span class="foreign">sidereal</span> (equal) ones. Meaning &quot;time of a particular happening; the time for a given activity&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">hour of death</span>) is mid-14c.</p>
<p>The <span class="foreign">h-</span> has persisted in this word despite not being pronounced since Roman times. Replaced Old English <span class="foreign">tid</span>, literally &quot;time&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">tide</span> (n.)) and <span class="foreign">stund</span> &quot;period of time, point of time, hour,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*stundo</span> (compare German <span class="foreign">Stunde</span> &quot;hour&quot;), which is of uncertain origin. German <span class="foreign">Uhr</span> likewise is from French.</p>
<p>Greek <span class="foreign">hora</span> could mean &quot;a season; &#x27;the season&#x27; (spring or summer).&quot; In classical times it sometimes meant &quot;a part of the day,&quot; such as morning, evening, noon, night. The Greek astronomers apparently borrowed the notion of dividing the day into twelve parts (mentioned in Herodotus) from the Babylonians. Night continued to be divided into four watches (see <span class="crossreference">watch</span> (n.)); but because the amount of daylight changed throughout the year, the hours were not fixed or of equal length.</p>
<p>As a measure of distance (&quot;the distance that can be covered in an hour&quot;) it is recorded from 1785. <span class="foreign"><strong>At all hours</strong></span> &quot;at all times&quot; is from early 15c.
