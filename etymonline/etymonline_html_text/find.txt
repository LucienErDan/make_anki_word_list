<p>The Germanic word is from PIE root <span class="foreign">*pent-</span> &quot;to tread, go&quot; (source also of Old High German <span class="foreign">fendeo</span> &quot;pedestrian;&quot; Sanskrit <span class="foreign">panthah</span> &quot;path, way;&quot; Avestan <span class="foreign">panta</span> &quot;way;&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">pontos</span> &quot;open sea,&quot; <span class="foreign">patein</span> &quot;to tread, walk;&quot; Latin <span class="foreign">pons</span> (genitive <span class="foreign">pontis</span>) &quot;bridge;&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pǫti</span> &quot;path,&quot; <span class="foreign">pęta</span> &quot;heel;&quot; Russian <span class="foreign">put&#x27;</span> &quot;path, way&quot;). The prehistoric sense development in Germanic would be from &quot;to go&quot; to &quot;to find (out),&quot; but Boutkan has serious doubts about this.</p>
<p>Germanic <span class="foreign">*-th-</span> in English tends to become <span class="foreign">-d-</span> after <span class="foreign">-n-</span>.
