<p>The Late Latin word apparently originally meant &quot;a woman&#x27;s head-covering,&quot; but the sense was transferred to &quot;hood of a cloak,&quot; then to &quot;cloak&quot; itself, though the various senses co-existed. Old English took in two forms of the Late Latin word, one meaning &quot;head-covering,&quot; the other &quot;ecclesiastical dress&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">cape</span> (n.1)). In most Romance languages, a diminutive of Late Latin <span class="foreign">cappa</span> has become the usual word for &quot;head-covering&quot; (such as French <span class="foreign">chapeau</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;soft, small, close-fitted head covering&quot; in English is from early 13c., originally for women; extended to men late 14c. <p>Meaning &quot;cap-shaped piece of copper lined with gunpowder and used to ignite a firearm&quot; is by 1825, hence <span class="foreign"><strong>cap-gun</strong></span> (1855); extended to paper version used in toy pistols, 1872 (<span class="foreign"><strong>cap-pistol</strong></span> is from 1879).</p>
