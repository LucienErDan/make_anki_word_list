<p>From early 15c. as &quot;born in a particular place, of indigenous origin or growth, not exotic or foreign,&quot; also &quot;of or pertaining to one by birth&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">native land</span>). Also used from early 15c. in a now-obsolete sense of &quot;bound; born in servitude or serfdom.&quot; Of metals, minerals, etc., &quot;occurring in a pure state in nature,&quot; 1690s.</p>

<p><span class="foreign"><strong>Native American</strong></span> in reference to the aboriginal peoples of the Americas is attested by 1908.</p>

Other sources trace the name to Charles <span class="foreign">Lynch</span> (1736-1796) a Virginia magistrate who fined and imprisoned Tories in his district c. 1782, but the connection to him is less likely. The surname is perhaps from Irish <span class="foreign">Loingseach</span> &quot;sailor.&quot;</p>

<blockquote>It implies lawless concert or action among a number of members of the community, to supply the want of criminal justice or to anticipate its delays, or to inflict a penalty demanded by public opinion, though in defiance of the laws. [Century Dictionary, 1895]</blockquote>

<p>Originally any sort of summary justice, done without authority of law, for a crime or public offense; it especially referred to flogging or tarring-and-feathering. At first the act was associated with frontier regions (as in the above citation), though from c. 1835 to the U.S. Civil War it also often was directed against abolitionists. The narrowing of the meaning to &quot;extra-legal execution by hanging&quot; is evident by the 1880s, and after c. 1893 <span class="foreign">lynching</span> mostly meant killings of blacks by white mobs (especially in retaliation for alleged sexual assaults of white women). This shift in use seems due in part to the work of African-American journalist and activist Ida B. Wells. <span class="foreign">Lynch mob</span> is attested from 1838. Compare earlier <span class="foreign">Lydford law</span>, from a place in Dartmoor, England, &quot;where was held a Stannaries Court of summary jurisdiction&quot; [Weekley], hence:</p>

<blockquote><span class="foreign">Lydford law</span>: is to hang men first, and indite them afterwards. [Thomas Blount, &quot;Glossographia,&quot; 1656]</blockquote>

<p>Also in a similar sense was <span class="foreign">Jedburgh justice</span> (1706) and, as a verb, to <span class="foreign">Dewitt</span> (1680s), a reference to two Dutch statesmen of that name, opponents of William of Orange, murdered by a mob in 1672. Related: <span class="foreign">Lynched</span>; <span class="crossreference">lynching</span>. The city of <span class="foreign">Lynchburg</span>, Virginia, dates to the 1750s when John <span class="foreign">Lynch</span>, brother to Charles but a peaceable Quaker, had a ferry landing on the
