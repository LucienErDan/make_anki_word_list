<p>In Middle English usually with a sense of &quot;skill in scholarship and learning&quot; (c. 1300), especially in the seven sciences, or <span class="foreign">liberal arts</span>. This sense remains in <span class="foreign">Bachelor of Arts</span>, etc. Meaning &quot;human workmanship&quot; (as opposed to <span class="foreign">nature</span>) is from late 14c. Meaning &quot;system of rules and traditions for performing certain actions&quot; is from late 15c. Sense of &quot;skill in cunning and trickery&quot; first attested late 16c. (the sense in <span class="crossreference">artful</span>, <span class="crossreference">artless</span>). Meaning &quot;skill in creative arts&quot; is first recorded 1610s; especially of painting, sculpture, etc., from 1660s.</p>
<blockquote>Supreme art is a traditional statement of certain heroic and religious truths, passed on from age to age, modified by individual genius, but never abandoned. The revolt of individualism came because the tradition had become degraded, or rather because a spurious copy had been accepted in its stead. [William Butler Yeats]</blockquote>
<p>Expression <span class="foreign"><strong>art for art&#x27;s sake</strong></span> (1824) translates French <span class="foreign">l&#x27;art pour l&#x27;art</span>. <p>Connection between Old English and Middle English words is uncertain, but if they are the same, meaning had shifted by c. 1400 to &quot;manly, gallant,&quot; and later moved via &quot;attractive, skillfully made,&quot; to &quot;fine,&quot; to &quot;beautiful in a slight way&quot; (mid-15c.). <p>Meaning &quot;not a few, considerable&quot; is from late 15c.
