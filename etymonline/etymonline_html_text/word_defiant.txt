<p>The Late Latin word is from Ecclesiastical Greek <span class="foreign">diabolos</span>, which in Jewish and Christian use was &quot;the Devil, Satan,&quot; and which in general use meant &quot;accuser, slanderer&quot; (thus it was a scriptural loan-translation of Hebrew <span class="foreign">satan;</span> see <span class="crossreference">Satan</span>). It is an agent noun from Greek <span class="foreign">diaballein</span> &quot;to slander, attack,&quot; literally &quot;to throw across,&quot; from <span class="foreign">dia</span> &quot;across, through&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">dia-</span>) + <span class="foreign">ballein</span> &quot;to throw&quot; (from PIE root <span class="crossreference">*gwele-</span> &quot;to throw, reach&quot;).</p>

<p>Jerome re-introduced <span class="foreign">Satan</span> in Latin bibles, and English translators have used both words in different measures. In Vulgate, as in Greek, <span class="foreign">diabolus</span> and <span class="foreign">dæmon</span> (see <span class="crossreference">demon</span>) were distinct, but they have merged in English and other Germanic languages.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;false god, heathen god&quot; is from c. 1200. Sense of &quot;diabolical person, person resembling a devil or demon in character&quot; is from late 12c. Playful use for &quot;clever rogue&quot; is from c. 1600. As an expletive and in expletive phrases from c. 1200.</p>

<p>Meaning &quot;sand spout, dust storm&quot; is from 1835. In U.S. place names, the word often represents a native word such as Algonquian <span class="foreign">manito</span>, more properly &quot;spirit, god.&quot; Phrase <span class="foreign">a devil way</span> (c. 1300) was originally &quot;Hell-ward, to Hell,&quot; but by late 14c. it was a mere expression of irritation. Meaning &quot;errand-boy in a printing office&quot; is from 1680s, perhaps because they were often blackened by the ink (devils then being popularly supposed to be black).</p>

<p><span class="foreign">Devil&#x27;s books</span> &quot;playing cards&quot; is from 1729, but the cited quote says they&#x27;ve been called that &quot;time out of mind&quot; (the four of clubs is <span class="foreign">the devil&#x27;s bedposts</span>); <p>It has been speculated that Old English had <span class="foreign">*diegan</span>, from the same source, but it is not in any of the surviving texts and the preferred words were <span class="foreign">steorfan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">starve</span>), <span class="foreign">sweltan</span> (see <span class="crossreference">swelter</span>), <span class="foreign">wesan dead</span> (&quot;become dead&quot;), also <span class="foreign">forðgan</span> and other euphemisms.</p>
<p>Languages usually don&#x27;t borrow words from abroad for central life experiences, but &quot;die&quot; words are an exception; they often are hidden or changed euphemistically out of superstitious dread. A Dutch euphemism translates as &quot;to give the pipe to Maarten.&quot;</p>
<p>Regularly spelled <span class="foreign">dege</span> through 15c., and still pronounced &quot;dee&quot; by some in Lancashire and Scotland. Of plants, &quot;become devitalized, wither,&quot; late 14c.; in a general sense of &quot;come to an end&quot; from mid-13c. Meaning &quot;be consumed with a great longing or yearning&quot; (as in <span class="foreign">dying to go</span>) is colloquial, from 1709. Used figuratively (of sounds, etc.) from 1580s; to <span class="foreign"><strong>die away</strong></span> &quot;diminish gradually&quot; is from 1670s. To <span class="foreign"><strong>die down</strong></span> &quot;subside&quot; is by 1834. Related: <span class="foreign">Died</span>; <span class="foreign">dies</span>.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>die out</strong></span> &quot;become extinct&quot; is from 1865. To <span class="foreign"><strong>die game</strong></span> &quot;preserve a bold, resolute, and defiant spirit to the end&quot; (especially of one facing the gallows) is from 1793. Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>never say die</strong></span> &quot;don&#x27;t give up or in&quot; is by 1822; the earliest contexts are in sailors&#x27; jargon.</p>
