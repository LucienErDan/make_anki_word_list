<p>Medieval Latin <span class="foreign">catholicus</span> was practically synonymous with <span class="foreign">Christian</span> and meant &quot;constituting or conforming to the church, its faith and organization&quot; (as opposed to local sects or heresies). With capital <span class="foreign">C-</span>, applied by Protestants to the Church in Rome c. 1554, after the Reformation began. General sense of &quot;embracing all, universal&quot; in English is from 1550s. Meaning &quot;not narrow-minded or bigoted&quot; is from 1580s. The Latin word was rendered in Old English as <span class="foreign">eallgeleaflic</span>.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/catholic#etymonline_v_5498"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of liberal" href="/word/liberal#etymonline_v_9466">liberal (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>mid-14c., &quot;generous,&quot; also &quot;nobly born, noble, free;&quot; from late 14c. as &quot;selfless, magnanimous, admirable;&quot; from early 15c. in a bad sense, &quot;extravagant, unrestrained,&quot; from Old French <span class="foreign">liberal</span> &quot;befitting free people; noble, generous; willing, zealous&quot; (12c.), and directly from Latin <span class="foreign">liberalis</span> &quot;noble, gracious, munificent, generous,&quot; literally &quot;of freedom, pertaining to or befitting a free person,&quot; from <span class="foreign">liber</span> &quot;free, unrestricted, unimpeded; unbridled, unchecked, licentious.&quot;</p>

<p>This is conjectured to be from PIE <span class="foreign">*leudh-ero-</span>, which probably originally meant &quot;belonging to the people,&quot; though the precise semantic development is obscure; but compare <span class="crossreference">frank</span> (adj.). This was a suffixed form of the base <span class="foreign">*leudh-</span> (2) &quot;people&quot; (source also of Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">ljudu</span>, Lithuanian <span class="foreign">liaudis</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">leod</span>, German <span class="foreign">Leute</span> &quot;nation, people;&quot; Old High German <span class="foreign">liut</span> &quot;person, people&quot;).</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">Who hath indeed, most like a liberal villain,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Confess&#x27;d the vile encounters they have had</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">A thousand times in secret.</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">[&quot;Much Ado,&quot; IV.1.93]</blockquote>

<p><span class="foreign">Liberal</span> was used 16c.-17c. as a term of reproach with the meaning &quot;free from restraint in speech or action.&quot; The Enlightenment revived it in a positive sense &quot;free from prejudice, tolerant, not bigoted or narrow,&quot; which emerged 1776-88. In 19c. often theological rather than political, opposed to <span class="foreign">orthodox</span>, used of Unitarians, Universalists, etc. For educational use, see <span class="crossreference">liberal arts</span>.</p>

<p>Purely in reference to political opinion, &quot;tending in favor of freedom and democracy,&quot; it dates from c. 1801, from French <span class="foreign">libéral</span>. In English the label at first was applied by opponents (often in the French form and with suggestions of foreign lawlessness) to the party more favorable to individual political freedoms. But also (especially in U.S. politics) tending to mean &quot;favorable to government action to effect social change,&quot; which seems at times to draw more from the religious sense of &quot;free from prejudice in favor of traditional opinions and established institutions&quot; (and thus open to new ideas and plans of reform), which dates from 1823.</p>
<blockquote>This is the attitude of mind which has come to be known as liberal. It implies vigorous convictions, tolerance for the opinions of others, and a persistent desire for sound progress. It is a method of approach which has played a notable and constructive part in our history, and which merits a thorough trial today in the attack on our absorbingly interesting American task. [Guy Emerson, &quot;The New Frontier,&quot; 1920]</blockquote></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/liberal#etymonline_v_9466"><span>Relate entries &amp;
