title="Origin and meaning of left" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_51860"><em>left</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, &quot;the left-hand side, the side opposite the right,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">left</span> (adj.). In military formations with reference to the center; of river banks it implies going in the direction the current flows; in an assembly in reference to the seat of the presiding officer; in baseball in reference to the point of view of the batter.</p>

<p>Political sense &quot;the democratic or liberal party&quot; arose from the custom of assigning those members of a legislative body to the left side of a chamber. This usage is first attested in English in 1837 (by Carlyle, in reference to the French Revolution), and probably is a loan-translation of French <span class="foreign">la gauche</span> (1791), said to have originated during the seating of the French National Assembly in 1789 in which the nobility took the seats on the President&#x27;s right and left the Third Estate to sit on the left. The term became general in U.S. and British political speech c. 1900. Century Dictionary and OED 2nd ed. both refer to this as primarily in reference to continental European politics.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_51860"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of left" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_43307"><em>left</em> </a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;remaining,&quot; past tense and past participle of <span class="crossreference">leave</span> (v.).</section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_43307"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of left" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_6652"><em>left</em> (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>c. 1200, &quot;opposite of right,&quot; probably from Kentish and northern English forms of Old English <span class="foreign">*lyft</span> &quot;weak; foolish&quot; (in <span class="foreign">lyft-adl</span> &quot;lameness, paralysis&quot;). Compare East Frisian <span class="foreign">luf</span>, Dutch dialectal <span class="foreign">loof</span> &quot;weak, worthless&quot;).</p>
<p>Sense of &quot;opposite of right&quot; is from the left being usually the weaker hand), a derived sense also found in cognate Middle Dutch and Low German <span class="foreign">luchter</span>, <span class="foreign">luft</span>. Compare Lithuanian <span class="foreign">kairys</span> &quot;left&quot; and Lettish <span class="foreign">kreilis</span> &quot;left hand&quot; both from a root that yields words for &quot;twisted, crooked.&quot;</p>
<p>The usual Old English <span class="foreign">winstre</span>/<span class="foreign">winestra</span> &quot;left&quot; (adj.); &quot;left hand,&quot; literally &quot;friendlier,&quot; a euphemism used superstitiously to avoid invoking the unlucky forces connected with the left side (compare <span class="crossreference">sinister</span>). The Kentish word itself might have been originally a taboo replacement, if instead it represents PIE <span class="foreign">*laiwo-</span> &quot;considered conspicuous&quot; (represented in Greek <span class="foreign">laios</span>, Latin <span class="foreign">laevus</span>, and Russian <span class="foreign">levyi</span>). Greek also uses a euphemism for &quot;left,&quot; <span class="foreign">aristeros</span> &quot;the better one&quot; (compare also Avestan <span class="foreign">vairyastara-</span> &quot;to the left,&quot; from <span class="foreign">vairya-</span> &quot;desirable&quot;).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;being on the left-hand side&quot; is from c. 1300. As an adverb from early 14c. For political senses, see <span class="crossreference">left</span> (n.). Used at least since c. 1600 in various senses of &quot;irregular, illicit;&quot; earlier proverbial sense was &quot;opposite of what is expressed&quot; (mid-15c.), for example <span class="foreign"><strong>over the left (shoulder)</strong></span> &quot;not at all,&quot; added to a statement to negate or neglect what was just said (1705). To have <span class="foreign"><strong>two left feet</strong></span> &quot;be clumsy&quot; is attested by 1902.</p>
<p>Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>out in left field</strong></span> &quot;out of touch with pertinent realities&quot; is attested from 1944, from the baseball fielding position that tends to be far removed from the play (<span class="foreign">left field</span> in baseball attested by 1867). The Parisian <span class="foreign"><strong>Left Bank</strong></span> (of the River Seine) has been associated with intellectual and artistic culture at least since 1893; <span class="foreign"><strong>Left Coast</strong></span> &quot;Pacific Coast of the U.S.&quot; is by 1980s.</p>
<p>German <span class="foreign">link</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">linker</span> &quot;left&quot; are said to be not directly related to these, being instead from Old High German <span class="foreign">slinc</span> and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">slink</span> &quot;left,&quot; related to Swedish <span class="foreign">linka</span> &quot;limp,&quot; <span class="foreign">slinka</span> &quot;dangle,&quot; and Old English <span class="foreign">slincan</span> &quot;crawl&quot; (Modern English <span class="crossreference">slink</span>).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/left#etymonline_v_6652"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of hand" href="/word/hand#etymonline_v_1424"><em>hand</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">hond</span>, <span class="foreign">hand</span> &quot;the human hand;&quot; also &quot;side, part, direction&quot; (in defining position, to either right or left); also &quot;power, control, possession&quot; (on the notion of the hand&#x27;s grip or hold), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*handuz</span> (source also of Old Saxon, Old Frisian, Dutch, German <span class="foreign">hand</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">hönd</span>, Gothic <span class="foreign">handus</span>), which is of uncertain origin.</p>
<p>The original Old English plural <span class="foreign">handa</span> was superseded in Middle English by <span class="foreign">handen</span>, later <span class="foreign">hands</span>. Indo-European &quot;hand&quot; words tend to be from roots meaning &quot;seize, take, collect&quot; or are extended from words originally meaning only a part of the hand (such as Irish <span class="foreign">lam</span>, Welsh <span class="foreign">llaw</span>, cognate with Latin <span class="foreign">palma</span> and originally meaning &quot;palm of the hand&quot;). One ancient root (<span class="crossreference">*man-</span> (2)), represented by Latin <span class="foreign">manus</span> is the source of Old English <span class="foreign">mund</span> &quot;hand,&quot; but more usually meaning &quot;protection, guardianship; a protector, guardian.&quot;</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;manual worker, person who does something with his hands&quot; is from 1580s, hence &quot;hired workman&quot; (1630s) and &quot;sailor in a ship&#x27;s crew&quot; (1660s). Meaning &quot;agency, part in doing something&quot; is from 1590s. Clock and watch sense is from 1570s. Meaning &quot;round of applause&quot; is from 1838. The linear measure of 4 inches (originally 3) is from 1560s, now used only in giving the height of horses. The meaning &quot;playing cards held in one player&#x27;s hand&quot; is from 1620s; that of &quot;a round at a card game&quot; is from 1620s. Meaning &quot;handwriting&quot; is from late 14c.; also &quot;one&#x27;s style of penmanship&quot; (early 15c.). The word in reference to the various uses of hands in making a pledge is by c. 1200; specifically &quot;one&#x27;s pledge of marriage&quot; by late 14c.</p>
<p><span class="foreign"><strong>First hand</strong></span><strong>, </strong><span class="foreign"><strong>second hand</strong></span>, etc. (mid-15c.) are from the notion of something being passed from hand to hand. <span class="foreign"><strong>At hand</strong></span> is from c. 1200 as &quot;near in time,&quot; c. 1300 as &quot;within reach.&quot; <span class="foreign"><strong>Out of hand</strong></span> (1590s) is opposite of <span class="foreign"><strong>in hand</strong></span> &quot;under control&quot; (c. 1200). Adverbial phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>hand-over-fist</strong></span> (1803) is nautical, suggestive of hauling or climbing by passing the hands one before the other alternately.</p>
<p>Phrase <span class="foreign"><strong>on the one hand ... on the other hand</strong></span> is recorded from 1630s, a figurative use of the physical sense of <span class="foreign">hand</span> in reference to position on one side or the other side of the body (as in <span class="foreign">the lefthand side</span>), which goes back to Old English <span class="foreign">Hands up!</span> as a command from a policeman, robber, etc., is from 1863, from the image of holding up one&#x27;s hands as a token of submission or non-resistance. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hand-to-hand</strong></span> &quot;in close contact,&quot; of fighting, is from c. 1400. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hand-to-mouth</strong></span> is from c. 1500. <span class="foreign"><strong>Hand-in-hand</strong></span> attested from c. 1500 as &quot;with hands clasped;&quot; figurative sense of &quot;concurrently&quot; recorded from 1570s.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/hand#etymonline_v_1424"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of hand" href="/word/hand#etymonline_v_41505"><em>hand</em> (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">c. 1400, &quot;take charge of, seize,&quot; from <span class="crossreference">hand</span> (n.).
