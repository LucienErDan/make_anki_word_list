Perhaps from Frankish <span class="foreign">*blund</span> or another Germanic source (compare Dutch, German, Danish <span class="foreign">blond</span>).</p>
<p>If it is a Germanic word, it is possibly related to Old English <span class="foreign">blonden-feax</span> &quot;gray-haired,&quot; from <span class="foreign">blondan</span>, <span class="foreign">blandan</span> &quot;to mix&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">blend</span> (v.)). According to Littré, the original sense of the French word was &quot;a colour midway between golden and light chestnut,&quot; which might account for the notion of &quot;mixed.&quot; [But Century Dictionary finds this &quot;hardly probable.&quot;]</p>
<p>Old English <span class="foreign">beblonden</span> meant &quot;dyed,&quot; so it is also possible that the root meaning of <span class="foreign">blonde</span>, if it is Germanic, may be &quot;dyed,&quot; as ancient Teutonic warriors were noted for dying their hair. Du Cange, however, writes that <span class="foreign">blundus</span> was a vulgar pronunciation of Latin <span class="foreign">flavus</span> &quot;yellow.&quot; Another guess (discounted by German etymologists), is that it represents a Vulgar Latin <span class="foreign">*albundus</span>, from <span class="foreign">alba</span> &quot;white.&quot;</p>
<p>The word was reintroduced into English 17c. from French, and was until recently still felt as French, hence <span class="foreign">blonde</span> (with French feminine ending) for females. Italian <span class="foreign">biondo</span>, Spanish <span class="foreign">blondo</span>, Old Provençal <span class="foreign">blon</span> are said to be ultimately of Germanic origin.</p>

In the time of Ovid (A.U.C. 711) much fair hair was imported from Germany, by the Romans, as it was considered quite the fashionable color.
