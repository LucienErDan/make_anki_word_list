This is conjectured to be from a PIE root <span class="foreign">*gheis-</span>, used in forming words involving the notions of excitement, amazement, or fear (source also of Sanskrit <span class="foreign">hedah</span> &quot;wrath;&quot; Avestan <span class="foreign">zaesha-</span> &quot;horrible, frightful;&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">usgaisjan</span>, Old English <span class="foreign">gæstan</span> &quot;to frighten&quot;).</p>
<p><span class="foreign">Ghost</span> is the English representative of the usual West Germanic word for &quot;supernatural being.&quot; In Christian writing in Old English it is used to render Latin <span class="foreign">spiritus</span> (see <span class="crossreference">spirit</span> (n.)), a sense preserved in <span class="foreign">Holy Ghost</span>. Sense of &quot;disembodied spirit of a dead person,&quot; especially imagined as wandering among the living or haunting them, is attested from late 14c. and returns the word toward its likely prehistoric sense.</p>
<p>Most Indo-European words for &quot;soul, spirit&quot; also double with reference to supernatural spirits. Many have a base sense of &quot;appearance&quot; (such as Greek <span class="foreign">phantasma</span>; French <span class="foreign">spectre</span>; Polish <span class="foreign">widmo</span>, from Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">videti</span> &quot;to see;&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">scin</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">giskin</span>, originally &quot;appearance, apparition,&quot; related to Old English <span class="foreign">scinan</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">skinan</span> &quot;to shine&quot;). Other concepts are in French <span class="foreign">revenant</span>, literally &quot;returning&quot; (from the other world), Old Norse <span class="foreign">aptr-ganga</span>, literally &quot;back-comer.&quot; Breton <span class="foreign">bugelnoz</span> is literally &quot;night-child.&quot; Latin <span class="crossreference">manes</span> probably is a euphemism.</p>
<p>The <span class="foreign">gh-</span> spelling appeared early 15c. in Caxton, influenced by Flemish and Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">gheest</span>, but was rare in English before mid-16c. Sense of &quot;slight suggestion, mere shadow or semblance&quot; (in <span class="foreign"><strong>ghost image</strong></span><strong>, </strong><span class="foreign"><strong>ghost of a chance</strong></span>, etc.) is first recorded 1610s; sense of &quot;one who secretly does work for another&quot; is from 1884. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost town</strong></span> is from 1908. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost story</strong></span> is by 1811. <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost-word</strong></span> &quot;apparent word or false form in a manuscript due to a blunder&quot; is from 1886 (Skeat). <span class="foreign"><strong>Ghost in the machine</strong></span> was British philosopher Gilbert Ryle&#x27;s term (1949) for &quot;the mind viewed as separate from the body.&quot; The American Indian <span class="foreign"><strong>ghost dance</strong></span> is from 1890. To <span class="foreign"><strong>give up the ghost</strong></span> &quot;die&quot; was in Old English.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/ghost#etymonline_v_6062"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of so long" href="/word/so%20long#etymonline_v_25685">so long (interj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>parting salutation, 1860, of unknown origin, perhaps from a German idiom (compare German parting salutation <span class="foreign">adieu so lange</span>, the full sense of which probably is something like &quot;farewell, whilst (we&#x27;re apart)&quot;); or perhaps from Hebrew <span class="foreign">shalom</span> (via Yiddish <span class="foreign">sholom</span>). Some have noted a similarity to Scandinavian leave-taking phrases, such as Norwegian <span class="foreign">Adjø så lenge, Farvel så lenge, Mor&#x27;n så lenge</span>, literally &quot;bye so long, farewell so long, morning so long;&quot; and Swedish <span class="foreign">Hej så länge</span> &quot;good-bye for now,&quot; with <span class="foreign">så länge</span> &quot;for now&quot; attested since 1850 according to Swedish sources. Most etymology sources seem to lean toward the German origin. <span class="foreign">So long</span> (adv.) &quot;for such a long time&quot; is from late Old English.</p>

<p>Earlier guesses that it was a sailors&#x27; corruption of a South Pacific form of Arabic <span class="foreign">salaam</span> are not now regarded as convincing. &quot;Dictionary of American Slang&quot; also adds to the list of candidates Irish <span class="foreign">slán</span> &quot;safe,&quot; said to be used as a salutation in parting. The phrase seems to have turned up simultaneously in America, Britain, and perhaps Canada, originally among lower classes. First attested use is in title and text of the last poem in Whitman&#x27;s &quot;Leaves of Grass&quot; in the 1860 edition.</p>

<blockquote class="poetry">An unknown sphere, more real than I dream&#x27;d, more direct, darts awakening rays about me -- So long!</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">Remember my words -- I may again return,</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">I love you -- I depart from materials;</blockquote>
<blockquote class="poetry">I am as one disembodied, triumphant, dead.</blockquote>

<p>Whitman&#x27;s friend and fan William Sloane Kennedy wrote in 1923:</p>
<blockquote>The salutation of parting -- &#x27;So long!&#x27; -- was, I believe, until recent years, unintelligible to the majority of persons in America, especially in the interior, and to members of the middle and professional classes. I had never heard of it until I read it in Leaves of Grass, but since then have quite often heard it used by the laboring class and other classes in New England cities. Walt wrote to me, defining &#x27;so long&#x27; thus: &quot;A salutation of departure, greatly used among sailors, sports, &amp; prostitutes -- the sense of it is &#x27;Till we meet again,&#x27; -- conveying an inference that somehow they will doubtless so meet, sooner or later.&quot; ... It is evidently about equivalent to our &#x27;See you later.&#x27; The phrase is reported as used by farm laborers near Banff, Scotland. In Canada it is frequently heard; &#x27;and its use is not entirely confined to the vulgar.&#x27; It is in common use among the working classes of Liverpool and among sailors at Newcastle-upon-Tyne, and in Dorsetshire. ... The London Globe suggests that the expression is derived from the Norwegian &#x27;Saa laenge,&#x27; a common form of &#x27;farewell,&#x27; au revoir. If so, the phrase was picked up from the Norwegians in America, where &#x27;So long&#x27; first was heard. The expression is now (1923) often used by the literary and artistic classes.</blockquote>
