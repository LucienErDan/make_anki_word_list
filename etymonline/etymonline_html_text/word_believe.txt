title="Origin and meaning of believe" href="/word/believe#etymonline_v_8277"><em>believe</em> (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">belyfan</span> &quot;to have faith or confidence&quot; (in a person), earlier <span class="foreign">geleafa</span> (Mercian), <span class="foreign">gelefa</span> (Northumbrian), <span class="foreign">gelyfan</span> (West Saxon), from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*ga-laubjan</span> &quot;to believe,&quot; perhaps literally &quot;hold dear (or valuable, or satisfactory), to love&quot; (source also of Old Saxon <span class="foreign">gilobian</span> &quot;believe,&quot; Dutch <span class="foreign">geloven</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">gilouben</span>, German <span class="foreign">glauben</span>), ultimately a compound based on PIE root <span class="crossreference">*leubh-</span> &quot;to care, desire, love&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">belief</span>).</p>
<p>Meaning &quot;be persuaded of the truth of&quot; (a doctrine, system, religion, etc.) is from mid-13c.; meaning &quot;credit upon the grounds of authority or testimony without complete demonstration, accept as true&quot; is from early 14c. General sense &quot;be of the opinion, think&quot; is from c. 1300. Related: <span class="foreign">Believed</span> (formerly occasionally <span class="foreign">beleft</span>); <span class="foreign">believing</span>.</p>
<p>The form <span class="foreign">beleeve</span> was common till 17c., the spelling then changed, perhaps by influence of <span class="foreign">relieve</span>, etc. To <span class="foreign">believe on</span> instead of <span class="foreign">in</span> was more common in 16c. but now is a peculiarity of theology; <span class="foreign">believe of</span> also sometimes was used in 17c. Expression <span class="foreign"><strong>believe it or not</strong></span> attested by 1874; Robert Ripley&#x27;s newspaper cartoon of the same name is from 1918. Emphatic <span class="foreign"><strong>you better believe</strong></span> attested from 1854.</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/believe#etymonline_v_8277"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of make-believe" href="/word/make-believe#etymonline_v_2239">make-<em>believe</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;pretense, false or fanciful representation,&quot; 1811, from the verbal phrase <span class="foreign">make believe</span>, which was used in children&#x27;s talk by 1773 for &quot;pretend;&quot; see <span class="crossreference">make</span> (v.) + <span class="crossreference">believe</span>.
