<p>The word and the practice were introduced in Newfoundland by Breton fishermen and spread from there to the Maritimes and New England.</p>
<blockquote>CHOWDER. A favorite dish in New England, made of fish, pork, onions, and biscuit stewed together. Cider and champagne are sometimes added. Pic-nic parties to the sea-shore generally have a dish of <span class="foreign">chowder</span>, prepared by themselves in some grove near the beach, from fish caught at the same time. [John Russell Bartlett, &quot;Dictionary of Americanisms,&quot; 1859]</blockquote>
<p> The modern form of it usually features clams. In New England, usually made with milk; the Manhattan version is made with tomatoes. <p>The English word was borrowed into Dutch, German, Swedish, Danish, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, French, and Russian. Used since 1800 in North America as a general (hostile) name for intoxicating liquors.</p>
