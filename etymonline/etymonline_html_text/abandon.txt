title="Origin and meaning of abandon" href="/word/abandon#etymonline_v_14"><em>abandon</em> (v.)<i class="fa fa-image" style="margin-left:5px;color:#888"></i></a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>late 14c., &quot;to give up (something) absolutely, relinquish control, give over utterly;&quot; also reflexively, &quot;surrender (oneself), yield (oneself) utterly&quot; (to religion, fornication, etc.), from Old French abandonner &quot;surrender, release; give freely, permit,&quot; also reflexive, &quot;devote (oneself)&quot; (12c.).</p>
<p>The Old French word was formed from the adverbial phrase à bandon &quot;at will, at discretion,&quot; from à &quot;at, to&quot; (from Latin ad; see <span class="crossreference">ad-</span>) + bandon &quot;power, jurisdiction,&quot; from Latin bannum, &quot;proclamation,&quot; which is from a Frankish or other Germanic word, from Proto-Germanic *bannan- &quot;proclaim, summon, outlaw&quot; (things all done by proclamation); see <span class="crossreference">ban</span> (v.).</p>
<blockquote><span class="foreign">Mettre sa forest à bandon</span> was a feudal law phrase in the 13th cent. = <span class="foreign">mettre sa forêt à permission</span>, i.e. to open it freely to any one for pasture or to cut wood in; hence the later sense of giving up one&#x27;s rights for a time, letting go, leaving, abandoning. [Auguste Brachet, &quot;An Etymological Dictionary of the French Language,&quot; transl. G.W. Kitchin, Oxford, 1878]</blockquote>
<p>Meaning &quot;to leave, desert, forsake (someone or something) in need&quot; is from late 15c.  Related: <span class="foreign">Abandoned</span>; <span class="foreign">abandoning</span>.</p>
<p>Etymologically, the word carries a sense of &quot;put (something) under someone else&#x27;s control,&quot; and the earliest appearance of the word in English is as an adverb (mid-13c.) with the sense &quot;under (one&#x27;s) control,&quot; hence also &quot;unrestricted.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>Again, as that which is placed at the absolute command of one party must by the same act be entirely given up by the original possessor, it was an easy step from the sense of conferring the command of a thing upon some particular person to that of renouncing all claim to authority over the subject matter, without particular reference to the party into whose hands it might come ; and thus in modern times the word has come to be used almost exclusively in the sense renunciation or desertion. [Wedgwood]</blockquote>
