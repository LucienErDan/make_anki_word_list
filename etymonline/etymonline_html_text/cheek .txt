class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;either of the two fleshy sides of the face below the eyes,&quot; Old English <span class="foreign">ceace</span>, <span class="foreign">cece</span> &quot;jaw, jawbone,&quot; in late Old English also &quot;the fleshy wall of the mouth,&quot; of uncertain origin, from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*kaukon</span> (source also of Middle Low German <span class="foreign">kake</span> &quot;jaw, jawbone,&quot; Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">kake</span> &quot;jaw,&quot; Dutch <span class="foreign">kaak</span>), not found outside West Germanic, probably a substratum word.</p>
<p>Words for &quot;cheek,&quot; &quot;jaw,&quot; and &quot;chin&quot; tend to run together in IE languages (compare PIE <span class="foreign">*genw-</span>, source of Greek <span class="foreign">genus</span> &quot;jaw, cheek,&quot; <span class="foreign">geneion</span> &quot;chin,&quot; and English <span class="crossreference">chin</span>); Aristotle considered the chin as the front of the &quot;jaws&quot; and the cheeks as the back of them. The other Old English word for &quot;cheek&quot; was <span class="foreign">ceafl</span> (see <span class="crossreference">jowl</span> (n.1)).</p>
<blockquote>A thousand men he [Samson] slow eek with his hond, And had no wepen but an asses cheek. [Chaucer, &quot;Monk&#x27;s Tale&quot;]</blockquote>
<p>In reference to the buttocks from c. 1600. Sense of &quot;brazen insolence&quot; is from 1840, perhaps from a notion akin to that which led to <span class="foreign">jaw</span> &quot;insolent speech,&quot; <span class="foreign">mouth off</span>, etc. To <span class="foreign"><strong>turn the other cheek</strong></span> is an allusion to Matthew v.39 and Luke vi.29. <span class="foreign"><strong>Cheek-by-jowl</strong></span> &quot;with cheeks close together,&quot; hence &quot;in intimate contact&quot; is from 1570s; earlier in same sense was cheek-by-cheek (early 14c.). In ballroom dancing, <span class="foreign"><strong>cheek-to-cheek</strong></span> is from 1919 (earlier it was a measurement of apples). </p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/cheek#etymonline_v_11212"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of cheeky" href="/word/cheeky#etymonline_v_27983">cheeky (adj.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>&quot;impudent, presumptuous,&quot; 1859 (1850 as the nickname of a misbehaving boy in a story), from <span class="crossreference">cheek</span> in its sense of &quot;insolence&quot; + <span class="crossreference">-y</span> (2). <p>Intransitive sense from c. 1400. Meaning &quot;gasp for breath&quot; is from early 15c.
