&quot;series, calculation,&quot; also &quot;story, tale, statement, deposition, narrative, fable, accusation, action of telling,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*talō</span> (source also of Dutch <span class="foreign">taal</span> &quot;speech, language,&quot; Danish <span class="foreign">tale</span> &quot;speech, talk, discourse,&quot; German <span class="foreign">Erzählung</span> &quot;story,&quot; Gothic <span class="foreign">talzjan</span> &quot;to teach&quot;), from PIE root <span class="foreign">*del-</span> (2) &quot;to recount, count.&quot; The secondary Modern English sense of &quot;number, numerical reckoning&quot; (c. 1200) probably was the primary one in Germanic; see <span class="crossreference">tell</span> (v.), <span class="crossreference">teller</span> and Old Frisian <span class="foreign">tale</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">tal</span>, Old Saxon <span class="foreign">tala</span>, Danish <span class="foreign">tal</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">zala</span>, German <span class="foreign">Zahl</span> &quot;number.&quot;</p>
<p>The ground sense of the Modern English word in its main meaning, then, might have been &quot;an account of things in their due order.&quot; Related to <span class="crossreference">talk</span> (v.) and <span class="crossreference">tell</span> (v.). Perhaps originally &quot;host of warriors:&quot; Compare Old Norse <span class="foreign">folk</span> &quot;people,&quot; also &quot;army, detachment;&quot; and Lithuanian <span class="foreign">pulkas</span> &quot;crowd,&quot; Old Church Slavonic <span class="foreign">pluku</span> &quot;division of an army&quot; (hence Russian <span class="foreign">polk</span> &quot;regiment&quot;), both believed to have been borrowed from Proto-Germanic. Old English <span class="foreign">folcstede</span> could mean both &quot;dwelling-place&quot; and &quot;battlefield.&quot; According to Watkins, from PIE <span class="foreign">*ple-go-</span>, suffixed form of root <span class="crossreference">*pele-</span> (1) &quot;to fill,&quot; which would make it cognate with Greek <span class="foreign">plethos</span> &quot;people, multitude,&quot; and Latin <span class="foreign">plebes</span>, &quot;the populace, the common people.&quot; Boutkan thinks both the Germanic and Balto-Slavic could be a common borrowing from a substrate language.</p>
<p>Superseded in most senses by <span class="crossreference">people</span>. Generally a collective noun in Middle English, however plural <span class="crossreference">folks</span> is attested from 15c. Old English <span class="foreign">folc</span> was commonly used in forming compounds (59 are listed in the Clark Hall dictionary), such as <span class="foreign">folccwide</span> &quot;popular saying,&quot; <span class="foreign">folcgemot</span> &quot;town or district meeting;&quot; <span class="foreign">folcwoh</span> &quot;deception of the public.&quot; Modern use of <span class="foreign">folk</span> as an adjective is from c. 1850 (see <span class="crossreference">folklore</span>).</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/folk#etymonline_v_11740"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of fairy-tale" href="/word/fairy-tale#etymonline_v_32947">fairy-<em>tale</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;oral narrative centered on magical tests, quests, and transformations,&quot; 1749, translating French <span class="foreign">Conte de feés</span>, the name given to her collection by Madame d&#x27;Aulnois (1698, translated into English 1699).
