<p>Sense of &quot;sing in a full, rolling voice&quot; (first attested 1570s) and that of &quot;fish with a moving line&quot; (c. 1600) both are extended technical uses from the general sense of &quot;roll, trundle,&quot; the former from &quot;sing in the manner of a catch or round,&quot; the latter perhaps confused with <span class="crossreference">trail</span> or <span class="crossreference">trawl</span>. Figurative sense of &quot;to lure on as with a moving bait, entice, allure&quot; is from 1560s. Meaning &quot;to cruise in search of sexual encounters&quot; is recorded from 1967, originally in homosexual slang.</p>
<p>The internet sense (everyone seems to have his own definition of it) seems to date to the late 1980s or early 1990s and the Newsgroups era, and the verbal use is perhaps older than the noun. It seems to combine <span class="foreign">troll</span> (v.) in the &quot;fish with a moving line&quot; sense (itself confused with <span class="foreign">trawl</span>) and <span class="crossreference">troll</span> (n.1) &quot;troublesome imp supposed to live underground.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/troll#etymonline_v_17856"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of bean" href="/word/bean#etymonline_v_8202">bean (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">bean</span> &quot;bean, pea, legume,&quot; from Proto-Germanic <span class="foreign">*bauno</span> (source also of Old Norse <span class="foreign">baun</span>, Middle Dutch <span class="foreign">bone</span>, Dutch <span class="foreign">boon</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">bona</span>, German <span class="foreign">Bohne</span>), and related to Latin <span class="foreign">faba</span> &quot;bean;&quot; Greek <span class="foreign">phakos</span> &quot;lentil;&quot; Albanian <span class="foreign">bathë</span> &quot;horse-bean;&quot; Old Prussian <span class="foreign">babo</span>, Russian <span class="foreign">bob</span> &quot;bean,&quot; but the original form is obscure. Watkins suggests a PIE reduplicated root <span class="foreign">*bha-bhā-</span> &quot;broad bean;&quot; de Vaan writes that the Italic, Slavic and Germanic &quot;are probably independent loanwords from a European substratum word of the form <span class="foreign">*bab-</span> (or similar) &#x27;bean&#x27;.&quot;</p>
<p>As a metaphor for &quot;something of small value&quot; it is attested from c. 1300 (<span class="foreign"><strong>hill of beans</strong></span> as something not much to amount to is from 1863). Meaning &quot;head&quot; is U.S. baseball slang 1905 (in <span class="foreign"><strong>bean-ball</strong></span> &quot;a pitch thrown at the head&quot;); thus slang verb <span class="foreign">bean</span> meaning &quot;to hit on the head,&quot; attested from 1910. <span class="foreign"><strong>Bean-shooter</strong></span> as a child&#x27;s weapon for mischief, a sort of small sling-shot to fire beans, is attested from 1876. Slang <span class="foreign"><strong>bean-counter</strong></span> &quot;accountant&quot; recorded by 1971.</p>
<p>The notion of lucky or magic beans in English folklore is from the exotic beans or large seeds, carried from the Caribbean or South America by the Gulf Stream, that wash up occasionally in Cornwall and western Scotland. They were cherished, believed to ward off the evil eye and aid in childbirth.</p>
<p>To <span class="foreign"><strong>not know beans</strong></span> &quot;be ignorant&quot; is attested by 1842 in American English, often said to be a New England phrase; it is perhaps from the &quot;object of little worth&quot; sense.
