title="Origin and meaning of gospel" href="/word/gospel#etymonline_v_9072"><em>gospel</em> (n.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined"><p>Old English <span class="foreign">godspel</span> &quot;glad tidings announced by Jesus; one of the four gospels,&quot; literally &quot;good spell,&quot; from <span class="foreign">god</span> &quot;good&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">good</span> (adj.)) + <span class="foreign">spel</span> &quot;story, message&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">spell</span> (n.1)). A translation of Latin <span class="foreign">bona adnuntiatio</span>, itself a translation of Greek <span class="foreign">euangelion</span> &quot;reward for bringing good news&quot; (see <span class="crossreference">evangel</span>). The first element of the Old English word originally had a long &quot;o,&quot; but it shifted under mistaken association with <span class="crossreference">God</span>, as if &quot;God-story&quot; (i.e. the history of Christ).</p>
<blockquote>The mistake was very natural, as the resulting sense was much more obviously appropriate than that of &#x27;good tidings&#x27; for a word which was chiefly known as the name of a sacred book or of a portion of the liturgy. [OED]</blockquote>
<p>The word passed early from English to continental Germanic languages in forms that clearly indicate the first element had shifted to &quot;God,&quot; such as Old Saxon <span class="foreign">godspell</span>, Old High German <span class="foreign">gotspell</span>, Old Norse <span class="foreign">goðspiall</span>. Used of anything as true as the Gospel from mid-13c.; as &quot;any doctrine maintained as of exclusive importance&quot; from 1650s. As an adjective from 1640s. <span class="foreign">Gospel music</span> is by 1955. <span class="foreign">Gospel-gossip</span> was Addison&#x27;s word (&quot;Spectator,&quot; 1711) for &quot;one who is always talking of sermons, texts, etc.&quot;</p></section></object><a data-more="true" class="word_4pc__footer--2yfj3" href="/word/gospel#etymonline_v_9072"><span>Relate entries &amp; more</span> <i class="fa fa-chevron-right" style="opacity:0"></i></a></div><div data-ad-container="true" class="adContainer--6CVz1 horizontal"><span class="adLabel--35hfi">Advertisement</span><ins class="adsbygoogle" data-ad-client="ca-pub-7093273770345366" style="display:block;width:100%" data-ad-format="fluid" data-ad-slot="1604734010" data-ad-layout-key="-fb+5w+4e-db+86"></ins></div><div class="word--C9UPa word_4pc--2SZw8"><object><a class="word__name--TTbAA word_thumbnail__name--1khEg" title="Origin and meaning of gospelize" href="/word/gospelize#etymonline_v_41151">gospelize (v.)</a><section class="word__defination--2q7ZH undefined">&quot;to preach the gospel,&quot; 1640s, from <span class="crossreference">gospel</span> + <span class="crossreference">-ize</span>. <blockquote>The term &#x27;spell&#x27; is generally used for magical procedures which cause harm, or force people to do something against their will -- unlike charms for healing, protection, etc. [&quot;Oxford Dictionary of English Folklore&quot;]</blockquote>
<blockquote>In general terms, the belief underlying the use of spells is that the wish that they embody will be fulfilled, regardless of its goodness or badness, so long as the formula has been correctly pronounced. [Enyclopaedia Britannica, 1941]</blockquote>
